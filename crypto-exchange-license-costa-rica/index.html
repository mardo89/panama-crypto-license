What Is a Crypto Exchange License in Costa Rica?

Costa Rica does not have a standalone crypto exchange license. Instead, crypto exchanges typically register as a Sociedad Anónima (SA) and, if dealing with securities or tokens that qualify as securities, must register with SUGEVAL. For pure crypto-to-crypto or crypto-to-fiat exchanges without security tokens, no specific financial license is required, but the business must comply with anti-money laundering (AML) and tax reporting obligations.

This regulatory approach means that obtaining a "crypto exchange license" in Costa Rica is essentially a company formation plus compliance registration. The lack of a dedicated regime can reduce upfront costs but also creates legal uncertainty. Many founders prefer jurisdictions with clear licensing frameworks, such as Panama, where the process is transparent and the cost is a flat EUR 6,000. In practice, a crypto exchange in Costa Rica operates under the same legal framework as any other financial technology company. You must register with the National Registry, obtain a tax ID, and comply with the General Directorate of Financial Intelligence (UGD) for AML. If your platform lists tokens that could be considered securities, SUGEVAL oversight becomes mandatory. This dual structure is similar to how many jurisdictions regulate crypto under existing frameworks.

Who Needs a Crypto License in Costa Rica?

  • ATMs and kiosk operators
  • OTC desks
  • Custodial wallet providers

If the platform lists tokens that are classified as securities, SUGEVAL registration becomes mandatory. Otherwise, general business registration and AML compliance suffice. License Type & Regulator

The primary regulator for crypto activities in Costa Rica is the Superintendencia General de Valores (SUGEVAL), which oversees securities markets. If your exchange deals with tokenized assets or security tokens, you must register as a securities intermediary. For non-security crypto activities, the General Directorate of Financial Intelligence (UGD) oversees AML compliance.

There is no single "crypto license" type. Instead, businesses operate under a general corporate license (Sociedad Anónima) and, where applicable, a securities intermediary license from SUGEVAL. This dual structure is similar to how many jurisdictions regulate crypto under existing frameworks. The UGD requires all obligated entities to register and submit suspicious transaction reports.

Capital Requirement

Costa Rica does not impose a statutory minimum capital requirement for crypto exchanges that do not handle securities. However, for SUGEVAL-registered entities, the regulator may require a minimum capital based on the type of securities activities. Typically, this ranges from $50,000 to $200,000 depending on the scope of operations.

For non-security exchanges, the company's capital is determined by the founders and should be sufficient to cover operational costs and demonstrate financial stability. Consulting24 recommends at least $10,000 in paid-up capital to satisfy bank due diligence. This is a practical consideration: banks and payment partners often require evidence of capital to open accounts.

Tax Treatment

Costa Rica taxes corporate income at a standard rate of 30%. However, crypto-to-crypto trades may be treated as barter transactions, and gains are subject to capital gains tax (15% on realized gains). VAT (13%) applies to services provided to Costa Rican residents, but crypto exchange fees to non-residents may be exempt.

The tax treatment of crypto is still evolving. Consulting24 advises working with a local tax advisor to structure your operations efficiently. For many founders, Panama's territorial tax system (no tax on foreign-source income) is more attractive. In Panama, income earned outside the country is tax-free, which is a major advantage for international crypto businesses.

For EU jurisdictions like Lithuania, the corporate tax rate is 15%, but the regulatory clarity under MiCA may offset the tax cost. Consulting24 can help you model the tax implications of each jurisdiction.

Step-by-Step Process

The process to set up a crypto exchange in Costa Rica involves the following steps:

  1. Company incorporation: Register a Sociedad Anónima with the National Registry. This includes drafting bylaws, appointing directors, and registering a legal address. Estimated cost: $1,000-$2,500.
  2. AML compliance: Appoint a compliance officer, implement KYC/AML procedures, and register with the UGD if required. This step is critical and often underestimated.
  3. SUGEVAL registration (if applicable): Submit an application with business plan, financial statements, and background checks. This can take 3-6 months.
  4. Bank account opening: Open a corporate bank account in Costa Rica or use an international payment provider. This step can be challenging; see the banking section.
  5. Tax registration: Register with the Ministry of Finance for income tax and VAT.
  6. Launch operations: After all registrations are complete, you can start trading. Ensure ongoing compliance with reporting obligations.

Frequently asked questions

Is there a specific crypto exchange license in Costa Rica?

No, Costa Rica does not have a dedicated crypto exchange license. Businesses operate under a general corporate license (Sociedad Anónima) and, if dealing with security tokens, must register with SUGEVAL. For non-security crypto activities, only AML compliance is required.

How much does a crypto license in Costa Rica cost?

Costs range from $13,000 to $37,500 depending on whether SUGEVAL registration is needed. This includes company incorporation ($1,000-$2,500), SUGEVAL fees ($5,000-$15,000), AML setup ($2,000-$5,000), and legal fees ($5,000-$15,000). Panama offers a flat EUR 6,000 alternative.

How long does it take to get a crypto license in Costa Rica?

The timeline is 3-8 months. Company incorporation takes 2-4 weeks, SUGEVAL registration (if needed) 3-6 months, and AML setup 4-8 weeks. Panama is faster at 4-6 weeks.

What is the minimum capital requirement for a crypto exchange in Costa Rica?

There is no statutory minimum for non-security exchanges. For SUGEVAL-registered entities, capital can range from $50,000 to $200,000. Consulting24 recommends at least $10,000 paid-up capital for bank due diligence.

What taxes apply to crypto exchanges in Costa Rica?

Corporate income tax is 30%. Crypto-to-crypto trades may be treated as barter, with capital gains taxed at 15%. VAT of 13% applies to services for residents. Panama's territorial system (0% on foreign income) is often more favorable.

Can I operate a crypto exchange in Costa Rica without SUGEVAL registration?

Yes, if your platform does not deal with security tokens. Pure crypto-to-crypto or crypto-to-fiat exchanges without security tokens only need company incorporation and AML compliance. However, legal advice is recommended to classify your tokens.

Is it easy to open a bank account for a crypto exchange in Costa Rica?

It can be challenging due to de-risking. Some local banks like BAC Credomatic and Banco Nacional may accept crypto businesses with thorough due diligence. Many exchanges use international payment processors instead.

What are the main benefits of Costa Rica for crypto businesses?

Benefits include political stability, no exchange controls, strategic location, growing tech talent, and low setup costs compared to the EU. However, regulatory uncertainty and longer timelines are drawbacks.

How does Costa Rica compare to Panama for crypto licensing?

Panama offers a dedicated crypto license with a flat EUR 6,000 fee, 4-6 week timeline, no capital requirement, and territorial tax (0% on foreign income). Costa Rica is cheaper upfront but slower and less certain.

What ongoing compliance is required for a Costa Rica crypto exchange?

Ongoing compliance includes AML reporting to the UGD, tax filings, and annual corporate renewals. If SUGEVAL registered, additional reporting on securities activities is required. Consulting24 offers ongoing support.

Can I use Costa Rica to serve customers in the EU?

Yes, but you must comply with EU MiCA regulations if you have EU customers. This may require a CASP license in an EU member state. Costa Rica alone does not provide EU passporting rights.
