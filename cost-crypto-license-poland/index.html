What Is the Poland Crypto License?

The Poland crypto license is a mandatory registration with the KNF for virtual asset service providers (VASPs). It is required for entities offering exchange services between cryptocurrencies and fiat, custody of virtual assets, and other crypto-related activities. The registration is governed by the Polish Act on Counteracting Money Laundering and Terrorism Financing (AML/CFT), which transposes the EU's 5th Anti-Money Laundering Directive. As of 2026, Poland has also implemented the Markets in Crypto-Assets (MiCA) regulation, meaning CASPs must comply with EU-wide standards. The registration process involves demonstrating strong AML procedures, fit and proper management, and adequate capital. Unlike a full license, the registration does not grant passporting rights automatically, but under MiCA, larger operators may transition to a CASP authorization that allows EU-wide operations. The KNF maintains a public register of registered entities, which adds transparency and trust for clients.

Who Needs a Poland Crypto License?

Any company that provides crypto services to Polish residents or operates from Poland must register. This includes:

  • Crypto-to-fiat exchanges
  • Fiat-to-crypto exchanges
  • Crypto-to-crypto trading platforms
  • Custodial wallet providers
  • ATMs dealing in virtual currencies
  • Issuers of crypto assets (if not covered by other exemptions)

Foreign entities targeting the Polish market must also register, often by establishing a local branch or subsidiary. If you are unsure whether your business model requires registration, Consulting24 can provide a preliminary assessment. For example, a crypto exchange based in Germany that actively markets to Polish users would need to register in Poland unless it holds a MiCA license passporting into Poland. The KNF has issued guidance that even non-custodial wallet providers may fall under the definition if they facilitate transfers.

License Type & Regulator

The regulatory body is the Polish Financial Supervision Authority (Komisja Nadzoru Finansowego, KNF). The registration is officially called 'entry in the register of virtual currency activities.' Under MiCA, this will transition to a full CASP authorization, but as of 2026, the KNF still uses the registration model for smaller operators, while larger ones may require a MiCA license. The registration is not a license per se but a mandatory compliance obligation. It does not limit the number of registrants, but the KNF has the power to reject applications if AML procedures are inadequate. The KNF also oversees compliance with MiCA capital tiers and conduct of business rules. For companies offering complex services like trading platforms or custody, the KNF may require a full MiCA authorization, which involves a more rigorous review process and higher capital requirements.

Capital Requirement

Under MiCA, which is fully in force in 2026, capital requirements for CASPs in Poland are tiered: EUR 50,000 for simple exchange services, EUR 125,000 for custody and exchange, and EUR 150,000 for more complex services like trading platforms. These funds must be held in a Polish bank account and be fully paid up before registration. The capital serves as a safeguard for clients and must be maintained throughout the business operations. Some exemptions may apply for smaller entities, but generally, the capital must be evidenced with bank statements or a letter of comfort. For example, a startup offering only crypto-to-fiat exchange would need EUR 50,000, while a full-service exchange with custody would need EUR 125,000. The capital cannot be used for operational expenses; it must remain as a reserve. Consulting24 can advise on the exact tier applicable to your business model.

Tax Treatment

Poland offers a relatively favorable tax environment for crypto businesses. Corporate income tax (CIT) is 19% (9% for small taxpayers with revenue under EUR 2 million). VAT is generally exempt for crypto exchange transactions, but other services may be subject to 23% VAT. Capital gains from crypto trading are taxed at 19% for individuals. There is no specific crypto tax regime, but the tax office has issued clarifications that crypto-to-fiat transactions are taxable events. It is essential to maintain proper accounting records. For example, if your company earns revenue from transaction fees, that income is subject to CIT. Losses from crypto trading can be offset against gains. Consulting24 can connect you with local tax advisors for a detailed analysis. Additionally, Poland has tax treaties with many countries to avoid double taxation.

Allowed Activities

Registered VASPs in Poland can engage in:

  • Exchange between virtual currencies and fiat currencies
  • Exchange between virtual currencies
  • Intermediation in the above exchanges
  • Custody of virtual currencies or private keys
  • Operation of crypto ATMs
  • Issuance of virtual currencies (subject to additional regulations)

Activities not covered include derivatives, securities tokens (regulated separately), and services to sanctioned entities. The KNF prohibits anonymous transactions and requires full KYC/AML compliance. For example, if you plan to offer crypto derivatives, you would need a separate MiFID license. The KNF also restricts services to entities from high-risk jurisdictions. It is important to define your service scope clearly in the application to avoid regulatory gaps.

Step-by-Step Application Process

  1. Pre-application assessment: Determine if your business model requires registration and which MiCA tier applies.
  2. Company setup: Incorporate a Polish company (usually a limited liability company, Sp. z o.o.) with a registered office in Poland.
  3. AML documentation: Prepare internal AML/CFT policies, risk assessment, procedures, and appoint an AML officer.
  4. Capital deposit: Open a Polish bank account and deposit the required minimum capital.
  5. Submit application: File with the KNF via the electronic system, including all supporting documents.
  6. KNF review: The KNF has 2-3 months to review; they may request additional information.
  7. Registration: If approved, the company is entered into the register and can commence operations.

Some banks that accept crypto clients include mBank, PKO BP, and Alior Bank, but they require a valid KNF registration or at least proof of application. As an alternative, payment institutions or EMI accounts (e.g., from Lithuania) can be used. Consulting24 can recommend banking partners and assist with introductions. For example, many clients open a primary account in Poland for capital deposit and a secondary EMI account for operational transactions. The KNF expects that you have a Polish bank account for the capital requirement, but you can use other payment services for daily operations.

  • Competitive cost: Compared to Panama (EUR 6,000 flat) or Lithuania, Poland offers a moderate cost with a clear regulatory framework.
  • Access to EU market: Poland is a gateway to Central and Eastern Europe.

Compliance & Trust

Maintaining compliance is an ongoing obligation. The KNF conducts periodic inspections and can impose fines or revoke registration for non-compliance. Key requirements include: appointing a local AML officer, conducting regular training, submitting suspicious transaction reports (STRs), and maintaining records for at least 5 years.   • Ignoring tax obligations: Failing to register for CIT/VAT can lead to penalties.
  • Choosing the wrong company structure: A branch may not be suitable; a subsidiary is often required.
  • Not engaging local experts: DIY applications often face delays or rejection.
