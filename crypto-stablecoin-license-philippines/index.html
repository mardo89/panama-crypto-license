What is the Crypto Stablecoin License Philippines?

The crypto stablecoin license Philippines is a regulatory authorization issued by the Bangko Sentral ng Pilipinas (BSP) under its Digital Asset and Stablecoin Framework. It allows entities to issue, redeem, and manage stablecoins-cryptocurrencies designed to maintain a stable value relative to a reference asset, typically the Philippine peso or a foreign currency like the US dollar. The BSP classifies stablecoins as a type of Virtual Asset Service Provider (VASP) activity, requiring a license for any entity that offers stablecoin services to the public. The license is part of the BSP's broader effort to foster innovation while ensuring financial stability, consumer protection, and anti-money laundering compliance.

Unlike some jurisdictions that treat stablecoins as securities or commodities, the Philippines has a dedicated framework that recognizes stablecoins as a distinct asset class. This clarity reduces legal uncertainty for issuers. The BSP's approach requires stablecoins to be fully backed by high-quality liquid assets, with regular attestations and audits. For example, a peso-backed stablecoin must maintain a 1:1 reserve in Philippine pesos held in a trust account with a BSP-accredited bank. The framework also mandates redemption on demand, ensuring users can convert stablecoins back to fiat at any time.

The license is not a one-size-fits-all; it includes specific conditions based on the stablecoin's design. For instance, stablecoins pegged to foreign currencies may have additional reporting requirements to manage foreign exchange risk. The BSP also prohibits algorithmic stablecoins, which are not backed by reserves, due to their potential to destabilize the financial system. This conservative stance aligns with international standards set by the Financial Stability Board and the International Monetary Fund.

Who Needs This License?

Any company that intends to issue, redeem, or facilitate the transfer of stablecoins in or from the Philippines must obtain this license. This includes:

  • Blockchain startups planning to launch a peso-backed stablecoin for local payments or remittances.
  • Fintech firms offering stablecoin-based payment solutions, such as merchant settlement or cross-border transfers.
  • Exchanges that list or trade stablecoins as a primary service, especially if they offer stablecoin-to-stablecoin pairs.
  • Remittance companies using stablecoins to reduce costs and settlement times for overseas Filipino workers.
  • Enterprises tokenizing real-world assets into stablecoins, such as real estate or commodities.
  • Non-bank financial institutions looking to issue stablecoins for lending or liquidity management.

If your business model involves creating or managing a stablecoin that is accessible to Philippine residents, you fall under the BSP's jurisdiction. Even if you are based overseas, serving Philippine users triggers the licensing requirement. For example, a foreign fintech that allows Philippine users to deposit pesos and receive a stablecoin must apply for a license. The BSP also requires that the stablecoin's reserve assets be held in the Philippines, which means foreign issuers must establish a local presence.

It is important to note that simply using stablecoins in your operations (e.g., accepting them as payment) does not require a license unless you are issuing or redeeming them. However, if your platform facilitates peer-to-peer trading of stablecoins, you may need a standard VASP license instead. License Type & Regulator

The license is formally known as a Virtual Asset Service Provider (VASP) License with Stablecoin Issuance Authorization. It is regulated by the Bangko Sentral ng Pilipinas (BSP), the central bank of the Philippines. The BSP's Digital Currency Unit oversees the application process and ongoing compliance. The license is distinct from the regular VASP license (for exchanges and custodians) and requires additional capital and operational safeguards specific to stablecoin issuance, such as reserve management and redemption policies.

The BSP has been a pioneer in digital asset regulation in Asia. It issued its first VASP guidelines in 2017 and updated them in 2021 to include stablecoins. The regulatory framework is based on the BSP's Circular No. 1108, which outlines the requirements for VASPs, and subsequent memoranda on stablecoins. The BSP also coordinates with other regulators, such as the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) for tokenized securities and the Insurance Commission for stablecoins that resemble insurance products.

For stablecoin issuers, the BSP requires a separate authorization beyond the standard VASP license. This includes submitting a detailed whitepaper describing the stablecoin's design, reserve management, redemption mechanism, and risk management. The BSP also mandates that the stablecoin's code be audited by an independent third party. Consulting24 can assist in preparing these documents and liaising with the BSP's Digital Currency Unit.

Capital Requirement

The BSP mandates a minimum paid-up capital of PHP 50 million (approximately USD 900,000) for entities seeking to issue stablecoins. This is higher than the PHP 25 million requirement for standard VASP licenses, reflecting the systemic importance of stablecoins. The capital must be maintained at all times and can be in the form of cash or liquid assets acceptable to the BSP. For foreign applicants, the capital must be remitted into the Philippines and converted to pesos.

The capital serves as a buffer against operational risks and potential losses. The BSP may require additional capital if the stablecoin's issuance volume exceeds a certain threshold. For example, if you plan to issue stablecoins worth more than PHP 1 billion, the BSP may demand a higher capital base. The capital requirement also applies to branches of foreign companies; they must maintain the PHP 50 million in the Philippines.

It is important to note that the capital is separate from the reserve assets backing the stablecoin. The reserve must be held in a trust account with a BSP-accredited bank and cannot be used for any other purpose. Tax Treatment

Stablecoin issuance and related activities are subject to Philippine tax laws. The Bureau of Internal Revenue (BIR) treats stablecoins as digital assets, with the following implications:

  • Income from stablecoin issuance (e.g., seigniorage) is subject to corporate income tax at 25% (or 20% for small businesses with net taxable income below PHP 5 million).
  • Value-added tax (VAT) at 12% applies to transaction fees earned from stablecoin transfers. However, if your services are considered financial services, VAT may be exempt under certain conditions.
  • No specific stablecoin tax exemption exists; standard tax rules apply. The BIR has not issued specific guidelines for stablecoins, so general digital asset rules apply.
  • Withholding tax on dividends and interest may apply to foreign investors. The rate is 15% for countries with a tax treaty with the Philippines, otherwise 30%.
  • Local government taxes, such as business taxes and real property taxes, also apply depending on the location of your office.

Tax planning is essential. For example, you may structure your operations to minimize VAT by offering services that qualify as financial intermediation. Allowed Activities

Under the stablecoin license, the following activities are permitted:

  • Issuance of stablecoins backed by fiat currency or other approved assets, such as government bonds or gold.
  • Redemption of stablecoins for the underlying asset at par, subject to the terms of the whitepaper.
  • Facilitation of stablecoin transfers between users, including peer-to-peer and merchant payments.
  • Integration with payment systems for merchant acceptance, such as through QR codes or payment gateways.
  • Custody of reserve assets (if self-custodied), but only if the issuer has a separate custody license or partners with a qualified custodian.
  • Marketing and distribution of stablecoins through authorized channels.

Activities not allowed include: issuing algorithmic stablecoins (not backed by reserves), lending stablecoins without a separate lending license, and offering interest-bearing accounts on stablecoins. The BSP prohibits stablecoins that are not fully backed by high-quality liquid assets. Additionally, stablecoins cannot be used for gambling or other illegal activities.

The BSP also restricts the use of stablecoins for cross-border transfers if the counterparty is in a sanctioned jurisdiction. You must implement geoblocking and transaction monitoring to comply with sanctions laws. Step-by-Step Application Process

  1. Pre-application consultation with BSP to discuss business model and requirements. This is mandatory and helps you understand the BSP's expectations.
  2. Company incorporation in the Philippines (e.g., domestic corporation or branch office). You must register with the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) and obtain a Tax Identification Number (TIN).
  3. Document preparation: business plan, AML/CFT policies, risk management framework, reserve management plan, financial projections, and a detailed whitepaper describing the stablecoin's design, technology, and governance.
  4. Submission of application to BSP Digital Currency Unit, including all supporting documents and fees. The BSP will issue an acknowledgment receipt.
  5. BSP review and possible requests for additional information (takes 3-6 months). The BSP may ask for clarifications or amendments to your whitepaper.
  6. On-site inspection by BSP to verify operational readiness, including your office, systems, and personnel.
  7. Approval and issuance of VASP license with stablecoin authorization. You will receive a certificate of registration.
  8. Post-licensing compliance: regular reporting (monthly/quarterly), annual audits, reserve attestations, and payment of supervision fees.

Banking & Payment Integration

Stablecoin issuers must maintain a reserve account with a BSP-regulated bank. Major Philippine banks like BDO, Metrobank, and BPI offer corporate accounts for VASPs, though due diligence is rigorous. You will need to provide your license, business plan, and AML policies to open an account. Some banks may require a minimum deposit or charge higher fees for VASP accounts.

For payment integration, stablecoins can be connected to the Philippine payment systems (e.g., InstaPay, PESONet) through licensed payment gateways. These gateways allow users to convert fiat to stablecoins and vice versa in real time. Some fintechs offer API-based solutions for smooth fiat-to-stablecoin conversion. For example, you can partner with a payment facilitator like PayMongo or GCash to enable stablecoin top-ups.

International banking is also possible. You may open a foreign currency account to hold reserves in USD or other currencies. However, the BSP requires that the majority of reserves be held in Philippine pesos if the stablecoin is peso-denominated. Compliance & Trust

Maintaining the license requires ongoing compliance: quarterly reserve attestations, annual audits, AML/CFT reporting, and BSP supervision fees. The BSP conducts periodic examinations, which can be announced or unannounced. You must also submit regular reports on transaction volumes, suspicious activities, and changes in management. Failure to comply can result in fines, suspension, or revocation of the license.

To build trust, you should publish regular reserve reports and undergo third-party audits. The BSP may require you to appoint a compliance officer with relevant experience. You must also have a strong AML program that includes customer due diligence, transaction monitoring, and sanctions screening. The Philippines is a member of the Financial Action Task Force (FATF), and the BSP follows FATF recommendations for virtual assets.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Underestimating capital: PHP 50 million is a hard minimum; many applicants need more for operational reserves. Ensure you have sufficient liquidity.
  • Ignoring local incorporation: Foreign entities cannot apply directly; a Philippine company is mandatory. You must also register with the SEC and BIR.
  • Weak AML policies: BSP scrutinizes AML frameworks; generic templates are rejected. Customize your policies to your business model.
  • Unclear reserve management: The reserve must be held in a separate trust account with a BSP-accredited bank. You cannot use the reserve for any other purpose.
  • Overlooking tax obligations: Both BIR and local government taxes apply; non-compliance leads to penalties. Register for all applicable taxes.
  • Incomplete whitepaper: The whitepaper must detail the stablecoin's design, technology, and risk management. Missing information can delay approval.

Alternatives & Comparison

Philippines vs Panama

Panama offers a flat-fee EUR 6,000 crypto license with no specific stablecoin framework, lower capital (no minimum), and faster setup (1-2 months). However, Panama lacks a dedicated stablecoin regulator, which may be a drawback for institutional partners. The Philippines provides stronger regulatory protection and market access to Asia, but at higher cost and longer timeline. For founders who need a quick and cheap license, Panama is attractive; for those targeting the Asian market, the Philippines is better.

Philippines vs Lithuania

Lithuania, under MiCA, requires EUR 125,000 capital for stablecoin issuance and offers EU passporting. The Philippines is more focused on the local market. For Asian founders, the Philippines may be more accessible; for European expansion, Lithuania is preferable. Lithuania also has a faster timeline (3-6 months) and lower ongoing costs. However, the Philippines has a larger remittance market and lower labor costs.

Philippines vs Dubai (VARA)

Dubai's VARA stablecoin license requires a local presence and higher capital (AED 10-50 million). It offers a global financial hub but with stricter Sharia compliance for some stablecoins. The Philippines is simpler for pure fiat-backed stablecoins and has a more straightforward tax regime. Dubai is better for targeting the Middle East and North Africa region.
