What is a Crypto Fund License in Costa Rica?

A crypto fund license in Costa Rica is an authorization from SUGEVAL to operate an investment fund that invests primarily in crypto assets, such as Bitcoin, Ethereum, or other digital tokens. The license is not a standalone "crypto" license but falls under the existing securities fund regime. Funds can be structured as open-end or closed-end, and must comply with the General Regulations of Investment Funds and the Securities Market Law.

Key characteristics include:

  • Approval by SUGEVAL of the fund's regulations and prospectus
  • Appointment of a licensed fund manager, custodian, and auditor
  • Ongoing reporting and disclosure obligations
  • Investor protection rules, including anti-money laundering (AML) compliance

This regime is attractive for fund managers seeking a reputable onshore jurisdiction with a stable legal system and no capital gains tax on crypto profits for foreign investors (under certain conditions). For example, a crypto hedge fund trading on Binance and Kraken would be considered foreign-source income and thus tax-exempt. The fund must have a clear investment strategy defined in its prospectus, which SUGEVAL reviews thoroughly.

Who Needs a Crypto Fund License in Costa Rica?

Any entity that pools investor capital to invest in crypto assets and expects to generate returns from trading, staking, or lending crypto must obtain a fund license. This includes:

  • Crypto hedge funds
  • Venture capital funds focused on blockchain startups
  • Tokenized real estate or commodity funds
  • Yield farming or staking pools structured as investment funds

If you are a single-family office or an individual investor, you typically do not need a license. However, if you offer investment opportunities to third parties, the fund license is mandatory. License Type & Regulator

The regulator for crypto fund licenses in Costa Rica is the Superintendencia General de Valores (SUGEVAL), which oversees all securities market participants. The license type is an "Investment Fund" registration, which can be for public or private offerings. Most crypto funds opt for private placement to avoid full public disclosure requirements.

SUGEVAL reviews the fund's constitutive documents, investment policy, risk management framework, and the suitability of the fund manager. The regulator also conducts background checks on directors and beneficial owners. Consulting24 coordinates with local legal counsel to prepare the application package. The fund manager must be a Costa Rican corporation (sociedad administradora) with a minimum capital of $50,000 USD. The regulator expects the fund to have at least $500,000 in initial AUM, though this is not a hard rule.

Capital Requirement

Costa Rica does not impose a fixed minimum capital for investment funds themselves, but the fund manager must maintain a minimum net worth. As of 2026, the fund manager (sociedad administradora) must have a minimum capital of approximately $50,000 USD (adjusted for inflation). Additionally, the fund must have sufficient initial assets to cover operational costs; SUGEVAL typically expects at least $500,000 in assets under management (AUM) for a private fund, though this is not a hard rule.

Consulting24 can advise on structuring the fund to meet SUGEVAL's expectations without overcapitalizing. For example, a fund with $500,000 AUM can cover legal, audit, and custodian fees for the first year. If you have less capital, you may need to demonstrate a credible plan to raise funds. The manager's capital must be maintained throughout the fund's life.

Tax Treatment

Costa Rica offers a territorial tax system: income earned within the country is taxed, while foreign-source income is generally exempt. For crypto funds, trading profits from foreign exchanges are considered foreign-source and thus not subject to Costa Rican income tax. However, any income from crypto activities conducted within Costa Rica (e.g., local mining, staking from domestic nodes) may be taxed at the corporate rate of 30%.

There is no capital gains tax on crypto for individuals, and dividends paid to foreign investors are often exempt from withholding tax. VAT (13%) applies to management fees charged to the fund. Consulting24 recommends a tax review with a local accountant to optimize the structure. For example, a fund that only trades on international exchanges will pay 0% corporate tax, while a fund that runs a local staking node may owe 30% on that income. Proper planning can minimize tax exposure.

Allowed Activities

A licensed crypto fund in Costa Rica can engage in the following activities:

  • Direct investment in cryptocurrencies (spot trading)
  • Derivatives and futures on crypto assets
  • Staking and lending of crypto assets
  • Investment in tokenized securities (security tokens)
  • Participation in DeFi protocols (subject to risk disclosure)

Prohibited activities include: operating as an exchange, providing custody services to third parties (unless separately licensed), and using investor funds for proprietary trading by the manager. The fund's investment policy must be clearly defined in the prospectus. For instance, a fund that wants to engage in DeFi yield farming must disclose the risks of smart contract failures and impermanent loss. SUGEVAL may require additional risk warnings for such activities.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Underestimating capital needs: The manager must have sufficient capital to operate for at least 12 months.

  • Vague investment policy: The prospectus must clearly define what assets the fund can invest in.
