What Is the Estonia vs Czech Republic Crypto License Comparison?

This comparison examines two European crypto licensing frameworks: Estonia's MiCA-compliant CASP license and the Czech Republic's MiCA-compliant CASP license. Both are fully in force as of 2026, replacing earlier regimes. Estonia previously operated a simple registration system (no minimum capital), but now enforces MiCA tiers. The Czech Republic similarly transitioned from a trade license system to MiCA. Key differences lie in capital requirements, tax treatment, timeline, and regulatory approach.

Who Needs a Crypto License in Estonia or the Czech Republic?

Any entity providing crypto-asset services to EU residents must hold a CASP license under MiCA. This includes exchanges, wallet providers, custodians, and brokers. Both Estonia and the Czech Republic require licensing for the same MiCA-defined services. If you plan to offer services in multiple EU countries, a license from either jurisdiction grants passporting rights across the EU.

Startups and established firms alike need a license. The choice between Estonia and the Czech Republic often depends on capital availability, tax preferences, and timeline. Consulting24 advises clients on which jurisdiction aligns with their specific activities and budget.

If your capital is tight, the Czech Republic's lower professional fees might make it more accessible. Conversely, if you have sufficient capital and want to minimize tax, Estonia is compelling. License Type and Regulator

Estonia: The regulator is the Financial Supervision Authority (Finantsinspektsioon). License type: Crypto-Asset Service Provider (CASP) under MiCA. The application is submitted via the Estonian Commercial Register and Finantsinspektsioon.

Czech Republic: The regulator is the Czech National Bank (Česká národní banka, ČNB). License type: CASP under MiCA. The application is submitted directly to the ČNB.

Both regulators are competent authorities under MiCA. The Czech National Bank is known for a thorough but predictable process. Finantsinspektsioon in Estonia has a similar reputation. Neither is overly burdensome compared to larger EU regulators.

In practice, the ČNB often requests a management interview to assess fitness and propriety. Finantsinspektsioon relies more on written documentation. Both require a local AML officer and registered office. Consulting24 prepares all documentation to meet each regulator's expectations, reducing the risk of delays or rejections.

Capital Requirement

Under MiCA, capital requirements are tiered by service class. Both Estonia and the Czech Republic apply the same MiCA tiers:

  • Class 1 (e.g., reception and transmission of orders): EUR 50,000
  • Class 2 (e.g., execution of orders, portfolio management): EUR 125,000
  • Class 3 (e.g., custody and administration, operating a trading platform): EUR 150,000

These are minimum own funds. If your business model includes multiple service classes, the highest applicable tier applies. Estonia and the Czech Republic do not impose additional local capital requirements beyond MiCA. However, both regulators may require higher capital if your business volume exceeds certain thresholds. For instance, a company offering both custody (Class 3) and order execution (Class 2) must hold EUR 150,000. If you also provide investment advice (Class 1), the requirement remains EUR 150,000. Capital must be held in liquid assets (cash or equivalents) and cannot be borrowed. Consulting24 helps structure your capital plan to meet regulatory scrutiny.

Tax Treatment

Estonia: Corporate income tax is 0% on retained and reinvested profits. Only distributed profits (dividends) are taxed at 20% (or 14% for regular dividends). This makes Estonia attractive for crypto companies that plan to reinvest earnings. VAT: Crypto-to-fiat transactions are generally exempt from VAT. Crypto-to-crypto transactions may be subject to VAT depending on the nature (e.g., mining vs. trading).

Czech Republic: Corporate income tax is a flat 19% on profits. No special tax regime for crypto companies. VAT: Crypto transactions are exempt from VAT under EU law. However, the Czech Republic applies standard VAT rules for ancillary services. Personal income tax for founders may be relevant if you are a sole trader.

Estonia's deferred tax system can be a significant advantage for growth-stage companies. The Czech Republic offers a straightforward, lower headline rate but immediate taxation on profits.

Consider a company earning EUR 500,000 profit annually. In Estonia, if all profits are reinvested, tax is EUR 0. In the Czech Republic, tax is EUR 95,000 (19%). Over three years, Estonia saves EUR 285,000 in tax, which can fund expansion. However, when dividends are eventually paid, Estonia's effective rate is 20% (or 14% for regular dividends), while the Czech Republic's rate on distributed profits remains 19%. Allowed Activities

Both jurisdictions license the full range of MiCA crypto-asset services. Under MiCA, the allowed activities include:

  • Custody and administration of crypto-assets
  • Operation of a trading platform
  • Exchange of crypto-assets for fiat currency
  • Exchange of crypto-assets for other crypto-assets
  • Execution of orders on behalf of clients
  • Placing of crypto-assets
  • Reception and transmission of orders
  • Portfolio management
  • Investment advice

Estonia and the Czech Republic do not restrict any of these activities beyond MiCA. However, certain stablecoin services may require additional authorization under the EU's Markets in Crypto-Assets (MiCA) regulation, which is fully in force. Both regulators treat stablecoins as e-money tokens or asset-referenced tokens, subject to separate rules.

For example, if you plan to issue a stablecoin, you may need an e-money license or a separate authorization under MiCA's Title III or IV. Consulting24 can advise on the full scope of activities and any ancillary licenses needed.

Step-by-Step Application Process

The process for obtaining a crypto license in Estonia or the Czech Republic follows similar steps, with local variations.

Estonia

  1. Incorporate a company in Estonia (e-residency available).
  2. Prepare a detailed business plan, AML/KYC policies, risk assessment, and internal procedures.
  3. Submit application to Finantsinspektsioon via the Commercial Register.
  4. Regulator reviews (3-6 months). May request additional information.
  5. Upon approval, license issued. Ongoing reporting obligations.

Czech Republic

  1. Incorporate a company in the Czech Republic (standard or s.r.o.).
  2. Prepare compliance documentation including AML program, business plan, and risk management.
  3. Submit application to the Czech National Bank.
  4. ČNB reviews (4-8 months). Interview with management may be required.
  5. License granted. Submit periodic reports.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Founders often make these errors when choosing between Estonia and the Czech Republic:

  • Ignoring capital requirements: Underestimating the needed own funds for your service class can delay approval.
  • Overlooking tax implications: Estonia's 0% tax on retained profits is attractive, but if you plan to distribute dividends, the effective tax rate is 20%.
  • Rushing the application: Incomplete documentation is the top reason for rejection. Both regulators expect a thorough AML program.
  • Choosing based on cost alone: The Czech Republic may be cheaper upfront, but Estonia's tax deferral can save significant money over time.
  • Ignoring banking access: Ensure you have a bank account lined up before applying. Some banks refuse crypto clients even with a license.
