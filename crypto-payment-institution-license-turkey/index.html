What Is the Crypto Payment Institution License Turkey?

The Crypto Payment Institution License Turkey is a regulatory authorization granted by the Capital Markets Board (CMB) under the Law on Payment and Securities Settlement Systems, Payment Services and Electronic Money Institutions (Law No. 6493) as amended in 2024. It allows entities to provide crypto asset payment services, including buying, selling, custody, and transfer of crypto assets. The license is part of Turkey's efforts to bring crypto activities under formal oversight, combating money laundering and ensuring consumer protection.

This license is distinct from the previous unregistered status of crypto exchanges. As of 2026, all crypto payment service providers must hold this license or face penalties. The regime aligns with FATF recommendations and includes strict AML/KYC obligations. For businesses, it offers a clear legal pathway to operate within Turkey's vibrant crypto market, which has one of the highest adoption rates globally. The CMB has published detailed communiqués outlining operational standards, including requirements for segregation of customer funds, transaction monitoring, and incident reporting. For example, licensed institutions must maintain a separate bank account for customer fiat funds and cannot use them for operational expenses. This level of oversight builds trust with users and partners.

Who Needs This License?

Any legal entity providing crypto asset services to Turkish residents must obtain the Crypto Payment Institution License. This includes:

  • Crypto exchanges (buying/selling crypto against fiat or other crypto)
  • Wallet providers offering custodial services
  • Payment processors facilitating crypto payments for merchants
  • OTC desks and crypto ATM operators
  • Brokerage platforms dealing in crypto derivatives (if considered payment services)

Foreign companies targeting Turkish customers also require a licensed local entity. If you are unsure whether your business model falls under the scope, Consulting24 can provide a preliminary assessment. The license is mandatory for any activity involving the transfer or conversion of crypto assets as a business. Even businesses that only facilitate peer-to-peer trading may need a license if they hold customer funds. The CMB has clarified that any service involving the safekeeping or transfer of crypto assets is subject to licensing. For example, a crypto lending platform that takes custody of collateral would need this license. If you are considering offering such services, it is safer to apply early.

License Type and Regulator

The regulator is the Capital Markets Board of Turkey (CMB), which oversees crypto payment institutions under the amended Law No. 6493. The license is officially called the "Crypto Payment Institution License." There are two tiers based on transaction volume:

  • Class A: For institutions with annual transaction volume above TRY 50 million (approx. EUR 1.5 million) - higher capital and compliance requirements.
  • Class B: For smaller institutions below that threshold - lower capital but still significant obligations.

The CMB also coordinates with the Financial Crimes Investigation Board (MASAK) for AML supervision. As of 2026, the CMB has issued licenses to over 30 entities, with many more applications pending. The regulatory environment is strict but transparent, with clear rulebooks for operational standards. The CMB publishes a list of licensed entities on its website, which can help you verify potential partners. For example, major Turkish exchanges like BtcTurk and Paribu have obtained licenses, setting a precedent for compliance. The CMB also conducts regular inspections and can impose fines for non-compliance. Understanding the tier system is crucial: if your projected volume is near the threshold, it may be more efficient to apply for Class A from the start to avoid a later upgrade.

Capital Requirement

The minimum paid-in capital for a Crypto Payment Institution License Turkey is set by the CMB based on the class:

  • Class B: Minimum TRY 500,000 (approx. EUR 15,000 as of 2026 exchange rates).
  • Class A: Minimum TRY 2,000,000 (approx. EUR 60,000).

This capital must be deposited in a Turkish bank and cannot be withdrawn without CMB approval. The capital serves as a safeguard for customer funds and operational solvency. Additionally, institutions must maintain a minimum level of own funds equivalent to 10% of annual operational costs. For comparison, Panama requires no minimum capital for its crypto license, making it a more flexible option for startups. The capital requirement is a key barrier: if you are a small startup, the EUR 15,000 minimum may be manageable, but the EUR 60,000 for Class A can be a stretch. Note that the capital must be paid in cash; in-kind contributions are not accepted. Your bank will need to confirm the deposit to the CMB. We recommend using a major Turkish bank like Garanti or İşbank for this purpose.

Tax Treatment

Turkey has a complex tax regime for crypto. As of 2026, the following applies:

  • Corporate income tax: 25% on net profits from crypto activities (standard rate).
  • VAT: Crypto transactions are exempt from VAT under Turkish law.
  • Withholding tax: No specific withholding on crypto income, but general rules apply.
  • Personal income tax: Individuals trading crypto may be subject to capital gains tax (up to 40%) if deemed trading income.

For licensed payment institutions, the corporate tax rate applies to fees and spreads. Tax planning is essential, and we recommend consulting a local tax advisor. Compared to Panama, which has territorial taxation (no tax on foreign-source income), Turkey's tax burden is higher but offers access to a large domestic market. Additionally, Turkey has a withholding tax on dividend distributions (15% standard, but may be reduced under tax treaties). Crypto transactions between licensed institutions may be subject to BSMV (Banking and Insurance Transactions Tax) at 5% if they involve fiat. However, crypto-to-crypto trades are generally exempt. The tax landscape is evolving, and the Turkish Revenue Administration (GİB) has issued several circulars clarifying the treatment. We advise maintaining detailed records of all transactions for audit purposes.

Allowed Activities

With a Crypto Payment Institution License Turkey, you can legally conduct the following activities:

  • Buying and selling crypto assets for fiat currency
  • Exchange of one crypto asset for another
  • Custody and safekeeping of crypto assets
  • Transfer of crypto assets on behalf of customers
  • Issuance of crypto payment instruments (e.g., prepaid cards)
  • Merchant acquisition for crypto payments
  • OTC trading services

Activities strictly prohibited include offering unlicensed derivatives, gambling-related services, and anonymous transactions. The CMB may expand the list over time. For a full scope, review the official CMB communiqué or contact Consulting24. Note that the license does not cover crypto mining or token issuance (ICOs) unless they involve payment services. If you plan to offer staking or lending, you may need additional authorization. The CMB has indicated that such services are under review. For now, focus on the allowed activities and ensure your business model fits within them. For example, a crypto exchange that also offers staking rewards may need to separate those services into a different legal entity.

Step-by-Step Application Process

  1. Pre-assessment: Review your business model against CMB requirements. Consulting24 can help identify gaps.
  2. Company incorporation: Register a Turkish joint-stock company (A.Ş.) with the required capital. Minimum share capital is TRY 50,000, but higher for license.
  3. Prepare documentation: Draft AML/KYC policies, risk management framework, business plan, and organizational structure. Appoint a compliance officer.
  4. Submit application: File with the CMB along with application fee. Include audited financial statements (if any) and background checks on directors.
  5. Review and feedback: CMB reviews within 90 days, may request additional information. Respond promptly.
  6. Approval and licensing: If approved, pay the license fee and receive the license certificate. Begin operations under CMB supervision.

The entire process typically takes 6-12 months. Our detailed application process guide provides more insights. A common pitfall is underestimating the time needed for company incorporation. Registering a Turkish A.Ş. can take 2-4 weeks, and you need a local registered address. The CMB also requires that at least one director be a Turkish resident. If you are a foreign founder, you may need to appoint a local nominee director. We can help coordinate these requirements. After approval, you must submit a post-licensing compliance report within 30 days, including proof of operational readiness.

Banking and Payment Partners

One of the biggest challenges for crypto payment institutions in Turkey is securing banking relationships. Turkish banks are cautious due to AML concerns. However, licensed entities have better chances. Recommended steps:

  • Open a corporate bank account with a Turkish bank that accepts crypto firms (e.g., Garanti BBVA, İşbank, Yapı Kredi - but policies change).
  • Partner with payment service providers for fiat on/off ramps (e.g., Papara, PayTR).
  • Consider using a payment aggregator that works with multiple banks.

For international operations, you may need a multi-currency account. Consulting24 can introduce you to banking partners familiar with the crypto sector. Alternatively, Panama offers a more bank-friendly environment with no capital controls. In practice, many licensed Turkish crypto firms use a combination of local banks for TRY transactions and international banks for EUR/USD. For example, you might use Garanti for domestic fiat and a European bank for crypto-to-fiat conversions. The CMB requires that customer fiat funds be held in a separate trust account, which must be with a bank that has a branch in Turkey. This limits your options but ensures regulatory compliance. We recommend starting the bank account application early, as it can take 2-3 months.

  • Regulatory clarity: The CMB framework provides legal certainty, reducing risk of shutdown.
  • Growing fintech ecosystem: Strong local talent and investor interest.

Additionally, the CMB license may facilitate partnerships with local banks and payment providers. Some global exchanges have set up Turkish subsidiaries to tap into this market. However, if your target is international, Panama or Lithuania may be more suitable due to lower costs and faster setup.

Compliance and Trust

Operating under the CMB license requires ongoing compliance:

  • Monthly transaction reporting to MASAK
  • Annual audit by a CMB-approved auditor
  • Maintain AML/KYC procedures as per Turkish law
  • Implement cybersecurity measures (ISO 27001 recommended)
  • Regular capital adequacy checks

Failure to comply can result in fines, suspension, or revocation of the license. Consulting24 provides compliance monitoring support. This is general guidance, not legal advice. Always consult with a Turkish law firm for your specific case. For example, the CMB may require you to submit a quarterly compliance report detailing transaction volumes, suspicious activity reports, and capital adequacy. Non-compliance can lead to fines of up to TRY 5 million (approx. EUR 150,000) per violation. To build trust with customers, display your license number on your website and link to the CMB's register. Many Turkish crypto users check the regulator's list before using a platform. Additionally, consider obtaining an ISO 27001 certification to demonstrate your commitment to security.

  • Underestimating capital: Ensure capital is fully paid and held in a Turkish bank before application.
  • Ignoring tax obligations: Crypto income is taxable; plan accordingly.
  • Choosing the wrong class: If your projected volume exceeds the threshold, apply for Class A to avoid later upgrade.
  • Not engaging local experts: The CMB expects local legal representation.

Another common mistake is failing to prepare for the CMB's on-site inspection. After approval, the CMB may visit your office to verify operational readiness. The CMB expects a detailed 3-year projection including revenue, expenses, and user growth. If your plan is unrealistic, the application may be rejected. Frequently asked questions

What is the Crypto Payment Institution License Turkey?

It is a license issued by the Capital Markets Board (CMB) under Law No. 6493, allowing companies to provide crypto payment services such as exchange, custody, and transfer. It is mandatory for any entity offering these services to Turkish residents as of 2026.

Who needs this license?

Any legal entity providing crypto payment services to Turkish residents, including exchanges, wallet providers, OTC desks, and payment processors. Foreign companies targeting Turkish customers must also obtain it through a local subsidiary.

What is the minimum capital requirement?

For Class B (annual volume under TRY 50 million), minimum capital is TRY 500,000 (approx. EUR 15,000). For Class A, it is TRY 2,000,000 (approx. EUR 60,000). Capital must be paid in cash and held in a Turkish bank.

How long does the licensing process take?

Typically 6-12 months from application to approval. The CMB has a 90-day review period, but preparation of documents and company incorporation can take additional months. A well-prepared application can be approved in 6 months.

What are the tax implications for crypto payment institutions in Turkey?

Corporate income tax is 25% on net profits. Crypto transactions are VAT-exempt. Individuals may pay capital gains tax up to 40%. Licensed institutions must also consider BSMV on fiat transactions. Consult a local tax advisor.

Can I operate internationally with a Turkish crypto license?

The license is primarily for serving Turkish residents. For international operations, you may need additional licenses in other jurisdictions. However, it can facilitate partnerships with Turkish banks and payment providers.

What are the ongoing compliance requirements?

Monthly transaction reporting to MASAK, annual audit by a CMB-approved auditor, AML/KYC maintenance, cybersecurity measures, and capital adequacy checks. Non-compliance can lead to fines or license revocation.

How does the Turkish license compare to Panama's?

Panama offers a flat fee of EUR 6,000, no minimum capital, and a 2-3 month timeline. Turkey has higher costs (EUR 30,000-100,000) and longer timeline but provides access to a large domestic market. Panama is better for global operations.

What are the allowed activities under the license?

Buying/selling crypto for fiat, crypto-to-crypto exchange, custody, transfer, issuance of crypto payment instruments, merchant acquisition, and OTC trading. Prohibited activities include unlicensed derivatives and anonymous transactions.

Do I need a local office in Turkey?

Yes, you need a physical office in Turkey with staff. The CMB may conduct on-site inspections. A local registered address is required for company incorporation. Foreign founders may need a local nominee director.

What happens if I operate without a license?

Operating without a license is illegal and can result in fines, criminal charges, and shutdown of your business. The CMB has been actively enforcing the law since 2024. To avoid penalties, apply for the license before offering services.
