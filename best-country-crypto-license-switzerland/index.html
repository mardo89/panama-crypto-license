What is the Swiss Crypto License?

The Swiss crypto license is not a single license but a set of authorisations issued by FINMA depending on the specific crypto activities a company intends to perform. Switzerland’s regulatory framework is principle-based, meaning FINMA assesses each business model against existing financial market laws, including the Anti-Money Laundering Act (AMLA) and the Financial Services Act (FinSA). For most crypto businesses, the key requirement is obtaining a FinTech license or a securities dealer license, or simply registering as a financial intermediary under the AMLA if the activity is limited to certain services.

In 2026, Switzerland continues to offer a unique “sandbox” approach for small-scale operations, allowing companies with deposits under a certain threshold to operate without a full license. This flexibility, combined with clear guidance from FINMA, makes Switzerland a top contender for the best country for a crypto license. However, the exact licensing route depends on the volume of transactions, the use of client funds, and the nature of the digital assets involved.

Who Needs a Swiss Crypto License?

Any company that provides professional crypto services to third parties in or from Switzerland may need a license. This includes:

  • Cryptocurrency exchanges (centralised and peer-to-peer platforms)
  • Wallet providers and custody services
  • Token issuers (STOs, ICOs, or DeFi protocols with governance tokens)
  • Brokerage and trading services
  • Payment services using crypto assets
  • Asset managers dealing with crypto assets

If your business involves holding client assets, executing orders, or providing investment advice in crypto, you likely fall under FINMA’s supervision. Even if you are based outside Switzerland but target Swiss clients, you may need to register. FINMA’s 2026 guidelines continue to treat crypto assets as “assets” under existing laws, meaning that traditional financial regulations apply. The FinTech license is a streamlined authorisation for companies that accept public deposits up to CHF 100 million but do not invest or pay interest on them. This is particularly popular among crypto startups.

Capital Requirement

Switzerland does not have a fixed minimum capital requirement for all crypto licenses. Instead, capital requirements are determined by the type of license and the risks involved. For a FinTech license, FINMA generally expects a minimum capital of CHF 300,000 (approximately EUR 300,000). For a full banking license (rarely needed for crypto), the requirement is CHF 10 million. For simpler registrations (SRO membership), capital may be as low as CHF 100,000.

It is important to note that these figures are indicative as of 2026. Exact capital requirements are assessed on a case-by-case basis. Consulting24 can help you determine the appropriate capitalisation for your business model.

Tax Treatment

Switzerland offers a highly favourable tax environment for crypto businesses. Corporate income tax rates vary by canton but typically range from 12% to 21%. There is no capital gains tax for private investors, and for professional traders, profits are taxed as ordinary income. Crypto assets are generally treated as assets for wealth tax purposes, and VAT is not applicable on most crypto transactions.

Switzerland has also issued guidance on the tax treatment of staking, lending, and DeFi yields, which are generally considered income at the time of receipt. The country’s tax authorities are known for their pragmatic approach, making it one of the best jurisdictions for crypto entrepreneurs seeking tax efficiency.

Allowed Activities

Under a Swiss crypto license, companies can perform a wide range of activities, including:

  • Operating a cryptocurrency exchange (order book, OTC, P2P)
  • Providing custody and wallet services
  • Issuing tokens (utility, security, stablecoins) subject to prospectus requirements
  • Facilitating crypto payments and remittances
  • Running a crypto ATM network
  • Offering crypto lending and borrowing (subject to banking license if taking deposits)
  • Managing crypto assets for third parties

Activities that involve taking deposits (e.g., lending platforms) may require a full banking license. FINMA provides a “no-action” letter process for new business models to confirm regulatory treatment.

Banking and Payments

Switzerland has a mature banking sector that is generally open to crypto businesses, though due diligence is rigorous. Several Swiss banks, such as SEBA Bank, Sygnum, and some cantonal banks, offer accounts to licensed crypto companies. For startups, opening a bank account can be challenging without a license; however, once licensed, access improves significantly.

Payment services for crypto companies are also well-developed, with providers like Bitcoin Suisse and Crypto Finance AG offering fiat on/off ramps. Consulting24 can assist in identifying suitable banking partners and preparing the necessary documentation to open accounts.

Benefits of a Swiss Crypto License

Switzerland offers several distinct advantages that make it the best country for a crypto license for many founders:

  • Regulatory clarity: FINMA provides clear guidance and is known for its innovation-friendly approach.
  • Low taxes: Corporate tax rates are among the lowest in Europe, and there is no VAT on crypto transactions.
  • Political stability: Switzerland’s stable legal and political environment reduces business risk.
  • Access to banking: Licensed crypto companies can access traditional banking services more easily than in many other jurisdictions.
  • Talent pool: Switzerland has a strong blockchain talent pool, especially in Zug (Crypto Valley).
  • International reputation: A Swiss license enhances credibility with partners, investors, and customers.

Compliance and Trust

Maintaining a Swiss crypto license requires ongoing compliance with AML, KYC, and reporting obligations. FINMA conducts periodic audits and expects companies to have strong internal controls. Failure to comply can result in fines or license revocation.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Founders often make these errors when pursuing a Swiss crypto license:

  • Underestimating the timeline: The process can take 6-12 months; plan accordingly.
  • Insufficient capital: FINMA expects adequate capitalisation; too little can lead to rejection.
  • Poor documentation: A vague business plan or weak AML policies are common reasons for delays.
  • Ignoring tax obligations: Even if you are not licensed, you may have tax reporting duties.
  • Choosing the wrong license type: Applying for a full banking license when a FinTech license suffices wastes time and money.
  • Not engaging local experts: Swiss regulatory language is German/French/Italian; working with local advisors is essential.

Frequently asked questions

Is Switzerland the best country for a crypto license in 2026?

Switzerland is widely regarded as one of the best countries due to its clear regulation, low taxes, and strong banking infrastructure. However, the best choice depends on your business model, budget, and timeline. Consulting24 can help you compare options.

What is the minimum capital for a Swiss crypto license?

For a FinTech license, FINMA typically expects at least CHF 300,000. For simpler registrations, capital may be as low as CHF 100,000. Exact requirements are assessed case by case.

How long does it take to get a Swiss crypto license?

The process usually takes 6 to 12 months from application to approval. Simpler registrations may be faster. Consulting24 advises on realistic timelines based on your specific situation.

What activities are allowed under a Swiss crypto license?

Allowed activities include exchange, custody, token issuance, payment services, and asset management, subject to the license type. Deposit-taking may require a full banking license.

Does Switzerland have a crypto tax advantage?

Yes. Corporate tax rates range from 12% to 21%, and there is no VAT on crypto transactions. Private investors pay no capital gains tax. Professional traders are taxed on income.

Can I get a Swiss crypto license if I am not based in Switzerland?

Yes, but you must incorporate a Swiss company with a registered office in Switzerland. The company’s management must be based in Switzerland or have local representation.

What is the difference between a FinTech license and a banking license in Switzerland?

A FinTech license is for companies that accept deposits up to CHF 100 million but do not invest or pay interest. A full banking license is for traditional banking activities and requires CHF 10 million capital.

Panama offers a flat fee of EUR 6,000, no capital requirement, and fast setup (2-4 weeks). Switzerland is more expensive and time-consuming but offers greater regulatory clarity and international credibility.

Do I need a Swiss crypto license if I only operate a DeFi protocol?

It depends on the protocol’s structure. Non-custodial protocols without intermediary functions may be unregulated. However, if you hold client assets or issue tokens, a license may be required.
