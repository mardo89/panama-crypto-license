What Is a Crypto License and Why Compare Panama and Singapore?

A crypto license is a regulatory authorization that allows a business to legally offer virtual asset services such as exchange, custody, and transfer. Both Panama and Singapore are attractive hubs, but they differ vastly in cost, regulatory rigor, and market access. Panama offers a flat EUR 6,000 company setup with a straightforward licensing process, while Singapore demands higher capital and compliance but provides access to a sophisticated Asian financial market. This comparison helps founders weigh trade-offs between speed, cost, and credibility. For example, a small exchange processing under EUR 1 million monthly may find Panama ideal, whereas a large institutional platform targeting Asian clients may prefer Singapore. The choice also affects banking relationships, tax obligations, and future expansion plans. Consulting24 has guided clients through both routes, and we share practical insights here.

Who Needs a Crypto License in These Jurisdictions?

Any business that provides cryptocurrency exchange, wallet services, custody, or payment processing needs a license. Panama’s regime is ideal for startups and mid-sized firms seeking a cost-effective, efficient route to compliance. Singapore is better suited for larger enterprises or those targeting institutional clients in Asia who require a strong regulatory stamp. Founders operating in both regions must comply with local anti-money laundering (AML) and counter-terrorism financing (CTF) rules. For instance, a DeFi protocol offering custodial services would need a license in both jurisdictions if serving local users. Panama’s lighter touch allows faster market entry, while Singapore’s rigorous process builds trust with partners and banks. Consulting24 helps assess which profile fits your business model best.

License Type and Regulator

Panama: The regulator is the Superintendence of Banks of Panama (SBP) for crypto-related activities, though the licensing framework is still evolving. Currently, Panama does not have a dedicated crypto license but operates under a registration system for virtual asset service providers (VASPs). The process is managed by the Financial Analysis Unit (UAF) for AML compliance. In practice, firms register as a Panama company and obtain a business license, then comply with AML obligations. The UAF requires submission of a compliance program, appointment of a compliance officer, and regular reporting. There is no specific capital requirement, but the company must demonstrate operational capacity.

Singapore: The Monetary Authority of Singapore (MAS) regulates crypto under the Payment Services Act (PSA). The main license is the Major Payment Institution (MPI) license, required for firms exceeding certain transaction thresholds. For smaller volumes, a Standard Payment Institution (SPI) license may suffice. The MAS is known for rigorous due diligence and high compliance standards. Applicants must submit detailed business plans, risk assessments, and undergo background checks on key personnel. The MAS also requires a local director and a physical office. Both licenses are valid for an indefinite period but subject to annual review.

Capital Requirements

Panama: There is no specific minimum capital requirement for crypto businesses under current law. However, a typical Panama corporation requires a minimum capital of USD 10,000 (approx. EUR 9,200) for incorporation, though this is often not strictly enforced. For AML purposes, having sufficient capital to cover operational costs is advisable. Many firms opt for a higher capital to appear credible to banks and partners. The capital can be in the form of cash or assets, and there is no requirement to maintain a minimum balance after incorporation.

Singapore: The MAS requires a minimum base capital of SGD 250,000 (approx. EUR 170,000) for an MPI license, and SGD 100,000 (approx. EUR 68,000) for an SPI license. Higher thresholds apply if the firm handles payment services beyond crypto. Capital must be maintained as liquid assets, such as cash or government bonds. The MAS may also require additional capital based on the risk profile and transaction volume. This capital acts as a buffer against operational risks and is a key factor in the regulator’s assessment of financial soundness.

Tax Treatment

Panama: Panama operates a territorial tax system. Only income sourced within Panama is taxed. Offshore income, including crypto trading and exchange revenues from non-Panamanian clients, is generally tax-exempt. There is no capital gains tax. Corporate income tax on local-source income is 25%. Panama also has no VAT on crypto services. This makes Panama highly attractive for firms that serve international clients. For example, an exchange earning fees from global users would pay zero tax on those fees, provided the services are not deemed to have a local source. However, careful structuring is needed to avoid triggering local taxation.

Singapore: Singapore taxes income at a flat corporate rate of 17%, with exemptions for new companies. Capital gains are not taxed. However, crypto trading profits may be considered income if the business is deemed to be trading. There is no GST on crypto transactions. Singapore has a wide network of double taxation treaties, which can reduce withholding taxes on cross-border payments. For a company with significant local operations, the effective tax rate can be as low as 8-10% after incentives. But for pure offshore income, Panama’s territorial system is more favorable.

Allowed Activities

Panama: Allowed activities include cryptocurrency exchange (fiat-to-crypto and crypto-to-crypto), custody, wallet services, and payment processing. There is no explicit ban on derivatives or DeFi, but activities must comply with AML laws. Panama does not restrict the issuance of tokens, though securities laws may apply if the token is deemed a security. For example, an exchange offering margin trading would be allowed, but must implement risk controls. Staking and lending are also permitted, as long as they do not involve unauthorized deposit-taking. The flexibility makes Panama suitable for a wide range of business models.

Singapore: The PSA covers digital payment token (DPT) services: exchange, transfer, and custodial wallet services. Derivatives and margin trading are allowed under specific conditions. MAS prohibits anonymous accounts and requires strict KYC. Staking and lending are not directly regulated but may fall under securities laws if they involve investment products. For instance, a platform offering interest on crypto deposits would need to comply with the Securities and Futures Act. The MAS also restricts advertising to the public, and firms must provide risk warnings. Overall, Singapore’s regime is more restrictive but offers legal clarity.

Step-by-Step Application Process

  1. Incorporate a company: In Panama, this is a quick online process (1-2 weeks) with a registered agent. In Singapore, it requires a local director and registered address (2-4 weeks) via the Accounting and Corporate Regulatory Authority (ACRA).
  2. Prepare AML/CFT policies: Both jurisdictions require detailed policies, risk assessments, and procedures. Panama expects a manual aligned with FATF recommendations; Singapore requires a comprehensive compliance framework including transaction monitoring and suspicious activity reporting.
  3. Submit application: Panama: register with UAF and obtain business license. Singapore: apply to MAS via the online portal with supporting documents such as business plan, financial projections, and background checks.
  4. Due diligence: Both regulators will vet beneficial owners, directors, and compliance officers. Singapore is more thorough, often requiring interviews and site visits. Panama’s UAF may request additional information but generally processes faster.
  5. License issuance: Panama issues a registration certificate; Singapore issues a license with conditions (e.g., transaction limits, reporting frequency).
  6. Ongoing compliance: Regular reporting, AML audits, and transaction monitoring are mandatory. Singapore requires annual audits and submission of compliance reports. Panama requires periodic updates to the UAF.

Banking and Payment Integration

Panama: Banking is relatively accessible. Local banks accept crypto companies, though some may require a relationship manager. International payment gateways like Stripe and PayPal are available. Panama has a USD-based economy, simplifying fiat handling. However, some banks may still be cautious due to reputational risks. Consulting24 can introduce clients to crypto-friendly banks. For example, Banco General and Banistmo are known to work with licensed VASPs. Opening an account typically takes 2-4 weeks with proper documentation.

Singapore: Banking is more restrictive. Major banks like DBS, OCBC, and UOB often require a local presence and a track record. Crypto firms may need to use specialized fintech-friendly banks or payment service providers. MAS has issued guidelines encouraging banks to serve crypto businesses under strict AML controls. However, many banks still decline due to compliance costs. Alternatives include digital banks like ANEXT or payment platforms like Xfers. The process can take 1-3 months. Benefits of Each Jurisdiction

Panama: Low cost (EUR 6,000 flat setup), fast timeline (2-4 months), territorial tax system (no tax on offshore income), no minimum capital, and a crypto-friendly environment. Ideal for startups and small to mid-sized businesses. Panama also offers privacy (bearer shares are allowed but discouraged) and a strategic location near the Americas. The lack of a dedicated crypto law provides flexibility but also regulatory uncertainty.

Singapore: High regulatory credibility, access to Asian markets, strong banking infrastructure, no capital gains tax, and a clear legal framework. Best for firms targeting institutional clients or high transaction volumes. Singapore’s license is recognized globally and can facilitate partnerships with banks and exchanges. The MAS’s proactive approach to innovation (e.g., sandbox) also benefits fintechs. However, the high cost and compliance burden may deter smaller players.

Common Mistakes When Choosing a Jurisdiction

  • Underestimating compliance costs - especially in Singapore, where ongoing expenses are high. Budget for annual audits, compliance officer salary, and transaction monitoring tools.
  • Ignoring tax implications - Panama’s territorial system is advantageous only if income is sourced outside Panama. If you serve local clients, you may owe 25% tax.
  • Choosing based solely on cost - a cheap license may not provide the credibility needed for partnerships. Singapore’s higher cost can be an investment in reputation.
  • Failing to plan for banking - both jurisdictions require a local bank account, which can be delayed. Start the banking process early, ideally before license application.
  • Not considering future expansion - a Panama license may not be recognized in other jurisdictions, while Singapore’s license is more portable. For example, a Panama license may not satisfy EU MiCA requirements, whereas Singapore’s may be accepted under equivalence provisions.
  • Overlooking local director requirements - Singapore mandates at least one local director who is a resident. Panama does not require local directors, but a registered agent is needed.

Frequently asked questions

What is the main difference between Panama and Singapore crypto licenses?

The main difference is cost and regulatory rigor. Panama offers a low-cost, fast setup (EUR 6,000 flat, 2-4 months) with territorial tax, while Singapore requires higher capital (SGD 250,000 for MPI), longer timeline (6-12 months), and more compliance, but provides stronger credibility and Asian market access.

Can I use a Panama crypto license to serve clients in Europe?

Yes, but you must comply with local laws in each European country. Panama’s license does not automatically grant passporting rights. For EU clients, a MiCA-compliant license in Lithuania or Estonia may be more suitable.

Is Singapore’s crypto license recognized globally?

Singapore’s MAS license is highly respected and often accepted by banks and partners worldwide. However, it does not automatically grant rights in other countries. For global operations, you may need multiple licenses.

How long does it take to get a Panama crypto license?

The entire process, from company incorporation to license issuance, typically takes 2-4 months. This includes company setup (1-2 weeks), AML policy preparation (2-4 weeks), and UAF registration (4-8 weeks).

What are the ongoing compliance costs for a Panama crypto license?

Ongoing costs are low, typically EUR 2,000-5,000 per year. This includes registered agent fees, AML compliance updates, and annual reporting. There are no mandatory audits unless triggered by transaction volume.

Does Panama require a physical office for a crypto license?

No, Panama does not require a physical office. A registered agent address is sufficient. However, having a local presence can facilitate banking and client trust.

Can I convert a Panama company to a Singapore company later?

No, you cannot convert a company across borders. You would need to incorporate a new Singapore entity and apply for a new license. However, you can restructure your group to include both entities.

What are the tax benefits of Panama for crypto businesses?

Panama’s territorial tax system exempts offshore income from tax. Crypto exchange fees from non-Panamanian clients are typically tax-free. There is no capital gains tax, and no VAT on crypto services.

Is Singapore’s crypto license suitable for a startup?

It depends on the startup’s budget and goals. The high capital requirement (SGD 250,000) and compliance costs (EUR 10,000+/year) may be prohibitive for early-stage startups. Panama or Lithuania may be better options.

What activities are prohibited under Panama’s crypto regime?

Panama does not explicitly prohibit any crypto activities, but securities laws apply to token offerings. Activities involving fraud, money laundering, or unlicensed deposit-taking are illegal. Derivatives and DeFi are generally allowed.

Can I operate a crypto exchange in Panama without a license?

No, all virtual asset service providers must register with the UAF and obtain a business license. Operating without registration can result in fines and legal action.
