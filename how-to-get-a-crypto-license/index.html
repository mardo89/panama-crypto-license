What Is a Crypto License and Why Do You Need One?

A crypto license is a regulatory authorisation that allows a business to offer virtual asset services legally. Depending on the jurisdiction, this might cover exchange, custody, transfer, or wallet services. In 2026, with MiCA fully operational, any company providing crypto services to EU residents must hold a CASP (Crypto Asset Service Provider) license from an EU member state. This license is mandatory, not optional. Operating without one exposes you to fines, legal action, and reputational damage.

Beyond compliance, a license builds trust with clients, banks, and partners. It signals that your business meets anti-money laundering (AML) and counter-terrorism financing (CTF) standards. For many founders, the question is not if to get licensed, but how to get a crypto license efficiently and cost-effectively. The answer depends on your business model, target market, and budget. Panama, for example, offers a straightforward company setup with no separate crypto license requirement for certain activities, while Estonia and Lithuania require a full CASP application. Consulting24 delivers directly in all three locations, so we can give you first-hand advice on each route.

Consider a real-world example: a European exchange startup wanting to serve clients across the EU. Without a CASP license, it cannot legally operate. By obtaining a license in Lithuania, it gains passporting rights to all 27 EU member states. In contrast, a non-EU firm focusing on Latin American markets might choose Panama for its territorial tax system and lower setup costs. The choice of jurisdiction directly impacts your timeline, capital outlay, and operational flexibility.

Who Needs a Crypto License?

Any business that provides one or more of the following services in or to the EU must obtain a CASP license under MiCA:

  • Exchange of crypto assets for fiat currency
  • Exchange of crypto assets for other crypto assets
  • Custody and administration of crypto assets on behalf of clients
  • Transfer services for crypto assets
  • Execution of orders for crypto assets
  • Placement of crypto assets
  • Reception and transmission of orders for crypto assets
  • Advising on crypto assets

Non-EU firms serving EU clients also need a license. If you are outside the EU, jurisdictions like Panama allow you to operate without a crypto-specific license, but you must still comply with local AML laws. License Type and Regulator

Under MiCA, the relevant license is the CASP (Crypto Asset Service Provider) license. Each EU member state designates a national competent authority (NCA) to issue and supervise these licenses. For example:

  • Estonia: Financial Supervision Authority (Finantsinspektsioon)
  • Lithuania: Bank of Lithuania (Lietuvos bankas)
  • Panama: No dedicated crypto regulator; company registration through the Public Registry (Registro Público) and AML compliance via the Financial Analysis Unit (UAF)

For non-EU jurisdictions like Panama, you do not obtain a "crypto license" per se. Instead, you register a company with a business activity that covers virtual asset services and ensure compliance with AML/CFT regulations. The Bank of Lithuania, for instance, is known for a thorough but efficient process, while Estonia's Finantsinspektsioon requires a detailed business plan and proof of local presence. Panama's process is simpler: you incorporate a company and register with the UAF for AML purposes.

Capital Requirements

Under MiCA, capital requirements are tiered based on the services offered:

  • EUR 50,000 for certain services (e.g., order execution, advice)
  • EUR 125,000 for exchange and transfer services
  • EUR 150,000 for custody services

These are minimums. The regulator may require higher capital based on your business volume and risk profile. In Panama, there is no statutory minimum capital for a standard corporation (Sociedad Anónima), but you must demonstrate sufficient funds to cover operational expenses. For example, a startup offering only crypto advice needs EUR 50,000, while an exchange handling both fiat and crypto requires EUR 125,000. If you also offer custody, the total minimum is EUR 150,000. Capital must be held in liquid assets and cannot be used for operational expenses. In Panama, we recommend capital of at least EUR 10,000 to show substance to banks and partners.

Tax Treatment of Crypto License Holders

Tax treatment varies by jurisdiction and your corporate structure. In Estonia, corporate profits are taxed only when distributed (0% on retained earnings). Lithuania applies a 15% corporate income tax (with possible 0% for small companies under certain conditions). Panama taxes only income sourced within Panama (territorial tax system), meaning foreign-source crypto income is generally tax-free. However, you must still file annual tax returns. Estonia's deferred tax system is attractive for reinvesting profits, while Lithuania offers a 0% rate for small companies (revenue under EUR 300,000 and fewer than 10 employees) for the first year.

Step-by-Step Process to Get a Crypto License

While the exact process differs by jurisdiction, the general steps are similar:

  1. Choose your jurisdiction based on your target market, budget, and timeline.
  2. Incorporate a company in the chosen jurisdiction (e.g., Panama company for EUR 6,000).
  3. Prepare documentation: business plan, AML/KYC policies, risk assessment, financial projections, and personal background checks.
  4. Submit the application to the regulator (or register with the relevant authority in Panama).
  5. Undergo review and respond to any queries from the regulator.
  6. Receive the license or registration certificate.
  7. Maintain compliance with ongoing reporting, audits, and AML obligations.

Common Mistakes When Applying for a Crypto License

Many applicants make avoidable errors. Here are the most common:

  • Incomplete documentation: Missing business plans, AML policies, or financial projections cause delays.
  • Underestimating capital requirements: Applying with insufficient capital leads to rejection.
  • Poorly defined business model: Regulators want clarity on how you operate, your target market, and revenue sources.
  • Ignoring local presence requirements: Many jurisdictions require a physical office, local director, or compliance officer.
  • Choosing the wrong jurisdiction: Picking a jurisdiction based solely on cost can backfire if it doesn't suit your business.

Frequently asked questions

What is the difference between a crypto license and a VASP registration?

Under MiCA, the term VASP (Virtual Asset Service Provider) has been replaced by CASP (Crypto Asset Service Provider). A CASP license is the new EU-wide authorisation. Older VASP registrations (e.g., Estonia's pre-MiCA regime) are no longer valid and must be upgraded to CASP. Panama uses a company registration model, not a license.

Can I get a crypto license remotely?

Yes, many jurisdictions allow remote applications. Consulting24 handles the entire process for Estonia, Lithuania, and Panama without you needing to travel. However, some regulators may require a local director or physical office. We arrange these services for you.

How long does a crypto license last?

CASP licenses under MiCA are valid indefinitely but require annual renewal and ongoing compliance. Panama company registration is perpetual but must file annual reports and renew AML registration each year. Failure to comply can lead to revocation.

What happens if I operate without a license?

Operating without a required license exposes you to regulatory fines, legal action, and potential criminal charges. In the EU, unlicensed activity can result in fines up to 5% of annual turnover or EUR 5 million. Your business may also be blacklisted by banks and partners.

Do I need a license for a crypto wallet?

If you provide custodial wallet services (holding private keys for clients), you need a CASP license with custody authorisation. Non-custodial wallets (where users control keys) typically do not require licensing, but check local laws.

Can I use a Panama company to serve EU clients?

Yes, but you must comply with MiCA if you actively market to EU residents. You would need a CASP license from an EU member state. Panama is best for non-EU clients or as a holding company. Consulting24 can help you structure a dual-entity setup.

What are the ongoing costs after getting a license?

Ongoing costs include annual regulatory fees (EUR 1,000-5,000), compliance software, audits, and legal/accounting services. In Panama, annual costs are around EUR 1,500. In Lithuania, expect EUR 3,000-5,000 per year. Consulting24 offers compliance packages to manage these.

Is a crypto license required for NFTs?

Under MiCA, NFTs that are unique and non-fungible are generally exempt. However, if you issue fractionalised NFTs or offer exchange services for NFTs, you may need a license. The classification depends on the specific asset. Consult with us for a case-by-case assessment.

Can I apply for multiple licenses in different jurisdictions?

Yes, many firms hold licenses in multiple jurisdictions for redundancy and market access. However, each application requires separate capital and compliance. Consulting24 can coordinate multi-jurisdiction strategies, using our network.

What is the success rate for crypto license applications?

Success rates vary by jurisdiction and the quality of your application. With Consulting24's preparation, most clients succeed on first submission. We have a strong track record for Lithuania and Estonia. Panama company registration is virtually guaranteed with proper documentation.
