What is the Bermuda Crypto Fund License?

The Bermuda crypto fund license is a regulatory authorization issued by the Bermuda Monetary Authority (BMA) under the Investment Funds Act 2006, as amended by the Digital Asset Business Act 2018. It allows fund managers to operate collective investment schemes that invest primarily in digital assets, including cryptocurrencies, tokens, and other blockchain-based assets.

Bermuda distinguishes between different fund structures: professional funds (for qualified investors), institutional funds, and public funds. The most common for crypto is the professional fund, which requires a minimum investment of USD 100,000 per investor and is limited to 20 investors. The BMA also requires the fund to have a licensed fund administrator, custodian, and auditor based in Bermuda.

As of 2026, Bermuda remains a non-EU jurisdiction, so it is not subject to MiCA. This gives fund managers flexibility in capital requirements and operational structure compared to EU member states. However, the BMA expects high standards of governance and transparency. For example, the fund must appoint a resident director and maintain a physical office in Bermuda. The license is ideal for funds targeting institutional investors who value regulatory oversight.

License Type and Regulator

The Bermuda crypto fund license is regulated by the Bermuda Monetary Authority (BMA), the island's integrated financial regulator. The BMA oversees both the fund itself and the fund's service providers (administrator, custodian, auditor). The license type is typically a 'Professional Class' fund under the Investment Funds Act, which requires:

  • Minimum initial investment per investor: USD 100,000
  • Maximum number of investors: 20
  • Fund must be closed-ended or open-ended as per the offering memorandum

The BMA also requires the fund to have a physical presence in Bermuda, including a registered office and at least one director who is a resident of Bermuda. The fund's constitutional documents must be approved by the BMA, and the offering memorandum must disclose all material risks.

For funds that wish to accept more than 20 investors or smaller minimum investments, the BMA offers institutional and public fund classes, but these come with higher capital requirements and ongoing disclosure obligations. Most crypto fund managers opt for the professional class due to its flexibility.

Capital Requirement

Bermuda does not prescribe a specific minimum capital requirement for professional funds under the Investment Funds Act. However, the BMA expects the fund to have sufficient capital to cover operational expenses and to demonstrate financial soundness. Typically, funds are capitalized with at least USD 100,000 in initial seed capital from the manager or investors. The fund's offering memorandum must disclose the capital structure.

In contrast, the EU's Markets in Crypto-Assets Regulation (MiCA) imposes capital tiers of EUR 50,000 / 125,000 / 150,000 for CASPs, but Bermuda's regime is more flexible, making it attractive for smaller fund managers. For example, a fund manager with only USD 200,000 in seed capital can launch a professional fund in Bermuda, whereas in Lithuania they would need at least EUR 125,000 for a CASP license. However, the fund must still demonstrate that it has enough capital to operate for at least 12 months.

Tax Treatment

Bermuda has no corporate income tax, capital gains tax, or withholding tax. Funds are exempt from Bermuda taxation, provided they are not resident in Bermuda for tax purposes (i.e., managed and controlled offshore). The fund must register for Bermuda's Economic Substance regime, but for pure investment funds, the requirement is minimal. Investors are taxed in their home jurisdictions; Bermuda does not impose any additional taxes on the fund or its distributions.

For example, a Bermuda fund that generates USD 1 million in capital gains pays zero tax in Bermuda. The fund's investors, if US-based, would be subject to US tax on their share of the gains, but they can benefit from the fund's tax transparency. Bermuda also has no VAT or stamp duty on fund transactions.

This tax neutrality is a major advantage over EU jurisdictions like Lithuania, where corporate income tax is 15% on profits. However, funds must ensure they do not create a permanent establishment in a high-tax jurisdiction. Consulting24 can help structure the fund to maintain tax efficiency.

Allowed Activities

A Bermuda crypto fund license permits the fund to engage in the following activities:

  • Investing in digital assets (cryptocurrencies, tokens, NFTs)
  • Staking and lending of digital assets (if disclosed in the offering memorandum)
  • Participation in DeFi protocols (subject to BMA approval)
  • Holding fiat currency for operational purposes
  • Issuing fund shares to qualified investors

The fund must not engage in proprietary trading, use beyond disclosed limits, or provide custody services to third parties (custody is outsourced to a licensed custodian). The BMA also requires that the fund's investment strategy be clearly defined in the offering memorandum. For example, a fund that plans to actively trade derivatives on crypto exchanges must disclose this and may need additional approvals.

Bermuda's regime is less restrictive than MiCA in terms of allowed activities. For instance, a Bermuda fund can invest in DeFi protocols without needing a separate license, whereas an EU CASP may need additional authorization for such activities. However, the BMA expects strong risk management and disclosure.

Compliance and Trust

Bermuda's regulatory regime is known for its high standards. The BMA conducts ongoing supervision, including annual reviews and on-site inspections. Funds must comply with anti-money laundering (AML) and counter-terrorist financing (CTF) regulations, as well as economic substance requirements. The fund must appoint a compliance officer and a money laundering reporting officer (MLRO) who are based in Bermuda.

Consulting24 advises on structuring your fund to meet these obligations. This is general guidance, not legal advice. Always consult a Bermuda-licensed lawyer for specific compliance matters. For example, the fund must maintain a risk-based AML program, including customer due diligence on all investors and ongoing transaction monitoring. The BMA expects the fund to file suspicious activity reports (SARs) when necessary.

Trust is built through transparency. The fund's audited financial statements must be filed with the BMA annually and made available to investors. The BMA also publishes a list of licensed funds on its website, which enhances credibility.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Underestimating costs: Annual service provider fees can be high; budget accordingly. Many first-time fund managers are surprised by the USD 40,000-100,000 in annual costs.
  • Ignoring economic substance: The fund must have a physical presence in Bermuda, including a registered office and local directors. Attempting to operate without substance can lead to license revocation.
  • Incomplete documentation: The BMA requires a detailed offering memorandum; incomplete submissions cause delays. Ensure all risk factors and investment strategies are fully disclosed.
  • Choosing the wrong fund structure: Professional funds are suitable for most, but public funds require more capital and disclosure. Selecting the wrong class can result in additional costs and regulatory scrutiny.
  • Delaying bank account opening: Bank account applications can take weeks; start early to avoid delaying the fund launch.
