What Is the South Korea Crypto License (VASP Registration)?

The South Korea crypto license is officially called the Virtual Asset Service Provider (VASP) registration. It is mandatory for any business that exchanges, stores, transfers, or manages virtual assets for customers. The registration is issued by the Korea Financial Intelligence Unit (KOFIU) under the Financial Services Commission (FSC). The legal basis is the Act on Reporting and Using Specified Financial Transaction Information, which was amended in 2021 to include virtual assets.

Key features of the VASP regime include:

  • Real-name account requirement: All customers must have verified accounts at local banks, linking their real identity to their crypto trading.
  • Information Security Management System (ISMS) certification from the Korea Internet and Security Agency (KISA). This is a rigorous security audit covering data protection, incident response, and system resilience.
  • Compliance with Anti-Money Laundering (AML) and Counter-Terrorism Financing (CTF) obligations, including transaction monitoring and reporting.
  • Reporting of suspicious transactions and large cash transactions (over 10 million KRW) to KOFIU.

The VASP registration is a strict regime designed to protect consumers and prevent illicit activities. It applies to both centralized exchanges and virtual asset wallet services. As of 2026, there are only a handful of licensed exchanges in South Korea, including Upbit, Bithumb, Coinone, and Korbit, reflecting the high barrier to entry.

Who Needs a South Korea Crypto License?

Any entity that provides virtual asset services to South Korean residents must obtain VASP registration. This includes:

  • Cryptocurrency exchanges (spot, margin, derivatives).
  • Virtual asset wallet providers (custodial and non-custodial).
  • Brokerage services for virtual assets.
  • Initial exchange offerings (IEOs) and token issuance platforms.
  • Virtual asset transfer services (remittance-like services).

Foreign companies that target South Korean users or have a physical presence in Korea are also required to register. Even if you operate from overseas, if your platform is accessible in Korean or markets to Korean users, you may fall under the FSC's jurisdiction. For example, a global exchange that offers KRW trading pairs or Korean-language support must register. License Type and Regulator

The South Korea crypto license is a single registration type for all VASPs. There are no separate categories for different services, but the scope of your business activities must be clearly defined in your application. The regulator does not issue different tiers based on trading volume or asset type; all VASPs are subject to the same core requirements.

The primary regulator is the Financial Services Commission (FSC), which oversees the financial system. The day-to-day supervision and registration process is handled by the Korea Financial Intelligence Unit (KOFIU), a division of the FSC. Additionally, the Korea Internet and Security Agency (KISA) is responsible for certifying the ISMS, a prerequisite for registration. The Bank of Korea also plays a role in monitoring virtual asset transactions for monetary stability.

Capital Requirement

South Korea does not have a fixed minimum capital requirement for VASP registration in the law itself. However, in practice, the FSC expects a company to have sufficient financial resources to operate safely and comply with AML obligations. Most applicants have at least 1 billion KRW (approximately $750,000 USD) in paid-up capital. This is not a formal requirement but a market benchmark. Some banks may require a higher deposit (1-2 billion KRW) to open real-name accounts.

Tax Treatment

South Korea taxes cryptocurrency gains as income. For individuals, gains from virtual asset trading are subject to a 20% capital gains tax (plus local income tax surcharge) on net profits exceeding 2.5 million KRW per year. This tax was originally scheduled for 2022 but has been delayed; as of 2026, it is in effect. The tax applies to both domestic and foreign exchanges if the user is a Korean resident.

For corporations, profits from virtual asset transactions are taxed as ordinary business income at the corporate tax rate (9% to 24% depending on income level). Value-Added Tax (VAT) is not applied to cryptocurrency trading, but commission fees may be subject to VAT (10%). Corporate entities must also comply with transfer pricing rules if they engage in cross-border transactions. Additionally, a 20% withholding tax may apply to certain payments to non-residents. It is advisable to engage a Korean tax advisor to structure your operations efficiently.

Allowed Activities Under the License

A VASP registration permits the following activities:

  • Exchange of virtual assets for fiat currency (KRW) or other virtual assets.
  • Transfer of virtual assets (custodial and non-custodial).
  • Safekeeping and administration of virtual assets (custodial wallets).
  • Participation in and provision of financial services related to virtual assets (e.g., staking, lending, margin trading if approved).

Activities that are not allowed include unregistered securities offerings, gambling-related tokens, and services that facilitate anonymous transactions (e.g., privacy coins may be restricted). The FSC closely monitors compliance and can revoke licenses for violations. For example, in 2023, the FSC banned the listing of certain privacy coins on Korean exchanges. It is important to review the allowed asset list with your compliance team.

Step-by-Step Application Process

Obtaining a South Korea crypto license involves several stages. Here is a typical process:

  1. Pre-incorporation planning: Determine your business model, corporate structure, and capital needs. Engage local legal and compliance advisors. This stage typically takes 1-2 months.
  2. Company incorporation: Register a Korean corporation (usually a stock company or limited liability company) with the Korea Trade Commission. Minimum capital of 100 million KRW is common. Cost: $5,000 - $10,000.
  3. ISMS certification: Implement an information security management system and obtain certification from KISA. This includes a security audit, documentation review, and on-site inspection. Cost: $50,000 - $100,000; timeline: 3-6 months.
  4. Open real-name accounts: Partner with a Korean bank to offer real-name accounts for your customers. This is a critical step and often the most challenging. Banks require a minimum deposit and a strong AML program. Cost: $10,000 - $30,000; timeline: 1-3 months.
  5. Prepare application: Compile all required documents: business plan, AML policy, risk management framework, financial statements, background checks on directors, and ISMS certificate. Cost: included in legal fees.
  6. Submit to KOFIU: File the VASP registration application through the FSC's electronic system. Pay the application fee (approximately $5,000). Timeline: 2-4 months for review.
  7. Review and interview: KOFIU reviews the application and may request additional information or conduct interviews with key personnel. Be prepared to explain your business model in detail.
  8. Approval: If all conditions are met, KOFIU issues the VASP registration certificate. The company is now licensed to operate. Annual renewal and ongoing compliance required.

Banking and Payment Partners

One of the biggest challenges for VASP applicants is securing a banking partnership for real-name accounts. Major Korean banks like Kookmin, Shinhan, Woori, and Hana Bank offer this service, but they have strict due diligence requirements. Banks typically require a minimum deposit of 1-2 billion KRW and a track record of AML compliance. They also conduct background checks on the company's directors and shareholders.

Without a real-name account, a VASP cannot operate legally in South Korea. Therefore, it is essential to start discussions with banks early in the process. Compliance and Ongoing Obligations

Once licensed, VASPs must maintain ongoing compliance:

  • Annual renewal of ISMS certification, which requires a security audit and updates to the system.
  • Regular AML/CTF audits and reporting to KOFIU, including submission of suspicious transaction reports (STRs) and large cash transaction reports (CTRs).
  • Submission of transaction reports for suspicious activities and large cash transactions (over 10 million KRW).
  • Maintaining real-name accounts for all customers, with proper KYC procedures.
  • Appointment of a compliance officer and AML personnel, who must be registered with KOFIU.
  • Conducting regular employee training on AML and data security.

Failure to comply can result in fines, suspension, or revocation of the license. For example, in 2024, a Korean exchange was fined for inadequate AML controls. Common Mistakes to Avoid

Many applicants face delays or rejections due to common errors:

  • Underestimating the time and cost of ISMS certification. Many companies fail the first audit and need to reapply, adding months to the timeline.
  • Not securing a banking partnership before applying. Without a real-name account, the application will be rejected.
  • Submitting an incomplete or inconsistent application. All documents must be in Korean or officially translated.
  • Lack of a strong AML policy tailored to Korean regulations. A generic policy will not pass scrutiny.
  • Ignoring the requirement for a local office and staff. The FSC expects a physical presence in Korea.
  • Failing to conduct proper background checks on directors. Any criminal record can disqualify the application.

Frequently asked questions

What is the difference between VASP registration and a crypto license in South Korea?

VASP registration is the official term for the crypto license in South Korea. It is a mandatory registration with KOFIU, not a traditional license. However, it functions as a license, granting permission to operate legally. There is no separate license type; all virtual asset service providers must obtain this registration.

Can a foreign company obtain a South Korea crypto license?

Yes, but the company must have a physical presence in South Korea, including a registered office and local staff. Foreign companies can incorporate a Korean subsidiary and then apply for VASP registration. The subsidiary must meet all requirements, including ISMS certification and banking partnerships.

How long does ISMS certification take?

ISMS certification typically takes 3 to 6 months, depending on the complexity of your systems and the readiness of your security measures. The process includes a documentation review, on-site audit, and potential remediation. Many companies require a second attempt, which can extend the timeline.

What are the ongoing costs after obtaining the license?

Ongoing costs include annual ISMS certification renewal (approx. $20,000 - $50,000), compliance staff salaries, legal and audit fees, and bank account maintenance. Total annual costs can range from $100,000 to $300,000, depending on the size of your operation.

Is there a minimum transaction volume required?

No, there is no minimum transaction volume requirement in the law. However, the FSC expects the business to be viable and have a realistic business plan. Very low volume may raise questions about the sustainability of the business, but it is not a formal criterion.

Can I use a virtual asset license from another country to operate in South Korea?

No, a foreign license does not allow you to operate in South Korea. You must obtain a separate VASP registration from KOFIU. However, having a license in a reputable jurisdiction (e.g., Estonia, Lithuania) may help demonstrate credibility during the application process.

What happens if I operate without a VASP registration?

Operating without registration is illegal and can result in criminal penalties, including fines and imprisonment. The FSC actively monitors unregistered services and can block websites, issue cease-and-desist orders, and refer cases to prosecutors. It is essential to obtain registration before offering services to Korean residents.

Does the South Korea crypto license allow me to offer services to non-Korean residents?

Yes, the license allows you to serve both Korean and non-Korean residents. However, you must comply with the laws of other jurisdictions where your customers are located. The license does not grant automatic permission to operate in other countries; separate registrations may be needed.
