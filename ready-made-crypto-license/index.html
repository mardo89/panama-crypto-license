What Is a Ready Made Crypto License?

A ready made crypto license is a company that has already been incorporated and licensed under a specific jurisdiction's crypto regulatory framework. The entity is fully compliant, with its license already issued, and can be transferred to a new beneficial owner. This means you skip the application queue and the initial setup delays.

In practice, the company has no trading history and is a 'shell' ready for your operations. The license is typically a virtual asset service provider (VASP) registration or a crypto exchange license, depending on the jurisdiction. The key advantage is speed: instead of waiting 3-6 months for a new license, you can take over a ready made entity in 1-4 weeks. The entity comes with a registered address, a compliance officer, and AML policies already in place. You simply undergo a change of control process with the regulator, which is usually a formality if the new owner passes KYC.

For example, a ready made license in Lithuania under the Bank of Lithuania's regime might include a company with a paid-up capital of EUR 125,000, a compliant AML manual, and a bank account already opened. You acquire the shares, update the beneficial ownership, and start operating. This is not a grey-market solution; it is a fully legal transfer of a regulated entity.

License Type and Regulator

The type of license depends on the jurisdiction. In Lithuania, the regulator is the Bank of Lithuania, which issues a crypto exchange or crypto wallet operator license under the Law on Prevention of Money Laundering and Terrorist Financing. In Estonia, the Financial Intelligence Unit (FIU) handles the license. In Panama, the regulator is the Superintendencia de Bancos de Panamá (SBP) for certain activities, but Panama is not yet a full EU-style regime. For a ready made crypto license, the entity will already hold a valid license. The license type is typically a VASP registration, which covers exchange services between crypto and fiat, crypto-to-crypto exchange, and custody of virtual assets.

Under MiCA, which is fully in force in 2026, EU member states apply a harmonised framework. The license is called a CASP (Crypto Asset Service Provider) license. The regulator is the national competent authority, such as the Bank of Lithuania or the Estonian FIU. The license scope is defined by service classes: Class 1 (simple wallet), Class 2 (exchange), Class 3 (custody). A ready made license will specify which classes it covers. You must ensure the license matches your business model.

Capital Requirement

Capital requirements vary by jurisdiction and activity. Under MiCA (fully in force in 2026), EU member states apply capital tiers: EUR 50,000 for simple wallet services, EUR 125,000 for exchange services, and EUR 150,000 for services involving custody of client assets. For a ready made license, the capital is already contributed to the company's bank account and is part of the transfer. In Lithuania, for example, a ready made exchange license will have EUR 125,000 in share capital. In Estonia, the minimum is EUR 12,000 for a VASP license, but under MiCA it will increase to EUR 50,000 for most activities.

In Panama, there is no mandatory minimum capital for a crypto license, but the company must show sufficient financial standing. Typically, a capital of EUR 10,000-50,000 is recommended to satisfy the regulator and banking partners. The capital is not locked; it can be used for operational expenses. However, you must maintain the required capital at all times. If you plan to offer custody services, you may need additional capital or insurance.

Tax Treatment

Tax treatment depends on the jurisdiction. Lithuania applies a corporate income tax of 15% (or 5% for small companies under certain conditions). Estonia has a unique system: 0% on retained profits, 20% on distributed profits (dividends). Panama taxes only income sourced within Panama (territorial system), making it attractive for international crypto businesses. For a ready made license, the entity's tax residency is already established. You should consult a tax advisor to understand your personal tax obligations and any transfer pricing issues.

For example, if you acquire a ready made license in Estonia and live in Germany, you may be subject to German CFC rules. Panama is often used for non-EU clients because of its territorial tax system. However, tax planning is complex. We recommend engaging a tax specialist early. The ready made entity may have a tax history (even if zero activity), so you need to file annual returns. Our team can coordinate with local accountants.

Allowed Activities

A ready made crypto license typically permits the following activities:

  • Exchange between virtual assets and fiat currencies
  • Exchange between one or more virtual assets
  • Transfer of virtual assets
  • Custody and administration of virtual assets on behalf of clients
  • Participation in and provision of financial services related to an issuer's offer of virtual assets

Some jurisdictions restrict activities to 'exchange and wallet' only. Always check the specific license scope. In Lithuania, the license covers exchange and custody. In Estonia, it covers exchange and wallet services. Panama's license is more flexible but may not be recognised in the EU. Our team at Consulting24 ensures the ready made license covers your intended business model. If you plan to offer staking or lending, you may need a separate license or a specific endorsement. Common Mistakes

  • Not verifying the license scope: Ensure the license covers your intended activities. Some licenses only allow exchange, not custody.
  • Ignoring tax implications: Transfer of ownership may trigger capital gains tax in the jurisdiction of the entity or your home country.
  • Skipping due diligence: The entity may have hidden liabilities, such as unpaid taxes or past compliance issues. Always audit the entity before purchase.
  • Underestimating ongoing costs: Annual compliance and banking fees can be significant. Budget for at least EUR 10,000 per year in ongoing costs.
  • Choosing the wrong jurisdiction: Some jurisdictions have higher ongoing requirements, such as mandatory audits or physical presence. Panama is low-maintenance; Lithuania requires a local compliance officer.
