What Is an Offshore Malta Crypto License?

The offshore Malta crypto license, formally a Class 3 VFA (Virtual Financial Asset) license, authorises firms to operate a virtual financial asset exchange, provide custodial wallet services, and offer investment services related to VFAs. It is designed for businesses targeting international clients, with Malta's EU membership offering passporting rights across the European Economic Area (EEA). The license is issued by the Malta Financial Services Authority (MFSA) under the VFAA and associated rules.

Key features include a strong regulatory environment, a favourable tax system (effective corporate tax rate as low as 5% through refunds), and access to the EU single market. However, the process is rigorous, requiring detailed business plans, IT security audits, and fit-and-proper checks on directors and shareholders. The license is not a simple registration; it is a full authorisation that demands ongoing compliance, including annual audits, AML/KYC procedures, and transaction monitoring.

Malta's regime is often compared to other EU member states, but it stands out for its early adoption and detailed rulebook. Since MiCA came into full force in 2025, Malta has aligned its VFA rules with MiCA's requirements, meaning that capital tiers for different service classes now follow the EU-wide standard: EUR 50,000 for advisory services, EUR 125,000 for exchange and custody, and EUR 150,000 if holding client assets. This alignment makes Malta a compliant gateway to the EU market.

Who Needs an Offshore Malta Crypto License?

This license is ideal for:

  • Crypto exchanges (both centralised and peer-to-peer platforms) that want to serve EU clients legally.
  • Digital asset custodians and wallet providers holding client funds or keys.
  • Brokers dealing in virtual financial assets for their own account or for clients.
  • Asset managers offering crypto investment services, including portfolio management.
  • Firms wanting to passport services across the EU/EEA without obtaining separate licenses in each country.

It suits established operators or well-funded startups that can meet compliance and capital requirements. Malta is less suitable for early-stage projects with limited budgets due to higher setup and ongoing costs compared to jurisdictions like Estonia or Panama. For example, a startup with a EUR 50,000 budget would struggle with Malta's EUR 125,000 capital requirement plus professional fees. License Type & Regulator

The regulator is the Malta Financial Services Authority (MFSA). Under the VFAA, there are three classes of VFA licenses:

ClassActivitiesMinimum Capital
Class 1Advising on VFAs, receiving and transmitting ordersEUR 50,000
Class 2Dealing in VFAs for own account, placing VFAsEUR 125,000
Class 3Operating a VFA exchange, custodial wallet, investment servicesEUR 125,000 (or EUR 150,000 if holding client assets)

Most offshore operations require a Class 3 license. The MFSA also requires a minimum of two directors, a compliance officer, and a money laundering reporting officer (MLRO) who are resident in Malta or the EU. Additionally, the company must have a physical office in Malta, which can be a serviced office but must be more than a virtual address. The MFSA conducts on-site inspections and expects to see real substance, including local staff and decision-making.

Compared to other EU regulators, the MFSA is known for thorough due diligence. For example, Lithuania's crypto license under MiCA requires EUR 50,000 capital for exchange services, but the application process is faster (2-4 months) and less costly. Estonia's crypto license has a EUR 12,000 capital requirement (under MiCA) and a 1-3 month timeline. Both are direct EU passporting options with lower barriers.

Capital Requirement

For a Class 3 VFA license, the minimum capital requirement is EUR 125,000. If the firm holds client assets (e.g., custodial wallets), the requirement increases to EUR 150,000. This capital must be maintained at all times and can be in the form of cash or liquid assets. The capital must be fully paid up before license issuance.

In contrast, Panama has no minimum capital requirement for a crypto license, making it a more accessible option for startups. For EU jurisdictions under MiCA, capital tiers are EUR 50,000 / 125,000 / 150,000 depending on services offered. Lithuania, for example, requires EUR 50,000 for exchange services, while Estonia requires EUR 12,000 (for limited services) or EUR 50,000 for full CASP. Malta's EUR 125,000 is on the higher end, reflecting its comprehensive scope.

The capital must be held in a Maltese bank account or invested in liquid assets. It cannot be used for operational expenses; it serves as a buffer for client protection. If you are considering Malta, ensure you have access to this capital upfront. For many founders, this requirement alone makes Panama or Lithuania more attractive.

Tax Treatment

Malta offers a highly attractive tax regime for offshore companies. The standard corporate tax rate is 35%, but through a full imputation system and tax refunds, the effective rate can be as low as 5%. For example, a company paying tax on trading profits can claim a refund of 6/7 of the tax paid, resulting in an effective 5% rate. Additionally, Malta has no withholding tax on dividends paid to non-residents, no capital gains tax on share disposals (for non-residents), and a wide double tax treaty network.

However, tax benefits require substance: the company must have real economic presence in Malta (office, staff, decision-making). Pure mailbox operations are not accepted. The MFSA also expects the company to be managed and controlled from Malta. This means you need local directors (or at least one), a local compliance officer, and regular board meetings in Malta.

In comparison, Panama offers a territorial tax system: income earned outside Panama is tax-free, and there is no corporate tax on offshore profits. Panama also has no capital gains tax, no withholding tax, and no VAT on services. For a truly offshore operation, Panama's tax regime is simpler and more cost-effective, with no substance requirements beyond a registered agent and local director (which we provide). Allowed Activities

With a Class 3 VFA license, the following activities are permitted:

  • Operating a virtual financial asset exchange (order matching, trading platform)
  • Providing custodial wallet services for VFAs
  • Brokerage and dealing in VFAs for clients
  • Investment advice and portfolio management related to VFAs
  • Underwriting and placing of VFAs
  • Passporting services to other EU/EEA member states

Activities not allowed include issuing own VFAs (requires a separate VFA issuer license) and providing services to US or sanctioned persons without proper compliance. The license also does not cover derivatives or securities tokens that fall under MiFID II; those require a separate investment firm license.

If your business model involves issuing tokens, you would need a VFA issuer license, which has additional requirements. For most exchange and custody operations, the Class 3 license is sufficient. However, if you plan to offer services globally without EU focus, Panama's license allows all crypto activities (exchange, custody, payment processing) without restrictions, and with no capital requirement.

Step-by-Step Process

The application process involves the following steps:

  1. Pre-application consultation with MFSA (optional but recommended). This helps clarify expectations and identify potential issues early.
  2. Company incorporation in Malta with a registered office and local directors. You need at least two directors, one of whom must be resident in Malta or the EU.
  3. Preparation of documentation: business plan, financial projections, IT security policies, AML/KYC procedures, fit-and-proper declarations for directors and shareholders, and a detailed risk assessment.
  4. Submission of application to MFSA with application fee (EUR 5,000-10,000). The MFSA will acknowledge receipt and begin its review.
  5. MFSA review: may request additional information or clarifications (typically 3-6 months). The regulator will also conduct background checks on key individuals.
  6. On-site inspection (if applicable) and IT audit. The MFSA may visit your premises to verify substance and IT security.
  7. License grant and commencement of operations. Once approved, you must maintain compliance with ongoing reporting obligations.

Banking & Payments

Malta has a well-developed banking sector, but crypto firms often face challenges opening accounts due to perceived risk. Options include:

  • Traditional Maltese banks (e.g., Bank of Valletta, HSBC Malta) - require strong compliance and may refuse crypto clients, especially if the business is not yet licensed.
  • EMI (Electronic Money Institutions) and payment service providers - more crypto-friendly, but may have higher fees and transaction limits.
  • International banks with Malta presence, such as Revolut Business or other fintechs.

We recommend starting the banking process early, ideally before submitting the license application. Some banks require a license to open an account, creating a chicken-and-egg problem. Consulting24 can introduce you to vetted banking partners who understand the crypto industry. For comparison, Panama offers easier banking for crypto firms, especially with international accounts. Panama has a well-established offshore banking sector, and many banks accept crypto-related businesses, provided they have proper compliance. Additionally, Panama does not require a local bank account for the license; you can use an international account.

In Lithuania, banking is also relatively accessible, with several EMIs and traditional banks accepting crypto firms. Estonia has a more restrictive banking environment, but we have established relationships with banks that work with licensed crypto companies.

Common Mistakes

Avoid these pitfalls when applying for a Malta crypto license:

  • Insufficient substance: no physical office or local staff - MFSA will reject. Ensure you have a real office lease and at least one local employee before applying.
  • Poor documentation: vague business plans or weak AML policies cause delays. Invest in professional compliance documentation.
  • Underestimating costs: total setup can exceed EUR 80,000; budget accordingly. Include professional fees, capital, and ongoing expenses.
  • Ignoring tax planning: structure ownership to maximise tax refunds. Use Maltese holding companies and ensure you qualify for the 5% effective rate.
  • Choosing the wrong class: applying for Class 2 when Class 3 is needed, or vice versa. Carefully define your activities and consult with experts.
  • Delaying banking: start the banking process early to avoid delays after license approval.

Frequently asked questions

What is the difference between a Class 2 and Class 3 VFA license in Malta?

Class 2 allows dealing in VFAs for own account and placing VFAs, with a minimum capital of EUR 125,000. Class 3 covers operating an exchange, custodial wallets, and investment services, with the same capital requirement (EUR 150,000 if holding client assets). Most offshore crypto businesses need Class 3.

Can I passport a Malta crypto license to other EU countries?

Yes, a Malta VFA license allows passporting to all EEA member states under MiFID/MiCA rules. You can provide services cross-border or establish branches without additional local licenses. This is a key advantage for EU-focused businesses.

How long does it take to get a Malta crypto license?

The process typically takes 6 to 12 months from application to license grant. This includes document preparation, MFSA review, and potential on-site inspections. Delays can occur if documentation is incomplete or if the regulator requests additional information.

What are the ongoing costs for a Malta crypto license?

Annual compliance and audit costs range from EUR 15,000 to EUR 30,000. You also need to maintain office rent, local staff salaries, and regulatory fees. Total annual costs can exceed EUR 50,000, depending on the complexity of your operations.

Is a Malta crypto license worth the cost?

It depends on your business model. If you need EU passporting and a prestigious regulatory stamp, and have the budget, Malta is a strong choice. For cost-sensitive startups, Panama (EUR 6,000 flat) or Lithuania (EUR 50,000 capital, lower setup) may be better.

What is the minimum capital for a Malta crypto license?

For a Class 3 license, the minimum capital is EUR 125,000. If you hold client assets, it increases to EUR 150,000. This capital must be fully paid up before the license is issued and maintained at all times.

Can I operate a crypto exchange from Malta without a license?

No. Operating a VFA exchange without a license is illegal in Malta and can result in criminal penalties. All VFA service providers must be licensed by the MFSA under the VFAA.

Does Malta require a physical office for a crypto license?

Yes. The MFSA requires the company to have a physical office in Malta, which can be a serviced office but must be more than a virtual address. You also need at least one local director or employee to demonstrate substance.

What is the tax rate for a Malta crypto company?

The standard corporate tax rate is 35%, but through tax refunds, the effective rate can be as low as 5%. This applies to trading profits and investment income. You need to structure the company properly to qualify for refunds.
