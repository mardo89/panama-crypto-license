Overview: Dubai VASP vs Czech CASP

In 2026, both Dubai and the Czech Republic offer regulated crypto licensing regimes, but their structures and market focus differ. Dubai's VARA license is designed for firms that want to serve the UAE and broader MENA region, with a strong emphasis on compliance and local substance. The Czech Republic, under MiCA, provides a passportable license across the European Economic Area (EEA), making it attractive for EU-wide operations.

The Dubai VASP license covers a wide range of virtual asset activities, including exchange, custody, and payment services. The Czech CASP license, aligned with MiCA, classifies services into three tiers with minimum capital requirements of EUR 50,000, 125,000, or 150,000 depending on the activities. Both require strong AML/KYC procedures, but Dubai mandates a physical office and local staff, while the Czech Republic allows a more flexible setup.

Who Needs a License in Dubai or Czech Republic?

Any business offering virtual asset services in or from Dubai must obtain a VASP license from VARA. This includes crypto exchanges, custodians, payment processors, and DeFi platforms with a direct presence. Similarly, in the Czech Republic, any entity providing crypto-asset services to EU residents must hold a CASP license under MiCA, regardless of where the company is registered.

Typical applicants include:

  • Crypto exchanges (centralized and decentralized with an intermediary role)
  • Wallet providers and custodians
  • Payment service providers using crypto
  • Token issuers and ICO/STO platforms
  • Brokers and dealers in virtual assets

If you are targeting the EU market, the Czech CASP license offers passporting rights. For the Middle East and Asia, Dubai's license is more strategic. Consulting24 helps you assess which market aligns with your customer base.

Capital Requirement

Capital requirements differ fundamentally between Dubai and the Czech Republic.

Dubai (VARA): VARA does not prescribe a fixed minimum capital amount in its regulations. Instead, it requires firms to maintain adequate financial resources based on the risk profile and scale of activities. In practice, applicants are expected to demonstrate a minimum of USD 100,000-500,000 in paid-up capital, depending on the license category. This must be held in a local bank account and cannot be withdrawn without regulator approval.

Czech Republic (CASP): Under MiCA, capital requirements are tiered by service class:

  • EUR 50,000 for simple services like custody of crypto-assets
  • EUR 125,000 for exchange services (fiat-to-crypto or crypto-to-crypto)
  • EUR 150,000 for more complex services, including operation of a trading platform

These amounts must be maintained as own funds. The Czech Republic does not require a physical office, reducing the overall capital burden.

Tax Treatment

Tax is a critical factor in the Dubai vs Czech Republic decision.

Dubai: The UAE has a 0% corporate tax rate for most businesses, including crypto firms, provided they are set up in a free zone. There is no VAT on crypto transactions. However, as of 2026, the UAE introduced a 9% corporate tax for mainland companies with profits exceeding AED 375,000, but free zone entities remain exempt if they meet substance requirements. No capital gains tax on crypto holdings.

Czech Republic: The standard corporate income tax rate is 19%. Crypto transactions are subject to VAT only when exchanging fiat for crypto (exempt for crypto-to-crypto). Capital gains on crypto held for more than 3 years are tax-exempt for individuals, but corporate holdings are taxed at 19%. The Czech Republic has a network of tax treaties that can reduce withholding taxes.

For most crypto founders, Dubai offers a more favourable tax environment, but the Czech Republic provides access to the EU single market.

Allowed Activities

Both jurisdictions allow a broad range of crypto activities, but with some differences.

Dubai (VARA): Licensed activities include:

  • Exchange services (fiat-to-crypto, crypto-to-crypto)
  • Lending and borrowing of virtual assets
  • Payment and remittance services
  • Portfolio management and advisory
  • Token issuance (subject to additional approval)

Czech Republic (CASP): Under MiCA, permitted services are:

  • Custody and administration of crypto-assets
  • Operation of a trading platform
  • Exchange of crypto-assets for fiat or other crypto
  • Execution of orders on behalf of clients
  • Placing of crypto-assets
  • Receiving and transmitting orders

Dubai's regime is more permissive for innovative activities like DeFi and NFTs, while the Czech Republic's is harmonised with EU standards, ensuring clarity and consumer protection.

Step-by-Step Application Process

The application process in both jurisdictions involves several stages. Consulting24 covers Dubai for comparison only - we do not file VARA applications and deliver licenses directly in Panama, Estonia, and Lithuania.

Dubai (VARA) Process:

  1. Pre-application: Business plan, AML policy, and compliance framework preparation.
  2. Initial approval: Submit application to VARA with required documents (passports, CVs, financial statements).
  3. In-principle approval: VARA reviews and grants in-principle approval (IPA) within 2-3 months.
  4. Setup: Incorporate a legal entity in the Dubai World Trade Centre (DWTC) free zone, lease an office, hire staff.
  5. Final approval: VARA conducts a compliance review and issues the VASP license.
  6. Post-licensing: Ongoing reporting and annual audits.

Czech Republic (CASP) Process:

  1. Preparation: Draft AML/KYC policies, appoint a compliance officer, and prepare a business plan.
  2. Application: Submit to the Czech National Bank via its electronic system, including capital proof and organisational structure.
  3. Assessment: CNB reviews within 3 months (can be extended).
  4. Approval: If compliant, CNB issues the CASP license.
  5. Passporting: Notify CNB if you wish to operate in other EU states.

Timelines are estimates; actual duration depends on the completeness of your application. For a detailed walkthrough, see our application process page.

Banking & Payment Integration

Access to banking is a common challenge for crypto firms. In Dubai, several local banks (e.g., Mashreq, RAKBank) and international banks with UAE presence (e.g., Standard Chartered) accept VARA-licensed entities, provided they have a physical office and a clean compliance record. The UAE also has a growing number of crypto-friendly payment processors.

In the Czech Republic, banks are generally more open to CASP-licensed firms, especially those with a strong AML framework. Major banks like ČSOB, Komerční banka, and Raiffeisenbank offer accounts, though some may require a minimum deposit. EU passporting also allows you to open accounts in other member states.

Compliance & Ongoing Obligations

Both jurisdictions require strong ongoing compliance. This is general guidance and not legal advice.

Dubai: VARA mandates quarterly and annual reporting, including financial statements, transaction volumes, and AML/CFT metrics. Firms must have a local compliance officer, a registered office, and maintain records for at least 5 years. Regular audits by a licensed auditor are required.

Czech Republic: Under MiCA, CASPs must submit annual reports to the CNB, including audited financials, AML compliance reports, and details of any breaches. A compliance officer must be appointed (can be remote). The CNB may conduct on-site inspections. Firms must also adhere to the EU's Travel Rule for crypto transfers.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Founders often make these errors when pursuing a crypto license:

  • Underestimating the timeline: Both VARA and CNB processes can take longer than expected. Plan for 6-8 months in Dubai and 4-6 months in Czech Republic.
  • Insufficient AML documentation: Both regulators require detailed AML policies. Generic templates are rejected.
  • Choosing the wrong jurisdiction for your market: A Dubai license does not automatically allow EU operations, and a Czech license may not be recognised in the UAE.
  • Ignoring substance requirements: Dubai requires a physical office and local staff. Failing to meet this can delay or derail the application.

Frequently asked questions

What is the main difference between a Dubai VASP and a Czech CASP license?

The Dubai VASP license is issued by VARA and requires a physical office and local staff, with no fixed capital but practical requirements around USD 100k. The Czech CASP license under MiCA has tiered capital (EUR 50k-150k) and allows EU passporting without a physical office. Dubai offers 0% tax in free zones, while Czech Republic has 19% corporate tax.

Which license is cheaper: Dubai or Czech Republic?

The Czech Republic is generally cheaper. Setup costs range from EUR 10,000 to 25,000, with no office requirement. Dubai costs USD 50,000-100,000+ due to regulatory fees, office rent, and staffing. Ongoing costs are also higher in Dubai.

Can I use a Czech CASP license to operate in other EU countries?

Yes. Under MiCA, a CASP license from the Czech Republic allows you to passport your services across all EU/EEA member states without additional licensing, subject to notification to the Czech National Bank.

Does Dubai's VARA license allow me to serve customers in the EU?

No. A Dubai VASP license is valid only within the UAE (Dubai emirate). To serve EU customers, you need a license from an EU member state, such as the Czech Republic, or comply with local laws in each EU country.

What are the capital requirements for a Czech crypto license?

Under MiCA, the minimum capital is EUR 50,000 for custody services, EUR 125,000 for exchange services, and EUR 150,000 for operating a trading platform or offering complex services. These amounts must be maintained as own funds.

Is there a physical office requirement in the Czech Republic?

No. The Czech Republic does not require a physical office for a CASP license. You can operate with a registered address and remote staff. This reduces setup costs significantly compared to Dubai.

How long does it take to get a Dubai VASP license?

The process typically takes 4 to 8 months from application to final approval. This includes initial review, in-principle approval, company setup, and final compliance checks. Delays can occur if documents are incomplete.

What taxes apply to crypto companies in Dubai?

Crypto companies in Dubai free zones pay 0% corporate tax and no VAT on crypto transactions. There is no capital gains tax. However, mainland companies may be subject to 9% corporate tax on profits above AED 375,000.
