What Is the Cyprus Crypto License for NFT Marketplaces?

The Cyprus crypto license is the registration required for any entity providing crypto-asset services in or from Cyprus. Under MiCA, which is fully in force in 2026, NFT marketplaces that allow trading of fractionalised NFTs, NFTs that represent financial instruments, or NFTs that are part of a series (e.g. gaming tokens) must be licensed. The license is issued by CySEC and is known as a Crypto Asset Service Provider (CASP) authorisation.

Key points:

  • Regulator: Cyprus Securities and Exchange Commission (CySEC).
  • License type: CASP authorisation under the Investment Services and Activities and Regulated Markets Law of 2024 (amended for MiCA).
  • Scope: Covers exchange, custodial wallet, and transfer services for crypto-assets including certain NFTs.

For example, a marketplace that lists only unique digital art NFTs may avoid licensing, but one that offers fractional ownership or royalty streaming likely falls under CASP rules. Who Needs a Cyprus Crypto License for an NFT Marketplace?

Any company that operates an NFT marketplace and provides one or more of the following services in or from Cyprus must obtain a CASP license:

  • Exchange of crypto-assets for fiat currency.
  • Exchange of crypto-assets for other crypto-assets.
  • Transfer of crypto-assets on behalf of clients.
  • Custody and administration of crypto-assets on behalf of clients.
  • Placing of crypto-assets (including NFTs).

If your NFT marketplace only lists non-fungible tokens that are unique, non-fractionalised, and not representing financial rights (e.g. pure digital art), you may fall outside MiCA scope. However, many NFT projects include royalty mechanisms, fractional ownership, or utility tokens that bring them under regulation. Consulting24 recommends a legal review of your tokenomics to determine if a license is required. For instance, an NFT marketplace that allows users to mint NFTs with embedded royalty rights likely needs a license because the royalty stream may be considered a financial instrument.

License Type and Regulator

The competent authority for crypto licensing in Cyprus is the Cyprus Securities and Exchange Commission (CySEC). The license type is a CASP authorisation under the national law implementing MiCA. Unlike the old 'registration only' regime that existed before 2025, Cyprus now requires a full authorisation with minimum capital, governance, and compliance obligations.

CySEC categorises services into three tiers for capital purposes (see capital requirement section). The license is valid across the EU under the MiCA passport, allowing you to serve clients in all member states from a single Cyprus entity. This passporting right is a major advantage over non-EU jurisdictions like Panama, which has no passport but offers faster setup and zero capital requirement.

Capital Requirement

Under MiCA, the minimum capital requirement for CASPs in Cyprus is tiered by service type:

  • Tier 1: EUR 50,000 for services limited to exchange of crypto-assets for fiat currency and/or exchange of crypto-assets for other crypto-assets (no custody).
  • Tier 2: EUR 125,000 for services including custody and administration of crypto-assets on behalf of clients.
  • Tier 3: EUR 150,000 for services including placing of crypto-assets (underwriting or placing with a firm commitment).

For an NFT marketplace that also provides custody of NFTs or acts as an exchange, you will likely need at least EUR 125,000 in initial capital. This capital must be maintained in liquid assets and cannot be used for operational expenses. It must be held in a bank account or in highly liquid instruments. CySEC may require proof of capital at the time of application. Consulting24 helps you structure the capital to meet regulatory requirements efficiently.

Tax Treatment

Cyprus offers a favourable tax regime for crypto businesses. Corporate income tax is 12.5%, one of the lowest in the EU. Additionally:

  • No withholding tax on dividends paid to non-resident shareholders.
  • No capital gains tax on disposal of securities (including crypto-assets) for companies.
  • VAT: Crypto-asset exchange services are exempt from VAT under EU law. However, NFT marketplace fees may be subject to VAT at 19% if the service is not considered a financial service. A detailed tax analysis is recommended.

Cyprus also has an extensive network of double tax treaties, making it an attractive holding jurisdiction. For example, if your NFT marketplace earns revenue from trading fees, those fees are subject to 12.5% corporate tax, but capital gains from selling your own crypto holdings may be exempt. Compare this with Panama, which has 0% corporate tax on territorial income but no treaties with many EU countries.

Allowed Activities

A Cyprus CASP license authorises the following activities, subject to the scope of your application:

  • Exchange of crypto-assets (including NFTs) for fiat or other crypto-assets.
  • Transfer of crypto-assets on behalf of clients.
  • Custody and administration of crypto-assets on behalf of clients.
  • Placing of crypto-assets (e.g. initial DEX offerings, NFT minting).
  • Reception and transmission of orders for crypto-assets.
  • Providing advice on crypto-assets.

Your license can be tailored to cover only the services you intend to offer. For an NFT marketplace, typical activities include exchange, custody, and placing of new NFT collections. You can also apply for a license that covers multiple service classes, but each class may require higher capital. Step-by-Step Application Process

The process to obtain a Cyprus crypto license involves several stages. Consulting24 advises and coordinates each step:

  1. Pre-application assessment: Review your business model, tokenomics, and determine the exact services to be licensed. This includes a gap analysis against CySEC requirements.
  2. Company incorporation: Set up a Cyprus limited company with a physical office and local director (if required). The company must have a registered address in Cyprus.
  3. Documentation preparation: Draft the application pack including business plan, AML policy, risk assessment, governance framework, and financial projections. CySEC expects detailed policies on customer due diligence, transaction monitoring, and record keeping.
  4. Submission to CySEC: File the application with the required fee. CySEC has 15 working days to acknowledge receipt and request additional information. Incomplete applications are rejected.
  5. Review and interviews: CySEC may request clarifications or conduct interviews with management. This phase can take 3-6 months. Be prepared to explain your business model and compliance framework.
  6. Approval and licensing: Once approved, you receive a CASP authorisation. You must then comply with ongoing reporting and supervision, including annual audits and transaction reports.

Total timeline: 6-12 months from submission. Pre-application preparation can take 2-4 months. Consulting24 can accelerate the process by ensuring documentation is complete and aligned with CySEC expectations.

Banking and Payment Processing

Opening a bank account for a crypto business in Cyprus can be challenging. Traditional banks often require a local presence and a proven track record. Options include:

  • Cyprus-based banks (e.g. Bank of Cyprus, Hellenic Bank) that accept CASP-licensed entities. These banks may require a minimum deposit and a personal meeting with directors.
  • EMI (electronic money institution) accounts for faster onboarding. EMIs like Revolut or Monese offer business accounts but may have restrictions on crypto transactions.
  • International banks with crypto-friendly policies, such as those in Switzerland or the UK, but these may not accept Cyprus-licensed entities.

  • Low corporate tax: 12.5% with exemptions for capital gains, making it one of the most tax-efficient EU jurisdictions.
  • Regulatory clarity: CySEC is a respected regulator with clear guidelines under MiCA, reducing uncertainty.
  • No stamp duty: On share transfers, saving costs during restructuring or exits.
  • Strategic location: Between Europe, Middle East, and Asia, facilitating cross-border business.

Compliance and Trust

Operating under a Cyprus CASP license demonstrates to users, partners, and investors that your NFT marketplace meets EU standards for AML, consumer protection, and operational resilience. Ongoing compliance includes:

  • Appointment of an AML compliance officer who must be a Cyprus resident or have a local representative.
  • Regular reporting to CySEC (financial, transaction, and audit reports). Transaction reports must be submitted quarterly.
  • External audit of financial statements by a Cyprus-approved auditor.
  • Maintenance of minimum capital at all times, with proof provided annually.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Misclassifying NFTs: Assuming all NFTs are outside scope. If your NFT marketplace features fractionalised or financial NFTs, you need a license. Get a legal opinion early.
  • Underestimating capital: Trying to operate with less than EUR 125,000 when custody is involved. CySEC will reject applications with insufficient capital.
  • Poor AML documentation: CySEC scrutinises AML policies. Generic templates are rejected. Your AML policy must be tailored to your business model and risk profile.
  • Ignoring ongoing costs: Annual supervision fees and compliance costs can exceed EUR 20,000 per year. Budget for these from the start.

Under MiCA, capital requirements are tiered: EUR 50,000 for exchange-only services, EUR 125,000 for custody services, and EUR 150,000 for placing services. For an NFT marketplace with custody, you typically need at least EUR 125,000.

How long does it take to get a Cyprus crypto license?

The application process takes 6 to 12 months from submission, plus 2-4 months for pre-application preparation. The timeline depends on the completeness of your documentation and CySEC's workload.

Is Cyprus tax-friendly for crypto businesses?

Yes, corporate income tax is 12.5%, with no capital gains tax on securities and no withholding tax on dividends. Cyprus also has an extensive double tax treaty network.
