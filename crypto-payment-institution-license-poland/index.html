What Is a Crypto Payment Institution License in Poland?

The Crypto Payment Institution License in Poland is a regulatory authorization that permits companies to offer virtual currency exchange services, custody, and payment processing. It is governed by the Polish Act on Payment Services and the Act on Counteracting Money Laundering and Terrorism Financing (AML/CFT). The license is issued by the Polish Financial Supervision Authority (KNF) and aligns with the EU's Markets in Crypto-Assets (MiCA) framework, which is fully in force in 2026.

This license is distinct from a simple registration; it requires meeting capital thresholds, implementing strong AML policies, and undergoing a thorough background check of directors and beneficial owners. It is designed for businesses that intend to operate as a crypto payment institution, handling customer funds and executing transactions. The license is recognized across the EU through passporting, allowing you to serve clients in other member states without additional licensing.

Who Needs a Crypto Payment Institution License in Poland?

Any company that wishes to offer the following services in or from Poland must obtain this license:

  • Exchange between virtual currencies and fiat currencies
  • Exchange between virtual currencies
  • Virtual currency custody and administration
  • Transfer of virtual currencies on behalf of clients
  • Payment initiation services using virtual currencies

This includes crypto exchanges, wallet providers, payment processors, and any fintech that facilitates crypto payments. If you are planning to serve Polish customers or establish a presence in the EU, this license is a strong option. For example, a startup building a crypto-to-fiat on-ramp for European users would need this license to operate legally in Poland. Similarly, a custody provider storing client assets must be licensed. Even if you operate remotely, if your target market includes Poland, you need this license to avoid regulatory penalties.

License Type and Regulator

The Polish crypto payment institution license is a full authorization under the Payment Services Directive (PSD2) extended to virtual currencies. The regulator is the Polish Financial Supervision Authority (KNF) (Komisja Nadzoru Finansowego). KNF oversees all financial services, including crypto activities, and enforces compliance with AML/CFT regulations.

The license is classified as a 'virtual currency exchange activity' license, but it is commonly referred to as a crypto payment institution license. It is not a limited-purpose license; it allows a broad range of activities as detailed below. KNF has a reputation for thorough but fair reviews, and it publishes a register of licensed entities. The regulator also coordinates with the European Banking Authority (EBA) under MiCA, ensuring consistency across the EU.

Capital Requirement

Under MiCA and Polish law, the minimum initial capital for a crypto payment institution is based on the services offered:

  • EUR 50,000 for exchange services only (crypto-to-crypto or crypto-to-fiat)
  • EUR 125,000 for custody and administration of virtual currencies
  • EUR 150,000 if providing all services including payment initiation

These figures are as of 2026 and are subject to verification during the application. The capital must be held in a bank account in Poland or another EU member state and be fully paid up before licensing. It can be in the form of cash or a bank guarantee. The capital serves as a buffer for operational risks and is a key requirement for KNF approval. You must also demonstrate the source of funds, and any loans or third-party contributions will be scrutinized.

Tax Treatment

Poland offers a relatively favorable tax environment for crypto businesses. Corporate income tax (CIT) is a flat 19% (or 9% for small taxpayers with revenue under EUR 2 million). VAT is generally exempt for crypto exchange and custody services under Polish law, aligning with the EU's VAT treatment of virtual currencies.

Capital gains from crypto trading for individuals are taxed at 19% (flat rate). However, businesses should seek specific tax advice as treatment may vary based on the nature of services. For example, if you charge fees in crypto, the tax point may be at the moment of conversion to fiat. For a comparative tax analysis, see our vs Lithuania page.

Allowed Activities

With a Polish crypto payment institution license, you can engage in the following activities:

  • Exchange of virtual currencies for fiat money and vice versa
  • Exchange of virtual currencies for other virtual currencies
  • Storage and administration of virtual currencies on behalf of clients (custody)
  • Transfer of virtual currencies (sending and receiving)
  • Payment initiation services using virtual currencies
  • Issuance of electronic money in virtual currencies (subject to additional e-money license)

These activities are strictly regulated, and any expansion requires prior approval from KNF. For example, adding a new crypto asset class or offering staking services would need regulatory clearance. The license does not cover derivatives or securities tokens, which fall under different regulations.

Step-by-Step Application Process

The process to obtain a crypto payment institution license in Poland involves several stages:

  1. Pre-application consultation with Consulting24 to assess eligibility and prepare documentation.
  2. Company incorporation in Poland (a limited liability company (sp. z o.o.) is typical) with a registered office.
  3. AML/CFT program development including risk assessment, internal procedures, and appointment of an AML officer.
  4. Capital deposit into a blocked account or bank guarantee.
  5. Submission of application to KNF with business plan, financial projections, ownership structure, and background checks.
  6. KNF review (3-6 months) with possible requests for additional information.
  7. License issuance and registration in the register of payment institutions.
  8. Ongoing compliance with reporting, audits, and AML obligations.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Many applicants face delays or rejection due to these common pitfalls:

  • Incomplete documentation: Missing business plans, AML policies, or financial projections.
  • Unclear ownership structure: Failure to disclose all beneficial owners or provide background checks.
  • Insufficient capital: Not meeting the minimum capital requirement or not proving source of funds.
  • Poor AML/CFT framework: Generic policies that do not address specific risks.
  • Ignoring local requirements: Not having a physical office or local director.

Frequently asked questions

What is the difference between a crypto payment institution license and a simple registration in Poland?

A simple registration (e.g., as a virtual currency exchange) was possible before MiCA, but as of 2026, all crypto service providers must obtain a full license under MiCA. The crypto payment institution license requires meeting capital thresholds, AML compliance, and KNF approval, whereas a registration was a lighter process. The license allows passporting across the EU.

Can I use the Polish license to operate in other EU countries?

Yes, under MiCA passporting, a Polish crypto payment institution license allows you to provide services in any EU member state without additional licensing. You must notify KNF of your intention to passport and comply with local consumer protection rules.

Is the Polish crypto license recognized under MiCA?

Yes, Poland has implemented MiCA fully as of 2026. The crypto payment institution license is a MiCA-compliant authorization, allowing EU-wide passporting. KNF coordinates with the European Banking Authority (EBA) for consistent supervision.

What are the capital requirements for a Polish crypto license?

Minimum capital ranges from EUR 50,000 for exchange services to EUR 150,000 for full services including payment initiation. The capital must be held in a bank account in the EU and fully paid up before licensing. It can be in cash or a bank guarantee.
