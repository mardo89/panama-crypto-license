What Is a Crypto License in Estonia and Switzerland?

A crypto license in Estonia is a Virtual Asset Service Provider (VASP) license under the Estonian Financial Intelligence Unit (FIU), now fully aligned with the EU's MiCA regulation. It allows companies to offer crypto exchange, wallet services, and other crypto activities. Switzerland, on the other hand, issues a FinTech license or a banking license for crypto businesses, regulated by FINMA. Swiss crypto firms often operate under a 'self-regulatory' framework via VQF or other SROs, but a formal license is required for certain activities.

Both jurisdictions are considered crypto-friendly, but Estonia offers a faster, cheaper entry point within the EU, while Switzerland provides a more established, high-trust environment. The choice depends on your target market, budget, and compliance appetite.

In Estonia, the license is now a MiCA-compliant CASP (Crypto Asset Service Provider) license, replacing the older VASP regime. This means full passporting rights across the EU. Switzerland's FinTech license is lighter than a full banking license but still requires a minimum capital of CHF 300,000 and a detailed business plan. In Estonia, this includes companies providing virtual currency exchange between fiat and crypto, crypto-to-crypto exchange, and crypto wallet services. In Switzerland, the scope is similar but also covers token issuance and complex DeFi activities under FINMA guidance.

Capital Requirements

Under MiCA, Estonia applies tiered capital requirements: EUR 50,000 for simple exchange services, EUR 125,000 for wallet services, and EUR 150,000 for more complex activities. Switzerland requires a minimum capital of CHF 300,000 (approx. EUR 310,000) for a FinTech license, and CHF 10 million for a full banking license. The Swiss capital must be fully paid up and maintained.

Estonia's lower capital makes it accessible for startups, while Switzerland's higher bar suits established firms. Consulting24 advises on structuring your capital efficiently.

For example, a startup offering only fiat-to-crypto exchange can meet Estonia's EUR 50,000 requirement, whereas a Swiss FinTech license demands nearly six times that amount. This difference is a key factor in jurisdiction choice.

Tax Treatment

Estonia's corporate tax is 0% on retained profits; tax is only due upon distribution (20% on dividends). This is highly advantageous for crypto companies that reinvest earnings. Switzerland's corporate tax varies by canton, typically 12-21% on profits. Swiss crypto businesses also face VAT on certain services, though many crypto transactions are exempt.

For crypto-to-crypto trades, Estonia treats them as barter transactions (taxable on disposal), while Switzerland treats them as taxable events with capital gains tax for individuals. Consulting24 can help you model the tax impact.

Consider a company that reinvests all profits: in Estonia, it pays no corporate tax until dividends are paid. In Switzerland, it pays 12-21% annually, reducing reinvestment capacity. This makes Estonia particularly attractive for growth-stage firms.

Allowed Activities

In Estonia, the VASP license covers: exchange between fiat and crypto, exchange between cryptocurrencies, transfer of virtual currencies, and custodian wallet services. Under MiCA, stablecoin issuance and DeFi services are also regulated. In Switzerland, FINMA allows a broader range: exchange, wallet, token issuance (under prospectus rules), and even limited lending/borrowing with a FinTech license.

Switzerland is more permissive for innovative activities like staking and DeFi, but requires detailed disclosure. Consulting24 can advise on which activities are best suited to each jurisdiction.

Step-by-Step Application Process

  1. Company setup: Incorporate a legal entity in the chosen jurisdiction. In Estonia, this can be done online via e-Residency. In Switzerland, a local director and physical office are required.
  2. Document preparation: Prepare a business plan, AML/KYC policies, risk assessment, and financial projections. Both jurisdictions require detailed compliance manuals.
  3. Application submission: Submit to the FIU (Estonia) or FINMA (Switzerland). Estonia uses an online portal; Switzerland requires a formal submission with legal notarisation.
  4. Review and interview: Both regulators may request additional information. Switzerland often conducts an in-person interview with management.
  5. Approval and ongoing compliance: Once approved, you must file regular reports, undergo audits, and maintain AML procedures. Consulting24 coordinates the entire process.

For Estonia, the entire process can be completed remotely thanks to e-Residency. Switzerland requires at least one in-person visit for the interview. Consulting24 provides checklists and templates to streamline document preparation.

Switzerland offers better banking access, with many cantonal banks and private banks willing to serve licensed crypto companies, though they still conduct rigorous due diligence.

Common Mistakes When Choosing Between Estonia and Switzerland

  • Underestimating capital requirements: Switzerland's CHF 300,000 is a hard minimum; Estonia's lower capital still requires proof of funds.
  • Ignoring tax implications: Estonia's tax deferral is attractive, but Swiss cantonal taxes can be higher. Model both scenarios.
  • Overlooking banking access: Even with a license, banks may reject you. Start banking applications early.
  • Assuming EU passporting is automatic: Estonia's MiCA license allows passporting, but you must notify host regulators. Switzerland has no passporting.

Frequently asked questions

What is the main difference between an Estonia and Switzerland crypto license?

Estonia offers a lower-cost, faster EU license under MiCA with 0% tax on retained profits. Switzerland provides a more prestigious FINMA license with higher capital requirements and better banking access, but at a higher cost and longer timeline.

Which jurisdiction is cheaper for a crypto startup in 2026?

Estonia is significantly cheaper, with total costs around EUR 3,000-EUR 5,000 plus consulting fees, compared to Switzerland's EUR 20,000-EUR 50,000. Estonia also has lower capital requirements (EUR 50,000-EUR 150,000 vs CHF 300,000).

Can I passport an Estonia crypto license to other EU countries?

Yes, under MiCA, an Estonia VASP license allows passporting across the EU. You must notify the host regulator and comply with local AML rules. Switzerland has no passporting rights.

What are the capital requirements for a crypto license in Switzerland?

Switzerland requires a minimum capital of CHF 300,000 (approx. EUR 310,000) for a FinTech license. For a full banking license, the minimum is CHF 10 million. Capital must be fully paid up and maintained.

How long does it take to get a crypto license in Estonia vs Switzerland?

Estonia takes 2-4 months from application to approval. Switzerland takes 6-12 months due to more rigorous due diligence and potential in-person interviews.

Which jurisdiction has better banking access for crypto companies?

Switzerland generally offers better banking access, with many cantonal and private banks serving licensed crypto firms. Estonia has fewer crypto-friendly banks, but LHV and some fintechs are options.

Is there a physical presence requirement in Estonia or Switzerland?

Estonia allows remote setup via e-Residency, but you need a local contact person. Switzerland requires a physical office and at least one local director.

What activities are allowed under an Estonia crypto license?

Estonia allows fiat-to-crypto exchange, crypto-to-crypto exchange, crypto wallet services, and transfers. Under MiCA, stablecoin issuance and DeFi services are also regulated.

What is the tax rate for crypto companies in Estonia?

Estonia has a 0% corporate tax on retained profits. Tax is only paid upon distribution at 20%. This makes it very attractive for reinvesting companies.
