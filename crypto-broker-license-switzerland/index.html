What Is the Crypto Broker License Switzerland?

The crypto broker license Switzerland refers to the authorization required to operate as a financial intermediary in the crypto space under FINMA supervision. In Switzerland, crypto brokers are typically regulated as securities dealers or financial intermediaries under the Financial Services Act (FinSA) and the Financial Institutions Act (FinIA). Depending on the business model, a license as a securities firm or financial intermediary may be required.

FINMA classifies crypto assets as securities when they are standardized and suitable for mass trading. A crypto broker that executes client orders, holds client assets, or provides custody services must obtain the appropriate license. The regime is principles-based, allowing flexibility but requiring strong compliance frameworks. As of 2026, Switzerland is not part of the EU, so MiCA does not apply; instead, Swiss law remains independent and is considered highly mature.

Key features of the Swiss crypto broker license include:

  • FINMA as the sole regulator.
  • License types: securities firm (for dealing on own account or executing client orders) or financial intermediary (for custody and transfer services).
  • Minimum capital requirements vary by license type (typically CHF 100,000 to CHF 500,000).
  • Ongoing compliance obligations under Anti-Money Laundering (AML) and know-your-customer (KYC) rules.

Who Needs a Crypto Broker License in Switzerland?

Any firm that provides crypto brokerage services to Swiss clients or operates from Switzerland must obtain a crypto broker license Switzerland. This includes:

  • Platforms that buy and sell cryptocurrencies against fiat or other crypto on behalf of clients.
  • Brokers that execute orders on exchanges or over-the-counter (OTC) desks.
  • Firms that offer custody of client crypto assets.
  • Businesses that provide automated trading or robo-advisory services for crypto.

Exemptions exist for pure advisory services that do not handle client funds or execute trades. Also, if a firm operates outside Switzerland and does not target Swiss clients, a Swiss license may not be required. However, any physical presence or active solicitation of Swiss clients triggers licensing obligations. License Type and Regulator

The primary regulator for the crypto broker license Switzerland is the Swiss Financial Market Supervisory Authority (FINMA). FINMA issues two main types of licenses relevant to crypto brokers:

License TypeActivities PermittedMinimum Capital
Securities Firm (FinIA)Dealing in securities (including crypto as securities), execution of client orders, underwriting, and custody of securities.CHF 500,000 (approx. EUR 500,000)
Financial Intermediary (FinIA)Acceptance and transfer of client assets, custody, and payment services for crypto.CHF 100,000 (approx. EUR 100,000)

Many crypto brokers operate under the securities firm license due to the nature of their activities. FINMA also requires firms to be a member of a self-regulatory organization (SRO) for AML supervision, unless they are directly supervised by FINMA. The licensing process involves a thorough review of the business plan, organizational structure, and compliance systems.

Capital Requirements

The minimum capital required for a crypto broker license Switzerland depends on the license type. For a securities firm, the minimum is CHF 500,000 (approx. EUR 500,000). For a financial intermediary, the minimum is CHF 100,000 (approx. EUR 100,000). These figures are set by FINMA and are subject to adjustment based on the firm's risk profile and business volume.

Capital must be fully paid up in cash and held in a Swiss bank account. FINMA may require additional capital if the firm engages in higher-risk activities or holds significant client assets. Tax Treatment for Crypto Brokers in Switzerland

Switzerland offers a favorable tax environment for crypto brokers. Corporate income tax rates vary by canton but typically range from 11% to 21%. Crypto assets are treated as assets for tax purposes; capital gains from trading are generally tax-free for companies, but income from brokerage fees is taxable. Value-added tax (VAT) is not applied to crypto trading activities in most cases, as they are considered financial services.

Stamp duty may apply to the issuance of securities (including crypto tokens) but not to secondary trading. Wealth tax is not applicable to corporations. Consulting24 recommends engaging a Swiss tax advisor to optimize your structure, as tax treatment can vary based on the specific activities and canton of incorporation.

Allowed Activities Under the Swiss Crypto Broker License

With a crypto broker license Switzerland, a firm can conduct a wide range of activities, subject to the scope of the license. Typical allowed activities include:

  • Execution of buy and sell orders for cryptocurrencies against fiat or other crypto.
  • Provision of custody services for client crypto assets.
  • OTC trading and market making.
  • Issuance of crypto securities (e.g., tokenized shares) if licensed as a securities firm.
  • Operation of an electronic trading platform for crypto.

Activities that are not permitted without additional licensing include: deposit-taking (banking license required), collective investment schemes, and insurance.
