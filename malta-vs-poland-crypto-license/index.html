What Is a Crypto License in Malta and Poland?

Under MiCA, both Malta and Poland require crypto asset service providers (CASPs) to obtain authorization. In Malta, the Malta Financial Services Authority (MFSA) oversees the Virtual Financial Assets (VFA) framework, which has been updated to align with MiCA. Poland's regulator, the Polish Financial Supervision Authority (KNF), operates a registration system for CASPs, now also harmonized with MiCA.

The core difference: Malta offers a full VFA license for a broader range of services, while Poland uses a simpler registration process with lower capital requirements but limited activity scope. Both are valid for EU-wide passporting under MiCA. For businesses seeking a non-EU alternative with zero tax, Panama offers a flat EUR 6,000 fee and no capital requirement, ideal for global markets outside the EU.

It is important to note that MiCA has been fully in force since 2025, so all EU member states, including Malta and Poland, have transposed its requirements into national law. This means that any crypto business serving EU clients must hold a license or registration from an EU regulator. The days of unregulated crypto operations in the EU are over. Who Needs a Crypto License?

Any business offering crypto asset services in the EU must hold a license or registration. This includes:

  • Exchanges (fiat-to-crypto and crypto-to-crypto)
  • Wallet providers (custodial and non-custodial)
  • Market makers and liquidity providers
  • Brokers and dealers in crypto assets
  • Investment firms offering crypto portfolio management
  • Issuers of crypto assets (if offering services beyond issuance)
  • Operators of crypto ATMs

Capital Requirement

Under MiCA, minimum capital requirements are tiered by service type:

  • Malta: EUR 50,000 (Class 1 - reception/transmission of orders), EUR 125,000 (Class 2 - execution of orders), EUR 150,000 (Class 3 - operating a trading platform)
  • Poland: EUR 50,000 for most CASP activities (registration does not have separate capital tiers, but MiCA minimum applies)

Both jurisdictions adhere to MiCA's capital tiers, but Malta's VFA license may require higher capital for certain classes. Poland's registration typically requires the EUR 50,000 minimum for basic services. In contrast, Panama has no minimum capital requirement, making it ideal for startups with limited funds. It is crucial to have the capital fully paid up and documented before application; otherwise, the regulator may reject the submission.

Tax Treatment

Tax is a key differentiator:

  • Malta: Corporate tax rate 35%, but with a full imputation system that can reduce effective rate to 5% for non-resident shareholders. No capital gains tax on crypto-to-crypto trades. VAT exempt on crypto services.
  • Poland: Corporate tax rate 19% (9% for small businesses). Crypto trading profits are subject to 19% capital gains tax. VAT exempt on crypto services.
  • Panama: 0% tax on offshore income (territorial taxation). No capital gains tax on crypto. VAT exempt on crypto services.

Malta's tax regime is more favorable for holding companies and long-term investors, while Poland's lower corporate rate benefits active trading firms. Panama offers zero tax for non-domestic income, which can be highly advantageous for global operations. However, note that Panama's territorial tax system means income from sources within Panama is taxable, but most crypto businesses can structure to avoid Panamanian source income.

Allowed Activities

Malta's VFA license covers a broad range of activities, including operating a trading platform, executing orders, providing custody, and offering investment advice. Poland's registration is limited to exchange and wallet services, with no explicit provision for derivatives, staking, or DeFi services.

If your business involves complex services like DeFi, token issuance, or derivatives, Malta is more suitable. For simple exchange or wallet services, Poland may suffice. For a more flexible non-EU option, Panama allows a wide range of crypto activities without restrictive licensing categories. Panama does not limit the type of crypto services you can offer, as long as you comply with AML obligations.

Step-by-Step Application Process

  1. Pre-application: Define business model, prepare documentation (business plan, AML policies, risk assessment, financial projections). This typically takes 4-8 weeks.
  2. Submit application: File with MFSA (Malta) or KNF (Poland) along with fee. Malta requires a detailed VFA license application; Poland uses a simpler registration form.
  3. Regulatory review: Regulator assesses fit and properness of directors, AML controls, and capital adequacy. Malta's review is more thorough, often requiring multiple rounds of questions. Poland's review is more streamlined.
  4. Interview/meeting: Malta requires a physical meeting with MFSA officials; Poland may request clarifications via correspondence.
  5. Approval: License granted (Malta) or registration confirmed (Poland). Malta issues a VFA license; Poland issues a registration certificate.
  6. Post-licensing: Ongoing compliance, regular reporting, and annual audits. Malta requires quarterly reporting; Poland requires annual updates.

Compliance is an ongoing obligation. Malta requires quarterly reporting on transactions and AML metrics; Poland requires annual updates. Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Underestimating capital requirements - ensure you have funds ready and documented. Malta's higher tiers catch many off guard.
  • Incomplete documentation - regulators reject incomplete applications. Prepare a thorough business plan and AML policies.
  • Choosing based solely on cost - consider long-term business needs. Poland's low cost may limit future expansion.
  • Ignoring tax implications - both jurisdictions have different tax treatments. Consult a tax advisor.
  • Failing to plan for banking - start banking discussions early. Banking can take 3-6 months to arrange.
  • Overlooking passporting requirements - ensure your license covers all EU markets you intend to serve.
  • Not having a local director - both Malta and Poland require at least one local director or representative. Plan for this.
  • Delaying AML policy preparation - regulators expect detailed AML procedures at application stage, not after.

Frequently asked questions

What is the main difference between a Malta and Poland crypto license?

Malta offers a comprehensive VFA license covering a broad range of services (trading platform, custody, execution) with higher capital requirements and a longer timeline (6-12 months). Poland uses a simpler registration system limited to exchange and wallet services, with lower costs and faster approval (2-4 months). Both are MiCA-compliant and allow EU passporting.

Which is cheaper: Malta or Poland?

Poland is significantly cheaper, with estimated costs of EUR 2,000-5,000, compared to Malta's EUR 10,000-30,000. However, Malta's license covers more activities. For a non-EU alternative, Panama costs a flat EUR 6,000 with no capital requirement.

What are the capital requirements for a Malta crypto license?

Under MiCA, Malta requires EUR 50,000 for Class 1 (reception/transmission of orders), EUR 125,000 for Class 2 (execution of orders), and EUR 150,000 for Class 3 (operating a trading platform). These amounts must be fully paid up and documented at application.

Can I passport a Poland crypto license to other EU countries?

Yes, Poland's CASP registration is recognized under MiCA and allows passporting to other EU member states. However, the scope of services you can passport is limited to those covered by your registration (exchange and wallet services). For broader activities, Malta's VFA license may be more suitable.

What is the tax rate for crypto businesses in Malta?

Malta's corporate tax rate is 35%, but through the full imputation system, non-resident shareholders can reduce the effective rate to 5%. Crypto-to-crypto trades are exempt from capital gains tax, and crypto services are VAT exempt.

Is Poland's corporate tax rate lower than Malta's?

Poland's standard corporate tax rate is 19% (9% for small businesses), which is lower than Malta's nominal 35%. However, Malta's effective rate can be as low as 5% for non-resident shareholders, making it more tax-efficient for holding structures. Poland also imposes a 19% capital gains tax on crypto trading profits.

How long does it take to get a crypto license in Poland?

Poland's registration process typically takes 2-4 months from submission to approval. The timeline depends on the completeness of your application and the KNF's workload. Consulting24 can help expedite the process by ensuring all documents are in order.

What activities are not allowed under Poland's crypto registration?

Poland's registration is limited to exchange and wallet services. Activities such as operating a trading platform, derivatives trading, staking, DeFi services, and custody are not explicitly covered. For these, Malta's VFA license or Panama's more flexible license may be better options.

Do I need a local director for a Malta or Poland crypto license?

Yes, both jurisdictions require at least one local director or representative. In Malta, the director must be physically present for the MFSA meeting. In Poland, a local representative is needed for registration. Consulting24 can help you find suitable local candidates.

Can I apply for a Malta crypto license remotely?

No, Malta requires a physical meeting with MFSA officials as part of the application process. This meeting typically takes place in Malta. Poland's process is largely remote, with correspondence via email or post. Panama also allows a fully remote application.

What is the ongoing compliance burden for a Malta VFA license?

Malta requires quarterly reporting on transactions, AML metrics, and financial statements. Annual audits by a licensed auditor are mandatory. The MFSA also conducts periodic inspections. Compliance costs can range from EUR 10,000-20,000 per year, depending on the complexity of operations.

Is Panama a good alternative to Malta or Poland?

Panama is an excellent alternative for businesses targeting non-EU markets. It offers a flat EUR 6,000 fee, no capital requirement, 0% offshore tax, and a fast 2-3 month timeline. However, Panama does not provide EU passporting. Consulting24 delivers Panama licenses directly.
