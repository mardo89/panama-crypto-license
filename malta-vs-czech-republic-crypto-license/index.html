What Is a Malta vs Czech Republic Crypto License?

A crypto license (or registration) is the regulatory authorisation required to operate a virtual asset service provider (VASP) in a given jurisdiction. In Malta, the Malta Financial Services Authority (MFSA) oversees the VFA framework under the Virtual Financial Assets Act. In the Czech Republic, the Czech National Bank (CNB) registers crypto asset service providers under the Act on Selected Services of the Information Society. Both regimes are now aligned with MiCA, but Malta applies stricter capital and governance requirements, while the Czech Republic offers a lighter-touch regime with lower entry barriers.

The choice between Malta and the Czech Republic depends on your business model, budget, and timeline. Malta is suited for larger, institutional players seeking a strong regulatory stamp. The Czech Republic is ideal for startups and SMEs looking for a cost-effective, fast EU passport. For example, a small exchange startup with limited capital may find the Czech Republic more accessible, whereas a large trading platform may prefer Malta's established ecosystem.

Both licenses allow passporting across the EU under MiCA, so your clients can be served anywhere in the European Economic Area. However, the local obligations for ongoing compliance, reporting, and audits differ. Consulting24 can help you evaluate which jurisdiction aligns with your operational needs.

Who Needs a Crypto License in Malta or the Czech Republic?

Any entity providing the following services in or from these jurisdictions must obtain a license or registration:

  • Exchange of crypto assets for fiat currency or other crypto assets
  • Custody and administration of crypto assets on behalf of clients
  • Operation of a trading platform (exchange)
  • Placement of crypto assets (underwriting)
  • Provision of transfer services for crypto assets
  • Advisory services on crypto assets

If your business targets EU clients, you must be licensed in at least one EU member state. Both Malta and the Czech Republic offer full MiCA passporting, meaning a license from either allows you to serve clients across the EU without additional authorisation. This is particularly relevant for businesses that plan to scale rapidly across multiple EU countries.

Additionally, if you are a non-EU company looking to enter the EU market, establishing a subsidiary in Malta or the Czech Republic and obtaining a local license is the standard route. Consulting24 can assist with company setup and license application in both jurisdictions.

License Type and Regulator

Malta: The regulator is the Malta Financial Services Authority (MFSA). The license is a Class 1, 2, 3, or 4 VFA license depending on the services offered. Class 1 is for simple exchange services, Class 2 for custody, Class 3 for both, and Class 4 for operating a trading platform. The regime is principles-based and requires a detailed whitepaper, governance policies, and capital. Malta also requires a VFA agent, typically a law firm or consultancy, to guide the application.

Czech Republic: The regulator is the Czech National Bank (CNB). The license is a single registration for all crypto asset services. The CNB registers entities under the Act on Selected Services of the Information Society (No. 480/2004 Coll.) as amended. The regime is notification-based but requires compliance with AML and MiCA standards. Unlike Malta, there is no formal agent requirement, but a local representative for AML is strongly recommended.

Both regulators are respected and part of the European Banking Authority (EBA) and ESMA networks. The MFSA is known for its thoroughness, while the CNB is pragmatic and efficient. Consulting24 has experience with both regulators and can prepare your application to meet their expectations.

Capital Requirement

Under MiCA, capital requirements are tiered based on the type of service:

  • EUR 50,000 for simple exchange and custody services (Class 1/2 in Malta, basic registration in Czechia)
  • EUR 125,000 for combined exchange and custody (Class 3 in Malta)
  • EUR 150,000 for operating a trading platform (Class 4 in Malta)

Malta generally applies the higher tiers, especially for Class 3 and 4 licenses. The Czech Republic tends to apply the minimum tier for most services, though the CNB may require higher capital if the business volume is significant. Capital must be held in liquid assets and maintained throughout the license period. For example, a startup offering only exchange services in the Czech Republic may only need EUR 50,000, while a similar entity in Malta would need EUR 125,000 if classified as Class 3. It is essential to correctly classify your services to avoid overcapitalisation. Consulting24 can help you determine the appropriate tier.

Tax Treatment

Malta: Corporate tax rate is 35%, but refunds can reduce effective rate to 5-10% for non-resident shareholders. No capital gains tax on crypto-to-crypto trades if held as capital assets. VAT is not applied to crypto transactions. Malta has no withholding tax on dividends paid to non-residents. This makes Malta attractive for international investors seeking tax efficiency.

Czech Republic: Corporate tax rate is 19%. Crypto transactions are subject to income tax, but long-term holdings (over 3 years) may be exempt for individuals. For companies, crypto is treated as intangible assets. VAT is not applied to crypto exchanges. The Czech Republic has a withholding tax of 15% on dividends. However, tax treaties may reduce this rate.

Both countries offer competitive tax regimes, but Malta's imputation system can be more attractive for international investors. For example, a Malta-incorporated company with non-resident shareholders may pay an effective tax rate as low as 5%, compared to 19% in the Czech Republic. However, the Czech Republic's lower corporate tax rate may be simpler to administer. Consulting24 can provide tax planning advice tailored to your structure.

Allowed Activities

Both jurisdictions allow the full range of MiCA services, including:

  • Exchange of crypto assets for fiat or other crypto
  • Custody and administration
  • Operation of a trading platform
  • Placement of crypto assets
  • Transfer services
  • Advisory services

Malta also allows the issuance of virtual financial assets (VFA) and security tokens under its VFA Act. The Czech Republic does not have a separate regime for security tokens; they fall under traditional securities law. Both prohibit anonymous transactions and require strict AML/KYC procedures. Additionally, both jurisdictions require transaction monitoring and suspicious activity reporting. For example, a token issuance project would be easier to license in Malta due to its dedicated VFA framework, whereas the Czech Republic would treat it as a securities offering. Step-by-Step Application Process

  1. Pre-application: Define your business model, select the service class, and prepare a business plan, AML policies, and governance documents. This stage typically takes 2-4 weeks.
  2. Engage local agents: In Malta, you need a VFA agent (usually a law firm or consultancy). In the Czech Republic, you may need a local representative for AML compliance. Consulting24 can act as your coordinator.
  3. Submit application: File with the MFSA (Malta) or CNB (Czech Republic) along with the fee and supporting documents. Ensure all documents are translated if required.
  4. Review and queries: Regulators will review and may request additional information. This stage takes 2-6 months depending on complexity. For example, Malta often requires multiple rounds of questions.
  5. Approval and registration: Once approved, you receive the license (Malta) or registration certificate (Czech Republic). You must maintain ongoing compliance, including annual audits and AML reporting.

Banking and Payment Services

Access to banking is a common challenge for crypto businesses. Malta has a more developed banking ecosystem for VFA firms, with several local banks (e.g., Bank of Valletta, HSBC Malta) accepting crypto clients under strict due diligence. However, the onboarding process can take 2-4 months and requires detailed documentation. The Czech Republic also has banks willing to serve crypto firms, such as Ceska Sporitelna and Komercni Banka, but the process can be slower and may require a local director.

Both jurisdictions allow payment institutions and e-money institutions to provide services, and some specialised crypto-friendly banks operate across the EU, such as Clear Junction and Frick. Compliance and Trust

Both Malta and the Czech Republic are fully compliant with MiCA, FATF recommendations, and EU AML directives. Malta's VFA framework is more prescriptive, requiring a detailed whitepaper, regular audits, and a compliance officer. The Czech regime is principle-based but still demands strong AML controls. Ongoing compliance includes transaction monitoring, suspicious activity reporting, and annual audits. Failure to comply can result in fines or license revocation. Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Underestimating capital requirements: Ensure you have the full capital available before applying. For example, a Class 4 Malta license requires EUR 150,000 in liquid assets.
  • Incomplete documentation: Missing policies or vague business plans cause delays. Prepare a comprehensive AML manual, business plan, and risk assessment.
  • Ignoring AML/KYC obligations: Both regulators scrutinise AML frameworks heavily. Ensure you have strong procedures for customer due diligence and transaction monitoring.
  • Choosing the wrong service class: Over-classifying can increase capital and costs unnecessarily. For instance, if you only offer exchange services, do not apply for a Class 4 license.
  • Not engaging a local agent: In Malta, a VFA agent is mandatory; in Czechia, a local representative is highly recommended. Consulting24 can act as your agent or coordinator.

Frequently asked questions

What is the main difference between a Malta and Czech Republic crypto license?

The main difference is cost and regulatory intensity. Malta requires higher capital (EUR 125,000-150,000) and has a longer timeline (6-12 months) but offers a stronger regulatory reputation. The Czech Republic has lower capital (EUR 50,000-125,000) and faster processing (3-6 months) but is less established for crypto. Both provide MiCA passporting.

Can I use a Malta or Czech Republic license to serve clients across the EU?

Yes, both licenses are fully MiCA-compliant and allow passporting to all EU member states. This means you can serve clients anywhere in the European Economic Area without needing additional licenses. However, you must comply with local marketing and AML rules in each country.

What are the capital requirements for a Malta crypto license?

Under MiCA, capital is tiered: EUR 50,000 for simple exchange or custody, EUR 125,000 for combined services, and EUR 150,000 for operating a trading platform. Malta typically applies the higher tiers, especially for Class 3 and 4 licenses. Capital must be held in liquid assets.

How long does it take to get a crypto license in the Czech Republic?

The Czech Republic's process typically takes 3 to 6 months from application to registration. This is faster than Malta due to the notification-based regime. However, delays can occur if documents are incomplete. Consulting24 can help prepare a complete application to expedite the process.

What are the ongoing compliance requirements for a Czech Republic crypto license?

Ongoing compliance includes AML/KYC procedures, transaction monitoring, suspicious activity reporting, and annual audits. You must also maintain the minimum capital and report any changes to the CNB. Failure to comply can result in fines or revocation. Consulting24 offers compliance support packages.

Is Malta or the Czech Republic better for a crypto startup?

For startups with limited capital, the Czech Republic is generally better due to lower costs and faster processing. Malta is more suitable for established businesses that can afford higher costs and benefit from a strong regulatory stamp. Consider your budget, timeline, and target market.

Do I need a local office in Malta or the Czech Republic to get a license?

In Malta, you need a registered office and a local director or compliance officer. In the Czech Republic, a registered address is required, and a local representative for AML is recommended. Consulting24 can provide registered office services and local representation.

What are the tax benefits of a Malta crypto license?

Malta offers a corporate tax rate of 35% but with refunds that can reduce the effective rate to 5-10% for non-resident shareholders. There is no capital gains tax on crypto-to-crypto trades and no withholding tax on dividends. This makes Malta highly tax-efficient for international investors.

Can I switch from a Czech Republic license to a Malta license later?

Yes, you can apply for a Malta license at any time, but you would need to meet Malta's higher capital and compliance requirements. You cannot simply transfer the license; it is a new application. Consulting24 can assist with the transition if needed.

What happens if I operate without a license in Malta or the Czech Republic?

Operating without a license is illegal and can result in fines, criminal charges, and imprisonment. Both regulators actively monitor and enforce compliance. It is essential to obtain the proper authorisation before offering services. Consulting24 can help you become compliant quickly.
