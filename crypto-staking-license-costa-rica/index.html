What Is a Crypto Staking License in Costa Rica?

A crypto staking license in Costa Rica is a regulatory authorization that allows a company to offer staking services, including operating validator nodes and delegating tokens on behalf of clients. In 2026, the primary regulatory instrument is the registration with SUGEF under the Law on Narcotics, Psychotropic Substances, and Related Activities (Law 8204), which was amended to include virtual assets. This registration is not a full license but a mandatory registration for any entity providing virtual asset services, including staking. The process involves submitting a compliance program, AML/CFT policies, and beneficial ownership information.

Staking is treated as a financial service, and the regulator expects firms to have strong risk management. Consulting24 advises clients on structuring their entity and preparing the necessary documentation to meet SUGEF’s requirements. The registration is valid indefinitely as long as the company remains compliant and files annual reports. For a detailed breakdown of the application steps, see our application process page.

In practice, the registration covers staking activities such as running validators for Ethereum, Solana, or Polkadot, and offering delegated staking to clients. The scope is broad enough to include both retail and institutional staking services. However, activities like custody or trading may require separate authorizations. Who Needs a Crypto Staking License in Costa Rica?

Any business that offers staking services to clients, whether as a standalone product or part of a larger exchange, must register with SUGEF. This includes:

  • Staking-as-a-service providers that pool client tokens and stake them on proof-of-stake networks.
  • Validator node operators that run infrastructure for networks like Ethereum, Solana, or Polkadot.
  • DeFi platforms that offer delegated staking to users.
  • Crypto exchanges that include staking in their product suite.
  • Institutional staking services for pension funds or asset managers.

Even if your company is based outside Costa Rica but targets Costa Rican clients, registration may be required. License Type & Regulator

The regulatory body for crypto staking in Costa Rica is the Superintendencia General de Entidades Financieras (SUGEF). The required authorization is a “Registration as a Virtual Asset Service Provider” (VASP). There is no separate license for staking; it falls under the broader VASP category. The registration is based on compliance with AML/CFT obligations under Law 8204 and its regulations.

Unlike EU jurisdictions under MiCA, Costa Rica does not have a tiered capital system. Instead, the focus is on the integrity of the company’s ownership and its AML program. The registration process includes a background check on directors and shareholders, including police clearance certificates and financial references. Consulting24 coordinates with local attorneys to ensure your application meets SUGEF’s standards. The regulator also requires a local compliance officer who is a Costa Rican resident or has a physical presence in the country.

SUGEF publishes a public registry of registered VASPs, which adds transparency and credibility to your business. Being listed on this registry can help with bank account opening and client trust. For more details on regulatory requirements, visit our requirements page.

Capital Requirement

Costa Rica does not impose a minimum capital requirement for VASP registration. However, the company must demonstrate sufficient financial resources to operate its staking business. In practice, the regulator expects the company to have at least enough capital to cover operational costs for the first year. Many founders choose to capitalise their company with EUR 10,000 to 20,000 to show substance. This capital can be used for server costs, salaries, and compliance expenses.

Unlike MiCA jurisdictions that require EUR 50,000 to 150,000 depending on services, Costa Rica’s flexible approach reduces upfront financial pressure. However, you must provide a business plan with financial projections showing how the company will sustain itself. Tax Treatment

Costa Rica taxes corporate income at a standard rate of 30%, but there are no specific crypto taxes. Staking rewards are generally treated as ordinary income and subject to corporate income tax. However, capital gains from selling staked tokens may be taxed differently. The country does not have a VAT on digital services. Transfer pricing rules apply if you have related-party transactions.

For international staking operations, tax residency is key. If your company is managed and controlled from Costa Rica, it is a tax resident and subject to worldwide income tax. However, if you structure operations through a Costa Rican entity but manage from abroad, you may limit tax exposure. Allowed Activities Under the License

The SUGEF registration permits the following activities related to staking:

  • Operating validator nodes for proof-of-stake blockchains.
  • Offering staking-as-a-service to clients, including token delegation.
  • Providing staking rewards distribution and reporting.
  • Managing staking pools and associated liquidity.
  • Offering slashing insurance or guarantees (subject to additional oversight).

Activities that are not explicitly covered include custody of client assets (which may require a separate license), trading of derivatives, and operating a crypto exchange. The registration is broad enough to cover most staking models, but you must clearly define your services in the application. Step-by-Step Application Process

Obtaining a crypto staking license in Costa Rica involves several stages. Consulting24 coordinates each step with local legal partners.

  1. Company Incorporation: Register a Costa Rican Sociedad Anónima (SA) with a local registered address and director. The process takes 1-2 weeks and costs EUR 1,500-2,500.
  2. AML Program Development: Create a comprehensive AML/CFT manual, including risk assessment, customer due diligence, transaction monitoring, and suspicious activity reporting. This must be tailored to staking services.
  3. Document Preparation: Gather beneficial ownership information, business plan, financial projections, background checks for all directors and shareholders, and police clearance certificates.
  4. SUGEF Submission: File the registration application with SUGEF, including all supporting documents and the application fee (EUR 500-1,000). The application must be in Spanish.
  5. Regulatory Review: SUGEF reviews the application, which may take 2-4 months. They may request additional information or clarifications. Consulting24 handles all communications.
  6. Approval: Once approved, the company is registered as a VASP and can commence staking operations. You will receive a registration certificate.

Ongoing compliance includes annual reporting, AML training, and maintaining a local presence. Consulting24 provides post-license support to ensure continuous compliance. For a detailed timeline, see our application process page.

Compliance & Trust: Ongoing Obligations

After registration, the company must maintain compliance with SUGEF’s requirements. This includes:

  • Annual submission of audited financial statements and AML reports within 90 days of year-end.
  • Ongoing AML/CFT training for staff, at least annually.
  • Transaction monitoring and suspicious activity reporting to SUGEF and the Financial Intelligence Unit.
  • Keeping beneficial ownership records up to date and reporting any changes within 15 days.
  • Maintaining a local compliance officer and registered office.

Failure to comply can result in fines up to EUR 50,000 or revocation of the registration. SUGEF conducts periodic inspections. Common Mistakes to Avoid

Founders often make these errors when applying for a Costa Rica staking license:

  • Submitting incomplete AML policies that do not meet SUGEF standards, such as missing risk assessment for staking-specific risks like slashing.
  • Underestimating the importance of a local director or physical office. The regulator requires a local presence.
  • Not preparing for background checks on all shareholders, including providing police clearance from their home countries.
  • Assuming the registration covers all crypto activities (e.g., custody or exchange). Staking registration is limited to staking services.
  • Delaying the application while operating in the grey area, which can lead to penalties.
  • Using a template AML manual without customising it to the staking business model.

Frequently asked questions

What is the difference between a crypto staking license and a VASP registration in Costa Rica?

In Costa Rica, there is no separate staking license. Staking falls under the broader VASP registration with SUGEF. The registration authorises you to offer virtual asset services, including staking. It is not a full license but a mandatory registration for compliance with AML/CFT laws.

How long does it take to get a crypto staking license in Costa Rica?

The process typically takes 3 to 6 months from company incorporation to SUGEF approval. This includes 1-2 weeks for incorporation, 2-4 weeks for AML program development, and 2-4 months for regulatory review. Delays can occur if the application is incomplete.

Is there a minimum capital requirement for a staking license in Costa Rica?

No, Costa Rica does not impose a minimum capital requirement for VASP registration. However, you must demonstrate sufficient financial resources to operate. Many founders capitalise with EUR 10,000-20,000 to show substance.

Can I use my Costa Rica staking license to offer services to clients in the EU?

Yes, but you must comply with EU regulations if you target EU clients. Under MiCA, you may need to register as a CASP in an EU member state or use a reverse solicitation exemption. Consulting24 can advise on cross-border compliance.

What are the ongoing compliance costs for a Costa Rica staking license?

Annual costs include local director fees (EUR 1,000-2,000), registered office (EUR 500-1,000), AML training (EUR 500-1,000), and accounting/audit fees (EUR 2,000-4,000). Total ongoing costs are approximately EUR 4,000-8,000 per year.

Does Costa Rica tax staking rewards?

Yes, staking rewards are generally treated as ordinary income and subject to corporate income tax at 30%. Capital gains from selling staked tokens may be taxed differently. There is no VAT on digital services. Consult a local tax advisor for your specific situation.

Can I operate a staking pool with a Costa Rica license?

Yes, operating a staking pool is allowed under the VASP registration. You can pool client tokens and delegate them to validators. However, you must have clear terms and conditions and comply with AML/CFT obligations for each client.

What happens if I operate without registration in Costa Rica?

Operating without SUGEF registration is illegal and can result in fines up to 10% of annual revenue, criminal charges, or imprisonment. The regulator actively monitors the market. It is essential to register before offering any staking services.

Is a local director required for a Costa Rica staking license?

Yes, SUGEF requires a local director who is a Costa Rican resident. This person must be involved in the company's management and compliance. Consulting24 can help you find a qualified local director.

How does Costa Rica compare to Panama for staking licenses?

Panama offers a lower cost (EUR 6,000 flat) and territorial tax (0% on foreign income), while Costa Rica has a more established regulatory framework and a public registry. Panama is faster (2-4 months) but may have less regulatory clarity. Consulting24 can help you decide.

Can I convert my Costa Rica staking license to a full exchange license later?

The VASP registration covers staking but not exchange activities. If you want to offer exchange services, you need a separate license or amendment. Consulting24 can guide you through the process of expanding your license scope.

What documents are needed for the SUGEF application?

You need: company incorporation documents, AML/CFT manual, business plan, financial projections, beneficial ownership information, police clearance for directors/shareholders, and proof of local address. All documents must be in Spanish or officially translated.
