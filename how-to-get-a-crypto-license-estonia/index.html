What Is the Estonia Crypto License?

The Estonia crypto license, formally known as the Virtual Asset Service Provider (VASP) license, is a regulatory authorization issued by the Estonian Financial Intelligence Unit (FIU). It permits companies to offer services such as exchanging virtual assets against fiat currency, transferring virtual assets, and providing virtual asset wallets. Since Estonia is an EU member state, the license is recognized across the European Economic Area under MiCA passporting rules.

Key features of the license include:

  • Full compliance with Estonian Anti-Money Laundering and Counter-Terrorist Financing (AML/CFT) laws.
  • Mandatory registration with the Estonian Commercial Register and FIU.
  • Ongoing reporting obligations and on-site inspections.
  • Possibility to passport services to other EU countries.

The license is valid indefinitely, but the FIU conducts periodic reviews. In practice, the FIU expects license holders to maintain active operations; dormant licenses may be revoked. As of 2026, Estonia has streamlined its regime after a 2022 overhaul that cancelled over 1,000 previously issued licenses for non-compliance. This means the current bar is higher, but the license carries more credibility.

Who Needs an Estonia Crypto License?

Any legal entity that provides virtual asset services in or from Estonia must hold a VASP license. This includes:

  • Cryptocurrency exchanges (fiat-to-crypto and crypto-to-crypto).
  • Virtual asset wallet providers.
  • Transfer services for virtual assets.
  • Initial coin offering (ICO) platforms, if they facilitate exchange or custody.

If your business involves any of these activities, you must apply for an Estonia crypto license before commencing operations. Foreign companies targeting Estonian customers or establishing a physical presence in Estonia are also required to obtain the license. Even if you plan to operate remotely, if your services are directed at Estonian residents or you have a branch in Estonia, the requirement applies. The FIU has been active in enforcing against unlicensed operators, including issuing fines and public warnings. For example, in 2023, the FIU fined several firms for operating without a license, highlighting the importance of compliance.

License Type and Regulator

The Estonia crypto license is a single license type covering all VASP activities. There is no differentiation between exchange, wallet, or transfer licenses. The regulator is the Estonian Financial Intelligence Unit (FIU), which operates under the Ministry of Finance. The FIU is responsible for licensing, supervision, and enforcement of AML/CFT obligations.

In 2026, the Estonian regime is fully aligned with MiCA, which means that the license also qualifies as a CASP (Crypto-Asset Service Provider) license under EU law. This alignment ensures that Estonian-licensed firms can passport their services to other EU member states without additional licensing.

For more details on the regulatory framework, visit the official Estonian FIU website.

The FIU has a dedicated licensing department that reviews applications. They also conduct on-site inspections and can request additional information at any stage. The regulator's approach is risk-based, meaning that applicants with higher-risk business models (e.g., anonymous transactions, high-volume exchanges) face stricter scrutiny.

Capital Requirement

Estonia does not impose a fixed minimum capital requirement for VASP licenses. Instead, the FIU assesses the applicant's financial standing on a case-by-case basis. As a general guideline, the company must demonstrate sufficient equity to cover operational expenses for at least six months. In practice, most successful applicants maintain a share capital of at least EUR 12,000 to EUR 25,000.

This is in contrast to other EU jurisdictions that have adopted MiCA's tiered capital requirements (EUR 50,000 / 125,000 / 150,000). Estonia's approach is more flexible but requires a strong business plan and financial projections. Consulting24 advises clients on preparing a convincing financial dossier.

The capital must be in the company's bank account and cannot be borrowed. The FIU may request proof of source of funds. For example, if the capital comes from personal savings, you need bank statements; if from another business, you need audited accounts. The key is to show that the capital is genuine and available.

Tax Treatment

Estonia operates a unique corporate income tax system: profits are not taxed until they are distributed as dividends. This means that a crypto license holder can reinvest profits tax-free. The standard corporate income tax rate on distributed profits is 20% (or 14% for regular dividends).

For crypto transactions, Estonia treats virtual assets as property, not currency. Therefore, capital gains from crypto trading are subject to income tax at the individual level (20%). VAT is not applicable to crypto-to-crypto exchanges, but fiat-to-crypto exchanges may be subject to VAT in certain circumstances. Consulting24 recommends engaging a local tax advisor to ensure full compliance.

One nuance: if your company holds crypto as an investment, any unrealized gains are not taxed. Only realized gains when crypto is sold or exchanged for fiat are taxable. For mining income, it is treated as business income and taxed accordingly. Estonia also has a withholding tax of 20% on certain payments to non-residents, but dividends to EU residents are exempt under the parent-subsidiary directive.

Allowed Activities Under the License

The Estonia crypto license permits the following activities:

  • Exchange of virtual assets against fiat currency.
  • Exchange of virtual assets against other virtual assets.
  • Transfer of virtual assets (on behalf of a client).
  • Custody and administration of virtual assets (wallet services).
  • Participation in and provision of financial services related to an issuer's offer of virtual assets (including ICOs).

Activities that are not covered include: operating a crypto ATM (requires a separate license), issuing stablecoins (may fall under e-money regulations), and providing investment advice (requires an investment firm license).

If your business model includes staking or lending, you need to check with the FIU. Generally, staking as a service may be considered custody, while lending may require a credit institution license. The FIU provides guidance on request. For a full list of permitted activities, see the exchange license page.

Step-by-Step Application Process

  1. Company Formation: Register a private limited company (OÜ) in Estonia with a minimum share capital of EUR 2,500 (or more as advised). The company must have a physical address in Estonia and a local contact person.
  2. AML/CFT Compliance: Develop internal policies, risk assessment, and procedures aligned with Estonian law. Appoint an AML officer.
  3. Prepare Documentation: Gather business plan, financial projections, ownership structure, CVs of management, and proof of source of funds.
  4. Submit Application: File the application with the FIU through the e-service portal. Pay the state fee of EUR 3,500.
  5. FIU Review: The FIU evaluates the application, which may include requests for additional information or an interview.
  6. Approval: If satisfied, the FIU issues the license. The company is then added to the public register of VASPs.

It is critical to respond promptly and thoroughly. Some applicants are invited for an in-person or video interview with the FIU to discuss their application.

One of the biggest challenges for crypto license holders is opening a bank account. Estonian banks have traditionally been cautious about crypto businesses. As of 2026, several licensed fintech banks and EMI (Electronic Money Institution) providers offer accounts to VASPs, but due diligence is rigorous.

Options include:

  • LHV Bank (Estonia) - known for working with licensed crypto firms.
  • Payments and e-money institutions such as Monese or Paysera.

Operating under an Estonia crypto license requires ongoing compliance. This includes:

  • Submitting annual AML reports to the FIU.
  • Conducting regular internal audits.
  • Reporting suspicious transactions.
  • Maintaining records for 5 years.
  • Cooperating with FIU inspections.

The FIU has the power to conduct unannounced on-site inspections. They may request access to systems, records, and personnel. License holders must have a local representative available for such inspections. Non-compliance can lead to penalties up to EUR 1 million or more for serious breaches. Common Mistakes in the Application Process

Many applications are delayed or rejected due to avoidable errors. Common pitfalls include:

  • Incomplete Documentation: Missing business plan, unclear source of funds, or lack of AML policies.
  • Unrealistic Financial Projections: Overly optimistic revenue forecasts without supporting assumptions.
  • Weak AML Framework: Generic policies not tailored to the company's specific activities.
  • Unqualified Management: Lack of relevant experience or criminal records.
  • Insufficient Capital: Not demonstrating adequate financial resources to sustain operations.

Consulting24 helps you avoid these mistakes by reviewing every document before submission. Learn more on our requirements page.

Another common mistake is failing to properly identify beneficial owners. The FIU requires full disclosure of the ownership chain, including ultimate beneficial owners (UBOs) with more than 25% shares. If UBOs are based in high-risk jurisdictions, additional due diligence is needed. Also, some applicants underestimate the importance of a detailed risk assessment. The AML policy must include a risk assessment that covers the specific products, customers, and geographies involved.

Frequently asked questions

What is the difference between an Estonia crypto license and a CASP license under MiCA?

The Estonia crypto license is a VASP license that, as of 2026, is fully aligned with MiCA. This means it qualifies as a CASP (Crypto-Asset Service Provider) license under EU law. The key difference is that the Estonia license is issued by the national regulator (FIU) but allows passporting across the EU, while a direct CASP license from another EU member state would have similar effect. The Estonia license is essentially a CASP license under Estonian law.

Can I apply for an Estonia crypto license if my company is registered outside the EU?

Yes, but you must first register a subsidiary or branch in Estonia. The license is issued to a legal entity registered in Estonia. Non-EU founders can use the e-residency program to incorporate and manage the company remotely. The company must have a physical address in Estonia and a local contact person. Consulting24 can assist with company formation.

How long is the Estonia crypto license valid?

The license is valid indefinitely, but the FIU conducts periodic reviews and may revoke it for non-compliance. License holders must submit annual AML reports and cooperate with inspections. There is no fixed renewal period, but the FIU may charge an annual supervision fee. In practice, as long as you maintain compliance, the license remains active.

What are the ongoing compliance costs for an Estonia crypto license?

Ongoing costs include annual AML report preparation (EUR 2,000-5,000), AML officer salary or outsourcing (EUR 5,000-15,000 per year), FIU supervision fee (EUR 1,000-2,000), and potential audit costs. Total annual compliance costs typically range from EUR 8,000 to EUR 20,000 depending on the complexity of your operations. Consulting24 offers ongoing compliance packages.

Is it possible to get an Estonia crypto license without a physical office in Estonia?

Yes, you can use a virtual office service that provides a registered address and mail forwarding. However, the FIU requires a local contact person who can be reached during business hours. Some virtual office providers offer this service. The company must also have a local bank account, which may require a physical presence for account opening. Consulting24 can recommend virtual office providers.

What happens if my application for an Estonia crypto license is rejected?

If rejected, the FIU will provide reasons. You can appeal the decision within 30 days or reapply after addressing the deficiencies. Common reasons for rejection include incomplete documentation, insufficient capital, or concerns about management fitness. Consulting24 can help you understand the rejection and prepare a stronger application. The state fee is non-refundable even if rejected.

Can I use the Estonia crypto license to operate a crypto ATM?

No, operating a crypto ATM requires a separate license under Estonian law. The VASP license covers exchange and wallet services but not ATM operations. If you plan to run ATMs, you need to apply for a specific ATM license, which has additional requirements such as physical security measures. Contact the FIU for details.

Does the Estonia crypto license allow me to offer staking services?

Staking as a service may be considered custody of virtual assets, which is covered under the license. However, if you offer staking with a promise of returns, it may be considered a security or investment product, requiring additional licensing. The FIU evaluates on a case-by-case basis. It is advisable to seek legal advice before offering staking services.

What is the minimum share capital for an Estonian company applying for a crypto license?

The minimum share capital for an OÜ is EUR 2,500, but the FIU expects higher capital to demonstrate financial stability. In practice, most successful applicants have at least EUR 12,000-25,000. The capital must be fully paid up before application. There is no statutory minimum for the license itself, but the FIU assesses adequacy based on your business plan.

Can I passport my Estonia crypto license to other EU countries?

Yes, under MiCA, an Estonia-licensed VASP can passport its services to any EU member state without additional licensing. You must notify the FIU of your intention to passport, and the FIU will inform the host country's regulator. This process is straightforward and typically takes a few weeks. Passporting allows you to serve clients across the EU from your Estonian base.
