What Is a Crypto Wallet Custody License in Switzerland?

A crypto wallet custody license in Switzerland authorizes a company to hold, store, and manage private keys or digital assets on behalf of clients. This is a regulated activity under Swiss law, primarily supervised by FINMA. The license is required for any entity that provides custodial wallet services, including hot and cold storage solutions.

Switzerland distinguishes between different types of crypto activities. Wallet custody is treated as a financial service under the Financial Services Act (FinSA) and is subject to anti-money laundering obligations under the AMBA. Companies must be registered with FINMA and comply with ongoing reporting and capital requirements.

Key characteristics of the license include: strict client asset segregation, mandatory insurance coverage, and regular audits. The Swiss approach is principles-based, allowing flexibility but requiring high standards of operational security and transparency. For example, a custody provider must implement multi-signature wallets and cold storage for the majority of client assets, with insurance covering at least 50% of custodial holdings. FINMA also expects a detailed disaster recovery plan and regular penetration testing.

Compared to EU jurisdictions under MiCA, Switzerland offers a more tailored framework for crypto custody, but with higher capital demands. For smaller firms, Panama provides a simpler alternative with a flat EUR 6,000 setup and no minimum capital requirement.

Who Needs a Crypto Wallet Custody License?

Any business that offers custodial services for cryptocurrencies or digital assets to third parties needs this license. This includes:

If you are a fintech startup, a traditional financial institution expanding into crypto, or a blockchain project offering custody features, you likely need this license. License Type and Regulator

The primary regulator for crypto wallet custody in Switzerland is the Swiss Financial Market Supervisory Authority (FINMA). FINMA oversees all financial market activities, including crypto custody services. The license type is typically a 'financial services provider' license under the Financial Services Act (FinSA) or a 'securities firm' license if the custody involves securities tokens.

For pure wallet custody (non-securities), companies must register as a financial intermediary under the AMBA and obtain a license from FINMA. The specific category depends on the scale and nature of the business. Smaller operations may qualify for a simplified regime under the Swiss Blockchain Act, which provides a lower capital requirement of CHF 100,000 for certain activities. However, most custody providers fall under the standard FinSA regime.

FINMA's approach is risk-based. It requires a detailed business plan, proof of adequate capital, and strong internal controls. The regulator also expects compliance with FATF recommendations and Swiss anti-money laundering laws. For example, FINMA may require a custodian to hold client assets in segregated accounts and to conduct regular AML training for staff. The application must include a description of the custody technology, security measures, and audit procedures.

Capital Requirement

Switzerland imposes a minimum capital requirement for crypto wallet custody license holders. As of 2026, the requirement is typically between CHF 100,000 and CHF 500,000, depending on the business volume and risk profile. This capital must be fully paid up and maintained at all times.

The capital serves as a buffer for operational risks and client protection. FINMA may require higher capital if the company handles large volumes of assets or engages in complex activities. The exact amount is determined during the licensing process based on the business plan. For instance, a custodian holding over CHF 10 million in client assets may need CHF 500,000 in capital.

For comparison, Panama offers a flat EUR 6,000 company setup with no minimum capital requirement for crypto activities. This makes Panama an attractive alternative for startups or smaller firms. Under MiCA in the EU, capital requirements range from EUR 50,000 to EUR 150,000 depending on services, which is lower than Switzerland's typical minimum.

Tax Treatment

Switzerland has a favorable tax regime for crypto businesses. Corporate income tax rates vary by canton, typically ranging from 11% to 21%. Capital gains from crypto trading are generally tax-exempt for companies, provided the trading is not considered a professional activity.

Value-added tax (VAT) applies to crypto custody services at the standard rate of 7.7% (as of 2026). However, some services may be exempt if they qualify as financial services. Detailed tax planning is recommended. For example, custody fees for institutional clients may be VAT-exempt if the client is a regulated financial institution.

Switzerland does not impose a withholding tax on dividends paid to foreign shareholders, making it attractive for international investors. Allowed Activities

With a crypto wallet custody license in Switzerland, you are permitted to:

  • Hold and store private keys for clients
  • Manage digital asset wallets (hot and cold storage)
  • Facilitate transfers of cryptocurrencies
  • Provide staking services (if approved)
  • Offer custody-related reporting and analytics

Activities not covered by the license include: operating a crypto exchange (requires a separate license), issuing securities tokens (requires a prospectus), and providing investment advice (requires additional authorization). For example, a custodian cannot offer trading services without an exchange license, but they can partner with a licensed exchange.

Step-by-Step Application Process

  1. Pre-assessment: Evaluate your business model and determine the appropriate license type. Consulting24 advises on suitability.
  2. Company incorporation: Set up a Swiss company (AG or GmbH) with a registered office and local directors. The company must have a physical presence in Switzerland.
  3. Prepare documentation: Draft business plan, AML policies, risk management framework, and capital proof. The business plan must detail custody technology, security measures, and projected volumes.
  4. Submit application: File with FINMA, including all required forms and fees. FINMA may request additional information within 30 days.
  5. Review and amendments: FINMA may request changes. Respond promptly to avoid delays. Typical review takes 2-4 months.
  6. Approval and licensing: Receive license, then fulfill ongoing compliance (audits, reporting). The license is valid indefinitely but subject to annual renewal.

Compliance and Trust

Compliance is paramount in Switzerland. License holders must adhere to strict AML/KYC procedures, report suspicious transactions, and undergo annual audits. FINMA conducts regular inspections and can impose fines or revoke licenses for non-compliance.

To maintain trust, companies should implement strong internal controls, conduct employee training, and engage external auditors. For example, a custodian must perform enhanced due diligence on clients with high-risk profiles, such as politically exposed persons. Annual audits must cover financial statements, AML compliance, and IT security.
