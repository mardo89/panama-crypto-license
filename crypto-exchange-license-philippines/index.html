What Is a Crypto Exchange License in the Philippines?

A crypto exchange license in the Philippines is a regulatory authorization issued by the Bangko Sentral ng Pilipinas (BSP) that allows a company to operate as a Virtual Asset Service Provider (VASP). This license is mandatory for any entity offering exchange services between fiat currency and virtual assets, or between different virtual assets. The BSP's regulatory framework ensures that exchanges comply with anti-money laundering (AML) and counter-terrorism financing (CTF) standards, providing a secure environment for users.

The license is officially termed a "Certificate of Registration as a Virtual Asset Service Provider" under the BSP's Circular No. 1108. It is a comprehensive license that covers all major exchange activities, including custody, trading, and transfer of virtual assets. As of 2026, the BSP continues to refine its regulations to align with international standards, making the Philippines a stable and reputable jurisdiction for crypto businesses. The BSP also requires exchanges to implement strong cybersecurity measures, including data encryption, multi-factor authentication, and regular penetration testing. Exchanges must also maintain a minimum IT security infrastructure, such as firewalls and intrusion detection systems, to protect against cyber threats.

The license is non-transferable and must be renewed every three years. The BSP may impose additional conditions based on the exchange's risk profile, such as higher capital requirements or enhanced reporting obligations. Who Needs a Crypto Exchange License in the Philippines?

Any business that facilitates the exchange of fiat currency for virtual assets, or vice versa, must obtain a crypto exchange license in the Philippines. This includes:

  • Centralized exchanges offering spot trading, margin trading, or derivatives.
  • Peer-to-peer (P2P) platforms that match buyers and sellers.
  • Over-the-counter (OTC) trading desks.
  • ATMs that convert fiat to crypto.
  • Any platform that provides custody or wallet services for virtual assets.
  • Companies offering crypto payment processing services.

Even if your business is based outside the Philippines, if you serve Philippine residents or process Philippine peso transactions, you are likely required to register with the BSP. The BSP has extraterritorial reach: any entity that solicits business from Philippine residents must comply. For example, a foreign exchange that advertises to Filipino users or accepts Philippine peso deposits must obtain a VASP license. License Type and Regulator

The regulator for crypto exchanges in the Philippines is the Bangko Sentral ng Pilipinas (BSP). The applicable license is the Virtual Asset Service Provider (VASP) Certificate of Registration. This is a single license type that covers all VASP activities. There are no separate licenses for exchange, custody, or transfer; the VASP registration encompasses all. The BSP also oversees compliance with the Anti-Money Laundering Act (AMLA) and the Terrorism Financing Prevention and Suppression Act.

The BSP requires applicants to be a corporation registered with the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) of the Philippines. The company must have a minimum paid-up capital as specified by the BSP (see next section). The license is non-transferable and must be renewed periodically, with ongoing compliance reporting. The BSP may also require the appointment of a resident compliance officer and a money laundering reporting officer (MLRO). The regulator conducts on-site examinations and off-site surveillance to ensure ongoing compliance.

Capital Requirement for a Crypto Exchange License in the Philippines

The BSP mandates a minimum paid-up capital of PHP 50,000,000 (approximately USD 900,000) for VASP applicants. This capital must be fully paid in cash and held in a bank account in the Philippines. The capital requirement is designed to ensure financial stability and operational capacity. Additional capital may be required based on the volume of transactions or risk profile. For instance, if an exchange plans to handle high transaction volumes or offer margin trading, the BSP may require a higher capital buffer.

Compared to other jurisdictions, the Philippines' capital requirement is substantial. For context, Panama offers a flat fee of EUR 6,000 with no minimum capital, making it an attractive alternative for smaller operations. Tax Treatment for Crypto Exchanges in the Philippines

Crypto exchanges in the Philippines are subject to corporate income tax at the standard rate of 25% (or 20% for small businesses with net taxable income not exceeding PHP 5 million and total assets not exceeding PHP 100 million). Additionally, a 12% value-added tax (VAT) applies to service fees charged by the exchange. There is no specific crypto tax law; transactions are treated under general tax principles. Capital gains from crypto trading by individuals may be subject to a 15% final withholding tax if held as capital assets.

The Bureau of Internal Revenue (BIR) has issued guidelines requiring exchanges to report transactions, including customer details and transaction amounts. Exchanges must also withhold taxes on certain payments, such as interest or royalties. It is essential to work with a local tax advisor to ensure compliance. Allowed Activities Under a Philippine Crypto Exchange License

A VASP license from the BSP permits the following activities:

  • Exchange of fiat currency for virtual assets (and vice versa).
  • Exchange of one virtual asset for another.
  • Transfer of virtual assets (on behalf of clients).
  • Custody and safekeeping of virtual assets.
  • Participation in initial coin offerings (ICOs) as a platform.
  • Provision of crypto payment services for merchants.

Note that the license does not cover securities activities (e.g., security token offerings) which fall under the SEC. Additionally, derivatives trading may require separate approval from the BSP or the SEC. The BSP prohibits anonymous transactions; all users must be KYC-verified. Exchanges must also implement transaction monitoring systems to detect suspicious activities, such as structuring or rapid trading patterns.

Step-by-Step Process to Obtain a Crypto Exchange License in the Philippines

  1. Company Incorporation: Register a corporation with the SEC Philippines. The company must have at least five incorporators, a minimum of three directors, and a paid-up capital of PHP 50 million. The corporate secretary must be a Filipino citizen.
  2. Prepare Documentation: Draft a comprehensive business plan, AML/CTF policies, risk management framework, IT security protocols, and a disaster recovery plan. Engage a local compliance officer and MLRO.
  3. Submit Application to BSP: File the VASP registration application with all supporting documents. Pay the application fee of approximately PHP 200,000. The BSP may request additional information or clarifications.
  4. BSP Review and Evaluation: The BSP conducts due diligence, including background checks on directors and shareholders. This may take 3-6 months. The BSP may also conduct a pre-approval inspection of the office and IT systems.
  5. Issuance of Certificate: Upon approval, the BSP issues a Certificate of Registration as a VASP. The exchange can then commence operations. The certificate is valid for three years and must be renewed.
  6. Ongoing Compliance: Submit regular reports to BSP (monthly, quarterly, annually), conduct annual audits, and maintain AML/CTF programs. The BSP may conduct on-site examinations at any time.

Major banks such as BDO, BPI, and Metrobank accept VASP clients, but they require the BSP license or at least a pending application. Common Mistakes When Applying for a Philippine Crypto License

  • Incomplete Documentation: Missing business plans or AML policies can delay the application or lead to rejection. Ensure all documents are complete and professionally prepared.
  • Underestimating Capital: The PHP 50 million must be fully paid and maintained; insufficient capital leads to rejection. Some applicants mistakenly believe they can use capital in other forms (e.g., crypto) but the BSP requires cash.
  • Ignoring Local Presence: The BSP requires a physical office and local staff. Virtual offices are not accepted. The office must have adequate security and IT infrastructure.
  • Poor Background Checks: Directors with criminal records or regulatory issues may be disqualified. Conduct thorough due diligence on all shareholders and directors.
  • Rushing the Process: The BSP takes time; expecting a quick turnaround is unrealistic. Plan for at least 6 months and allocate resources accordingly.
  • Neglecting IT Security: The BSP requires strong cybersecurity measures. Failure to implement them can result in rejection or revocation of the license.

Frequently asked questions

What is the minimum capital requirement for a crypto exchange license in the Philippines?

The BSP requires a minimum paid-up capital of PHP 50,000,000 (approximately USD 900,000). This capital must be fully paid in cash and held in a Philippine bank account. The amount is fixed and does not vary by service type.

How long does it take to get a crypto exchange license in the Philippines?

The entire process, from company incorporation to license issuance, typically takes 6 to 12 months. The BSP review alone can take 3-6 months. The timeline depends on the completeness of the application and the complexity of the business model.

Can a foreign company apply for a Philippine crypto exchange license?

Yes, but the company must be incorporated in the Philippines as a domestic corporation. Foreign ownership is allowed, but at least one director must be a Philippine resident. The company must also have a physical office in the Philippines.

What activities are covered under the VASP license?

The VASP license covers exchange of fiat for crypto, crypto-to-crypto exchange, transfer of virtual assets, custody services, and participation in ICOs. It does not cover securities activities or derivatives trading, which require separate approvals.

Is the Philippine crypto license recognized internationally?

Yes, the BSP is a member of the Financial Action Task Force (FATF), and the license is recognized as a reputable regulatory approval. This facilitates banking relationships and partnerships with international entities.

What are the tax obligations for a crypto exchange in the Philippines?

Exchanges are subject to 25% corporate income tax (20% for small businesses) and 12% VAT on service fees. Capital gains from crypto trading by individuals may be taxed at 15%. Exchanges must also report transactions to the BIR.

Can I use a virtual office for the Philippine license?

No, the BSP requires a physical office with adequate security and IT infrastructure. Virtual offices or co-working spaces are not accepted. The office must be staffed during business hours.

What happens if I operate without a license in the Philippines?

Operating without a VASP license is illegal and can result in fines, imprisonment, or both. The BSP and SEC have enforcement powers, and unlicensed exchanges may be shut down. It is essential to obtain the license before offering services.

How does the Philippine license compare to Panama?

Panama offers a flat fee of EUR 6,000 with no minimum capital and a faster process (2-3 months). The Philippines requires PHP 50 million capital and takes 6-12 months. Panama is more cost-effective for smaller operations, while the Philippines offers access to the Asian market.
