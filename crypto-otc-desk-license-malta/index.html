What Is a Crypto OTC Desk License Malta?

A crypto OTC desk license Malta is a regulatory authorisation issued by the MFSA under the VFAA. It allows a company to operate an over-the-counter (OTC) trading desk for virtual financial assets, matching buyers and sellers directly without using a public exchange. The licence covers activities such as order execution, dealing on own account, and providing investment advice related to crypto assets.

Unlike exchange licenses that cater to multiple counterparties through an order book, an OTC desk license focuses on large-volume trades with institutional clients, offering privacy and price certainty. Malta's VFA framework is aligned with the EU's Markets in Crypto-Assets Regulation (MiCA), ensuring that licensed firms can passport services across the European Economic Area. MiCA, fully in force since 2025, imposes capital tiers of EUR 50,000, 125,000, and 150,000 by service class, and Malta's Class 3 aligns with the EUR 125,000 tier for dealing on own account.

Key features of the licence include compliance with anti-money laundering (AML) rules, client asset segregation, and ongoing reporting to the MFSA. Who Needs a Malta Crypto OTC Desk License?

Any business that facilitates the direct exchange of crypto assets between counterparties, either on a principal or agency basis, needs this licence. Typical applicants include:

  • Institutional OTC desks serving hedge funds, family offices, and high-net-worth individuals.
  • Proprietary trading firms that deal in crypto assets for their own account.
  • Brokerages that match buyers and sellers off-exchange.
  • Fintech companies integrating OTC trading into their platforms.

If your firm handles large block trades, provides liquidity, or offers price negotiation outside of public exchanges, the Malta crypto OTC desk license is the appropriate regulatory vehicle. Even firms based outside Malta may need the licence if they target EU clients, due to MiCA's territorial scope. For instance, a UK-based OTC desk serving EU institutional investors must hold a MiCA-compliant licence from an EU member state like Malta.

License Type and Regulator

The regulator is the Malta Financial Services Authority (MFSA). Under the VFAA, there are four classes of VFA licences. For OTC desk operations, the relevant class is Class 3, which permits:

  • Execution of orders on behalf of clients.
  • Dealing on own account.
  • Portfolio management.
  • Investment advice.
  • Reception and transmission of orders.

Class 3 is the most comprehensive licence for trading activities. It requires a minimum capital of EUR 125,000, which must be maintained at all times. The MFSA conducts a thorough fit-and-proper test for directors and shareholders, and requires a local presence including a physical office and at least one resident director. The regulator also assesses the firm's business plan, risk management framework, and AML controls. Compared to Estonia's crypto license, Malta's requirements are more demanding but offer EU passporting rights.

Capital Requirement

For a Class 3 VFA licence (OTC desk), the minimum capital requirement is EUR 125,000. This capital must be fully paid up and held in a liquid form, such as cash or cash equivalents. The MFSA may require a higher amount based on the firm's risk profile, trading volume, and operational expenses. Capital must be maintained throughout the licence period and is subject to ongoing monitoring.

In comparison, lower classes (Class 1 and 2) have lower capital requirements but do not permit dealing on own account. Class 4 (exchange) requires EUR 730,000. The EUR 125,000 threshold is specifically designed for OTC desks that both execute client orders and trade on their own behalf. Under MiCA, the EU-wide minimum for dealing on own account is EUR 125,000, so Malta's requirement is aligned. For a capital-light alternative, consider Panama where no minimum capital is required for the crypto license.

Tax Treatment

Malta offers a highly competitive tax regime for licensed crypto firms. The standard corporate tax rate is 35%, but through Malta's full imputation system and refund mechanisms, the effective tax rate can be as low as 5%. This is achieved via a 6/7 refund of the tax paid on distributed profits (or 5/7 for certain types of income).

Additionally, Malta has no withholding tax on dividends paid to non-residents, no capital gains tax on trading profits (only on realised gains from certain assets), and a wide network of double taxation treaties. VAT on crypto trading services is generally exempt. This tax structure makes Malta attractive for OTC desk operators seeking to optimise their global tax burden. For example, a firm with EUR 1 million in profits could pay as little as EUR 50,000 in effective tax after refunds.

Allowed Activities

With a Class 3 VFA licence, a crypto OTC desk can engage in the following activities:

  • Execution of orders for clients (matching buy/sell orders off-exchange).
  • Dealing on own account (proprietary trading).
  • Portfolio management of crypto assets.
  • Investment advice on VFAs.
  • Reception and transmission of orders.
  • Safe custody of client assets (if ancillary to trading).

Activities not permitted include operating a multilateral trading facility (exchange) or issuing VFAs. The licence is strictly for OTC and brokerage services. All activities must be conducted in compliance with AML/CFT obligations and MFSA conduct of business rules. For exchange operations, a Class 4 licence is required, which has a higher capital threshold of EUR 730,000.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Applicants often make errors that delay or derail their licence application. Common pitfalls include:

  • Insufficient substance: The MFSA requires a physical office and local staff. Remote-only setups are rejected.
  • Weak AML policies: The MFSA scrutinises AML/CFT frameworks. Generic policies copied from other jurisdictions will be flagged.
  • Unrealistic business plans: Overly optimistic projections or vague strategies lead to rejection.
  • Poor fit-and-proper submissions: Directors with undisclosed criminal records or financial issues will cause delays.
  • Underestimating timeline: Many applicants expect 3-4 months but the process often takes 9-12 months.
