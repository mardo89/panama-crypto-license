What Is a Crypto Exchange License in Switzerland?

A crypto exchange license in Switzerland is a regulatory authorization granted by FINMA to operate a trading platform for crypto assets. Under Swiss law, crypto exchanges are treated as securities dealers or financial intermediaries, depending on the services offered. FINMA classifies tokens and coins as either payment tokens, utility tokens, or asset tokens, and the license type varies accordingly.

The most common route for exchange operators is to apply for a securities dealer license or a financial intermediary license under the Anti-Money Laundering Act (AMLA). As of 2026, Switzerland has fully integrated the FATF recommendations and maintains a strong AML framework. The license allows you to offer spot trading, order matching, and custody services to both retail and institutional clients.

Switzerland's approach is principle-based, meaning FINMA focuses on the economic function of the tokens rather than rigid categories. This flexibility is attractive for innovative exchange models. However, the application process is thorough and requires a detailed business plan, organizational structure, and risk management framework.

Who Needs a Swiss Crypto License?

Any entity that operates a platform for the exchange of crypto assets against fiat currency or other crypto assets must obtain a license from FINMA. This includes centralized exchanges, decentralized exchanges (if they have a central operator), and peer-to-peer platforms that facilitate trading. Additionally, companies that provide custody services for client assets or engage in proprietary trading may also fall under licensing requirements.

Switzerland does not have a de minimis exemption for small exchanges; any commercial activity requires authorization. However, pure software developers or non-custodial wallet providers may not need a license if they do not handle client funds. License Type and Regulator

The primary regulator for crypto exchanges in Switzerland is the Swiss Financial Market Supervisory Authority (FINMA). FINMA is responsible for licensing and supervising financial institutions, including securities dealers and financial intermediaries. For crypto exchanges, the most common license types are:

  • Securities dealer license - for platforms that trade asset tokens (which are considered securities under Swiss law). Requires higher capital and organizational requirements.
  • Financial intermediary license - for platforms dealing with payment tokens (e.g., Bitcoin, Ethereum) and utility tokens. This is typically easier to obtain but still requires AML compliance.
  • Bank license - for exchanges that also offer lending, deposits, or other banking services. This is rare and reserved for large institutions.

FINMA also recognizes the concept of a DLT trading facility under the Distributed Ledger Technology (DLT) Act, which came into force in 2021. This allows for a streamlined license for DLT-based exchanges. As of 2026, FINMA has processed dozens of applications and continues to refine its approach.

Capital Requirement

Switzerland imposes a minimum capital requirement for crypto exchange licenses, which varies by license type. For a securities dealer license, the minimum capital is CHF 500,000 (approximately EUR 500,000). For a financial intermediary license, the minimum is CHF 100,000 (approximately EUR 100,000). These amounts must be fully paid up in cash and held in a Swiss bank account.

Additionally, FINMA may require higher capital based on the volume of transactions, the number of clients, and the risk profile of the business. The capital must be maintained at all times and cannot be used for operational expenses. Consulting24 can help you structure your capital planning and ensure compliance with FINMA's requirements.

Tax Treatment for Crypto Exchanges in Switzerland

Switzerland offers a favorable tax environment for crypto businesses. Corporate income tax rates vary by canton but typically range from 12% to 24% on net profits. There is no capital gains tax for companies, and crypto assets are treated as currency or assets for tax purposes depending on their classification.

Value Added Tax (VAT) is not applied to crypto trading activities, as they are considered exempt financial services. However, advisory and management fees may be subject to VAT at the standard rate of 7.7% (reduced to 2.5% for certain services). Stamp duty may apply to the issuance of tokens if they qualify as securities.

Switzerland has a wide network of double taxation treaties, making it attractive for international operations. Consulting24 can coordinate with local tax advisors to ensure your exchange is optimized for tax efficiency.

Allowed Activities Under a Swiss Crypto License

A Swiss crypto exchange license permits the following activities:

  • Operating a trading platform for crypto-to-crypto and crypto-to-fiat pairs
  • Order matching and execution services
  • Custody of client crypto assets
  • Staking and lending services (with additional authorization)
  • Issuance of security tokens (if licensed as a securities dealer)
  • Proprietary trading (subject to capital requirements)

Activities that are not allowed without a banking license include taking deposits, offering interest on deposits, and providing credit. FINMA strictly separates banking from non-banking activities. If you plan to offer a full suite of services, you may need to consider a Swiss crypto license combined with a banking license or partner with a Swiss bank.

Step-by-Step Application Process

The application process for a Swiss crypto exchange license involves several stages. Consulting24 advises and coordinates each step to ensure a smooth submission.

  1. Pre-assessment - Evaluate your business model against FINMA's requirements. Determine the appropriate license type and capital needs.
  2. Documentation - Prepare a detailed business plan, AML/KYC policies, risk management framework, organizational chart, and financial projections. All documents must be in German, French, or Italian (or accompanied by certified translations).
  3. Capital deposit - Open a Swiss bank account and deposit the minimum capital. The bank must confirm the funds are available and unencumbered.
  4. Submission - File the application with FINMA, including all supporting documents and the application fee. FINMA acknowledges receipt and begins its review.
  5. Review and queries - FINMA may request additional information or clarifications. This stage can take 3 to 6 months.
  6. Decision - FINMA issues a license or rejects the application. If approved, the license is subject to ongoing supervision.
  7. Post-licensing - Submit periodic reports, maintain capital, and comply with AML obligations. Annual audits are required.

Banking and Payment Services for Swiss Crypto Exchanges

One of the biggest challenges for crypto exchanges in Switzerland is obtaining a bank account. Swiss banks are cautious about crypto businesses due to AML concerns. However, several cantonal banks and private banks now offer accounts to licensed exchanges. FINMA's license itself serves as a strong signal of legitimacy.

For fiat on-ramps and off-ramps, exchanges typically partner with Swiss payment processors or use SEPA transfers. Some exchanges also use stablecoins to facilitate transactions. Compliance and Ongoing Obligations

Once licensed, Swiss crypto exchanges must comply with ongoing obligations:

  • AML/KYC - Implement and maintain a strong AML program, including client due diligence, transaction monitoring, and reporting of suspicious activities to the Money Laundering Reporting Office (MROS).
  • Audits - Annual financial audits by a licensed auditor. FINMA may also conduct on-site inspections.
  • Capital maintenance - The minimum capital must be maintained at all times. Any reduction must be reported to FINMA.
  • Reporting - Submit annual reports, including financial statements and AML compliance reports.
  • Changes - Any change in ownership, management, or business model requires FINMA approval.

Non-compliance can result in fines, license revocation, or criminal charges. Consulting24 advises on compliance frameworks and can coordinate with Swiss legal experts to ensure your exchange remains compliant. This is general guidance, not legal advice.

Common Mistakes When Applying for a Swiss Crypto License

Many applicants make avoidable errors that delay or derail their application. Common mistakes include:

  • Insufficient documentation - FINMA requires a comprehensive business plan, not a one-page summary. Many applicants underestimate the level of detail needed.
  • Underestimating capital requirements - Some applicants try to meet the minimum capital but fail to show it is fully paid up and unencumbered.
  • Ignoring AML requirements - FINMA places heavy emphasis on AML. A weak AML framework is a common reason for rejection.
  • Choosing the wrong license type - Applying for a securities dealer license when a financial intermediary license would suffice (or vice versa) can lead to unnecessary costs and delays.
  • Lack of local presence - While not strictly required, having a local director or office in Switzerland can speed up the process.

Frequently asked questions

What is the minimum capital for a crypto exchange license in Switzerland?

The minimum capital depends on the license type: CHF 100,000 for a financial intermediary license and CHF 500,000 for a securities dealer license. These amounts must be fully paid up in cash and held in a Swiss bank account.

How long does it take to get a Swiss crypto license?

The process typically takes 6 to 12 months from submission, with an additional 3 to 6 months for pre-submission preparation. The timeline can vary based on the complexity of the application and FINMA's workload.

Does Switzerland require a physical office for a crypto exchange?

While not explicitly required, having a registered office and local management in Switzerland is strongly recommended. FINMA expects the company to have substance in Switzerland, including local directors and compliance officers.

Can I use a Swiss crypto license to operate in the EU?

No, a Swiss license does not automatically grant passporting rights in the EU. However, Switzerland has bilateral agreements that facilitate cross-border services. For EU operations, consider a MiCA-compliant license in Lithuania or Estonia.

What is the tax rate for crypto exchanges in Switzerland?

Corporate income tax rates range from 12% to 24% depending on the canton. There is no capital gains tax for companies, and crypto trading is exempt from VAT. Stamp duty may apply to security token issuances.

Is it possible to convert a Swiss financial intermediary license to a securities dealer license later?

Yes, you can upgrade your license as your business grows. This requires a new application to FINMA, including additional capital and documentation. Consulting24 can advise on the process.

What are the main differences between a Swiss crypto license and a Panama company setup?

Switzerland offers a regulated license with high credibility but significant costs and timeline. Panama provides a fast, low-cost corporate structure with no specific crypto licensing, suitable for startups. Panama costs EUR 6,000 flat and can be set up in weeks.

Does FINMA require a local bank account for the capital deposit?

Yes, the minimum capital must be deposited in a Swiss bank account. The bank must confirm the funds are available and not encumbered. Consulting24 can introduce you to banks that accept crypto businesses.

Can I apply for a Swiss crypto license if I am a non-EU resident?

Yes, there are no residency restrictions for shareholders or directors. However, having local directors can facilitate the application. FINMA will assess the suitability of all beneficial owners.

What happens if my application is rejected by FINMA?

FINMA will provide reasons for rejection. You can address the deficiencies and resubmit, or appeal the decision. Consulting24 can help you understand the reasons and prepare a stronger application.

Are there any ongoing costs after obtaining the license?

Yes, you must pay annual supervisory fees to FINMA (CHF 5,000-20,000), maintain capital, and cover audit and compliance costs. Ongoing legal and advisory fees may also apply.

Does Switzerland accept stablecoins as a means of payment for capital?

No, the capital must be deposited in fiat currency (CHF or EUR) in a Swiss bank account. Stablecoins are not accepted as capital for licensing purposes.
