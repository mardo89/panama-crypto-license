What Is the Singapore Crypto License?

In Singapore, the crypto license is formally known as the Digital Payment Token (DPT) service license under the Payment Services Act (PSA). It is regulated by the Monetary Authority of Singapore (MAS). The license is required for businesses that provide any of the following services: buying or selling DPTs, facilitating the exchange of DPTs, or providing custodial services for DPTs. The regime came into full effect in 2020 and has been continuously updated, with the latest amendments in 2024 and 2025 focusing on enhanced consumer protection and anti-money laundering (AML) measures.

Singapore's approach is often cited as one of the best country singapore crypto license frameworks because it balances innovation with strict regulatory oversight. The MAS does not issue a separate 'crypto license' per se; instead, it grants a payment institution license with a DPT service scope. There are two tiers: the standard payment institution (SPI) license and the major payment institution (MPI) license, with the latter applying to businesses exceeding certain transaction thresholds.

Who Needs a Singapore Crypto License?

Any business that provides DPT services in Singapore or to Singapore residents must obtain a license from MAS. This includes:

  • Cryptocurrency exchanges (centralized and decentralized platforms that facilitate trading)
  • Over-the-counter (OTC) trading desks
  • Digital wallet providers that allow storage and transfer of DPTs
  • Custodians of digital assets
  • Payment service providers that use DPTs as a medium of exchange

If your business is based outside Singapore but targets Singapore customers, you may still need a license. The MAS has extraterritorial reach under the PSA. Capital Requirement

As noted, the base capital requirement for a Standard Payment Institution is SGD 100,000 (approx. EUR 65,000). For a Major Payment Institution, it is SGD 250,000 (approx. EUR 165,000). However, MAS may require higher capital based on the risk assessment of the business. The capital must be maintained as liquid assets and cannot be withdrawn without MAS approval. This is significantly higher than Panama's EUR 6,000 fixed capital, making Singapore more suitable for established businesses with larger operations. Tax Treatment

Singapore has a territorial tax system, and income tax is capped at a flat rate of 17% for corporations. There is no capital gains tax. For crypto businesses, the Inland Revenue Authority of Singapore (IRAS) treats cryptocurrency as property for tax purposes. This means that gains from trading or investing in crypto may be subject to income tax if they are considered revenue in nature. However, long-term capital gains are generally not taxed. The GST (Goods and Services Tax) treatment of crypto transactions is also favorable: as of 2020, the supply of DPTs is exempt from GST. This tax regime is one reason why Singapore is considered the best country singapore crypto license for tax efficiency. For specific tax advice, Consulting24 recommends engaging a local tax advisor.

Allowed Activities

Under the DPT service license, the following activities are permitted:

  • Buying and selling of DPTs
  • Facilitating the exchange of DPTs (including operating a trading platform)
  • Providing custodial services for DPTs
  • Cross-border money transfer using DPTs (if combined with other payment services)

Activities that are NOT allowed without additional licensing include: issuing DPTs (unless under a separate securities license), offering derivatives or margin trading (requires CMS license), and providing lending or staking services (may require additional approval). Step-by-Step Application Process

The process for obtaining a Singapore crypto license involves several stages:

  1. Pre-application preparation: Define your business model, prepare AML/CFT policies, appoint a local director (if required), and engage a compliance consultant.
  2. Submission of application: File the application through MAS's online portal (APEX), including all required documents such as business plan, financial projections, and risk assessments.
  3. Review and queries: MAS will review the application and may issue queries. This stage typically takes 3-6 months.
  4. In-principle approval (IPA): If MAS is satisfied, it grants an IPA, which allows the applicant to set up the necessary infrastructure (e.g., bank accounts, technology).
  5. Final approval: After meeting all conditions (e.g., capital injection, audit), MAS issues the full license.
  6. Post-licensing compliance: Ongoing reporting, audits, and AML/CFT obligations.

Banking and Payments

One of the biggest challenges for crypto businesses in Singapore is opening a bank account. Many traditional banks are cautious about onboarding crypto clients due to AML risks. However, Singapore has several banks that are open to licensed crypto firms, including DBS, OCBC, and UOB, as well as some digital banks like Trust Bank. Additionally, there are payment service providers that offer multi-currency accounts and virtual IBANs. The MAS has also introduced a regulatory sandbox for digital assets, which can facilitate banking relationships. Compliance is a critical aspect of maintaining a Singapore crypto license. Licensees must adhere to strict AML/CFT requirements, including customer due diligence (CDD), transaction monitoring, and suspicious transaction reporting. The MAS conducts regular inspections and can impose penalties for non-compliance. Building trust with regulators and clients requires a strong compliance framework. Common Mistakes to Avoid

Many applicants make avoidable errors that delay or derail their license application. Common mistakes include:

  • Incomplete documentation: Missing business plans, financial projections, or AML policies.
  • Underestimating capital requirements: Not having sufficient liquid capital at the time of application.
  • Poorly designed compliance framework: Policies that do not meet MAS standards.
  • Ignoring local director requirements: MAS may require at least one director who is a Singapore resident or has relevant experience.
  • Overlooking ongoing obligations: Failing to submit regular reports or maintain adequate records.

What is the Singapore crypto license called?
