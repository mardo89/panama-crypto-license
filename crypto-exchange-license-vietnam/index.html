Overview: Crypto Regulation in Vietnam

Vietnam has taken a cautious but increasingly engaged approach to cryptocurrency. The government recognizes blockchain's potential but has not yet enacted a comprehensive virtual asset law. As of 2026, crypto exchanges are not explicitly prohibited, but they operate in a grey area. The State Bank of Vietnam (SBV) has stated that cryptocurrencies are not legal tender, and the government discourages their use as a payment method. However, trading and investment are not illegal.

For a crypto exchange license Vietnam, the primary legal basis is the Enterprise Law, which allows companies to register business lines such as "financial services" or "investment advisory" that may encompass crypto activities. Additionally, the Cybersecurity Law requires operators to store user data locally and obtain certain certifications. Consulting24 advises clients that while a dedicated license does not exist, proper company setup and compliance with anti-money laundering (AML) regulations are essential.

Who Needs a Crypto Exchange License in Vietnam?

Any entity planning to operate a cryptocurrency exchange platform serving Vietnamese residents needs to consider the local regulatory requirements. This includes both domestic startups and foreign companies looking to tap into Vietnam's growing crypto market. Specifically, the following entities typically require a compliant setup:

  • Centralized exchanges (CEX) offering spot trading, margin trading, or futures.
  • Decentralized exchanges (DEX) that have a legal entity in Vietnam or target Vietnamese users.
  • Over-the-counter (OTC) desks facilitating large trades.
  • Brokerage platforms that match buyers and sellers.
  • Any platform that holds customer funds or facilitates crypto-to-fiat conversions.

Foreign operators often prefer to establish a Vietnamese subsidiary to comply with local data localization requirements. Consulting24 advises and coordinates with local legal experts to determine the best corporate structure.

License Type & Regulator

Vietnam does not issue a specific "crypto license" but rather requires companies to register under the Law on Enterprises with appropriate business codes. The key regulators are:

  • State Bank of Vietnam (SBV): Oversees monetary policy and payment systems. It has warned against using crypto as payment but does not directly license exchanges.
  • Ministry of Finance (MoF): Regulates securities and investment activities. It is expected to be the main regulator for any future crypto framework.
  • Ministry of Public Security (MPS): Enforces cybersecurity and data protection laws.

Currently, the government is piloting a regulatory sandbox for crypto exchanges, but it has not been fully implemented. For international operators, consulting24 advises using a fully regulated jurisdiction like Panama or Lithuania to serve Vietnamese clients, as these offer clear licensing paths.

Capital Requirement

Vietnam does not prescribe a minimum capital requirement specifically for crypto exchanges. However, under the Enterprise Law, the company's charter capital must be sufficient to cover its operational scale. In practice, the Ministry of Planning and Investment may require a minimum charter capital of around VND 10-20 billion (approximately EUR 380,000 - 760,000) for financial services companies. This is a guideline and not a strict rule.

For foreign investors, a higher capital is often expected to demonstrate commitment. Consulting24 advises that capital should be at least EUR 100,000 to be credible. For a more predictable framework, many founders opt for jurisdictions like Lithuania where minimum capital is EUR 125,000 for exchange services.

Tax Treatment for Crypto Exchanges in Vietnam

Vietnam's tax regime for crypto is still evolving. As of 2026, there is no specific tax law for cryptocurrencies. However, the general tax rules apply:

  • Corporate Income Tax (CIT): Standard rate of 20% on profits. Crypto trading profits are considered taxable income.
  • Value Added Tax (VAT): Crypto transactions are generally exempt from VAT, but exchange fees may be subject to 10% VAT.
  • Personal Income Tax (PIT): Individuals trading crypto are not explicitly taxed on capital gains, but the government has proposed a 0.1% transaction tax.

Given the ambiguity, many exchanges structure their operations to minimize tax exposure. Consulting24 recommends seeking local tax advice. For a clearer tax environment, Panama offers 0% tax on offshore income.

Allowed Activities Under a Vietnamese Crypto Exchange Setup

While there is no explicit license, the following activities are generally permissible under the current legal framework:

  • Operating a cryptocurrency exchange platform for trading digital assets.
  • Providing wallet services for storing cryptocurrencies.
  • Offering peer-to-peer (P2P) trading services.
  • Facilitating initial coin offerings (ICOs) or token sales (with caution).
  • Providing crypto-to-crypto trading pairs.

Activities that are restricted or prohibited include:

  • Using crypto as payment for goods and services (SBV prohibits).
  • Issuing securities tokens without a securities license.
  • Operating without proper data localization and cybersecurity measures.

Consulting24 advises that the grey nature of regulation means operators must stay updated. Many choose to serve Vietnamese users from a licensed entity in a more regulated jurisdiction.

Step-by-Step Process to Set Up a Crypto Exchange in Vietnam

Setting up a compliant crypto exchange in Vietnam involves several steps. Consulting24 coordinates with local partners to streamline the process:

  1. Company Registration: Incorporate a company under the Law on Enterprises with a registered address and charter capital. Business codes should include "financial services" or "investment activities".
  2. Cybersecurity Compliance: Implement data localization by storing user data on servers within Vietnam. Obtain a cybersecurity assessment certificate from the Department of Information Security.
  3. AML/KYC Program: Develop and implement anti-money laundering policies, including customer due diligence and transaction monitoring. Register with the State Bank's AML department.
  4. Bank Account Opening: Open a corporate bank account with a Vietnamese bank. This can be challenging for crypto companies; some banks refuse service.
  5. Tax Registration: Register for corporate income tax and VAT. Obtain a tax code.
  6. Ongoing Compliance: File periodic reports, conduct audits, and update AML policies.

The entire process typically takes 2-4 months. For a faster, more certain route, consider Panama where the process takes 4-6 weeks.

Banking & Payment Integration

Banking is one of the biggest challenges for crypto exchanges in Vietnam. Most local banks are reluctant to work with crypto companies due to regulatory uncertainty. However, some banks like TPBank and OCB have shown openness. Foreign-owned banks are generally more restrictive.

Payment integration for fiat on-ramps/off-ramps often requires partnerships with payment intermediaries that have banking relationships. Options include using third-party payment processors or setting up a separate entity for payment services. Consulting24 advises that this is a critical area where local expertise is invaluable. Alternatively, using a licensed entity in a crypto-friendly jurisdiction like Panama can simplify banking.

Operating a crypto exchange in Vietnam requires a proactive compliance approach. Key areas include:

  • AML/CFT: Implement strong AML procedures. The State Bank can impose sanctions for non-compliance.
  • Consumer Protection: Ensure transparent fee structures and dispute resolution mechanisms.
  • Regular Audits: Conduct annual audits to demonstrate financial integrity.

Common Mistakes When Pursuing a Crypto Exchange License Vietnam

Founders often make these errors:

  • Assuming a License Exists: Many expect a formal license like in Singapore or Malta. Vietnam does not have one yet.
  • Ignoring Data Localization: Failing to store data locally can lead to website blocking or fines.
  • Underestimating Banking Hurdles: Opening a bank account is difficult; planning ahead is crucial.
  • Neglecting AML Compliance: Even without a license, AML laws apply. Non-compliance can result in criminal liability.
  • Choosing the Wrong Business Code: The business registration must accurately reflect activities to avoid legal issues.

Frequently asked questions

Is a crypto exchange license required in Vietnam?

As of 2026, Vietnam does not issue a specific crypto exchange license. Operators must register a company under the Enterprise Law and comply with cybersecurity and AML regulations. The government is piloting a sandbox, but it is not yet operational.

How long does it take to register a crypto exchange in Vietnam?

The process usually takes 2-4 months from company incorporation to full compliance. This timeline can vary based on the complexity of your operations and the responsiveness of local authorities.

What is the minimum capital for a crypto exchange in Vietnam?

There is no legally mandated minimum capital for crypto exchanges. However, a charter capital of at least VND 10-20 billion (EUR 380,000-760,000) is often expected for financial services. Consulting24 recommends a minimum of EUR 100,000 for credibility.

Can foreigners own a crypto exchange in Vietnam?

Yes, foreigners can own 100% of a company in most sectors, including technology services. However, certain business lines may require a local partner. It is advisable to consult with local legal experts.
