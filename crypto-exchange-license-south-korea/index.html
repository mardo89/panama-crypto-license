What Is a South Korea Crypto Exchange License?

A South Korea crypto exchange license is a mandatory registration with the Korea Financial Intelligence Unit (KoFIU) under the Financial Services Commission (FSC). It allows a business to operate a virtual asset exchange, including trading of cryptocurrencies like Bitcoin and Ethereum, as well as token listings. The license is governed by the Act on Reporting and Use of Specific Financial Transaction Information (often called the Specific Financial Information Act).

Key features of the license:

  • Required for all virtual asset service providers (VASPs) offering exchange, wallet, or custody services.
  • Mandatory real-name account linkage with a Korean bank for all users.
  • Stringent AML and counter-terrorism financing (CTF) obligations.
  • Annual reporting and compliance audits.

Without this license, operating a crypto exchange in South Korea is illegal and can lead to severe penalties, including criminal charges. The FSC and KoFIU actively enforce the rules, and several exchanges have been shut down for non-compliance.

Who Needs a South Korea Crypto Exchange License?

Any entity that provides virtual asset trading services to South Korean residents must obtain the license. This includes:

  • Centralized exchanges (CEX) offering spot trading, margin trading, or derivatives.
  • Decentralized exchanges (DEX) if they have a Korean presence or target Korean users.
  • Over-the-counter (OTC) desks and peer-to-peer (P2P) platforms.
  • Wallet providers and custodians that hold private keys for users.

Foreign exchanges that onboard Korean users or offer services in Korean language also fall under the scope. The FSC has clarified that even non-resident entities must register if they have a Korean user base. License Type and Regulator

The South Korea crypto exchange license is technically a registration with the Korea Financial Intelligence Unit (KoFIU), which operates under the Financial Services Commission (FSC). The FSC is the primary financial regulator, while KoFIU handles AML/CTF supervision. There is no separate license for different service types; all VASPs register under the same framework, but the scope of activities must be disclosed.

As of 2026, the FSC has not implemented a tiered capital system like the EU's MiCA. Instead, the capital requirement is a fixed amount (see next section). The regulator conducts rigorous background checks on shareholders, directors, and key personnel. The registration is valid indefinitely, subject to annual renewal of the AML program and compliance reports.

For comparison, other jurisdictions like Panama offer a simpler registration process, but South Korea provides access to one of the largest crypto markets in Asia.

Capital Requirement

As of 2026, the minimum capital requirement for a crypto exchange license in South Korea is KRW 2 billion (approximately USD 1.5 million). This capital must be fully paid up and held in a Korean bank account. The requirement is set by the FSC to ensure financial stability and consumer protection.

Additional capital may be required based on the volume of transactions or the number of users. The FSC can impose higher capital thresholds for exchanges that offer margin trading or derivatives. The capital must be maintained at all times; falling below the threshold can result in suspension or revocation of the license.

For comparison, Panama has no minimum capital requirement for crypto licenses, making it a more accessible option for startups. However, South Korea's capital requirement reflects the market's maturity and regulatory rigor.

Tax Treatment

South Korea has a comprehensive tax framework for crypto exchanges. As of 2026, the following tax rules apply:

  • Corporate income tax: Standard rate of 9% to 24% (progressive) on net profits from exchange operations.
  • Value-added tax (VAT): 10% on commission fees charged to users. Crypto trading itself is VAT-exempt.
  • Withholding tax: 20% on dividends and interest paid to foreign investors, subject to tax treaties.
  • Capital gains tax on crypto trading: 20% on gains exceeding KRW 2.5 million per year (applicable to individual traders, but exchanges must report).

Exchanges must also comply with tax reporting requirements, including issuing tax invoices and submitting annual financial statements. The National Tax Service (NTS) actively audits crypto exchanges. Consulting24 advises on tax structuring to optimize your liability within legal boundaries.

Allowed Activities

A South Korea crypto exchange license permits the following activities:

  • Operation of a virtual asset exchange for spot trading of cryptocurrencies.
  • Provision of wallet services (custodial and non-custodial).
  • Token listing and delisting, subject to FSC guidelines.
  • Margin trading and futures (with additional regulatory approval).
  • OTC trading and P2P platform services.

Activities that are not allowed include:

  • Offering interest-bearing accounts (unless licensed as a bank).
  • Operating as a decentralized autonomous organization (DAO) without a legal entity.
  • Any service that violates the Specific Financial Information Act, such as anonymous trading.

The FSC may impose additional conditions on a case-by-case basis. For example, some exchanges are restricted from listing certain tokens deemed securities. Consulting24 helps you define your business scope to align with regulatory expectations.

Step-by-Step Application Process

Obtaining a South Korea crypto exchange license involves several stages. Consulting24 advises and coordinates each step with local partners.

  1. Company incorporation: Set up a Korean corporation (usually a limited liability company) with a physical office in South Korea. The company must have a local director and shareholder.
  2. Bank account opening: Open a corporate bank account with a Korean bank. This is critical for the real-name account linkage required for users.
  3. AML program development: Draft a comprehensive AML/CTF policy, including customer due diligence (CDD), transaction monitoring, and suspicious transaction reporting.
  4. Information security management system (ISMS): Obtain ISMS certification from the Korea Internet & Security Agency (KISA). This is a mandatory technical security audit.
  5. Submit application to KoFIU: File the registration application with all supporting documents, including business plan, AML policy, ISMS certificate, and financial statements.
  6. Review and inspection: KoFIU reviews the application and may conduct on-site inspections. The process can take 3-6 months.
  7. Approval and registration: Once approved, the exchange is registered as a VASP and can begin operations.

Post-approval, the exchange must submit quarterly reports and undergo annual audits. Non-compliance can lead to fines or license revocation.

Banking and Payment Integration

One of the biggest challenges for crypto exchanges in South Korea is securing a banking partnership for real-name accounts. The FSC mandates that all users must have a real-name account with a Korean bank linked to their exchange account. Without this, the exchange cannot operate legally.

Major Korean banks (Kookmin, Shinhan, Woori, Hana) have been cautious about partnering with crypto exchanges due to AML risks. As of 2026, only a few exchanges have secured such partnerships, often requiring a significant security deposit (up to KRW 10 billion) and a rigorous compliance review.

Alternative payment methods include:

  • Integration with Korean payment gateways (e.g., KakaoPay, Naver Pay) for fiat deposits and withdrawals.
  • Use of stablecoins or tokenized deposits for on-ramp/off-ramp.

Compliance and Ongoing Obligations

Once licensed, exchanges must maintain rigorous compliance. Key ongoing obligations include:

  • Real-time transaction monitoring and reporting of suspicious transactions to KoFIU.
  • Annual AML program review and update.
  • External audit of financial statements and AML compliance.
  • ISMS recertification every three years.
  • Reporting changes in shareholders, directors, or business scope to KoFIU.

Non-compliance can result in fines up to KRW 50 million (approx. USD 40,000) or imprisonment for serious violations. The FSC has the power to suspend or revoke licenses. Common Mistakes to Avoid

Many applicants fail due to avoidable errors. Common mistakes include:

  • Incomplete AML documentation: The AML policy must be detailed and tailored to the exchange's specific risks.
  • Underestimating capital requirements: The KRW 2 billion minimum is strictly enforced; some exchanges need more.
  • Ignoring the real-name account requirement: Without a bank partnership, the license is useless.
  • Poor ISMS preparation: The technical security audit is thorough; many fail on the first attempt.
  • Choosing the wrong legal structure: A foreign entity cannot directly hold a license; a local subsidiary is required.

Frequently asked questions

What is the minimum capital for a crypto exchange license in South Korea?

As of 2026, the minimum capital is KRW 2 billion (approximately USD 1.5 million). This must be fully paid up and held in a Korean bank account. Additional capital may be required based on transaction volume or services offered.

How long does it take to get a South Korea crypto exchange license?

The typical timeline is 6 to 12 months from company incorporation to final registration. Delays can occur due to incomplete documentation, security audit failures, or difficulty securing a banking partnership.

Can a foreign company apply for a South Korea crypto exchange license?

Yes, but the foreign company must establish a local subsidiary in South Korea. The subsidiary must have a local director and shareholder. The license is issued to the Korean entity, not the foreign parent.

What are the tax rates for crypto exchanges in South Korea?

Corporate income tax is progressive from 9% to 24%. VAT of 10% applies on commission fees. Individual traders pay 20% capital gains tax on crypto profits exceeding KRW 2.5 million annually. Exchanges must comply with tax reporting.

Is a real-name bank account mandatory for users?

Yes, the FSC requires that all users have a real-name account with a Korean bank linked to their exchange account. This is a key AML measure and a major hurdle for new exchanges.

What is the ISMS certification and why is it required?

ISMS (Information Security Management System) certification from KISA is a mandatory technical audit that ensures the exchange meets security standards. It covers data protection, system integrity, and incident response.

Can I operate a DEX under the South Korea license?

Yes, but only if the DEX has a legal entity and complies with all AML obligations. Fully decentralized platforms without a central operator may not be eligible. The FSC evaluates each case individually.

What happens if I operate without a license in South Korea?

Operating without a license is illegal and can result in criminal penalties, including imprisonment of up to 5 years and fines of up to KRW 50 million. The FSC actively monitors and shuts down unlicensed exchanges.

How does the South Korea license compare to Panama?

Panama has no capital requirement, lower costs (EUR 6,000 flat), and a faster timeline (2-3 months). However, South Korea offers access to a large local market and regulatory clarity. Panama is better for global operations without a local focus.
