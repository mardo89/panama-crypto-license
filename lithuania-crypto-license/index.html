This license allows you to passport your services across all EEA member states, giving you access to a market of over 450 million consumers.

What Is the Lithuania Crypto License (MiCAR CASP)?

The Lithuania crypto license, officially a MiCAR CASP authorization, is a regulatory permit issued by the Bank of Lithuania. It allows firms to provide crypto-asset services legally within Lithuania and across the European Economic Area (EEA) under the EU passporting regime. MiCAR, fully in force since 2026, replaced national regimes and introduced harmonized standards for capital, governance, and consumer protection.

Key features of the license:

  • Regulated by the Bank of Lithuania (BoL)
  • Passportable to all 30 EEA countries
  • Minimum share capital from EUR 50,000 to EUR 150,000 depending on activities
  • Requires local substance: a director, compliance officer, and physical office in Lithuania
  • Subject to ongoing supervision, audits, and reporting

Compared to other EU jurisdictions, Lithuania offers a relatively fast and cost-effective process while maintaining high regulatory standards. For a comparison with other options, see our jurisdictions overview.

Who Needs a Lithuania Crypto License?

A Lithuania crypto license is essential for any business planning to offer crypto-asset services to EU residents. Under MiCAR, the following activities require authorization:

  • Custody and administration of crypto-assets on behalf of clients
  • Operation of a crypto-asset trading platform
  • Exchange of crypto-assets for funds or other crypto-assets
  • Execution of orders for crypto-assets on behalf of clients
  • Placing of crypto-assets
  • Reception and transmission of orders for crypto-assets
  • Advice on crypto-assets
  • Portfolio management of crypto-assets

If your business involves any of these activities and you target EU clients, you need a MiCAR CASP license. Lithuania is an attractive choice due to its pragmatic regulator, established fintech ecosystem, and relatively quick processing times. Even if you are already licensed elsewhere, you may need a Lithuanian entity to passport into the EEA post-Brexit or if your home regime is not recognized under MiCAR.

License Type & Regulator

The Lithuania crypto license is a MiCAR Crypto-Asset Service Provider (CASP) authorization. The competent authority is the Bank of Lithuania (BoL), which also supervises payment institutions, electronic money institutions, and credit institutions. BoL is known for its innovation-friendly approach and clear regulatory expectations.

Under MiCAR, CASPs are classified by the services they provide, with corresponding capital requirements. The license is a single authorization covering all selected activities, but each activity must be explicitly applied for and approved. The BoL conducts a thorough assessment of the application, including fitness and propriety of management, operational resilience, and AML/CTF controls.

Once granted, the license allows passporting across the EEA without additional authorizations, making Lithuania a gateway to the entire European crypto market.

Capital Requirements

Under MiCAR, the minimum share capital for a Lithuania crypto license depends on the specific services offered. The capital must be held as paid-up share capital and maintained throughout the license period. The tiers are:

  • EUR 50,000 - for advice on crypto-assets, portfolio management, placing/listing, transfer, execution of orders, reception/transmission of orders
  • EUR 125,000 - for custody & administration of crypto-assets, exchanging crypto-assets for funds or other crypto-assets
  • EUR 150,000 - for operating a trading platform

If you apply for multiple activities, the highest applicable capital requirement applies. For example, if you offer custody and a trading platform, you need EUR 150,000. The capital must be in liquid assets (cash or equivalents) and cannot be used for operational expenses without BoL approval. This ensures the firm has a financial buffer to protect clients.

Tax Treatment for Crypto Businesses in Lithuania

Lithuania offers a competitive tax regime for crypto businesses. The standard corporate income tax rate is 15%, but a reduced rate of 0% applies to small companies (up to EUR 300,000 revenue and fewer than 10 employees) for their first year, and 5% thereafter. However, crypto firms often exceed these thresholds quickly.

Key tax points:

  • Profit from crypto-asset trading and services is subject to corporate income tax (15%)
  • VAT: Crypto-asset exchange is generally exempt from VAT under EU law
  • Withholding tax on dividends: 15% (reduced under tax treaties)
  • Transfer pricing rules apply for related-party transactions

Lithuania has a double tax treaty network with over 50 countries. It is advisable to structure your operations with local tax advice. For more details on costs, see our cost page.

Allowed Activities Under the Lithuania Crypto License

A Lithuania crypto license permits the following activities, as defined by MiCAR:

  • Custody and administration of crypto-assets - holding or controlling crypto-assets on behalf of clients
  • Operation of a trading platform - matching buyers and sellers of crypto-assets
  • Exchange of crypto-assets for funds - converting crypto to fiat and vice versa
  • Exchange of crypto-assets for other crypto-assets
  • Execution of orders - concluding contracts to buy or sell crypto-assets on behalf of clients
  • Placing of crypto-assets - underwriting or placing crypto-assets
  • Reception and transmission of orders - relaying client orders to third parties
  • Advice on crypto-assets - providing personalized recommendations
  • Portfolio management - managing crypto-asset portfolios on a discretionary basis

Each activity requires specific policies and procedures documented in the application. The BoL expects a clear business model and robust risk management for each service.

Banking & Payment Services for Crypto Firms

Access to traditional banking is a common challenge for crypto businesses. Lithuania has a relatively open banking environment, but compliance with AML/KYC requirements is strict. As a licensed CASP, you will need a business bank account to hold client funds (if applicable) and operational funds.

Options include:

  • Lithuanian banks (e.g., Swedbank, SEB, Luminor) - they accept licensed crypto firms but require thorough due diligence
  • EMI/PSP accounts - payment institutions like PayrNet or ConnectPay offer dedicated accounts for crypto firms
  • International banks with EU presence

We advise engaging a banking partner early in the process. The BoL expects you to have a clear plan for safeguarding client assets and handling fiat transactions. Our team can introduce you to banking partners who understand the crypto space.

Compliance & Trust: Ongoing Obligations

Once licensed, your firm must maintain ongoing compliance with MiCAR and BoL requirements. Key obligations include:

  • Annual external audit - Financial statements must be audited by a licensed auditor (cost ~EUR 10,000)
  • AML/CTF compliance - Ongoing transaction monitoring, suspicious activity reporting, and annual AML audit
  • Capital maintenance - Share capital must be kept at the required level; any reduction needs BoL approval
  • Reporting - Periodic reports to BoL on financials, risk, and operations
  • Information security - IT systems must meet EU financial institution standards; regular penetration testing
  • Staff requirements - Local director and compliance officer must be continuously employed; their qualifications and experience are subject to BoL scrutiny

Failure to comply can result in fines, suspension, or revocation of the license. Consulting24 provides ongoing compliance support to ensure you meet all obligations. General guidance, not legal advice.

Common Mistakes When Applying for a Lithuania Crypto License

Many applicants face delays or rejections due to avoidable errors. Here are the most common pitfalls:

  • Inadequate documentation - Policies must be detailed and tailored to your business; generic templates are often rejected
  • Weak local substance - The director and compliance officer must have relevant degrees and experience; hiring unqualified individuals is a red flag
  • Unrealistic business plan - Financial projections must be credible and based on sound assumptions
  • Lack of AML/CTF controls - The BoL expects robust procedures for customer due diligence, transaction monitoring, and reporting
  • Ignoring IT security standards - Your systems must meet EU financial institution standards; a simple website won't suffice
  • Underestimating timeline - While BoL aims for 2-4 months, delays can occur if documents are incomplete

Frequently asked questions

What is the minimum capital for a Lithuania crypto license?

The minimum share capital ranges from EUR 50,000 to EUR 150,000 depending on the services offered. EUR 50,000 for advisory and order transmission, EUR 125,000 for custody and exchange, and EUR 150,000 for operating a trading platform.

How long does it take to get a Lithuania crypto license?

The process takes approximately 7-8 weeks to prepare the company and documentation, followed by 2-4 months for Bank of Lithuania authorization. Total time is typically 4-6 months from engagement.

Can I passport a Lithuania crypto license to other EU countries?

Yes, under MiCAR, a Lithuania CASP license is passportable across all 30 EEA member states without additional authorizations.

What are the ongoing costs for a Lithuania crypto license?

Ongoing costs include staff salaries (approx. EUR 6,000-6,500/month for director and compliance officer), annual external audit (~EUR 10,000), IT/SaaS costs, and office rent. Total annual compliance costs are around EUR 80,000-100,000.

Do I need a local director and compliance officer in Lithuania?

Yes, local substance is mandatory. The director must have a degree in Law/Economics or MBA/BBA and 1-2 years senior experience. The compliance officer needs a degree in Economics or MBA/BBA, AML/CTF certification, and 1+ year compliance experience.

What documents are required for a Lithuania crypto license application?

Required documents include: Program of Operations, Business Plan, Governance policies, Business Continuity Framework, Risk Management Framework, AML/CTF Policies, Information Security Framework, Compliance Handling Procedure, Accounting Policy, and activity-specific policies.

Is Lithuania a good jurisdiction for crypto startups?

Yes, Lithuania offers a relatively fast and cost-effective licensing process, a pragmatic regulator, and access to the EU market. It is particularly attractive for startups that can meet the capital and substance requirements.

What is the tax rate for crypto businesses in Lithuania?

The standard corporate income tax rate is 15%. Small companies may benefit from a 0% rate in the first year and 5% thereafter, but many crypto firms exceed the thresholds. VAT on crypto exchange is generally exempt.
