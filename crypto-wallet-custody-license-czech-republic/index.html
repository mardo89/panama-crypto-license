What Is the Crypto Wallet Custody License in Czech Republic?

The crypto wallet custody license in the Czech Republic is an authorisation granted by the Czech National Bank (CNB) to legal entities that wish to provide custody and administration of crypto assets for third parties. Under Czech law, this activity falls under the definition of a 'virtual asset service provider' (VASP) and requires registration with the CNB. Since MiCA came into full effect in 2025, the license also complies with EU-wide standards for crypto asset services.

Key features of the license include:

  • Permission to safekeep and administer clients' cryptographic keys and crypto assets.
  • Obligation to implement strong security measures, including cold storage and multi-signature wallets.
  • Compliance with anti-money laundering (AML) and counter-terrorism financing (CTF) regulations.
  • Regular reporting to the CNB on financial health and operational status.

This license is distinct from a general VASP registration because it specifically covers custody services, which are considered higher risk and subject to additional capital requirements. For a broader comparison of licensing options, see our jurisdictions overview.

Who Needs This License?

Any business that intends to hold or control crypto assets on behalf of clients in the Czech Republic or offer custody services to Czech residents must obtain this license. Typical applicants include:

  • Crypto wallet providers (hot and cold wallets).
  • Exchange platforms that offer custodial wallets.
  • Asset managers dealing with digital assets.
  • Traditional financial institutions expanding into crypto custody.

If your business model involves generating, storing, or managing private keys for clients, you fall under the custody definition. Even if you outsource key management, the ultimate responsibility lies with the license holder. For those considering alternatives, the Panama regime offers a simpler, lower-cost option, but it does not provide EU passporting rights.

License Type and Regulator

The regulator for crypto wallet custody in the Czech Republic is the Czech National Bank (Česká národní banka, CNB). The CNB oversees all VASPs under the Act on Selected Services in the Digital Economy (Zákon o vybraných službách v digitální ekonomice) and MiCA. The license type is a 'registration for custody of crypto assets', which is part of the broader VASP authorisation.

Unlike some EU jurisdictions that separate custody from other services, the Czech Republic treats custody as a distinct activity requiring its own capital and compliance framework. The CNB has a reputation for thorough but fair scrutiny, focusing on AML controls and consumer protection. For more on the application process, see our application process page.

Capital Requirement

Under MiCA, the capital requirement for crypto custody services in the Czech Republic is set at EUR 125,000. This is the minimum own funds that must be maintained at all times. The capital can be in the form of share capital, reserves, or subordinated loans, subject to CNB approval.

This requirement is higher than the EUR 50,000 for simple exchange services but lower than the EUR 150,000 for trading platforms. The capital must be unencumbered and available to cover operational risks, including potential losses from security breaches. For comparison, Panama has no minimum capital requirement, making it attractive for smaller firms.

Tax Treatment

The Czech Republic applies a corporate income tax rate of 19% to crypto-related activities, including custody fees. Capital gains from crypto assets held by the company are also subject to this rate. However, there is no specific crypto tax law; general tax principles apply.

Value-added tax (VAT) is generally not applied to crypto custody services, as they are considered exempt financial services under EU VAT rules. However, advisory or management fees may be subject to VAT at 21%. It is advisable to consult a tax specialist for your specific situation. For a jurisdiction with zero corporate tax, see Panama.

Allowed Activities Under the License

The crypto wallet custody license in the Czech Republic authorises the following activities:

  • Safekeeping and administration of crypto assets on behalf of clients.
  • Generation and storage of private keys.
  • Recovery of lost keys (subject to security protocols).
  • Provision of wallet software with custodial features.

It does not permit the operation of a crypto exchange, trading on own account, or providing investment advice, unless separately licensed. If you need to offer multiple services, you may require a broader VASP authorisation. For a full list of activities, refer to the CNB guidelines.

Compliance and Ongoing Obligations

After obtaining the license, you must maintain ongoing compliance with CNB regulations. Key obligations include:

  • Annual submission of audited financial statements.
  • Regular AML/CTF training for staff.
  • Periodic internal and external audits.
  • Reporting of suspicious transactions to the Financial Analytical Office (FAO).
  • Maintenance of minimum capital at all times.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Applicants often make the following errors when applying for a Czech crypto wallet custody license:

  • Insufficient AML documentation: The CNB expects detailed AML policies that cover all risk areas. Generic templates are rejected.
  • Underestimating capital requirements: The EUR 125,000 must be fully paid up and held in a Czech bank account before submission.
  • Poor IT security: Custody services require strong security measures. A weak audit report can delay or derail the application.
  • Incomplete ownership structure disclosure: The CNB requires full transparency on beneficial owners and source of funds.
  • Ignoring ongoing obligations: Post-license compliance is as important as the application itself. Non-compliance can lead to penalties.

Frequently asked questions

What is the capital requirement for a Czech crypto wallet custody license?

The minimum capital requirement is EUR 125,000, as per MiCA. This must be held in a Czech bank account and maintained at all times. Capital can be in share capital, reserves, or subordinated loans approved by the CNB.

How long does it take to get a Czech crypto custody license?

The typical timeline is 3 to 6 months from application submission to decision. Preparation can take an additional 2-3 months depending on your readiness. Consulting24 advises and coordinates to streamline the process.

Can I passport the Czech license across the EU?

Yes, under MiCA, a Czech crypto wallet custody license allows you to provide custody services across all EU member states without needing separate licenses. This is a key advantage of the Czech regime.

What activities are not allowed under the custody license?

The license does not permit operating a crypto exchange, trading on own account, or providing investment advice. These require separate authorisations under Czech law.

Is the Czech Republic tax-friendly for crypto custody?

Corporate income tax is 19% on profits. No VAT on custody services. However, advisory fees may be subject to 21% VAT. Panama offers zero corporate tax, but lacks EU passporting.

Do I need to incorporate a Czech company to apply?

Yes, you must have a legal entity registered in the Czech Republic, typically a s.r.o. (limited liability company) or a.s. (joint-stock company). Consulting24 can assist with incorporation.

What are the main compliance obligations after licensing?

Ongoing obligations include annual audited financial statements, AML training, periodic audits, suspicious transaction reporting to the FAO, and maintaining minimum capital. Non-compliance can lead to fines or revocation.

Can I outsource key management and still hold the license?

Yes, but the ultimate responsibility for custody remains with the license holder. You must ensure your outsourcing partner meets CNB security and compliance standards.
