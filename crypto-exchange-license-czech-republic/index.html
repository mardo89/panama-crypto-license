What is the Czech Republic Crypto Exchange License?

The Czech Republic's crypto exchange license is technically a registration as a Virtual Asset Service Provider (VASP) under Act No. 253/2008 Coll., on Selected Measures against Legitimisation of Proceeds of Crime and Financing of Terrorism (AML Act). This registration is mandatory for any entity providing exchange services between virtual assets and fiat currencies, or between virtual assets themselves, as well as for custodial wallet services. The regulator is the Czech National Bank (CNB), which maintains a register of authorized VASPs.

This regime is not a full MiCA license but is compliant with the EU's 5th AML Directive. As of 2026, MiCA is fully in force across the EU, and the Czech Republic is in the process of transposing it into national law. However, the current VASP registration remains valid and is expected to be transitioned into MiCA authorization over time. For founders, this means a relatively accessible entry point with clear requirements. The registration is a prerequisite for any crypto business operating in or from the Czech Republic, and it provides a legal basis for offering exchange and custody services.

Who Needs a Czech Republic Crypto License?

Any business that offers the following services to Czech residents or operates from Czech territory must obtain a VASP registration:

  • Exchange of virtual assets for fiat currency
  • Exchange of virtual assets for other virtual assets
  • Custodial wallet services (holding private keys on behalf of clients)
  • Transfer services of virtual assets
  • Participation in and provision of financial services related to an issuer's offer of virtual assets (if not covered by other exemptions)

This applies to both Czech-domiciled companies and foreign entities that target Czech customers. If you plan to operate a crypto exchange, an OTC desk, or a wallet provider, you need this registration. Even if your business is based outside the Czech Republic but actively solicits Czech clients, you may be required to register. The CNB has been increasingly active in enforcing these rules, so compliance is essential.

License Type and Regulator

The Czech Republic does not issue a single 'license' but rather a registration as a VASP. The competent authority is the Czech National Bank (CNB), which is the central bank and financial market supervisor. The registration is not a full prudential license but a mandatory AML registration. However, the CNB has the power to impose conditions and conduct inspections.

For companies that also engage in activities beyond simple exchange (e.g., operating a trading platform with order matching), additional authorization under the Capital Markets Act may be required. Capital Requirement

The Czech VASP registration does not have a statutory minimum capital requirement. However, the CNB expects the company to have sufficient financial resources to operate in compliance with AML obligations. In practice, many applicants demonstrate capital of at least EUR 20,000 to cover operational costs and compliance infrastructure. For companies that also require a MiCA license (e.g., for operating a trading platform), capital tiers apply: EUR 50,000 for simple exchange services, EUR 125,000 for exchange and custody, and EUR 150,000 for more complex services. As of 2026, MiCA is fully in force, so if your activities fall under MiCA, the capital requirements will apply.

It is important to assess your business model early. If you plan to offer only simple exchange services without custody, the lower capital tier may suffice. Consulting24 can help you determine which capital requirement applies to your specific case.

Tax Treatment

Czech tax law treats income from crypto transactions as either capital gains (for individuals) or business income (for companies). The corporate income tax rate is 19% for most companies. Crypto-to-crypto exchanges are taxable events, and VAT does not apply to exchange services (following EU VAT rules). The Czech Republic has a relatively favorable tax environment compared to some EU peers. However, specific tax advice is recommended, as tax treatment can vary based on the nature of the activity (e.g., trading vs mining).

For example, if your company holds crypto as inventory for trading, gains are taxed as ordinary income. If you hold crypto as an investment, capital gains rules apply. The Czech tax authorities have issued guidance clarifying that crypto transactions are subject to standard tax rules. Consulting24 can connect you with local tax advisors for a detailed analysis.

Allowed Activities

With a Czech VASP registration, you can legally provide the following services:

  • Exchange of virtual assets for fiat currency (and vice versa)
  • Exchange of virtual assets for other virtual assets
  • Custodial wallet services
  • Transfer of virtual assets (e.g., sending on behalf of clients)
  • OTC trading desk operations
  • ATM operations (subject to additional AML requirements)

Activities that require a full investment firm license (e.g., operating a multilateral trading facility) are not covered by the VASP registration and would need separate authorization. Step-by-Step Application Process

  1. Company incorporation: Set up a Czech limited liability company (s.r.o.) with a registered office in the Czech Republic. The company must have a beneficial owner identified.
  2. AML compliance preparation: Draft an AML manual, risk assessment, internal policies, and procedures. Appoint an AML officer (can be external).
  3. Submit application: File the VASP registration application with the CNB via the official electronic system. Include all required documents (company deed, AML policies, identification of beneficial owners, etc.).
  4. CNB review: The CNB reviews the application and may request additional information. This stage typically takes 2-4 months.
  5. Registration: Upon approval, the company is entered into the CNB's register of VASPs. A registration certificate is issued.
  6. Ongoing compliance: Submit annual AML reports, maintain records, and ensure continuous compliance with AML obligations.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Underestimating AML requirements: The CNB expects detailed policies, not just boilerplate documents.
  • Incomplete beneficial ownership disclosure: All ultimate beneficial owners must be clearly identified.
  • Ignoring MiCA implications: If your activities exceed simple exchange, you may need a full MiCA license.
  • Delaying bank account opening: Start the banking process early, as it can take months.
  • Operating without registration: Providing crypto services without VASP registration is illegal and can lead to severe penalties.

Alternatives and Comparisons

Compared to other jurisdictions, the Czech Republic offers a balanced approach. Frequently asked questions

What is the difference between a Czech VASP registration and a MiCA license?

A Czech VASP registration is an AML-based registration under national law, while a MiCA license is a full EU-wide authorization with prudential requirements. As of 2026, MiCA is fully in force, and the Czech Republic is transitioning VASP registrations to MiCA. Currently, VASP registration is sufficient for simple exchange and custody services, but for more complex activities, a MiCA license may be required.

Do I need a physical office in the Czech Republic?

Yes, you need a registered office address in the Czech Republic. This can be a virtual office or a shared workspace, but it must be a physical location where official correspondence can be received. The CNB may inspect the premises, so a professional address is recommended.

Can I use the Czech license to serve customers in other EU countries?

Currently, the Czech VASP registration does not automatically allow passporting. However, once MiCA is fully transposed and your registration is converted to a MiCA license, you will be able to passport services across the EU. Until then, you may need to register separately in each target country.

What are the ongoing compliance costs after registration?

Ongoing costs include AML officer fees (if external), annual reporting, transaction monitoring software, and legal updates. These typically range from EUR 5,000 to EUR 15,000 per year, depending on the complexity of your operations. Consulting24 offers ongoing compliance packages.

How long does the CNB take to process a VASP application?

The CNB typically takes 2-4 months to review a complete application. However, if additional information is requested, the process can extend to 6 months. Submitting a well-prepared application with all required documents can help expedite the process.

Is there a minimum capital requirement for a Czech VASP?

No statutory minimum capital requirement exists for VASP registration. However, the CNB expects the company to have sufficient funds to operate. In practice, demonstrating at least EUR 20,000 in capital is common. If your activities require a MiCA license, capital tiers of EUR 50,000, 125,000, or 150,000 apply.

Can a foreign company apply for a Czech VASP registration?

Yes, but the company must have a branch or subsidiary in the Czech Republic with a registered office. The application must be submitted by the Czech entity. Consulting24 can help set up the Czech company and coordinate the application.

What happens if I operate without a VASP registration?

Operating without registration is illegal and can result in fines, criminal charges, and seizure of assets. The CNB actively monitors the market and can impose penalties of up to CZK 5 million (approx. EUR 200,000) for non-compliance.

Does the Czech Republic tax crypto-to-crypto trades?

Yes, crypto-to-crypto trades are taxable events. The gain or loss is calculated based on the fair market value at the time of trade. Corporate income tax of 19% applies to profits. VAT is not charged on exchange services.

Can I convert my Czech VASP registration to a MiCA license later?

Yes, the Czech government is expected to provide a transition mechanism. Once MiCA is fully transposed, existing VASP registrations will be converted to MiCA licenses, subject to meeting additional requirements such as capital and governance standards.
