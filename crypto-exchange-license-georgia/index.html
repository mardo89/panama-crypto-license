What Is the Georgia Crypto Exchange License?

The Georgia crypto exchange license is a regulatory authorisation issued by the National Bank of Georgia (NBG) under the Law on the Facilitation of the Development of the Capital Market and the Law on Anti-Money Laundering. It allows companies to provide virtual asset exchange services, including trading between crypto and fiat, crypto-to-crypto exchanges, and custodial wallet services. The regime applies to both legal entities registered in Georgia and foreign companies establishing a local presence.

Georgia's approach is principles-based, focusing on AML/CFT compliance rather than prescriptive capital rules. This makes it an attractive option for startups and established firms alike. The license is valid for an indefinite period, subject to ongoing supervision by the NBG. Unlike EU member states under MiCA, Georgia does not impose capital tiers based on service class. Instead, the NBG evaluates each applicant's risk profile and operational plan. This flexibility can be advantageous for smaller firms that might struggle with the EUR 50,000 minimum capital required in the EU for certain services.

For example, a crypto-to-crypto exchange with no fiat handling may face lower scrutiny than a full fiat on-ramp. The NBG also considers the applicant's experience, the strength of AML controls, and the transparency of beneficial ownership. Who Needs a Georgia Crypto License?

Any business offering virtual asset services in or from Georgia must obtain a license from the NBG. This includes:

  • Crypto exchanges (fiat-to-crypto and crypto-to-crypto)
  • Over-the-counter (OTC) trading desks
  • Custodial wallet providers
  • Payment processors using virtual assets
  • Brokerage services for digital assets
  • ATMs that exchange virtual assets

Foreign companies targeting the Georgian market or serving Georgian residents also fall under the licensing obligation. Even if your operations are remote, you may need a license if you have a physical presence or target customers in Georgia. License Type & Regulator

The sole regulator for crypto licensing in Georgia is the National Bank of Georgia (NBG). The license is categorised as a Virtual Asset Service Provider (VASP) license. There is no distinction between exchange and wallet licenses; the same authorisation covers both activities. The NBG oversees compliance with AML/CFT regulations, consumer protection, and operational standards.

Unlike EU member states under MiCA, Georgia does not impose capital tiers based on service class. Instead, the NBG evaluates each applicant's risk profile and operational plan. This flexibility can be advantageous for smaller firms that might struggle with the EUR 50,000 minimum capital required in the EU. The NBG also does not require a local office or director, though having a local representative can facilitate the process. The regulator is known for its pragmatic approach, but it expects high standards of AML compliance. Consulting24 has a strong track record of preparing applications that satisfy NBG requirements.

Capital Requirement

Georgia does not impose a statutory minimum capital requirement for crypto license applicants. However, the NBG expects the company to have sufficient financial resources to cover operational costs and AML compliance for at least the first year. In practice, most applicants maintain a capital of around EUR 10,000 to EUR 20,000 to demonstrate financial stability.

This is a significant advantage over EU jurisdictions where minimum capital starts at EUR 50,000 (for exchange services under MiCA). For startups and small exchanges, Georgia offers a lower barrier to entry while still maintaining regulatory oversight. The capital can be held in a Georgian bank account or a reputable foreign bank. Tax Treatment

Georgia offers a highly favourable tax environment for crypto businesses. Corporate income tax is 15%, but only on distributed profits (retained earnings are tax-free). Additionally, transactions involving virtual assets are exempt from VAT (0% rate). Personal income tax on crypto gains for individuals is 20%, but corporate entities can benefit from the retained earnings exemption.

There is no specific crypto tax law; general tax principles apply. The NBG and the Revenue Service have issued guidance confirming that crypto-to-crypto trades are not subject to VAT. This clarity makes Georgia one of the most tax-efficient jurisdictions for crypto exchanges in Europe. For example, if your exchange earns EUR 1 million in trading fees and reinvests all profits, you pay zero corporate tax. Only when you distribute dividends does the 15% tax apply. This is a powerful incentive for growth-stage companies.

Compared to Panama, which also offers 0% tax on offshore income, Georgia requires a physical presence but provides a clear regulatory framework. In contrast, EU countries like Lithuania impose 15% corporate tax on all profits and 21% VAT on fees, making Georgia more attractive for tax planning.

Allowed Activities

Under the Georgia VASP license, you are permitted to conduct the following activities:

  • Exchange between virtual assets and fiat currencies
  • Exchange between one or more forms of virtual assets
  • Transfer of virtual assets
  • Custodial wallet services
  • Participation in and provision of financial services related to an issuer's offer or sale of a virtual asset

The license does not cover derivatives, securities tokens (unless separately regulated), or banking services. If you plan to offer margin trading or staking, additional analysis is needed. The NBG may require a separate license for security tokens if they qualify as securities under Georgian law. For staking, if it involves custody, it is generally covered. Step-by-Step Application Process

The process to obtain a Georgia crypto license involves several stages. Consulting24 coordinates each step to ensure a smooth application.

  1. Company incorporation - Register a legal entity in Georgia (LLC is typical). This takes 1-2 weeks. You will need a local registered address and a director (can be a foreigner).
  2. Document preparation - Draft AML/KYC policies, business plan, risk assessment, and internal controls. This requires 2-4 weeks. The AML policy must align with FATF recommendations and include customer due diligence, transaction monitoring, and reporting procedures.
  3. Submission to NBG - File the application with supporting documents. The NBG reviews within 30 days, but may request additional information. Common requests include clarification on source of funds for beneficial owners and proof of technical infrastructure.
  4. NBG review & approval - The regulator conducts a thorough assessment, including background checks on beneficial owners. This phase takes 1-2 months. The NBG may also interview the compliance officer.
  5. License issuance - Once approved, the license is issued. You can then commence operations. You must also register with the Revenue Service for tax purposes.

Total timeline: 2-4 months. Consulting24 handles all communication with the NBG to expedite the process. We also provide templates for AML policies and business plans to speed up preparation.

Compliance & Ongoing Obligations

After obtaining the license, you must comply with ongoing AML/CFT requirements. This includes appointing a local AML officer, conducting regular risk assessments, and reporting suspicious transactions to the Financial Monitoring Service of Georgia. Annual audits and financial reporting to the NBG are also mandatory. The NBG may conduct on-site inspections to verify compliance.

Failure to comply can result in fines or license revocation. For example, missing a suspicious transaction report deadline can lead to a penalty of up to GEL 50,000 (approx. EUR 15,000). Many applicants face delays or rejections due to avoidable errors. Common pitfalls include:

  • Unclear source of funds for beneficial owners - Provide full documentation, including bank statements and proof of wealth.
  • Underestimating the time needed for document preparation - Start early and allocate at least 4 weeks for this phase.
