What Is the Gibraltar Crypto Token Issuance License?

The Gibraltar crypto token issuance license is a regulatory authorisation granted by the GFSC under the Financial Services (Distributed Ledger Technology Providers) Regulations 2017. It allows firms to issue tokens, whether utility, security, or payment tokens, in compliance with local laws. The license is part of the broader DLT Provider regime, which covers activities like operating a DLT exchange, providing custody, and facilitating token transactions.

Gibraltar's approach is principles-based. Firms must adhere to nine principles, including honesty, integrity, adequate financial resources, and effective risk management. This gives issuers flexibility but demands a high standard of governance. The license is suitable for projects that want a regulated environment without the rigidity of prescriptive rules.

Key features include:

  • Regulated by the GFSC, a respected EU (pre-Brexit) and now UK-aligned regulator.
  • No specific maximum token supply or lock-up period, but the GFSC reviews each project individually.
  • Requires a physical presence in Gibraltar, including a registered office and at least one director resident in Gibraltar.
  • Annual compliance obligations, including audited financial statements and AML reporting.

Who Needs This License?

This license is for any entity planning to issue a crypto token to the public or to investors. Typical applicants include:

  • Startups conducting a token generation event (TGE) or initial coin offering (ICO).
  • Established businesses migrating to a tokenised model, such as security token offerings (STOs).
  • Decentralised finance (DeFi) projects that issue governance tokens.
  • Non-fungible token (NFT) platforms issuing fungible tokens alongside NFTs.

If you are a project with a global user base, Gibraltar's reputation as a well-regulated jurisdiction adds credibility. However, if your token is purely a utility token with no investment characteristics, you may still need a license if it is traded on a DLT exchange. The GFSC assesses each case on its merits. License Type & Regulator

The relevant license is the DLT Provider License (Class 1 for token issuance). The regulator is the Gibraltar Financial Services Commission (GFSC). The GFSC operates under the Financial Services (DLT Providers) Regulations 2017, which were updated in 2020 to include token issuance explicitly.

There are three classes of DLT Provider license:

  • Class 1: Token issuance and related services (minimum capital EUR 50,000).
  • Class 2: Custody and administration of tokens (minimum capital EUR 125,000).
  • Class 3: Operating a DLT exchange (minimum capital EUR 150,000).

Many firms apply for Class 1 plus Class 3 if they plan to list their own token. The GFSC also requires a designated individual (senior manager) to be approved for each class. Consulting24 coordinates the application process, ensuring all documentation meets GFSC standards.

Capital Requirement

The minimum capital for a Class 1 DLT Provider license (token issuance) is EUR 50,000. This must be held in a Gibraltar bank account or as a bond. The capital must be unencumbered and available to cover operational risks. For Class 2 and Class 3 licenses, the minimum capital is EUR 125,000 and EUR 150,000 respectively.

The GFSC may require additional capital based on the project's risk profile, such as the volume of tokens issued or the number of investors. Consulting24 advises on structuring your capital to meet GFSC expectations without overcapitalising. Compare this to Lithuania, where the minimum capital for a CASP license is EUR 125,000 (for custody) or EUR 50,000 (for exchange).

Tax Treatment

Gibraltar offers a favourable tax regime for token issuers. The corporate tax rate is a flat 10% on profits. There is no capital gains tax, no VAT on token transactions, and no withholding tax on dividends. Token issuers are not subject to stamp duty on token transfers.

However, the tax treatment of token issuance itself can be complex. The GFSC and Gibraltar Tax Authority treat tokens as intangible assets. For most utility tokens, the issuance is not a taxable event. For security tokens, the proceeds may be considered capital, not income. Consulting24 recommends obtaining a tax ruling from the Gibraltar Tax Authority to confirm your specific treatment. For comparison, Malta has a similar 5% effective tax rate for certain structures, while Panama has 0% tax on foreign-source income.

Allowed Activities

Under a Class 1 DLT Provider license, you can:

  • Issue tokens (fungible or non-fungible) to the public.
  • Conduct token generation events (TGEs) and initial coin offerings (ICOs).
  • Facilitate the transfer of issued tokens on your own platform.
  • Provide advisory services related to token issuance.

If you also hold a Class 3 license, you can operate a DLT exchange where your token is traded. Class 2 allows custody of client tokens. The GFSC does not restrict the type of token (utility, security, payment) but requires that the token's characteristics are clearly disclosed in a whitepaper. Step-by-Step Application Process

The application process for a Gibraltar crypto token issuance license involves several stages:

  1. Pre-application consultation: Meet with the GFSC to discuss your project. This is mandatory.
  2. Document preparation: Prepare a business plan, AML/CFT policies, risk assessment, and token whitepaper.
  3. Appoint local representatives: Engage a Gibraltar-based lawyer and a compliance officer.
  4. Submit application: File the application with the GFSC, including all supporting documents and the application fee.
  5. GFSC review: The GFSC assesses the application, may request additional information, and conducts background checks on directors.
  6. Approval: If successful, you receive a conditional license, which becomes permanent after meeting post-approval conditions (e.g., capital deposit).

Compliance & Ongoing Obligations

Once licensed, you must meet ongoing compliance requirements:

  • Annual AML/CFT audit: Submit an audit report to the GFSC.
  • Financial statements: File audited accounts annually.
  • Transaction monitoring: Implement systems to detect suspicious activity.
  • Risk management: Maintain a risk register and update it regularly.
  • Senior manager approval: Any changes in key personnel require GFSC approval.

The GFSC conducts periodic inspections. Non-compliance can result in fines or license revocation. Consulting24 advises on setting up a compliance framework that meets GFSC expectations. This is general guidance, not legal advice. Engage a local compliance consultant for specific obligations.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Many applicants face delays due to avoidable errors. Common mistakes include:

  • Incomplete whitepaper: The token whitepaper must clearly describe the token's rights, risks, and use of proceeds.
  • Lack of local presence: The GFSC requires a physical office and a resident director. Using a virtual office is not acceptable.
  • Insufficient capital: Ensure the minimum capital is held in a Gibraltar bank account before application.
  • Poor AML/CFT policies: The GFSC scrutinises AML procedures. Generic policies are rejected.
  • Underestimating timeline: Plan for 6 months, not 3. Delays often occur during the review phase.
