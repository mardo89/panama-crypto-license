What is the Gibraltar Crypto Stablecoin License?

The Gibraltar crypto stablecoin license is a regulatory authorization issued by the GFSC for companies that issue, redeem, or manage stablecoins. It falls under the Distributed Ledger Technology (DLT) Provider framework, established in 2018 and updated to include stablecoin-specific rules. The license ensures that stablecoin issuers meet standards for governance, capital adequacy, custody, and consumer protection.

Gibraltar's approach is principles-based, focusing on nine core principles rather than prescriptive rules. This provides flexibility for innovation while maintaining regulatory oversight. The license is mandatory for any firm that issues a stablecoin pegged to a fiat currency or other assets from Gibraltar. For example, a company launching a EUR-pegged stablecoin for European payments must obtain this license. The GFSC requires issuers to maintain a one-to-one reserve of high-quality liquid assets, with regular attestations. This framework has attracted notable projects, though the number of licensed DLT providers remains modest (around 15 as of 2026).

Who Needs This License?

Any company that intends to issue a stablecoin (e.g., USDT, USDC-type tokens) from Gibraltar must obtain a DLT provider license. This includes:

  • Stablecoin issuers (both fiat-collateralized and crypto-collateralized)
  • Payment service providers using stablecoins for remittances or merchant settlements
  • Fintech firms launching their own stablecoin for loyalty programs or DeFi
  • Exchanges that offer stablecoin trading pairs and issue their own stablecoin

If you are planning to issue a stablecoin for use in payments, remittances, or DeFi, Gibraltar is a strong jurisdiction. License Type & Regulator

The Gibraltar crypto stablecoin license is a DLT Provider license issued by the Gibraltar Financial Services Commission (GFSC). The GFSC is the sole regulator for financial services in Gibraltar, including DLT activities. The license is technology-neutral and covers all DLT-based activities, including stablecoin issuance.

There is no separate 'stablecoin license' - stablecoin issuance falls under the existing DLT framework. The GFSC applies additional guidance for stablecoins, including requirements for reserve management, redemption rights, and transparency. For instance, issuers must publish monthly reserve reports and ensure redemption within a specified timeframe. The GFSC also conducts regular on-site inspections. This contrasts with EU MiCA, which has a separate regime for asset-referenced tokens (ARTs) and e-money tokens (EMTs).

Capital Requirement

Gibraltar does not prescribe a fixed minimum capital for DLT providers. Instead, the GFSC applies a risk-based approach. For stablecoin issuers, the capital requirement is typically based on the volume of stablecoins in circulation and the risk profile of the business. As a general guide, expect capital in the range of EUR 100,000 to 500,000 or more. The exact figure is determined during the application process.

This is a key difference from EU MiCA, which sets fixed capital tiers of EUR 50,000 / 125,000 / 150,000 by service class. Gibraltar's flexibility can be an advantage for smaller issuers but requires a strong business plan. For example, a startup issuing a small stablecoin for a niche community may need only EUR 100,000, while a large-scale payment stablecoin may require EUR 500,000 or more. Tax Treatment

Gibraltar offers a favorable tax regime for stablecoin issuers. The corporate tax rate is a flat 10% on profits. There is no capital gains tax, no VAT on financial services, and no withholding tax on dividends. Additionally, Gibraltar has no stamp duty or inheritance tax.

Stablecoin transactions may be exempt from VAT under the EU VAT directive (as Gibraltar follows UK VAT rules). However, tax treatment depends on the specific structure. For instance, if your stablecoin is used as a payment method, it may be treated as a financial service, which is VAT-exempt. If it is treated as a digital asset, capital gains tax may not apply. Consulting24 recommends engaging a local tax advisor to optimize your tax position. Compare this to Lithuania which has a 15% corporate tax, or Estonia with 20% on distributed profits.

Allowed Activities

With a Gibraltar DLT provider license for stablecoins, you can:

  • Issue and redeem stablecoins
  • Manage reserve assets backing the stablecoin
  • Provide custody for stablecoins
  • Offer payment services using stablecoins
  • Integrate with exchanges and wallets

Activities not covered include lending or borrowing stablecoins (which may require a separate license) and offering derivatives. The GFSC assesses each application on a case-by-case basis. For example, if you want to offer staking on your stablecoin, you may need additional authorization. The license also does not cover fiat currency exchange or remittance services unless explicitly included. Consulting24 can help you scope your activities correctly.

Compliance & Ongoing Obligations

Once licensed, stablecoin issuers must comply with ongoing requirements:

  • Annual audit of financial statements and reserve assets by a GFSC-approved auditor.
  • Maintain AML/CFT policies and report suspicious transactions to the Gibraltar Financial Intelligence Unit.
  • Submit annual regulatory returns to the GFSC, including financial data and risk assessments.
  • Notify the GFSC of any material changes to the business, such as new products or key personnel.
  • Maintain adequate capital on a continuous basis, with periodic stress testing.
  • Conduct regular reserve attestations (e.g., monthly) to ensure stablecoin is fully backed.

Failure to comply can result in fines or license revocation. Consulting24 provides ongoing compliance support. This is general guidance, not legal advice.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Insufficient capital: underestimating the capital required can delay approval. Prepare a detailed capital plan.
  • Poor governance: lack of clear board structure and risk management. Ensure you have independent directors.
  • Inadequate AML/CFT: weak procedures are a common reason for rejection. Use a qualified AML officer.
  • Unrealistic business plan: overestimating market share or revenue. Base projections on realistic data.
  • Ignoring tax: not planning for tax obligations in Gibraltar and other jurisdictions. Engage a tax advisor early.
  • Delaying bank account: banking can take weeks, so start the process as soon as possible.
