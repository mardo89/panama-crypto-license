What Is a Crypto Stablecoin License in Switzerland?

A crypto stablecoin license in Switzerland is a regulatory authorization issued by FINMA that allows an entity to issue and manage stablecoins legally. The license type depends on the stablecoin's design: fiat-backed stablecoins (e.g., USDC-style) are typically treated as bank deposits and require a banking license, while asset-referenced stablecoins (backed by a basket of assets) may fall under the FMIA as a payment system. In practice, many issuers opt for a payment system license under the FMIA, which is more tailored than a full banking license.

Switzerland's approach is principles-based, meaning FINMA evaluates each application on its merits. The key is demonstrating strong governance, risk management, and compliance with anti-money laundering (AML) rules. As of 2026, the Swiss regime has matured, with several stablecoin issuers already licensed, including those backed by the Swiss franc and other major currencies.

Who Needs a Swiss Stablecoin License?

Any entity that issues stablecoins in or from Switzerland, or that offers stablecoin services to Swiss residents, likely needs a license. This includes:

  • Fiat-backed stablecoin issuers (e.g., 1:1 USD or CHF reserves)
  • Asset-referenced stablecoin issuers (e.g., backed by a basket of currencies or commodities)
  • Algorithmic stablecoin projects (subject to higher scrutiny)
  • Stablecoin wallet providers that also issue the token
  • Decentralized finance (DeFi) protocols that issue stablecoins with governance tokens

If your project involves issuing a stablecoin that is used as a means of payment or store of value, you fall under FINMA's purview. Even if you are based outside Switzerland, if you target Swiss users, you may need a license. Consulting24 can help you assess your specific obligations.

License Type & Regulator

The primary regulator for stablecoin licensing in Switzerland is the Swiss Financial Market Supervisory Authority (FINMA). FINMA is responsible for licensing and supervising financial institutions, including stablecoin issuers. The specific license type depends on the stablecoin's characteristics:

Stablecoin TypeTypical LicenseLegal Basis
Fiat-backed (single currency)Banking license (if redeemable on demand) or payment system licenseBanking Act (BA) or FMIA
Asset-referenced (basket)Payment system license (FMIA) or DLT trading facilityFMIA or DLT Act
AlgorithmicMay require banking license or be prohibitedBA or FMIA

In practice, most stablecoin issuers apply for a payment system license under the FMIA, which is designed for financial market infrastructures. This license is less burdensome than a full banking license but still requires significant compliance. FINMA also applies its 2019 guidelines on stablecoins, updated in 2022, which classify tokens as payment, utility, or asset tokens.

Capital Requirement

Switzerland does not impose a fixed minimum capital requirement for stablecoin licenses under the FMIA. Instead, FINMA requires that the issuer maintains sufficient financial resources to cover operational risks and potential losses. For payment system licenses, the capital is determined based on the risk profile, typically ranging from CHF 100,000 to CHF 500,000 (approximately EUR 100,000 to 500,000).

For banking licenses, the capital requirement is higher, starting at CHF 10 million (about EUR 10 million), but this is rare for stablecoin issuers. Most stablecoin projects opt for the FMIA route, where capital is negotiated with FINMA during the application process. Tax Treatment

Switzerland offers a favorable tax environment for crypto businesses, including stablecoin issuers. Key points:

  • Corporate income tax: The effective corporate tax rate varies by canton, ranging from 11.9% to 21.6%. Many stablecoin issuers choose cantons like Zug (known as Crypto Valley) or Zurich for lower rates.
  • VAT: Stablecoin issuance and exchange services are generally exempt from Swiss VAT, as they are considered financial services.
  • Wealth tax: Stablecoins held as assets are subject to cantonal wealth tax, but this is typically low.
  • Stamp duty: No stamp duty on the issuance of stablecoins.

Stablecoin issuers should also consider the tax treatment of reserves (e.g., interest earned on fiat reserves) and pass-through taxation. Consulting24 can coordinate with Swiss tax advisors to optimize your structure.

Allowed Activities

A Swiss stablecoin license permits the following activities:

  • Issuance and redemption of stablecoins
  • Management of reserve assets (e.g., fiat currency, bonds, or other collateral)
  • Provision of stablecoin wallets for users
  • Cross-border payment services using the stablecoin
  • Integration with decentralized finance (DeFi) protocols
  • Staking or lending of stablecoins (if within the license scope)

Activities not automatically permitted include proprietary trading, offering other crypto assets without separate licenses, and providing investment advice. Each license is tailored, so the exact scope is defined in the approval letter from FINMA.

Step-by-Step Application Process

Obtaining a Swiss stablecoin license involves several stages. Consulting24 advises and coordinates each step:

  2. Business plan preparation: Draft a detailed business plan covering governance, risk management, AML/CFT policies, and reserve management.

  6. Review and interview: FINMA reviews the application, often conducting interviews with management. This stage takes 1-3 months.
  7. Approval: Once satisfied, FINMA issues the license, subject to ongoing supervision.

Compliance & Ongoing Obligations

Once licensed, stablecoin issuers must comply with ongoing requirements:

  • AML/CFT: Implement and maintain an AML program, including KYC, transaction monitoring, and reporting to the Money Laundering Reporting Office (MROS).
  • Audit: Annual financial audit by a licensed auditor.
  • Reporting: Submit periodic reports to FINMA on financial condition, reserve composition, and material changes.
  • Reserve management: For fiat-backed stablecoins, reserves must be held in trust or segregated accounts, with regular attestations.
  • Governance: Maintain a board of directors with at least one member resident in Switzerland.

Common Mistakes & Pitfalls

Many applicants stumble on these issues:

  • Underestimating capital: Even without a fixed minimum, FINMA expects sufficient capital to cover risks. Too low can lead to rejection.
  • Inadequate AML controls: FINMA is strict on AML. A weak compliance framework is a common reason for delays.
  • Choosing the wrong license type: Some issuers apply for a banking license unnecessarily, increasing costs and timeline.
  • Poor documentation: Incomplete or inconsistent business plans cause back-and-forth with FINMA.
  • Ignoring local presence: FINMA requires a substantive presence in Switzerland, including a registered office and local management.

Frequently asked questions

What is the difference between a payment system license and a banking license for stablecoins in Switzerland?

A payment system license under the FMIA is for stablecoins that are used as a means of payment and are not redeemable on demand at par. A banking license is required if the stablecoin is redeemable at par and functions like a deposit. Most stablecoin issuers prefer the FMIA route as it is less capital-intensive.

How long does the Swiss stablecoin license application take?

The typical timeline is 3 to 6 months from submission to approval. Complex cases involving algorithmic stablecoins or asset-referenced tokens may take up to 9 months.

What are the ongoing costs for a Swiss stablecoin license?

Ongoing costs include annual FINMA supervision fees (typically CHF 5,000-20,000), audit fees, compliance staff salaries, and legal retainer. Total annual costs range from EUR 50,000 to 150,000 depending on scale.

Is there a minimum capital requirement for a stablecoin license in Switzerland?

No fixed minimum, but FINMA expects capital proportional to risk. For payment system licenses, typical capital is CHF 100,000 to 500,000. For banking licenses, it is CHF 10 million.

What is the tax rate for stablecoin issuers in Switzerland?

Corporate income tax ranges from 11.9% to 21.6% depending on canton. Zug and Zurich are popular for lower rates. Stablecoin issuance is VAT-exempt.

Can I use a Swiss stablecoin license to operate in the EU?

Switzerland is not an EU member, so a Swiss license does not automatically passport into the EU. However, under MiCA, third-country issuers may need to comply with EU rules. Some Swiss issuers set up an EU subsidiary for passporting.

What happens if I issue a stablecoin without a license in Switzerland?

Operating without a license is illegal and can lead to criminal prosecution, fines, and forced cessation of operations. FINMA actively monitors the market and can issue cease-and-desist orders.

Does Switzerland allow algorithmic stablecoins?

FINMA treats algorithmic stablecoins with caution. They may be considered securities or require a banking license if they function like deposits. Each case is assessed individually.

How does the Swiss stablecoin license compare to Panama?

Panama offers a simpler, cheaper (EUR 6,000 flat) and faster process with no specific crypto regulation, but less regulatory credibility. Switzerland provides a prestigious, regulated environment at higher cost and longer timeline.
