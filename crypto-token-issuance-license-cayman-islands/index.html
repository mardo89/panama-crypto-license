What is the Crypto Token Issuance License in the Cayman Islands?

The crypto token issuance license, formally known as a VASP registration under the Virtual Asset Service Provider Act, authorises firms to issue virtual assets (tokens) to the public. This includes initial coin offerings (ICOs), security token offerings (STOs), and other token generation events. The license also covers related services such as token custody and transfer.

Unlike some jurisdictions that treat token issuance as a securities offering, the Cayman Islands has a dedicated framework that distinguishes between different token types. Issuers must comply with anti-money laundering (AML) and counter-terrorist financing (CTF) obligations, but the regime is designed to be business-friendly while meeting FATF recommendations. The VASP Act was updated in 2024 to align with the latest FATF guidance, and as of 2026, CIMA actively enforces these rules.

For example, a project issuing a utility token for a decentralised application must register as a VASP, but the token itself may not be classified as a security if it has no profit-sharing rights. License Type and Regulator

The regulator is the Cayman Islands Monetary Authority (CIMA). Under the VASP Act, there is one main registration category: Virtual Asset Service Provider (VASP). This covers token issuance, exchange, custody, and transfer services. There is no separate license for token issuance alone; it falls under the VASP umbrella.

Applicants must be incorporated in the Cayman Islands, typically as an exempted company or limited liability company (LLC). The registration is not a full license but a mandatory registration with ongoing supervision. CIMA requires a fit and proper test for directors and shareholders, plus a detailed business plan. The VASP Act also mandates the appointment of a local AML officer and a registered office.

As of 2026, CIMA has increased scrutiny on beneficial ownership and source of funds. All directors and shareholders must provide detailed background checks, including police certificates and professional references. Capital Requirement

The VASP Act does not prescribe a fixed minimum capital for token issuance. However, CIMA expects applicants to have sufficient financial resources to operate soundly. As a guideline, most approved VASPs maintain at least USD 100,000 in liquid capital. For token issuers, additional capital may be required depending on the size of the offering and risk profile.

Tax Treatment

The Cayman Islands is a tax-neutral jurisdiction. There is no corporate income tax, capital gains tax, withholding tax, or VAT. Token issuers pay no tax on profits from token sales or trading. The only government levies are registration fees and annual renewal fees.

However, issuers must consider tax obligations in the countries where their token holders reside. For example, if your token is sold to US investors, US tax laws may apply. The Cayman Islands itself does not impose taxes on virtual asset transactions. This makes it an attractive base for token projects seeking to minimise tax burden.

Allowed Activities

Under a VASP registration, the following activities are permitted:

  • Issuance of virtual assets (tokens) to the public
  • Exchange of virtual assets for fiat or other virtual assets
  • Custody and administration of virtual assets
  • Transfer services (sending tokens on behalf of clients)
  • Operating a trading platform for virtual assets

Activities not covered include traditional banking, securities brokerage (unless separately licensed), and gambling. The license does not permit lending or borrowing of virtual assets unless specifically applied for. If your business model includes staking or lending, you may need additional approvals.

It is important to note that the VASP registration is not a banking license. You cannot accept deposits from the public or offer interest-bearing accounts. Step-by-Step Application Process

  1. Company incorporation: Register a Cayman Islands exempted company or LLC with a registered office. This typically takes 1-2 weeks.
  2. Appoint local service providers: Engage a registered office provider, AML officer, and auditor. These must be Cayman-based entities.
  3. Prepare documentation: Business plan, AML/CTF policies, risk assessment, financial projections, and board resolutions. This is the most time-consuming step.
  4. Submit VASP application: File with CIMA including all supporting documents and pay the fee. CIMA will acknowledge receipt within 5 business days.
  5. Due diligence review: CIMA conducts background checks on directors, shareholders, and beneficial owners. This can take 2-3 months.
  6. Approval and ongoing compliance: Once registered, submit annual returns, maintain AML procedures, and report material changes. The first annual return is due 12 months after registration.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Insufficient AML documentation: Many applications are rejected due to weak AML policies. Ensure your procedures are detailed and aligned with FATF guidance.
  • Ignoring beneficial ownership requirements: CIMA requires full disclosure of all beneficial owners. Failure to do so can delay or deny the application.
  • Unrealistic financial projections: Provide conservative, well-supported forecasts. Overly optimistic numbers raise red flags.
  • Not engaging local expertise: The Cayman Islands has specific legal and regulatory nuances. Working with local advisors is essential.
  • Delaying bank account opening: Start the banking process early, as it can take several months.
  • Overlooking ongoing reporting: After registration, you must submit annual returns and report any material changes. Non-compliance can lead to revocation.

Frequently asked questions

What is the difference between a VASP registration and a full license in the Cayman Islands?

The Cayman Islands uses a registration model rather than a full license. VASP registration is mandatory for virtual asset service providers, including token issuers. It is not a license per se but a registration with ongoing supervisory obligations. The requirements are similar to a license, including AML/CTF compliance and fit and proper checks.

Can I issue a security token under the VASP Act?

Yes, the VASP Act covers the issuance of all virtual assets, including security tokens. However, if the token is classified as a security under Cayman Islands law, additional securities regulations may apply. You should seek legal advice to determine the classification. The typical timeline is 3 to 6 months from company incorporation to registration approval. Delays can occur if CIMA requests additional information or if due diligence reveals issues. Consulting24 helps minimise delays by ensuring complete and accurate submissions.

What are the ongoing compliance obligations after registration?

You must submit annual returns to CIMA, maintain AML/CTF policies, conduct regular risk assessments, and report any material changes (e.g., change of directors or business model). You also need to keep records of transactions for at least 5 years. Consulting24 offers ongoing compliance support.

Is there a minimum capital requirement for token issuance?

No fixed minimum capital is prescribed, but CIMA expects you to have sufficient financial resources. Most approved VASPs maintain at least USD 100,000 in liquid capital. Consulting24 recommends budgeting for at least USD 50,000 to cover initial costs.

Can I use the Cayman Islands VASP registration to passport services into the EU?

No, the Cayman Islands is not an EU member state, so there is no passporting. If you need EU market access, consider an EU jurisdiction like Estonia or Lithuania, which offer passporting under MiCA. Consulting24 can advise on both options.

What are the tax implications for token issuers in the Cayman Islands?
