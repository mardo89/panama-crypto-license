What is the Cyprus Crypto Broker License?

The Cyprus crypto broker license, officially a Crypto Asset Service Provider (CASP) authorization under MiCA, is issued by CySEC. It allows companies to offer crypto-related services such as exchange, custody, and brokerage within the European Economic Area (EEA) under a single passport. Since MiCA is fully in force in 2026, the Cyprus CASP replaces the former registration regime and imposes strict capital, governance, and AML requirements.

Key features include:

  • Regulated by CySEC, an EU competent authority.
  • Passporting rights across all EU/EEA member states.
  • Minimum capital tiers based on service type (EUR 50,000 for simple brokerage, EUR 125,000 for exchange, EUR 150,000 for custody).
  • Comprehensive AML/KYC obligations under the 5th AML Directive.

For example, a crypto broker that only executes client orders without holding funds needs EUR 50,000 capital, while a custodian wallet provider must hold EUR 150,000. The license is essential for any firm targeting EU retail or institutional clients.

Who Needs a Cyprus Crypto Broker License?

Any company intending to provide crypto asset services to EU clients on a professional basis must obtain a CASP license in an EU member state. Cyprus is an attractive choice due to its favorable tax regime, English-speaking workforce, and established financial infrastructure. Typical applicants include:

  • Crypto brokers matching buy/sell orders for clients.
  • Exchange platforms (centralized or decentralized with an intermediary).
  • Custodian wallet providers holding private keys on behalf of clients.
  • Market makers and liquidity providers dealing in crypto assets.
  • Portfolio managers offering crypto asset management.

If your business model involves any of these activities and you target EU customers, the Cyprus CASP is a strong option. For non-EU clients, Panama may offer a simpler and cheaper alternative with no minimum capital and a flat fee of EUR 6,000.

License Type & Regulator

The Cyprus crypto broker license is a CASP authorization under MiCA. The regulator is the Cyprus Securities and Exchange Commission (CySEC), based in Nicosia. CySEC is a well-respected EU regulator with extensive experience in investment services (CIF licenses). The CASP license is distinct from the CIF but follows similar principles of governance, capital adequacy, and investor protection.

Key regulatory details:

  • Legal framework: Investment Services and Activities and Regulated Markets Law (as amended for MiCA).
  • License type: Class 1 (simple brokerage) or Class 2 (exchange/custody) depending on services.
  • Supervision: Ongoing reporting, AML audits, and capital monitoring.

CySEC also requires the appointment of a local compliance officer and a registered office in Cyprus. The regulator conducts on-site inspections and reviews AML procedures annually.

Capital Requirement

Under MiCA, minimum capital requirements are tiered by service type:

  • EUR 50,000 for providing only order execution (brokerage without custody).
  • EUR 125,000 for exchange services (matching orders).
  • EUR 150,000 for custody of client crypto assets.

These amounts must be maintained as own funds, typically in cash or highly liquid assets. Additional capital may be required based on operational risk. For example, a firm offering both exchange and custody must meet the highest tier (EUR 150,000). For comparison, Panama has no minimum capital, making it a lower-cost entry point for non-EU businesses.

Tax Treatment

Cyprus offers one of the most favorable tax regimes in the EU for crypto businesses:

  • Corporate income tax: 12.5% on net profits.
  • No withholding tax on dividends paid to non-resident shareholders.
  • No capital gains tax on disposal of crypto assets held as investments (subject to conditions).
  • VAT: crypto-to-fiat transactions are exempt from VAT; crypto-to-crypto may be subject to VAT under certain interpretations.

Cyprus also has an extensive double tax treaty network. Proper tax structuring is essential; we recommend engaging a local tax advisor. For example, a Cyprus CASP firm can repatriate profits to a parent company in another treaty country with minimal withholding tax.

Allowed Activities

The Cyprus CASP license permits the following activities under MiCA:

  • Execution of orders for crypto assets on behalf of clients.
  • Placing of crypto assets (underwriting).
  • Operation of a trading platform (exchange).
  • Custody and administration of crypto assets on behalf of clients.
  • Transfer services for crypto assets.
  • Advice on crypto assets.
  • Portfolio management of crypto assets.

Each activity requires separate authorization within the license scope. Activities not covered include issuing crypto assets (requires a white paper under MiCA) and decentralized finance (DeFi) without an intermediary. If your business model includes DeFi, you may need to restructure to qualify as a CASP.

Benefits of the Cyprus Crypto License

Choosing Cyprus for your crypto broker license offers distinct advantages:

  • EU passporting: serve all 27 EU member states from one license.
  • Low corporate tax (12.5%) and no capital gains tax on crypto.
  • Established legal framework under MiCA, providing clarity and investor confidence.

Compliance & Trust

Operating under a Cyprus CASP license signals strong regulatory compliance to clients and partners. Key compliance obligations include:

  • Appointment of a compliance officer, AML officer, and internal auditor.
  • Annual external audit of financial statements and AML procedures.
  • Transaction monitoring and suspicious activity reporting to CySEC.
  • Capital adequacy and liquidity management.

This is general guidance, not legal advice. You should engage a local law firm for specific compliance requirements. For example, CySEC expects AML policies to cover customer due diligence, ongoing monitoring, and record keeping for at least five years. Non-compliance can result in fines or license revocation.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Applicants often make these errors when applying for a Cyprus CASP:

  • Underestimating capital requirements: ensure you have the full minimum capital in liquid assets.
  • Incomplete AML policies: CySEC expects detailed, operational AML/KYC procedures.
  • Unclear business model: vague descriptions of services lead to queries and delays.
  • Ignoring ongoing costs: budget for annual compliance, audits, and legal fees (typically EUR 10,000-20,000 per year).
  • Choosing the wrong service class: selecting a higher capital tier than needed increases costs unnecessarily.

Frequently asked questions

What is the difference between a Cyprus CIF and a CASP license?

A CIF (Cyprus Investment Firm) license is for traditional financial instruments (stocks, forex). A CASP license under MiCA is specifically for crypto asset services. Both are regulated by CySEC, but CASP has its own capital requirements and rules.

Can I passport the Cyprus CASP license to other EU countries?

Yes, under MiCA, a CASP license from any EU member state can be passported across the entire European Economic Area. This means you can serve clients in all 27 EU countries plus Iceland, Liechtenstein, and Norway without additional licenses.

What are the ongoing compliance costs for a Cyprus CASP?

Annual costs typically include legal and compliance fees (EUR 5,000-10,000), audit fees (EUR 3,000-5,000), AML software subscriptions (EUR 2,000-5,000), and CySEC supervisory fees (EUR 1,000-3,000). Total ongoing costs range from EUR 10,000 to 20,000 per year.

Is there a physical presence requirement in Cyprus?

Yes, you must have a registered office in Cyprus and appoint a local compliance officer. CySEC may also require a local director or manager. Many firms use serviced offices and outsourced compliance providers to meet this requirement.

How long does the Cyprus CASP application take?

The typical timeline is 6 to 12 months from submission to approval. Delays often occur if the application is incomplete or if CySEC has a high workload. Pre-submission preparation can reduce the risk of delays.

Can I apply for a Cyprus CASP if my company is already registered in another EU country?

Yes, you can establish a subsidiary in Cyprus and apply for the CASP license. Alternatively, you may be able to passport your existing CASP license from another EU member state to Cyprus, but this depends on the specific license and services.

What happens if I don't meet the capital requirement?

You must maintain the minimum capital at all times. If capital falls below the threshold, CySEC may suspend or revoke your license. You may also be required to inject additional funds or restructure your operations.

Does Cyprus tax crypto-to-crypto transactions?

Under current rules, crypto-to-crypto transactions may be subject to VAT if they involve a supply of services. However, most crypto-to-fiat transactions are VAT-exempt. Corporate income tax applies to profits from trading, not to individual transactions.

Can I use the Cyprus CASP to offer DeFi services?

DeFi services without an intermediary are generally not covered by the CASP license. If your DeFi platform involves an intermediary (e.g., a front-end that executes orders), it may qualify. You should discuss your specific model with a legal advisor.

What is the difference between Cyprus and Panama for a crypto license?

Cyprus offers EU passporting and a regulated environment but requires higher capital (EUR 50,000+) and a longer timeline (6-12 months). Panama has no minimum capital, a flat fee of EUR 6,000, and a faster process (1-2 months), but no EU passporting. Choose based on your target market.
