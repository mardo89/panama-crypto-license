What Is the Switzerland Crypto License?

The Switzerland crypto license is not a single license but a set of authorizations under the Swiss Financial Market Supervisory Authority (FINMA). Depending on your business model, you may need a banking license (for custodial services over CHF 1 million in third-party assets), a securities dealer license (for trading on own account), or a FinTech license (for deposit-taking up to CHF 100 million). Most crypto startups apply for a VASP (Virtual Asset Service Provider) registration under the Anti-Money Laundering Act (AMLA), which is mandatory for any entity that professionally trades, transfers, or custodies crypto assets.

FINMA classifies crypto assets as either payment tokens (like Bitcoin), utility tokens, or asset tokens (securities). The classification determines which license applies. As of 2026, the Swiss regime is fully operational and aligned with FATF recommendations, but Switzerland is not an EU member, so MiCA does not apply directly. However, Swiss law is harmonized with international standards, making it a respected jurisdiction for crypto operations.

Who Needs a Crypto License in Switzerland?

Any company that provides crypto-related services to third parties on a professional basis must be licensed or registered. This includes:

  • Crypto exchanges (centralized and decentralized if operating as a business)
  • Wallet providers that hold private keys for customers
  • OTC trading desks
  • Payment service providers handling crypto payments
  • Asset managers dealing with crypto assets
  • Issuers of stablecoins or other crypto tokens

If you are a founder planning to offer any of these services, you must obtain the appropriate authorization. Even if you are a non-Swiss company, you may need to set up a Swiss subsidiary and apply for a license if you target Swiss customers or have a physical presence in Switzerland.

License Type and Regulator

The primary regulator is the Swiss Financial Market Supervisory Authority (FINMA). For most crypto businesses, the relevant authorization is the VASP registration under the Anti-Money Laundering Act (AMLA). This is not a full financial license but a registration with a self-regulatory organization (SRO) approved by FINMA. The SRO (e.g., VQF, PolyReg, or ARIF) conducts the initial due diligence and ongoing supervision.

If your business model involves taking deposits or holding customer assets above certain thresholds, you will need a FinTech license (max CHF 100 million in deposits) or a full banking license. For securities-related activities (e.g., trading asset tokens), a securities dealer license may be required. The choice of license depends on the services offered and the volume of assets under custody.

Capital Requirement

Switzerland imposes minimum capital requirements based on the license type:

  • VASP registration (AMLA): No statutory minimum capital, but FINMA expects at least CHF 100,000 in fully paid-up equity to demonstrate substance and operational readiness.
  • FinTech license: Minimum capital of CHF 300,000, with additional capital based on deposit volume.
  • Banking license: Minimum capital of CHF 10 million for a full bank, or CHF 3 million for a securities dealer.

Capital must be held in a Swiss bank account and be freely available. The exact amount required for your business will be determined during the licensing process, taking into account your business plan and risk profile.

Tax Treatment

Switzerland offers a favorable tax environment for crypto businesses. Corporate income tax rates are cantonal, ranging from approximately 11.9% to 21% (effective average around 12-14% in Zug, the crypto valley). Holding companies benefit from participation relief. There is no capital gains tax on private crypto investments, but professional traders (trading as a business) are subject to corporate tax on gains.

VAT treatment: Crypto transactions are generally exempt from Swiss VAT if they qualify as financial services. However, mining and staking rewards may be subject to VAT. Transfer taxes (stamp duties) may apply to securities tokens. Detailed tax advice is essential, as tax obligations depend on the specific activities and cantonal regulations.

Allowed Activities Under the License

Depending on the license, allowed activities include:

  • Operating a crypto exchange (order matching, trading)
  • Custody of crypto assets (private key management)
  • Transfer of crypto assets (payment services)
  • Brokerage (acting as intermediary)
  • Issuance of tokens (ICO/STO)
  • Asset management involving crypto
  • Staking and lending (subject to additional regulations)

Activities that are not allowed without a full banking license include taking deposits from the public (over CHF 100 million) and operating a payment system that processes fiat payments. Each license type has specific activity boundaries; a consultation with FINMA or a specialized advisor is recommended to confirm your scope.

Step-by-Step Application Process

  1. Business planning: Define your services, target market, and compliance structure. Prepare a detailed business plan including financial projections, AML policies, and risk management.
  2. Company incorporation: Set up a Swiss company (usually a GmbH or AG) with a registered office in Switzerland. Minimum share capital for AG is CHF 100,000 (50% paid up).
  3. Choose an SRO: Select a FINMA-approved self-regulatory organization (e.g., VQF, PolyReg, ARIF) and submit your membership application. The SRO will review your AML framework.
  4. Prepare application: Compile all required documents: business plan, organizational chart, AML policy, risk assessment, financial statements, background checks on directors, and proof of capital.
  5. Submit to FINMA: File the license application with FINMA. The regulator will conduct a thorough review, including interviews with management.
  6. Approval and ongoing supervision: Once approved, you will be subject to periodic reporting and audits by your SRO and FINMA.

Banking and Payment Services

Opening a corporate bank account in Switzerland for a crypto company can be challenging due to the perceived risk. Many traditional banks are reluctant to serve crypto businesses. However, there are crypto-friendly banks and fintechs such as SEBA Bank, Sygnum, and Hypothekarbank Lenzburg that specialize in blockchain companies.

For fiat on-ramp/off-ramp, you may need to partner with Swiss payment processors that support both fiat and crypto. The SRO may also require you to maintain a bank account in Switzerland to hold the minimum capital. Benefits of a Swiss Crypto License

Switzerland offers several advantages for crypto businesses:

  • Regulatory clarity: FINMA provides clear guidelines and a supportive environment for innovation.
  • Low corporate tax: Effective rates around 12% in crypto-friendly cantons like Zug.
  • Stable legal system: Swiss law is predictable and respected globally.
  • Access to talent: Strong blockchain community and skilled workforce.
  • International reputation: A Swiss license enhances credibility with partners and customers.
  • No EU MiCA constraints: Switzerland sets its own rules, often more flexible than the EU.

For founders seeking a reputable jurisdiction with a mature crypto ecosystem, Switzerland is a top choice.

Compliance and Trust

Compliance is at the heart of the Swiss licensing regime. You must implement strong AML/KYC procedures, appoint a local AML officer, and conduct regular audits. The SRO will monitor your compliance with Swiss AML law. Failure to comply can result in fines, revocation of license, or criminal prosecution.

To build trust, ensure your business has transparent ownership, a physical office in Switzerland, and qualified directors. Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Underestimating capital requirements: Even for VASP registration, expect to show at least CHF 100,000 in capital.
  • Choosing the wrong SRO: Some SROs are stricter than others; choose one that matches your business profile.
  • Incomplete AML documentation: FINMA expects detailed policies, including transaction monitoring and sanctions screening.
  • Ignoring local substance: A Swiss license requires a physical presence, including a registered office and local management.
  • Not preparing for bank account opening: Start the process early, as it can take months.

Frequently asked questions

What is the minimum capital for a Swiss crypto license?

For VASP registration under AMLA, FINMA expects at least CHF 100,000 in paid-up capital. For a FinTech license, the minimum is CHF 300,000. For a full banking license, CHF 10 million. Exact requirements depend on your business model.

How long does it take to get a Swiss crypto license?

Typically 6 to 12 months from company incorporation to final approval. The FINMA review itself takes 3-6 months. Complex cases, such as banking licenses, can take up to 18 months.

Can I get a Swiss crypto license without a physical office?

No. FINMA requires a registered office in Switzerland and local substance, including at least one director residing in Switzerland or the EU/EFTA. Virtual offices are not accepted.

What is the corporate tax rate for crypto companies in Switzerland?

The effective corporate tax rate varies by canton, typically between 11.9% and 21%. In Zug (Crypto Valley), the rate is around 11.9%. There is no capital gains tax on private crypto holdings.

Is a Swiss crypto license recognized in the EU?

No, Switzerland is not an EU member. However, Swiss licenses are respected globally and often facilitate partnerships with EU banks. For EU operations, a separate MiCA license (e.g., in Estonia or Lithuania) is needed.

What activities are allowed under a Swiss VASP registration?

You can operate a crypto exchange, provide custody, transfer services, brokerage, and token issuance. For deposit-taking or payment systems, you need a FinTech or banking license.

Do I need to be physically present in Switzerland during the application?

Yes, FINMA may require interviews with management. You should have at least one director who can travel to Switzerland for meetings. Some SROs also require in-person onboarding.

How much does the entire process cost?

First-year costs including company formation, SRO membership, legal fees, and FINMA application typically range from CHF 50,000 to 150,000, plus the minimum capital. Exact pricing depends on complexity.

What happens if I operate without a license in Switzerland?

Operating without the required authorization is a criminal offense. FINMA can impose fines, order cessation of business, and refer cases for prosecution. It can also blacklist the company internationally.
