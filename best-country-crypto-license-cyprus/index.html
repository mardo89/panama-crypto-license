What Is the Cyprus Crypto License?

The Cyprus crypto license, officially the CASP registration under CySEC, is a regulatory authorization required to provide crypto asset services in or from Cyprus. As of 2026, MiCA is fully in force, meaning Cyprus applies EU-wide capital tiers: EUR 50,000 for simple services like custody, EUR 125,000 for exchange and wallet services, and EUR 150,000 for more complex activities like operating a trading platform. This license is part of the EU's harmonized framework, offering passporting rights across all EU member states.

Cyprus is often considered the best country for a crypto license due to its favorable tax regime (12.5% corporate tax, no withholding tax on dividends) and its reputation as a fintech hub. However, the application process is thorough, requiring detailed business plans, AML policies, and fit-and-proper assessments of key personnel.

Who Needs a Cyprus Crypto License?

Any entity that provides crypto asset services to EU residents or operates from Cyprus must hold a CASP license. This includes:

  • Cryptocurrency exchanges (centralized and decentralized)
  • Wallet providers (custodial and non-custodial)
  • Brokers and dealers in crypto assets
  • Platforms offering crypto lending, staking, or yield products
  • Payment processors using crypto
  • Token issuers (if the token qualifies as a crypto asset under MiCA)

If your business targets EU clients, Cyprus is a strong contender for the best country for a crypto license due to its passporting benefits. License Type and Regulator

The regulator for crypto licensing in Cyprus is the Cyprus Securities and Exchange Commission (CySEC). The license type is the Crypto Asset Services Provider (CASP) registration under the Investment Services and Activities and Regulated Markets Law of 2017, as amended to incorporate MiCA. CySEC is a well-respected EU regulator, known for its rigorous oversight.

CySEC issues different authorizations based on the services offered, each with specific capital requirements. The license is valid for the entire EU via passporting. For businesses seeking the best country for a crypto license with EU access, Cyprus is a top choice due to CySEC's established framework and the island's business-friendly environment.

Capital Requirement

Under MiCA, the minimum capital requirements for CASP licenses in Cyprus are tiered by service type:

  • EUR 50,000 for custody and administration of crypto assets
  • EUR 125,000 for exchange of crypto assets for fiat or other crypto assets
  • EUR 150,000 for operating a trading platform or providing complex services

These amounts must be maintained as own funds (e.g., paid-up capital, reserves). Cyprus does not require additional local capital beyond the EU minimums. Compared to other jurisdictions, Cyprus sits in the mid-range: Estonia has no minimum capital for its VASP license (but MiCA will apply from 2026), while Lithuania requires EUR 125,000 for exchange services. For the best country for a crypto license with moderate capital needs, Cyprus is competitive.

Tax Treatment

Cyprus offers one of the most attractive tax regimes in the EU for crypto businesses:

  • Corporate income tax: 12.5% (one of the lowest in the EU)
  • No withholding tax on dividends, interest, or royalties paid to non-residents
  • No capital gains tax on disposal of securities (including crypto assets held as investments, subject to conditions)
  • VAT: Crypto-to-fiat exchanges are VAT-exempt; other services may be subject to 19% VAT
  • Notional interest deduction on new equity capital (NID) reduces effective tax rate further

This tax-friendly environment makes Cyprus a strong candidate for the best country for a crypto license, especially for companies with significant profits. However, tax advice should be sought from a qualified professional. For a zero-tax alternative, Panama offers territorial taxation (no tax on foreign-source income).

Allowed Activities

A Cyprus CASP license permits the following activities under MiCA:

  • Providing custody and administration of crypto assets on behalf of clients
  • Operating a trading platform for crypto assets
  • Exchanging crypto assets for fiat currency
  • Exchanging crypto assets for other crypto assets
  • Executing orders for crypto assets on behalf of clients
  • Placing crypto assets (underwriting or placing with a firm commitment)
  • Receiving and transmitting orders for crypto assets
  • Providing advice on crypto assets
  • Managing crypto assets on a discretionary basis
  • Transfer services for crypto assets on behalf of clients

Activities not covered include issuing tokens that qualify as financial instruments (MiFID) or e-money (EMD). Cyprus is often the best country for a crypto license if you need a broad scope of services under one license. For simpler operations, Poland or Czech Republic may offer lighter regimes.

Step-by-Step Application Process

The process to obtain a Cyprus crypto license involves several stages. Consulting24 coordinates each step with local legal partners.

  1. Pre-application assessment: Determine the appropriate license tier and prepare a business plan, AML policies, and risk assessment.
  2. Company incorporation: Register a Cyprus company (if not already established) with a physical office and local directors (or at least one local director).
  3. Document preparation: Compile all required documents: business plan, AML manual, organizational structure, source of funds, and personal questionnaires for key personnel.
  4. Application submission: Submit the application to CySEC along with the application fee. CySEC reviews for completeness within 1-2 months.
  5. Review and interviews: CySEC may request additional information and conduct interviews with directors and compliance officers.
  6. Approval and licensing: Upon approval, pay the license fee and receive the CASP registration. Total time: 6-12 months.

Compliance and Ongoing Obligations

Once licensed, Cyprus CASPs must comply with ongoing requirements under MiCA and local AML laws:

  • Maintain minimum capital at all times
  • Submit annual audited financial statements and compliance reports to CySEC
  • Appoint a local compliance officer and AML officer
  • Conduct regular AML/CFT training
  • Implement transaction monitoring and suspicious activity reporting
  • Undergo periodic CySEC inspections

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Many applicants make errors that delay or derail their Cyprus license application. Avoid these pitfalls:

  • Incomplete documentation: Missing business plans, AML policies, or personal questionnaires cause delays.
  • Underestimating capital: Failing to maintain the required own funds.
  • Ignoring fit-and-proper requirements: Directors with criminal records or poor financial history may be rejected.
  • Overlooking substance: CySEC requires a physical office and local staff (at least one director or manager).
  • Not engaging local experts: Attempting to DIY the application often leads to rejection.
