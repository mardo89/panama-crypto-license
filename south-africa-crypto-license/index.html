What Is the South Africa Crypto License?

The South Africa crypto license, formally known as a Category I or II Financial Services Provider (FSP) license under FAIS, is mandatory for any entity that provides crypto asset services. The FSCA declared crypto assets as financial products in October 2022, and by 2026 the licensing regime is fully operational. CASPs must comply with the same rules as traditional financial service providers, including fit and proper requirements, capital adequacy, and ongoing reporting.

This license covers activities such as:

  • Broking and dealing in crypto assets
  • Providing crypto asset advice
  • Operating a crypto exchange or trading platform
  • Custody or safekeeping of crypto assets
  • Facilitating crypto payments or transfers

The FSCA's approach is risk-based, with higher capital requirements for more complex services. For example, a simple advisory firm might only need Category I, while a full exchange with custody requires Category I with additional conditions. Who Needs a South Africa Crypto License?

Any person or company that, in the ordinary course of business, provides one or more crypto asset services to clients in South Africa must hold a valid FSCA license. This includes:

  • Crypto exchanges (centralized and decentralized if they have a South African nexus)
  • OTC trading desks
  • Crypto asset managers and advisors
  • Custodians and wallet providers
  • Payment processors using crypto

Foreign entities that target South African clients may also need to register. The FSCA has extraterritorial reach if services are directed at residents. For instance, a European exchange offering services to South African residents without a local license could face enforcement. Capital Requirement

The FSCA requires CASPs to maintain minimum capital as per FAIS. For Category I FSPs, the minimum is ZAR 1 million (approx. EUR 50,000). For Category II, it is ZAR 5 million (approx. EUR 250,000). These funds must be held in liquid form and be unencumbered. The capital must be maintained at all times and may be subject to periodic verification.

In addition to initial capital, the FSCA may require a guarantee or professional indemnity insurance. For example, a high-volume exchange may need to hold additional capital proportional to transaction volumes. The exact amount depends on the volume and risk of your operations. Tax Treatment

South Africa treats crypto assets as financial products, and income from crypto activities is subject to normal income tax or capital gains tax, depending on the nature of the transaction. The South African Revenue Service (SARS) has issued guidance that crypto transactions are taxable. Key points:

  • Frequent trading is considered revenue and taxed at income tax rates (up to 45%).
  • Long-term holding may attract capital gains tax (effective rate up to 21.6% for individuals).
  • VAT: Crypto asset services are generally exempt from VAT, but advisory fees may be subject to 15% VAT.
  • Withholding tax: No specific crypto withholding tax, but dividends or interest from crypto may be subject to 20% withholding tax.

For example, a crypto exchange earning trading fees would pay income tax on profits, while a long-term investor would pay CGT on disposal. Allowed Activities

A South Africa crypto license allows the holder to perform the following activities within the scope of their license category:

  • Advising clients on crypto assets (Category I)
  • Broking and dealing in crypto assets (Category I)
  • Managing discretionary crypto asset portfolios (Category II)
  • Operating a crypto exchange (Category I with additional conditions)
  • Providing crypto custody services (Category I)
  • Facilitating crypto payments and transfers (Category I)

Activities not covered include issuing crypto tokens (security token offerings may require additional approvals) and operating a crypto ATM (may require separate registration). The FSCA may also restrict certain activities for smaller licensees. For example, a Category I licensee may not be allowed to offer use trading without specific approval. Compliance and Trust

Once licensed, CASPs must comply with ongoing obligations under FAIS and FICA. These include:

  • Annual financial statements and audit
  • Fit and proper re-assessments every 2 years
  • AML/CFT reporting to the Financial Intelligence Centre (FIC)
  • Record keeping for at least 5 years
  • Regular reporting to the FSCA on financial and operational metrics

Failure to comply can result in fines, suspension, or revocation of the license. For example, missing a FIC report deadline could result in a penalty of up to ZAR 10 million. Common Mistakes to Avoid

Many applicants face delays or rejections due to avoidable errors. Common mistakes include:

  • Incomplete documentation: Missing or poorly drafted AML policies are a top reason for rejection.
  • Incorrect category: Applying for Category II when your activities only require Category I can increase costs and scrutiny.
  • Underestimating timeline: Expecting approval in 3 months when the FSCA often takes 6-12 months.
  • Ignoring fit and proper requirements: Key individuals with past regulatory issues may be disqualified.
  • Poor capital planning: Not having the required capital readily available in liquid form.
