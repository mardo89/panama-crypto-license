What Is a Crypto OTC Desk License in Hong Kong?

A crypto OTC desk license in Hong Kong is a mandatory registration for businesses that facilitate the exchange of virtual assets (cryptocurrencies) directly between buyers and sellers outside of a public order book. These desks are typically used for large trades that require privacy and minimal market impact. The license is issued by the Customs and Excise Department under the AMLO framework, which came into full effect in June 2023. As of 2026, the regime is fully operational and strictly enforced.

Key features of the license include:

  • Regulatory oversight by the C&ED, not the Securities and Futures Commission (SFC).
  • Applicable to both fiat-to-crypto and crypto-to-crypto OTC transactions.
  • Mandatory for any business that actively solicits or provides OTC services to Hong Kong residents.
  • Requires strong AML/CFT policies, including customer due diligence and transaction monitoring.

The license is not a “licence” in the traditional sense but a registration under the AMLO. However, it is commonly referred to as a license. It is valid for one year and must be renewed annually.

Who Needs a Crypto OTC Desk License?

Any person or company that operates a virtual asset OTC desk in or from Hong Kong must be licensed. This includes:

  • OTC brokers that match buyers and sellers for a commission.
  • Market makers that provide liquidity to OTC trades.
  • Cryptocurrency exchanges that offer OTC trading desks.
  • Individual traders who regularly facilitate large OTC transactions for third parties.

Exemptions may apply to businesses that only trade on their own account (not as a service) or that operate entirely outside Hong Kong without targeting Hong Kong residents. However, the C&ED takes a broad view of “carrying on business in Hong Kong,” so even offshore desks that market to Hong Kong clients may need to register.

License Type and Regulator

The crypto OTC desk license in Hong Kong is a registration under the AMLO, administered by the Customs and Excise Department (C&ED). Unlike the SFC’s licensing regime for virtual asset trading platforms (VATP), which applies to centralized exchanges, the C&ED regime covers all other virtual asset service providers, including OTC desks, peer-to-peer platforms, and custodians.

The C&ED is responsible for AML/CFT supervision. It does not regulate the commercial conduct of OTC desks, such as pricing or dispute resolution. However, it requires licensees to implement strict AML/CFT controls, including:

  • Customer due diligence (CDD) for all transactions above HKD 8,000 (approx. EUR 950).
  • Record keeping for at least 7 years.
  • Reporting suspicious transactions to the Joint Financial Intelligence Unit (JFIU).
  • Appointing a compliance officer and a money laundering reporting officer.

The C&ED conducts periodic inspections and can impose fines or revoke the registration for non-compliance.

Capital Requirement

As of 2026, Hong Kong does not impose a statutory minimum capital requirement for crypto OTC desk license applicants. However, the C&ED expects that the business has sufficient financial resources to operate in a sound and prudent manner. In practice, this means you must demonstrate that you have enough capital to cover operational expenses and potential liabilities.

Typical expectations include:

  • At least HKD 500,000 (approx. EUR 60,000) in paid-up capital for a standard OTC desk.
  • Higher amounts may be required if the desk handles large transaction volumes or holds client assets.
  • The C&ED may ask for proof of capital adequacy, such as bank statements or audited accounts.

This is not a fixed requirement but a guideline. Consulting24 can help you structure your capital to meet regulatory expectations.

Tax Treatment

Hong Kong operates a territorial tax system. Only profits sourced in Hong Kong are subject to profits tax at a rate of 16.5% (for corporations). For crypto OTC desks, the tax treatment depends on the nature of the transactions:

  • Trading profits from OTC activities are generally considered ordinary income and subject to profits tax if the trading is carried out in Hong Kong.
  • Capital gains are not taxed, but the distinction between trading and investment is based on facts (frequency, intent, holding period).
  • No VAT, GST, or sales tax applies to crypto transactions.
  • Stamp duty may apply to certain documents but is rarely relevant for OTC trades.

Allowed Activities

A licensed crypto OTC desk in Hong Kong may engage in the following activities:

  • Facilitating the exchange of virtual assets (cryptocurrencies) for fiat currency or other virtual assets on a principal or agency basis.
  • Providing price quotes for large blocks of cryptocurrencies.
  • Executing trades outside of a public order book.
  • Offering custody services for virtual assets in connection with OTC trades (if separately registered as a custodian).

Prohibited activities include:

  • Operating a centralized exchange (requires an SFC VATP license).
  • Dealing in securities or derivatives that are not virtual assets.
  • Providing margin trading or use to clients (unless separately licensed).
  • Engaging in activities that contravene AML/CFT requirements.

The license does not cover initial coin offerings (ICOs) or decentralized finance (DeFi) operations.

Compliance and Ongoing Obligations

Once licensed, OTC desks must comply with ongoing AML/CFT obligations. These include:

  • Conducting customer due diligence for all transactions above HKD 8,000.
  • Monitoring transactions for suspicious activity and reporting to the JFIU.
  • Keeping records for at least 7 years.
  • Submitting annual returns to the C&ED.
  • Undergoing periodic inspections by the C&ED.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Applicants often make these mistakes when applying for a crypto OTC desk license in Hong Kong:

  • Incomplete AML policies: The C&ED requires detailed, practical policies, not generic templates.
  • Underestimating the timeline: Many expect approval in 2 months; realistic is 3-6 months.
  • Ignoring the territorial scope: If you serve Hong Kong clients from abroad, you may still need a license.
  • Poor record keeping: The C&ED expects strong systems from day one.
  • Choosing the wrong corporate structure: A Hong Kong company with a local director is essential.
