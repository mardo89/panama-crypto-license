What is the Thailand Crypto Stablecoin License?

The Thailand crypto stablecoin license is a regulatory approval issued by the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) Thailand under the Digital Asset Business Decree. It allows companies to legally operate a digital asset exchange, broker, dealer, or fund manager involving stablecoins. The license covers both fiat-collateralized stablecoins (e.g., USDT, USDC) and algorithmic stablecoins, subject to additional scrutiny.

Thailand's approach is comprehensive: stablecoins are treated as digital assets unless specifically designated as e-money. The SEC requires a strong risk management framework, AML/CFT compliance, and regular reporting. The license is essential for any entity that wants to offer stablecoin trading pairs, custody, or issuance services to Thai residents.

Key features of the license include a minimum registered capital of THB 5 million (approximately EUR 130,000), a mandatory digital asset custody system, and a requirement to maintain a reserve fund for stablecoin redemption. The SEC also imposes strict marketing and disclosure rules to protect investors.

Who Needs a Thailand Crypto Stablecoin License?

Any business that engages in the following activities with stablecoins in Thailand must obtain a license from the SEC:

  • Operating a digital asset exchange that lists stablecoin trading pairs.
  • Providing stablecoin brokerage or dealer services.
  • Managing a digital asset fund that invests in stablecoins.
  • Issuing a stablecoin (including fiat-backed or algorithmic) to the Thai public.
  • Offering stablecoin custody or wallet services to Thai residents.

Foreign companies targeting Thai users also need a license. The SEC has extraterritorial reach: if you market to Thai customers or have a Thai user base, you must comply. Exemptions exist for limited offerings to institutional investors or via private placements, but these are narrow.

License Type & Regulator

The regulator for crypto stablecoin licensing in Thailand is the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) Thailand. The SEC operates under the Digital Asset Business Decree B.E. 2561 (2018) and subsequent amendments. There are four main license types:

License TypeScope
Digital Asset ExchangeOperating a platform for trading digital assets, including stablecoins.
Digital Asset BrokerBrokering deals between buyers and sellers.
Digital Asset DealerDealing as principal in digital assets.
Digital Asset Fund ManagerManaging portfolios of digital assets for clients.

For stablecoin issuers, the SEC may require additional approval under the Payment System Act if the stablecoin is deemed a payment instrument. As of 2026, the SEC has not issued a separate "stablecoin license" but treats stablecoin activities under the existing digital asset license categories. The capital requirement for exchange/dealer licenses is THB 5 million (approx. EUR 130,000), while fund managers need THB 10 million (approx. EUR 260,000).

Capital Requirement

The minimum registered capital for a digital asset exchange or dealer in Thailand is THB 5 million (approximately EUR 130,000). For fund managers, the requirement is THB 10 million (approx. EUR 260,000). This capital must be fully paid-up and maintained throughout the license period.

In addition to registered capital, the SEC requires licensees to maintain a reserve fund for stablecoin redemption if they issue stablecoins. The reserve must be at least 100% of the outstanding stablecoin value, held in Thai baht or high-quality liquid assets. This is similar to the requirements in other jurisdictions like the EU under MiCA (EUR 50,000-150,000 capital tiers) but with a specific stablecoin reserve.

Capital can be in the form of cash or bank guarantees. The SEC may also impose a net capital requirement based on business volume. Consulting24 can help structure your capital to meet these requirements efficiently.

Tax Treatment

Thailand has a favorable tax regime for digital asset businesses. As of 2026, corporate income tax is 20% on net profits. However, the Revenue Department has issued clarifications that stablecoin transactions are subject to value-added tax (VAT) at 7% for exchange services, but transfers of stablecoins themselves may be exempt from VAT if treated as currency.

For stablecoin issuers, the issuance event is not subject to corporate tax, but any gains from trading or interest income are taxable. Withholding tax on dividends is 10% for local companies. There are no specific crypto taxes beyond the general framework, but the SEC requires detailed transaction reporting to the tax authorities.

Allowed Activities

Under the Thailand Digital Asset Business Decree, licensed entities can engage in the following activities with stablecoins:

  • Operating an exchange for trading stablecoins against other digital assets or fiat.
  • Brokering stablecoin trades.
  • Dealing in stablecoins as principal.
  • Managing investment funds that include stablecoins.
  • Providing custody services for stablecoins.
  • Issuing stablecoins (subject to additional approval from the Bank of Thailand if deemed e-money).

Activities that are not allowed without a license include: lending stablecoins (unless under a securities license), offering derivatives on stablecoins, and using stablecoins for gambling. The SEC also prohibits the use of stablecoins for anonymous transactions; all transfers must be traceable.

Step-by-Step Process

Obtaining a Thailand crypto stablecoin license involves the following steps:

  1. Pre-assessment: Evaluate your business model against SEC requirements. Consulting24 can conduct a gap analysis.
  2. Company registration: Incorporate a Thai company with the Ministry of Commerce. Minimum capital must be deposited.
  3. Prepare documentation: Draft business plan, AML/CFT policy, risk management framework, IT security audit, and shareholder background checks.
  4. Submit application: File with the SEC along with the application fee. The SEC will acknowledge receipt within 30 days.
  5. Review and interview: The SEC reviews the application and may request additional information. A management interview may be required.
  6. Approval: If approved, the SEC issues a license. Conditions may apply (e.g., capital maintenance, reporting).
  7. Post-licensing: Implement ongoing compliance, submit periodic reports, and undergo annual audits.

The entire process typically takes 8-12 months. Consulting24 coordinates with local legal partners to manage the timeline. For a faster route, consider Estonia (3-4 months) or Panama (1-2 months).

Banking & Payments

One of the biggest challenges for crypto businesses in Thailand is opening a bank account. Most major Thai banks (Bangkok Bank, Kasikornbank, Siam Commercial Bank) are wary of crypto-related accounts. However, the SEC has a list of approved banks that accept digital asset licensees. As of 2026, these include some local banks and international branches.

For stablecoin issuers, a reserve account must be maintained with a Thai bank to back the stablecoin. This account must be segregated from operational funds. Payment service providers (PSPs) like Omise and 2C2P offer integration for fiat on-ramps.

Compliance & Trust

Compliance is the cornerstone of the Thailand SEC's approach. Licensees must adhere to strict AML/CFT regulations, including customer due diligence (CDD), transaction monitoring, and suspicious activity reporting. The SEC conducts on-site inspections and can revoke licenses for non-compliance.

For stablecoin issuers, additional requirements include: maintaining a 100% reserve, regular audits by an approved auditor, and public disclosure of reserve composition. The SEC also requires a compliance officer who is a Thai resident and has relevant experience.

Common Mistakes

Many applicants face delays or rejection due to the following common mistakes:

  • Incomplete documentation: Missing business plans, AML policies, or IT security audits.
  • Underestimating capital: Failing to maintain the minimum capital or reserve requirements.
  • Ignoring local presence: The SEC requires a physical office in Thailand and at least one director who is a Thai resident.
  • Overlooking stablecoin-specific rules: Assuming stablecoins are treated the same as other digital assets.
  • Poor banking relationships: Not securing a bank account before applying.
