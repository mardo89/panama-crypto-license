What is a Crypto License in Lithuania and Malta?

A crypto license is an authorization to operate virtual currency exchange or wallet services legally. In Lithuania, the Bank of Lithuania regulates crypto-asset service providers under the Law on Markets in Crypto-Assets (MiCA) implementation. In Malta, the Malta Financial Services Authority (MFSA) oversees licensing under the Virtual Financial Assets (VFA) Act.

Both jurisdictions require compliance with AML/CFT regulations, but the scope of services and capital tiers differ. Lithuania's regime is more streamlined and cost-effective for smaller operations, while Malta offers a broader range of license classes for different business models.

  • Lithuania: CASP license under MiCA, capital EUR 50,000 to 150,000 depending on services.
  • Malta: VFA license (Class 1, 2, 3, 4), capital from EUR 100,000 to over 730,000.

Under MiCA, which is fully in force from 2025, all EU member states must align their regimes. Lithuania has adopted MiCA directly, while Malta's VFA Act remains in place but is being harmonized. For new applicants, the CASP license in Lithuania is the standard, while Malta still issues VFA licenses but may transition to a MiCA-compliant framework by late 2026. Who Needs a Lithuania or Malta Crypto License?

Any business offering crypto exchange, wallet custody, transfer, or trading services to EU residents typically needs a license. Lithuania and Malta are popular for startups and established firms seeking EU passporting rights under MiCA.

  • Startups with limited capital often prefer Lithuania due to lower setup costs and faster processing. For example, a small exchange with EUR 50,000 capital can apply for a simple exchange license in Lithuania, while Malta's minimum for Class 2 is EUR 100,000.
  • Exchanges and custodians may choose Malta for its comprehensive legal framework and higher credibility, especially if they plan to offer multiple services like market making or own-account trading.
  • DeFi projects should assess both regimes carefully, as Malta has specific rules for VFA agents and requires a whitepaper for token offerings, while Lithuania is more flexible for decentralized platforms.

Capital Requirements

Under MiCA, Lithuania requires minimum capital of EUR 50,000 for simple exchange services, EUR 125,000 for custody and exchange, and EUR 150,000 for full-service providers. Malta's VFA Act sets capital at EUR 100,000 for Class 2, EUR 200,000 for Class 3, and EUR 730,000 for Class 4. Both jurisdictions may require additional capital based on risk assessment, such as higher volumes or complex services.

Capital must be maintained in liquid assets (cash or equivalents) and held in a bank account in the jurisdiction or an EU member state. The regulator may require proof of capital at application and during periodic reviews. Consulting24 advises clients on structuring capital to meet regulatory expectations, including options for share capital or retained earnings.

For startups, Lithuania's lower capital threshold is a significant advantage. For example, a simple exchange can start with EUR 50,000, while a Malta Class 2 would require EUR 100,000. However, Malta's higher capital may provide more credibility with partners and banks. Consulting24 helps clients evaluate the trade-off.

Tax Treatment

Lithuania imposes a corporate income tax of 15% (reduced to 5% for small businesses under certain conditions, such as fewer than 10 employees and turnover under EUR 300,000). Crypto transactions are subject to VAT only in specific cases, such as when providing advisory services. Malta offers a corporate tax rate of 35%, but with refund mechanisms effective rate can be as low as 5% for non-resident shareholders through the imputation system. No capital gains tax on crypto in Malta for certain holdings, such as long-term investments.

Both jurisdictions have tax treaties with many countries to avoid double taxation. However, tax treatment of crypto varies by activity: trading, mining, staking, and DeFi may be classified differently. Allowed Activities

Lithuania's CASP license permits exchange between crypto and fiat, exchange between crypto assets, custody, transfer services, and advisory. It also allows staking and lending if not involving principal protection. Malta's VFA license allows similar activities but with more granularity: Class 1 (advice), Class 2 (exchange and custody), Class 3 (market making), Class 4 (full service including own account trading).

Both restrict unregistered securities tokens unless separately regulated under securities law. Lithuania is more flexible for DeFi and NFT platforms, as long as they do not involve regulated financial instruments. Malta has specific rules for VFA agents and requires a whitepaper for token offerings. Step-by-Step Application Process

  1. Pre-application preparation: Define business model, select license class, prepare AML/KYC policies, appoint board members and AML officer, and set up a local registered office.
  2. Documentation: Submit business plan, financial projections for 3 years, source of funds for capital, internal controls, IT security policy, and risk assessment. In Malta, a VFA whitepaper may be required.
  3. Application submission: File with Bank of Lithuania or MFSA, pay the government fee. The regulator will acknowledge receipt and assign a case officer.
  4. Review and interview: Regulator may request clarifications, interview key personnel (CEO, AML officer, board members). This stage can take 1-3 months in Lithuania, 2-6 months in Malta.
  5. Approval: License granted, subject to ongoing supervision. You must maintain compliance and submit periodic reports.

Frequently asked questions

What is the main difference between Lithuania and Malta crypto licenses?

Lithuania's CASP license under MiCA is cheaper and faster (3-6 months, capital from EUR 50,000). Malta's VFA license offers multiple classes, higher capital (EUR 100,000+), and longer timeline (6-12 months). Lithuania suits startups; Malta suits established firms seeking credibility.

Which is cheaper: Lithuania or Malta crypto license?

Lithuania is generally cheaper, with total setup costs (fees + consulting) around EUR 20,000-30,000, versus EUR 35,000-60,000 for Malta. Annual compliance costs are also lower in Lithuania, typically EUR 10,000-20,000 compared to EUR 20,000-40,000 in Malta.

Can I passport a Lithuania CASP license to other EU countries?

Yes, under MiCA, a CASP license from Lithuania allows passporting to all EU/EEA member states, subject to notification procedures. This means you can serve customers across the EU without obtaining separate licenses in each country.

What is the minimum capital for a Malta VFA Class 2 license?

The minimum capital for Class 2 (exchange and custody) is EUR 100,000. Higher classes require more capital: Class 3 (market making) EUR 200,000, Class 4 (full service) EUR 730,000. Capital must be maintained in liquid assets.

How long does it take to get a crypto license in Lithuania vs Malta?

Lithuania typically takes 3-6 months from application to approval. Malta takes 6-12 months due to more extensive review. Both timelines depend on application completeness and regulatory workload.

Do I need a local office and staff for a Lithuania or Malta crypto license?

Yes, both require a physical registered office and at least one local director or manager. In Lithuania, you need a local AML officer; in Malta, a local VFA agent may be required. Consulting24 can help arrange these.

What are the tax rates for crypto companies in Lithuania and Malta?

Lithuania corporate tax is 15% (5% for small businesses). Malta corporate tax is 35% but effective rate can be as low as 5% for non-resident shareholders via refunds. Both have no VAT on most crypto transactions.

Can I offer DeFi or NFT services under a Lithuania or Malta license?

In Lithuania, DeFi and NFT platforms are generally allowed if they do not involve regulated financial instruments. Malta has specific rules; NFTs may be classified as virtual financial assets. Consulting24 can assess your specific case.

What are the ongoing compliance obligations for a Lithuania CASP license?

You must submit annual AML reports, financial statements, and undergo periodic audits. Transaction monitoring and travel rule compliance are required. Consulting24 offers ongoing compliance support.

Is Malta still a good option after FATF grey-listing?

Malta was removed from the FATF grey list in 2022 and has strengthened its AML framework. It remains a reputable jurisdiction, but compliance costs and scrutiny are higher than in Lithuania.
