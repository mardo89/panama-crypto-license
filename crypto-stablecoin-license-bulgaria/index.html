What is a Crypto Stablecoin License in Bulgaria?

A crypto stablecoin license in Bulgaria is an authorization granted by the Bulgarian Financial Supervision Commission (FSC) under the MiCA regulation. It allows entities to issue asset-referenced tokens (ARTs) or e-money tokens (EMTs) - commonly known as stablecoins - and to provide related crypto asset services. The license is fully compliant with EU standards, enabling passporting rights across all 27 member states.

Bulgaria has transposed MiCA into national law, and the FSC acts as the competent authority. The licensing process involves a rigorous assessment of the applicant's governance, capital adequacy, operational resilience, and compliance with anti-money laundering (AML) rules. Once licensed, stablecoin issuers must adhere to ongoing reporting, reserve management, and consumer protection obligations.

For crypto founders, Bulgaria offers a balanced approach: a credible EU regulatory framework without the high costs and lengthy timelines of some other member states. The corporate income tax rate is a flat 10%, one of the lowest in the EU, making it attractive for stablecoin operations.

Who Needs a Crypto Stablecoin License in Bulgaria?

Any entity that intends to issue stablecoins (ARTs or EMTs) or provide crypto asset services in or from Bulgaria needs this license. This includes:

  • Startups planning to launch a new stablecoin pegged to a fiat currency, commodity, or basket of assets.
  • Existing crypto exchanges or wallets that want to offer stablecoin trading or custody.
  • Fintech companies integrating stablecoin payments or remittance services.
  • Decentralized finance (DeFi) projects that issue algorithmic stablecoins (though these may face additional scrutiny).
  • Traditional financial institutions expanding into digital assets.

License Type and Regulator

The regulator for crypto stablecoin licensing in Bulgaria is the Financial Supervision Commission (FSC). Under MiCA, there are two main license types relevant to stablecoins:

  • Asset-Referenced Token (ART) Issuer: For tokens that aim to maintain a stable value by referencing multiple assets (e.g., a basket of currencies or commodities).
  • E-Money Token (EMT) Issuer: For tokens that reference a single fiat currency and function similarly to electronic money.

Additionally, if you provide services such as custody, exchange, or transfer of stablecoins, you may need a Crypto Asset Service Provider (CASP) license. The FSC oversees all these licenses, ensuring compliance with MiCA's capital, governance, and consumer protection requirements.

Bulgaria's approach is aligned with other EU jurisdictions like Lithuania and Estonia, but with a lower corporate tax rate. For a comparison of EU options, see our jurisdictions page.

Capital Requirement for Stablecoin Issuers in Bulgaria

Under MiCA, capital requirements for stablecoin issuers are tiered based on the type of token and the services provided. For Bulgaria, the FSC applies the following minimum capital thresholds:

  • EUR 350,000 for issuers of asset-referenced tokens (ARTs).
  • EUR 350,000 for issuers of e-money tokens (EMTs) - note that this is higher than the standard e-money directive requirement, reflecting MiCA's additional safeguards.
  • EUR 125,000 for CASP license holders (if not issuing stablecoins themselves).

These amounts must be held as own funds, and additional capital may be required based on the size of the reserve and the risk profile of the stablecoin. For precise capital planning, we recommend a consultation with Consulting24.

Tax Treatment for Crypto Stablecoin Businesses in Bulgaria

Bulgaria offers one of the most favorable tax regimes in the EU for crypto businesses:

  • Corporate income tax: Flat 10% on profits.
  • Value-added tax (VAT): Crypto transactions, including stablecoin issuance and exchange, are generally exempt from VAT under EU law.
  • Withholding tax: 5% on dividends (reduced under tax treaties).
  • No capital gains tax for individuals on crypto-to-crypto transactions (though professional trading may be subject to income tax).

This low-tax environment, combined with EU passporting, makes Bulgaria a compelling choice for stablecoin projects. However, tax treatment depends on your specific activities and structure. Always seek professional tax advice.

Allowed Activities Under a Bulgarian Crypto Stablecoin License

A Bulgarian crypto stablecoin license permits a wide range of activities, subject to the scope of your license:

  • Issuance of asset-referenced tokens (ARTs) and e-money tokens (EMTs).
  • Provision of custody and administration of stablecoins on behalf of clients.
  • Operation of a trading platform for stablecoins and other crypto assets.
  • Exchange of stablecoins for fiat currency or other crypto assets.
  • Execution of orders for stablecoins.
  • Transfer services for stablecoins.
  • Advising on stablecoin investments.

Stablecoin issuers must also maintain a reserve of assets equal to the value of tokens in circulation, with strict segregation and transparency requirements. The FSC may impose additional conditions for high-risk or large-scale issuances.

Compliance and Trust: Ensuring Regulatory Integrity

Operating a stablecoin under a Bulgarian license requires strong compliance. The FSC expects:

  • AML/CFT program: Including customer due diligence, transaction monitoring, and suspicious activity reporting.
  • Reserve management: Assets backing the stablecoin must be held with a regulated custodian and audited regularly.
  • Disclosure: White paper approved by the FSC, detailing the token's mechanics, risks, and rights.
  • Governance: Fit and proper management, independent board members, and internal controls.

Failure to comply can result in fines, suspension, or revocation of the license. Consulting24 helps you build a compliance framework that meets FSC expectations. This is general guidance, not legal advice.

Common Mistakes When Applying for a Bulgarian Crypto License

Avoid these pitfalls to ensure a smooth application:

  • Incomplete documentation: Missing policies or unclear business models cause delays.
  • Underestimating capital requirements: Ensure you have the full EUR 350,000 available and documented.
  • Poorly designed tokenomics: The FSC scrutinizes reserve backing and stabilization mechanisms.
  • Ignoring tax obligations: Even with low tax, proper accounting and reporting are mandatory.
  • Choosing the wrong license type: Confusing ART and EMT requirements can lead to rejection.

Frequently asked questions

What is the minimum capital for a stablecoin license in Bulgaria?

The minimum capital is EUR 350,000 for both ART and EMT issuers. This must be held as own funds. Additional capital may be required based on the reserve size.

How long does it take to get a Bulgarian crypto stablecoin license?

The process typically takes 4 to 8 months, depending on the complexity of your application and the FSC's review timeline.

Can I passport a Bulgarian license to other EU countries?

Yes. Under MiCA, a Bulgarian license allows you to provide services across the entire EEA without needing additional licenses in each member state.

What is the corporate tax rate for crypto businesses in Bulgaria?

The corporate income tax rate is a flat 10%, one of the lowest in the EU. Crypto transactions are generally exempt from VAT.

Does Bulgaria require a physical office for a crypto license?

Yes, you need a registered office in Bulgaria. This can be a virtual office provided by your legal advisor or a serviced office space.

Can I issue a stablecoin pegged to the US dollar in Bulgaria?

Yes, as long as it qualifies as an e-money token (EMT) or asset-referenced token (ART) under MiCA. The reserve must be held in a regulated custodian.

What are the ongoing compliance requirements after licensing?

You must submit regular reports to the FSC, including financial statements, reserve attestation, AML reports, and any material changes to the token or business.

Is Panama a better option than Bulgaria for stablecoin issuance?

Panama offers faster setup (1-2 months) and no capital requirement, but it is not EU-regulated. If you need EU passporting, Bulgaria is better. For global operations, Panama is cost-effective.

What happens if I already have a license in another EU country?

You may be able to passport into Bulgaria without a full application. However, if you plan to issue stablecoins, you may need a separate authorization under MiCA.
