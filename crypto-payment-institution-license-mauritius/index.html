What Is the Crypto Payment Institution License in Mauritius?

The crypto payment institution license (also referred to as a virtual asset service provider license) is a regulatory approval issued by the Financial Services Commission (FSC) of Mauritius. It allows companies to offer services such as digital asset custody, exchange, and payment processing under the VAITOS Act. This license is designed to bring crypto activities into the formal financial sector, ensuring compliance with anti-money laundering (AML) and counter-terrorism financing (CTF) standards. It is a mandatory requirement for any entity operating a crypto payment business in or from Mauritius.

Key features include a clear legal framework, a supportive tax environment, and a streamlined application process. The license is valid for one year and must be renewed annually. The FSC maintains a public register of licensed VASPs, which adds credibility and trust for your clients. Who Needs This License?

Any business that provides virtual asset services to clients in Mauritius or from Mauritius requires this license. Typical applicants include:

  • Crypto payment processors facilitating merchant transactions
  • Digital asset exchanges (centralized and peer-to-peer)
  • Custodial wallet providers
  • Token issuers conducting initial token offerings (ITOs)
  • Brokers dealing in virtual assets
  • ATMs and kiosk operators

If your business involves holding, transferring, or exchanging cryptocurrencies for customers, you fall under the VAITOS Act. Even remote companies serving Mauritian clients may need to register. The FSC has issued guidance clarifying that any entity with a place of business in Mauritius or targeting Mauritian residents must be licensed. License Type & Regulator

The regulator is the Financial Services Commission (FSC) of Mauritius, specifically its Virtual Asset and Initial Token Offering Services (VAITOS) division. The license type is a Class 1 or Class 2 Virtual Asset Service Provider (VASP) license, depending on the services offered.

Class 1 covers services where the provider holds client assets, such as custody, exchange, and payment processing. This is the most common license for payment institutions. Class 2 covers advisory and other non-custodial services, such as portfolio management or token advisory. Most payment institutions require a Class 1 license, which involves stricter capital and compliance requirements.

The FSC assesses applications based on fit and proper criteria, business plan viability, AML/CTF policies, and operational capability. The regulator has a reputation for being thorough but fair, with a typical processing time of 3-6 months. Capital Requirement

The minimum capital requirement for a crypto payment institution license in Mauritius is not explicitly fixed by law but is determined by the FSC based on the nature and scale of operations. Typically, the FSC expects a minimum of EUR 50,000 to EUR 100,000 in paid-up capital for Class 1 licenses. For Class 2 licenses, the requirement is lower, often around EUR 25,000.

This capital must be maintained at all times and can be in fiat or virtual assets, subject to valuation rules. The capital serves as a buffer against operational risks and demonstrates financial substance. For example, if you plan to handle large transaction volumes, the FSC may require a higher capital amount. Tax Treatment

Mauritius offers a highly attractive tax regime for licensed crypto businesses. The corporate tax rate is a flat 15%, but with an 80% partial exemption on certain income types, the effective rate can be as low as 3%. This exemption applies to income from virtual asset services, including trading, custody, and payment processing. Additionally, there is no capital gains tax, no withholding tax on dividends, and no VAT on crypto transactions.

Mauritius has a wide network of double taxation treaties, which can further reduce tax liabilities for international operations. The tax environment is a major draw for crypto firms. For example, a company earning EUR 1 million in profit could pay as little as EUR 30,000 in corporate tax, compared to 15-25% in many other jurisdictions. Allowed Activities

Under the VAITOS Act, a licensed crypto payment institution can engage in the following activities:

  • Exchange between virtual assets and fiat currencies
  • Exchange between one or more forms of virtual assets
  • Transfer of virtual assets
  • Safekeeping and administration of virtual assets (custody)
  • Participation in and provision of financial services related to an issuer's offer or sale of a virtual asset
  • Payment services using virtual assets
  • Staking and lending of virtual assets (subject to FSC approval)

These activities are broadly defined, allowing for innovation. However, any activity not explicitly permitted requires prior approval from the FSC. Compliance & Ongoing Obligations

Once licensed, you must comply with ongoing requirements:

  • Annual AML/CTF audit by an approved auditor
  • Submission of annual returns and financial statements to the FSC
  • Maintenance of minimum capital at all times
  • Reporting of suspicious transactions to the Financial Intelligence Unit
  • Renewal of license annually, with a fee and updated documentation
  • Appointment of a local compliance officer (if not already in place)

Failure to comply can result in fines, suspension, or license revocation. For example, the FSC may impose penalties for late filing or inadequate AML controls. Common Mistakes to Avoid

  1. Incomplete documentation: Missing or poorly drafted AML policies cause delays. Ensure your AML manual covers all required elements, such as customer due diligence, transaction monitoring, and record-keeping.
  2. Underestimating capital: The FSC may require more than the minimum, especially if your business model involves high volumes or complex services. Prepare a realistic capital plan.
  3. Ignoring local director requirement: Some structures require a local director. The FSC may insist on at least one director being a resident of Mauritius. Plan for this early.

Alternatives: Mauritius vs Panama vs Lithuania vs Dubai

While Mauritius offers a strong package, other jurisdictions may suit your needs better. Panama provides a flat fee of EUR 6,000 for a crypto license with no minimum capital and no tax on offshore income, but it lacks the regulatory depth of Mauritius and may not be accepted by all banks. Lithuania offers an EU CASP license under MiCA with capital tiers of EUR 50,000-150,000, but requires a local presence and VAT registration. Lithuania is ideal for EU market access but has higher ongoing costs.

Dubai (VARA) offers a comprehensive regime with a 0% corporate tax for qualifying entities, but the application process is more complex and costly, with capital requirements starting at AED 500,000 (approx. EUR 125,000). Dubai is best for firms targeting the Middle East and Asia.

Mauritius is ideal for firms targeting Africa or seeking a low-tax, well-regulated environment. Panama suits those wanting a simple, low-cost setup with minimal compliance.
