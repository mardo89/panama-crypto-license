What Is a Crypto Gambling License in Japan?

Japan does not have a specific "crypto gambling license" per se. Instead, crypto-related businesses must register under the Payment Services Act (PSA) as a Crypto Asset Exchange Service Provider (CAESP). Gambling activities, including online casinos and sports betting, are strictly regulated under the Criminal Code and the Act on Control and Improvement of Amusement Businesses, which generally prohibit gambling. Crypto gambling is not explicitly legalized, and any platform offering gambling services with crypto would likely be considered illegal unless it fits within narrow exemptions (e.g., prize competitions with limited stakes).

In practice, obtaining a CAESP registration does not authorize gambling. The FSA requires all crypto exchanges to comply with anti-money laundering (AML) and know-your-customer (KYC) rules, and any gambling-related transactions are scrutinized. As of 2026, no licensed crypto exchange in Japan is permitted to offer gambling services. Therefore, a "crypto gambling license" in Japan is effectively unavailable for most operators.

For those seeking a clear legal framework for crypto gambling, jurisdictions like Panama offer a more straightforward path, with a flat fee of EUR 6,000 for a crypto license that covers gambling activities.

Who Needs a Crypto Gambling License in Japan?

Typically, any business that wants to operate a crypto exchange, wallet service, or custodial service in Japan must register with the FSA. However, if your business model involves gambling, you will likely not be able to obtain a license. The only entities that might consider Japan are those offering non-gambling crypto services (e.g., exchange, custody) and who want to serve Japanese residents. Even then, the process is rigorous.

Founders who are considering Japan for a crypto gambling platform should be aware of the high barriers. License Type and Regulator in Japan

The relevant regulator is the Financial Services Agency (FSA), Japan's primary financial watchdog. The license type for crypto businesses is the Crypto Asset Exchange Service Provider (CAESP) registration. This registration is mandatory for any entity that exchanges crypto assets for fiat, manages crypto assets for customers, or provides a crypto wallet.

There is no separate license for gambling. The FSA strictly prohibits crypto exchanges from engaging in gambling activities. Additionally, the Japan Casino Regulatory Commission oversees land-based casinos (integrated resorts) but does not cover online crypto gambling. As of 2026, online crypto gambling remains unregulated and effectively illegal.

Capital Requirement for Japan Crypto License

The FSA requires crypto exchanges to maintain a minimum capital of JPY 10 million (approximately EUR 65,000) for registration. However, in practice, the FSA expects much higher capital to ensure operational stability. Most successful applicants have capital of at least JPY 100 million (EUR 650,000) or more. Additionally, the FSA may require a security deposit (guarantee bond) equivalent to a percentage of customer funds, which can be substantial.

For gambling-related activities, no specific capital requirement exists because such activities are not licensed. In Panama, the capital requirement is minimal and often satisfied by the company's share capital. For example, a Panama corporation can be set up with a nominal capital of USD 10,000, and no additional deposit is needed for the crypto license. This low barrier is a major advantage for startups.

Tax Treatment for Crypto Gambling in Japan

Japan taxes crypto gains as miscellaneous income, with progressive rates up to 55% (including local taxes). For corporations, the effective corporate tax rate is around 30%. However, gambling income is treated differently: if a platform operates illegally, it may face seizure of assets and criminal penalties. There is no favorable tax regime for crypto gambling.

In contrast, Panama offers territorial taxation: only income sourced within Panama is taxed. Offshore crypto gambling income is tax-free. This makes Panama a popular choice for crypto gambling operators. For more details, see our requirements page. Additionally, Panama has no capital gains tax, no withholding tax on dividends, and no VAT, which further enhances its appeal.

Allowed Activities Under a Japan Crypto License

Under a CAESP registration, allowed activities include:

  • Exchange of crypto assets for fiat currency
  • Exchange of one crypto asset for another
  • Custody and wallet services
  • Brokerage of crypto assets

Gambling, betting, casino games, and lottery are strictly prohibited. The FSA also prohibits lending or staking services that resemble gambling. Any platform that offers gambling will not be granted registration and may face enforcement action.

Step-by-Step Process for Japan Crypto License

The process to register as a CAESP with the FSA is rigorous and involves multiple stages:

  1. Pre-application preparation: Establish a Japanese entity (e.g., Kabushiki Kaisha), appoint a local representative, and draft business plans and AML policies.
  2. System development: Build a compliant trading platform with strong security, KYC, and transaction monitoring.
  3. Application submission: Submit a detailed application to the FSA, including financial statements, system descriptions, and compliance manuals.
  4. FSA review and interviews: The FSA conducts a thorough review, often taking 6-12 months, including on-site inspections and interviews with management.
  5. Registration and ongoing compliance: Once approved, the exchange must submit regular reports and undergo annual audits.

For gambling operators, this process is not viable. Consulting24 advises clients to consider alternative jurisdictions. For a streamlined process, see our application process page. In Panama, the process is far simpler: incorporate a company, apply for the license, and complete compliance checks within weeks.

Banking and Payment Partners in Japan

Japanese banks are generally cautious about working with crypto businesses. Only a few banks, such as SBI Sumishin Net Bank and some regional banks, provide services to registered crypto exchanges. Gambling-related businesses are almost universally rejected. Crypto gambling operators often face difficulty opening bank accounts in Japan.

In Panama, banking is more accessible, and many international payment processors support crypto gambling. Benefits of a Japan Crypto License (for Non-Gambling)

For non-gambling crypto businesses, a Japan license offers:

  • Access to one of the largest crypto markets in Asia
  • High trust and credibility with Japanese customers
  • Clear regulatory framework under the FSA

However, for gambling, the benefits are nonexistent. Operators should look elsewhere. Even for non-gambling, the high cost and long timeline may deter startups. Many choose to first establish in a more flexible jurisdiction like Panama or Estonia and later expand to Japan if needed.

The process takes 12 to 18 months on average. This includes preparation, application submission, FSA review, and on-site inspections. The timeline can extend if the application is incomplete or if the FSA requests additional information.

The FSA requires a minimum capital of JPY 10 million (about EUR 65,000), but in practice, applicants need significantly more, often JPY 100 million or higher, to demonstrate financial stability.
