What Is the Crypto Fund License Czech Republic?

The crypto fund license in the Czech Republic is a regulatory authorisation issued by the Czech National Bank (CNB) for investment funds that focus on crypto assets. It falls under the Alternative Investment Fund Managers Directive (AIFMD) regime, adapted for digital assets. The license allows the fund to raise capital from investors and invest in cryptocurrencies, tokens, and other digital assets.

Unlike a simple crypto exchange or wallet license, a fund license involves ongoing supervision, reporting, and compliance with investor protection rules. The Czech Republic has been proactive in regulating crypto funds, making it an attractive jurisdiction for fund managers within the EU.

As of 2026, the Czech Republic has fully implemented MiCA (Markets in Crypto-Assets Regulation). However, for funds, the AIFMD framework remains primary, with additional crypto-specific requirements from the CNB. The license is categorised as a “qualified investor fund” or “specialised fund” depending on the investor base. This distinction affects the applicable rules and capital requirements.

Funds licensed under this regime can invest in a wide range of digital assets, including coins, tokens, stablecoins, and security tokens. They can also engage in staking, lending, and active trading. The license is designed for professional fund managers who want to offer a regulated product to institutional and high-net-worth investors.

If you manage a collective investment scheme that invests in crypto, you likely need this license. Exemptions exist for small funds (below EUR 100 million assets under management, or EUR 500 million if unused and no redemption rights for 5 years), but most professional funds will require full authorisation.

Similarly, a blockchain venture capital firm that pools capital from limited partners to invest in token projects must be licensed. Even family offices that manage assets for multiple family members on a commercial basis may fall under the licensing requirement.

License Type and Regulator

The license is a type of Alternative Investment Fund Manager (AIFM) license, specifically for crypto assets. The regulator is the Czech National Bank (Česká národní banka, CNB). The CNB oversees the fund's compliance with the AIFMD, anti-money laundering (AML) rules, and investor protection.

As of 2026, the Czech Republic has fully implemented MiCA (Markets in Crypto-Assets Regulation). However, for funds, the AIFMD framework remains primary, with additional crypto-specific requirements from the CNB. The license is categorised as a “qualified investor fund” or “specialised fund” depending on the investor base.

The CNB is a respected regulator within the EU. It conducts thorough due diligence on applicants, including background checks on key personnel, review of the fund's business plan, and assessment of risk management systems. The regulator also requires the fund to have a depositary (usually a bank) to safeguard assets. For crypto funds, the depositary role can be challenging due to the nature of digital assets, but the CNB accepts qualified custodians that meet specific standards.

The license is valid indefinitely, subject to ongoing compliance. The CNB may conduct periodic inspections and requires annual reports, audited financial statements, and regular AML updates. Failure to comply can result in fines or revocation of the license.

Capital Requirement

The minimum capital requirement for a crypto fund license in the Czech Republic depends on the fund's structure and assets under management. For an AIFM managing a fund with assets under management (AUM) below EUR 250 million, the initial capital is typically EUR 125,000. For larger funds or those with discretionary management, the requirement can be EUR 150,000 or more.

These figures align with MiCA's capital tiers for CASPs, but for funds, the AIFMD baseline applies. Additional own funds may be required based on operational risks. The capital must be held in liquid assets and cannot be used for investment purposes.

For example, a fund with AUM of EUR 100 million would need initial capital of EUR 125,000. If the fund also provides discretionary portfolio management, the requirement increases to EUR 150,000. The capital must be deposited in a Czech bank account and evidenced to the CNB as part of the application.

It is important to note that the capital requirement is separate from the fund's AUM. The fund must maintain this capital at all times. If the fund's AUM grows significantly, the CNB may require additional capital. Consulting24 advises clients to maintain a buffer above the minimum to avoid compliance issues.

Tax Treatment

The Czech Republic offers a favourable tax regime for investment funds. Crypto funds are generally subject to a 5% corporate income tax on their profits, provided they meet the conditions of a qualified investor fund. Capital gains from crypto trading within the fund are tax-exempt if the fund holds the assets for more than 3 years (for individuals) or 5 years (for corporations).

Investors are taxed on distributions and capital gains at their personal tax rates (15% for individuals, 19% for corporations). Withholding tax on dividends is 15% (reduced under double tax treaties). VAT is not applicable on fund management services. Consulting24 recommends a detailed tax review with a local advisor.

For example, a fund that trades crypto actively and realises gains within a year would pay 5% corporate tax on those gains. If the fund holds a token for 4 years and sells at a profit, that gain is tax-exempt at the fund level. Investors who receive dividends from the fund pay 15% withholding tax, unless a lower treaty rate applies.

The Czech tax system also allows for loss carryforwards, which can offset future profits. Funds structured as qualified investor funds benefit from these rules, making the Czech Republic one of the most tax-efficient EU jurisdictions for crypto funds.

Allowed Activities

  • Managing a portfolio of crypto assets (coins, tokens, stablecoins).
  • Investing in initial coin offerings (ICOs) and security token offerings (STOs).
  • Staking and lending crypto assets to generate yield.
  • Trading on behalf of the fund (active management).
  • Providing custody of crypto assets (if licensed separately).
  • Marketing fund shares to qualified investors within the EU.

The fund cannot offer retail banking services or operate as an exchange. It must restrict its activities to investment management. The CNB may impose additional conditions based on the fund's strategy.

For example, a fund that wants to engage in staking must have strong risk management procedures to handle slashing risks. A fund that invests in ICOs must conduct thorough due diligence on each project. The CNB expects the fund to have a clear investment policy that outlines these activities.

Additionally, the fund can use derivatives and use, subject to limits set in the fund's prospectus. The CNB requires that all activities are in the best interest of investors and that conflicts of interest are managed appropriately.

Step-by-Step Application Process

  1. Pre-application consultation: Discuss your fund structure with Consulting24 to determine the best approach. We assess your eligibility and recommend the optimal legal form.
  2. Company setup: Incorporate a Czech company (typically a joint-stock company or limited partnership) with the appropriate legal form for a fund. This involves registering with the Commercial Register and obtaining a tax ID.
  3. Prepare documentation: Draft the fund's prospectus, risk management policy, AML/KYC procedures, and business plan. These documents must be comprehensive and tailored to your fund's strategy.
  4. Capital deposit: Deposit the minimum capital in a Czech bank account and obtain a confirmation from the bank. The bank will conduct due diligence on the source of funds.
  5. Submit application: File the application with the CNB, including all required documents and fees. The CNB will acknowledge receipt and begin its review.
  6. CNB review: The CNB assesses the application, may request clarifications, and conducts interviews with key personnel. This stage can take 2-4 months.
  7. Approval and licensing: If approved, the CNB issues the license. The fund can then begin operations. You will receive a license certificate.
  8. Ongoing compliance: Submit regular reports, maintain AML procedures, and undergo periodic audits. The CNB may conduct on-site inspections.

Alternatively, specialised payment institutions like Panama-based providers or EU-licensed EMI accounts can be used.

Compliance and Trust

Obtaining a crypto fund license in the Czech Republic requires a strong compliance framework. The CNB expects funds to have a designated AML officer, regular risk assessments, and independent audits. Consulting24 advises on structuring compliance to meet CNB standards. This is general guidance, not legal advice. Trust is built through transparency. The CNB publishes a list of licensed funds, and investors can verify your status. Maintaining clean records and timely reporting is critical to avoid sanctions.

For example, the fund must conduct ongoing monitoring of transactions and report suspicious activities to the Financial Analytical Unit (FAU). The AML officer must be a Czech resident or a person with sufficient knowledge of local regulations. The fund's risk assessment must be updated annually.

Compliance also extends to the fund's service providers. The depositary, auditor, and any external advisors must be approved by the CNB. The fund must have written agreements with all service providers that outline their responsibilities. Consulting24 helps you select and vet these providers.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Underestimating capital requirements: Ensure you have the full capital available before applying. The CNB will verify the deposit.
  • Incomplete AML procedures: The CNB scrutinises AML policies; generic templates are not enough. Tailor your AML programme to your fund's specific activities.
  • Poorly drafted prospectus: The fund's offering document must be clear and compliant with AIFMD. It should include all material risks and costs.
  • Ignoring tax obligations: Even if the fund is tax-exempt, filing requirements must be met. Late filings can result in penalties.
  • Choosing the wrong legal structure: The fund's legal form impacts licensing and tax. Get advice early. For example, a limited partnership may be more suitable than a joint-stock company for certain investors.
  • Not preparing for the interview: The CNB may interview key personnel. Be ready to explain your business model and compliance knowledge.

Frequently asked questions

What is the minimum capital for a crypto fund license in the Czech Republic?

The minimum capital is typically EUR 125,000 for funds with AUM below EUR 250 million, and EUR 150,000 for larger funds or those with discretionary management. This capital must be held in a Czech bank account and cannot be used for investments.

How long does it take to get a crypto fund license in the Czech Republic?

The process takes 4 to 9 months, depending on the complexity of the fund and the completeness of the application. The CNB's review typically takes 2-4 months, but preparation and company setup can add additional time.

What is the tax rate for crypto funds in the Czech Republic?

Qualified investor funds pay a 5% corporate income tax on profits. Capital gains on assets held for more than 3 years (individuals) or 5 years (corporations) are tax-exempt. Investors pay 15% tax on dividends and capital gains.

Can I passport the Czech crypto fund license to other EU countries?

Yes, under the AIFMD, once licensed in the Czech Republic, you can market the fund to qualified investors across the EU without additional licensing in each country. This is a key benefit of the Czech license.

What activities can a Czech crypto fund engage in?

The fund can manage a portfolio of crypto assets, invest in ICOs/STOs, stake and lend crypto, trade actively, and market shares to qualified investors. It cannot operate as an exchange or offer retail banking services.

What are the ongoing compliance requirements?
