What Is a Cyprus Crypto License?

A Cyprus crypto license, formally a Crypto Asset Service Provider (CASP) authorisation under MiCA, is a regulatory permit issued by CySEC. It allows firms to legally offer crypto-related services such as exchange, custody, and wallet management within Cyprus and across the EU via passporting. Under MiCA, all CASPs must comply with uniform rules on capital, governance, market abuse prevention, and consumer protection. The license is essential for any crypto business that wants to operate legally in the European Economic Area (EEA) after the MiCA transition period ends in 2026.

Cyprus has positioned itself as a pro-business hub with a pragmatic regulator. Unlike some EU states that have imposed additional national requirements, Cyprus largely aligns with MiCA’s baseline, making the process predictable. The license covers both fiat-to-crypto and crypto-to-crypto services, as well as the safekeeping and administration of crypto assets on behalf of clients. Who Needs a Cyprus Crypto License?

Any entity providing one or more of the following services in or from Cyprus must hold a CASP license under MiCA:

  • Exchange of crypto assets for fiat currency
  • Exchange of crypto assets for other crypto assets
  • Transfer of crypto assets on behalf of clients
  • Custody and administration of crypto assets (including wallet services)
  • Placing of crypto assets (underwriting or placing without firm commitment)
  • Reception and transmission of orders for crypto assets
  • Advice on crypto assets

This applies to both startups and established firms that plan to serve EU clients. If you already hold a license in another EU member state, you may be able to passport into Cyprus, but direct authorisation is often simpler for new entrants. Non-EU firms seeking EU market access also benefit from establishing a Cyprus subsidiary and obtaining the license locally.

License Type & Regulator

The Cyprus crypto license is a single CASP authorisation under MiCA, regulated by the Cyprus Securities and Exchange Commission (CySEC). CySEC is a well-respected EU regulator with a track record of overseeing investment firms, fund managers, and now crypto asset service providers. The license is categorised by the services offered, but all CASPs must meet the same core requirements under MiCA.

Unlike some jurisdictions that offer multiple tiers (e.g., Estonia’s virtual currency service license), Cyprus follows a unified regime. However, capital requirements vary based on the services provided and the nature of the crypto assets held. CySEC also requires that the firm’s management body includes at least two persons with sufficient expertise and good repute. The regulator conducts a thorough fit-and-proper test for all senior managers and significant shareholders.

Capital Requirement

Under MiCA, capital requirements for Cyprus CASPs are tiered based on the services offered. The minimum initial capital must be maintained at all times:

  • EUR 50,000 - for firms only providing exchange services between crypto assets (no fiat) and/or reception/transmission of orders.
  • EUR 125,000 - for firms providing custody and administration of crypto assets, transfer services, or placing without firm commitment.
  • EUR 150,000 - for firms providing all the above services, including exchange with fiat currency.

These amounts are lower than the capital required for a full investment firm license but still ensure that the firm has adequate financial resources. Capital can be held in cash or high-quality liquid assets. CySEC may also require additional capital based on the firm’s risk profile, as assessed during the application process.

Tax Treatment

Cyprus offers one of the most attractive corporate tax regimes in the EU, with a standard corporate income tax rate of just 12.5%. This applies to profits from crypto asset services, including trading fees, custody fees, and staking income. There is no capital gains tax on the sale of crypto assets held as investments, though trading profits are taxable. VAT is not applicable on crypto-to-crypto exchanges, but fiat conversions may be subject to VAT at 19%.

Additionally, Cyprus has a notional interest deduction on new equity, which can reduce effective tax rates further. The country also has an extensive network of double tax treaties, making it a preferred holding jurisdiction. For a comparison, see our Cyprus vs Lithuania guide.

Allowed Activities Under the Cyprus Crypto License

A Cyprus CASP license permits the following activities, as defined by MiCA:

  • Exchange of crypto assets for fiat currency
  • Exchange of crypto assets for other crypto assets
  • Transfer of crypto assets on behalf of clients
  • Custody and administration of crypto assets (including wallet services)
  • Placing of crypto assets (without firm commitment)
  • Reception and transmission of orders for crypto assets
  • Advice on crypto assets

Notably, the license does not cover the issuance of asset-referenced tokens (ARTs) or e-money tokens, which require separate authorisation under MiCA. Firms also cannot engage in proprietary trading with client assets. The license is strictly for service provision to third parties. If you plan to offer margin trading or derivatives, a separate Cyprus Investment Firm (CIF) license may be needed.

Step-by-Step Application Process

Obtaining a Cyprus crypto license involves several stages. Consulting24 advises and coordinates each step:

  1. Pre-incorporation & business plan - Define your services, target market, and compliance framework. Prepare a detailed business plan, including financial projections and risk management policies.
  2. Company incorporation - Register a Cyprus limited liability company (Ltd) with a registered office and at least two directors (individuals). The company must have substance in Cyprus.
  3. Drafting of policies - Develop AML/KYC procedures, custody policies, IT security protocols, and a complaints handling policy. These must align with CySEC’s requirements.
  4. Appointment of key personnel - Hire a compliance officer, a money laundering reporting officer (MLRO), and senior managers. All must pass CySEC’s fit-and-proper test.
  5. Submission to CySEC - File the application with all supporting documents, including the company’s constitutional documents, policies, and personal questionnaires for key individuals.
  6. CySEC review & interview - The regulator may request additional information or schedule an interview with the management team. This typically takes 3-6 months.
  7. Approval & ongoing compliance - Once approved, the firm must submit periodic reports, maintain capital, and undergo annual audits.

Banking & Payment Solutions

Opening a bank account for a Cyprus CASP can be challenging due to the perceived risk of crypto businesses. However, several banks in Cyprus and the wider EU accept crypto clients, provided they have a valid license. Options include:

  • Traditional Cypriot banks (e.g., Bank of Cyprus, Hellenic Bank) - require a license and a physical presence.
  • EU-based fintech banks (e.g., PayrNet, Satchel) - offer faster onboarding but may have limits on crypto transactions.
  • EMI (Electronic Money Institution) accounts - suitable for fiat on/off ramps but not for custody.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Many applicants face delays or rejections due to avoidable errors. Here are the most common pitfalls:

  • Insufficient substance - CySEC expects a real physical presence in Cyprus, including a registered office, local directors, and employees. A mailbox company will not pass.
  • Poorly drafted policies - AML/KYC procedures must be tailored to your business model, not copied from generic templates.
  • Unqualified management - Key personnel must have relevant experience and clean criminal records. CySEC conducts thorough background checks.
  • Underestimating capital - Ensure you have the required capital fully paid up before submission.
  • Ignoring MiCA transition rules - If you already operate in Cyprus under a temporary regime, you must apply for full authorisation before the deadline.

Frequently asked questions

How long does it take to get a Cyprus crypto license?

Typically 6-12 months from company incorporation to CySEC approval. The regulator has a 3-month statutory review period, but preparation and company setup take additional time.

What is the minimum capital for a Cyprus CASP?

Under MiCA, capital tiers are EUR 50,000, 125,000, or 150,000 depending on services offered. The highest tier applies if you exchange crypto for fiat.

Can I passport my Cyprus license to other EU countries?

Yes. Under MiCA, a Cyprus CASP can provide services across the EEA without additional authorisation, subject to notification to CySEC.

Does Cyprus require a physical office?

Yes. CySEC expects a registered office and substance in Cyprus, including local directors and employees. A virtual office alone is insufficient.

What are the tax benefits of Cyprus for crypto firms?

Corporate tax is 12.5%, with no capital gains tax on crypto asset sales. Cyprus also offers a notional interest deduction and extensive double tax treaties.

Can Consulting24 guarantee approval of my Cyprus license?

No. We provide expert advisory and coordination, but CySEC makes the final decision. Our structured approach maximises your chances.

What is the difference between a Cyprus CASP and a CIF?

A CASP is for crypto asset services under MiCA. A Cyprus Investment Firm (CIF) is for traditional financial instruments like securities and derivatives. Some activities may require both.

Is English sufficient for the Cyprus application process?

Yes. CySEC accepts applications in English, and business in Cyprus is conducted primarily in English.

How does Cyprus compare to Lithuania for crypto licensing?

Lithuania is faster (3-6 months) and cheaper, but has a higher corporate tax (15%) and stricter substance requirements. Cyprus offers a lower tax rate and a more established regulator.

What happens if I already operate in Cyprus without a license?

You must apply for a CASP license before the MiCA transition deadline. Operating without authorisation can result in penalties and forced closure.
