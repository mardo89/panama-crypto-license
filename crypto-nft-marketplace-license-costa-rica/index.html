What Is a Crypto NFT Marketplace License in Costa Rica?

This license allows a company to legally operate a platform where users can buy, sell, and trade non-fungible tokens (NFTs) and other crypto assets. In Costa Rica, the license is part of the broader "Virtual Assets Service Provider" (VASP) registration under SUGEF. It covers marketplaces that facilitate peer-to-peer transactions, including primary sales and secondary trading of NFTs.

The regime is designed to balance innovation with anti-money laundering (AML) compliance. Unlike some jurisdictions that require a physical office or local staff, Costa Rica permits remote management, making it attractive for international teams. The license is not a securities license; it focuses on the exchange and custody of virtual assets as defined by local law. For example, an NFT marketplace that allows users to trade digital art for ETH or USDC falls under this license, while a platform that issues tokenised securities would need separate registration with the securities regulator. The license is valid indefinitely, subject to annual compliance filings and payment of the annual legal representation fee (included in the first year, then typically EUR 1,500-2,000 per year).

Who Needs This License?

Any entity operating an NFT marketplace that handles crypto assets for users in or from Costa Rica must obtain this license. Typical applicants include:

  • NFT trading platforms (e.g., OpenSea-style marketplaces)
  • Launchpads for NFT collections
  • Marketplaces for tokenised real estate or art
  • Platforms offering fractional NFT ownership
  • Businesses providing custody of NFTs and crypto for marketplace users

If your platform only lists fiat-denominated digital goods (e.g., standard e-commerce with no crypto), you generally do not need this license. However, any involvement with crypto payments, wallet services, or token issuance triggers the requirement. For instance, if your NFT marketplace allows users to pay with Bitcoin or Ethereum, or if you hold private keys for user wallets, you fall under VASP regulation. Even if your platform is based outside Costa Rica, if you have users in Costa Rica or market to Costa Rican residents, you may need to register. License Type and Regulator

The relevant authority is the General Superintendence of Financial Entities (SUGEF), which oversees the registration of Virtual Asset Service Providers (VASPs). The license is a "VASP registration" specifically for exchange and custody services, which covers NFT marketplace operations.

There is no separate "NFT license" - the activity falls under the broader crypto asset service provider category. The registration is valid indefinitely, subject to annual compliance filings. SUGEF requires the company to have a local legal representative and a registered office address (which Consulting24 can arrange). The regulator follows FATF recommendations and expects VASPs to implement strong AML/CFT controls. As of 2026, Costa Rica has not yet enacted a separate crypto-specific law; the FinTech law of 2020 provides the framework, and SUGEF has issued binding criteria for VASP registration. This means the regulatory environment is still evolving, but the current regime is stable and operational.

Capital Requirement

Costa Rica imposes no mandatory minimum capital for VASP registration. This is a major advantage over EU jurisdictions where MiCA requires EUR 50,000 (for exchange services) up to EUR 150,000 (for custody of client assets). You can incorporate with a nominal capital (e.g., EUR 1,000) and increase later if needed. There is no requirement to maintain a specific amount of liquid assets.

However, SUGEF may expect the company to have sufficient financial resources to cover operational expenses and potential liabilities. The regulator does not publish a fixed threshold, so the capital is determined on a case-by-case basis during the application review. Consulting24 advises clients on a reasonable capital amount based on their business plan. For example, if your marketplace plans to hold significant user funds in custody, a higher capital (e.g., EUR 10,000-20,000) may be prudent to demonstrate financial stability. In practice, most applicants incorporate with EUR 1,000-5,000 and are approved without issue.

Tax Treatment

Costa Rica does not tax capital gains on crypto assets, including profits from NFT trading. The country operates a territorial tax system: income sourced outside Costa Rica is generally exempt. For a Costa Rican company that manages an NFT marketplace, the following tax rules apply:

  • Corporate income tax: 30% on locally sourced income. However, most marketplace revenue (fees from users abroad) is considered foreign-source and thus not taxed.
  • Withholding tax: 15% on dividends paid to non-residents, but can be reduced under tax treaties.
  • VAT: 13% on services provided to Costa Rican residents. Services to non-residents are zero-rated.
  • No wealth tax, no CFC rules, no stamp duty.

This tax framework makes Costa Rica a highly efficient jurisdiction for global NFT platforms. Consulting24 recommends a tax review with a local accountant to optimise structure. For example, if your marketplace charges a 2.5% fee on each trade, and 90% of users are outside Costa Rica, only 10% of revenue is subject to Costa Rican corporate tax. Additionally, there is no capital gains tax on the sale of NFTs held by the company, which is a significant benefit compared to jurisdictions like the US or UK.

Allowed Activities Under the License

The Costa Rica VASP registration permits the following activities for NFT marketplaces:

  • Operation of an electronic platform for buying and selling NFTs (primary and secondary markets)
  • Custody of crypto assets (including NFTs) on behalf of users
  • Exchange of crypto assets for fiat or other crypto
  • Wallet services (hot and cold storage)
  • Token issuance (subject to securities law exemption)
  • Staking or lending of crypto (if not constituting a security)

Activities that are not allowed without additional licensing: operating a bank, issuing securities (unless registered with the securities regulator), or offering derivatives. The license does not cover gambling or gaming-related NFTs unless explicitly approved. For example, if your platform offers NFT-based betting, you would need a separate gambling license. Similarly, if you issue NFTs that represent equity in a company, that may be considered a security and require registration with the Costa Rican securities regulator (SUGEVAL). Compliance and Trust

Operating an NFT marketplace requires strong AML/KYC procedures. Costa Rica's VASP regime mandates:

  • Customer due diligence (CDD) for all users, including identity verification and beneficial ownership checks.
  • Transaction monitoring and reporting of suspicious activity to the Financial Analysis Unit (UAF).
  • Appointment of a compliance officer (can be outsourced) who is responsible for AML program implementation.
  • Annual AML audit by an external auditor.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Founders often make these errors when applying for a Costa Rica crypto license:

  • Incomplete business plan: SUGEF requires a detailed description of your platform, revenue model, and risk management. A generic plan can lead to rejection or requests for clarification.
  • Ignoring AML requirements: Even if you are a small marketplace, you must have written policies. Some founders assume that because Costa Rica is not in the EU, AML rules are lax - this is false. SUGEF strictly enforces FATF standards.
  • Using a shell company: The regulator expects substance - a registered office and a local representative are mandatory. A PO box is not sufficient.
  • Not budgeting for ongoing costs: Annual compliance fees, legal representation, and accounting can add EUR 2,000-3,000 per year. Ensure you have a budget for these.
  • Assuming no tax liability: While foreign income is exempt, local sales (e.g., to Costa Rican users) are subject to VAT and income tax. You must register for VAT if your local sales exceed the threshold (approx. EUR 30,000 per year).
