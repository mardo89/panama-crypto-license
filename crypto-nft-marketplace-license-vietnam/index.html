Overview: What Is a Crypto NFT Marketplace License in Vietnam?

Vietnam currently lacks a specific license category for crypto or NFT marketplaces. The government has not yet enacted a comprehensive digital asset law. Instead, crypto activities fall under general business registration, subject to restrictions on using crypto as payment. The State Bank of Vietnam prohibits the issuance, supply, and use of virtual currencies as legal tender. However, owning and trading crypto as a commodity or asset is not explicitly illegal, creating a grey area.

For NFT marketplaces, the legal status is similarly uncertain. NFTs are often treated as digital assets under the Law on Information Technology, but no specific licensing regime exists. This means that operating a crypto NFT marketplace in Vietnam carries regulatory risk, including potential crackdowns. Many founders therefore choose to incorporate in a licensed jurisdiction like Panama, where a flat fee of EUR 6,000 covers company setup and license application, with a clear regulatory framework.

In contrast, regulated jurisdictions such as Lithuania under MiCA require a CASP license with capital tiers of EUR 50,000 to EUR 150,000. Estonia's VASP license requires EUR 12,000 paid-up capital. These frameworks offer legal certainty and access to banking, which Vietnam cannot provide. Consulting24 can help you evaluate which jurisdiction aligns with your business model.

Tax Treatment for NFT Marketplaces in Vietnam

Vietnam taxes corporate income at 20% and VAT at 10% (0% for exported services). Crypto transactions are not explicitly exempt, so revenue from NFT sales may be subject to standard taxes. Personal income tax on crypto gains is unclear, but capital gains from securities are taxed at 0.1% of sale price. For businesses, it is advisable to consult a local tax advisor.

Panama offers territorial taxation: only income sourced within Panama is taxed. Offshore companies pay no tax on foreign-sourced income. Lithuania taxes corporate income at 15% (0% for small companies under certain conditions). Estonia has a 20% corporate income tax, but only on distributed profits.
