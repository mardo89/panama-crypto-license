What is the Gibraltar Crypto Broker License?

The Gibraltar crypto broker license is a regulatory authorisation issued by the Gibraltar Financial Services Commission (GFSC) under the Financial Services (Distributed Ledger Technology Providers) Regulations 2017. It allows firms to conduct DLT activities, including operating a crypto broker, exchange, or custodian wallet service. Gibraltar was one of the first jurisdictions to introduce a bespoke framework for DLT, making it a pioneer in crypto regulation.

The license is technology-neutral and covers a range of services, from order matching to safekeeping of client assets. Unlike some EU member states now operating under MiCA, Gibraltar (as a British Overseas Territory) maintains its own regulatory regime, which is recognised as equivalent by many international partners. This gives firms flexibility while ensuring high standards of investor protection and anti-money laundering (AML) compliance.

Gibraltar's DLT framework has been in place since 2018 and has attracted a number of well-known crypto firms. The GFSC regularly updates its guidance to reflect market developments, including rules on stablecoins and decentralised finance (DeFi) activities. For founders, this means a predictable and mature regulatory environment.

Who Needs a Gibraltar Crypto Broker License?

Any firm that intends to provide brokerage, exchange, or custody services in or from Gibraltar must hold a DLT provider license. This applies to:

  • Crypto brokerages matching buyers and sellers of digital assets
  • Centralised exchanges offering spot trading pairs
  • Custodians holding client private keys or digital assets
  • Firms providing DLT-related advisory or portfolio management
  • OTC desks facilitating large block trades

If your business model involves holding client funds or assets, or facilitating transactions on behalf of third parties, you likely fall under the GFSC's scope. License Type and Regulator

The GFSC issues a single DLT provider license, which covers multiple activities. There is no separate 'broker' license - the DLT license authorises brokerage, exchange, and custody under one regime. The regulator is the Gibraltar Financial Services Commission (GFSC), an independent statutory body responsible for financial supervision.

Key regulatory references:

  • Financial Services (Distributed Ledger Technology Providers) Regulations 2017
  • GFSC Guidance Notes on DLT Providers
  • AML/CFT obligations under the Proceeds of Crime Act 2015
  • Data Protection Act 2018 (GDPR equivalent)

The GFSC applies a risk-based approach, requiring applicants to demonstrate strong governance, capital adequacy, and operational resilience. As of 2026, the GFSC continues to refine its expectations, particularly around custody of digital assets and cybersecurity. The regulator also expects firms to have a local physical office and at least one resident director.

Capital Requirement

The GFSC requires a minimum initial capital of EUR 125,000 (or equivalent in GBP) for DLT providers. This capital must be held in liquid assets and maintained at all times. The amount is set by the GFSC based on the nature and scale of operations; higher capital may be required for firms handling significant client assets or complex risk profiles.

Capital must be unencumbered and available to cover operational and regulatory risks. The GFSC may also require additional capital for custody activities or if the firm plans to offer use. As of 2026, the capital requirement aligns with MiCA's Tier 2 level (EUR 125,000) for custody and exchange services, though Gibraltar is not an EU member. Firms must provide audited proof of capital as part of the application.

Tax Treatment

Gibraltar offers a favourable corporate tax regime: the standard rate is 10% on profits, with no capital gains tax, no VAT, and no withholding tax on dividends or interest. For DLT providers, income from crypto brokerage and exchange activities is treated as ordinary business income, subject to the 10% rate.

Gibraltar has a territorial tax system, meaning only profits derived from Gibraltar are taxed. There is also a tax exemption for certain intellectual property income (patent box). Firms must comply with OECD transfer pricing rules and substance requirements. Consulting24 advises and coordinates tax structuring for crypto firms in Gibraltar. It is important to note that while Gibraltar is not in the EU, it follows EU tax transparency standards.

Allowed Activities

Under the DLT provider license, firms may conduct the following activities:

  • Operating a crypto brokerage (matching orders, executing trades)
  • Running a digital asset exchange (order book, AMM, OTC)
  • Custody and safekeeping of digital assets (hot and cold wallets)
  • Providing DLT-related advisory services
  • Issuing and redeeming stablecoins or tokenised assets (subject to additional approval)
  • Staking and lending of client assets (with disclosure and risk warnings)

The GFSC prohibits activities like deposit-taking, lending without a banking license, and offering derivatives without proper authorisation. Firms must also comply with travel rule obligations for transfers over EUR 1,000. The GFSC may impose activity-specific conditions on the license.

Step-by-Step Application Process

  1. Pre-application consultation - Engage with GFSC informally to discuss your business model and regulatory expectations. This is optional but recommended.
  2. Prepare documentation - Business plan, AML/CFT policies, risk assessment, governance structure, financial projections, and capital proof. All documents must be in English.
  3. Appoint local representatives - Directors and compliance officer must be fit and proper; at least one director must be resident in Gibraltar. The compliance officer should have relevant experience.
  4. Submit application - Complete the GFSC's DLT provider application form with all supporting documents and fee. The GFSC will acknowledge receipt within 5 business days.
  5. Review and interview - GFSC assesses the application, may request clarifications, and conducts interviews with key personnel. This stage can take 3-6 months.
  6. License grant - If approved, the firm receives a DLT provider license with conditions (e.g., capital maintenance, reporting). The license is valid indefinitely subject to annual renewal.
  7. Ongoing compliance - Annual returns, audited financial statements, AML reporting, and regulatory filings. The GFSC may conduct periodic inspections.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Insufficient capital - Underestimating the minimum capital or not maintaining it throughout the license period. Ensure capital is always above the threshold.
  • Weak AML policies - Failing to tailor AML procedures to the specific business model. Generic policies are not accepted.
  • Lack of local substance - Not having a physical office or resident directors in Gibraltar. The GFSC expects genuine local presence.
  • Incomplete application - Submitting without all required documents, causing delays. Use a checklist and engage professionals.
  • Ignoring travel rule - Not implementing FATF travel rule compliance for transfers. This is a key requirement.
  • Overlooking ongoing reporting - Missing annual returns or audit deadlines can lead to penalties or license revocation.

Frequently asked questions

What is the minimum capital for a Gibraltar crypto broker license?

The GFSC requires a minimum capital of EUR 125,000 (or equivalent in GBP). This must be held in liquid assets and maintained at all times. Higher capital may be required depending on the scale and risk profile of the business.

How long does it take to get a Gibraltar crypto license?

The typical timeline is 6 to 12 months from submission to license grant. This depends on the completeness of the application and the GFSC's review process. Pre-application consultation can help streamline the process.

Can I operate a crypto exchange with a Gibraltar DLT license?

Yes, the DLT provider license covers operating a digital asset exchange, including order book, AMM, and OTC trading. The license is technology-neutral and allows multiple activities under one authorisation.

What are the tax benefits of Gibraltar for crypto firms?

Gibraltar offers a 10% corporate tax rate, no capital gains tax, no VAT, and no withholding tax on dividends or interest. It has a territorial tax system, so only Gibraltar-sourced profits are taxed.

Do I need a physical office in Gibraltar?

Yes, the GFSC expects firms to have a substantive presence, including a physical office in Gibraltar and at least one resident director. This demonstrates commitment to local regulation and oversight.

Is Gibraltar's crypto license recognised in the EU?

Gibraltar is not an EU member, but its regulatory regime is considered equivalent by many international partners. However, after Brexit, Gibraltar does not have automatic passporting rights into the EU. Firms may need to comply with local laws in each EU country.

What activities are prohibited under the Gibraltar DLT license?

Prohibited activities include deposit-taking, lending without a banking license, and offering derivatives without proper authorisation. The GFSC also prohibits activities that fall outside the DLT framework, such as traditional securities brokerage.

What is the difference between Gibraltar and Panama for crypto licensing?

Gibraltar has a dedicated DLT regulatory framework with capital requirements and ongoing compliance. Panama has no specific crypto license and operates under general business law, with a flat cost of EUR 6,000. Panama is cheaper but offers less regulatory clarity.
