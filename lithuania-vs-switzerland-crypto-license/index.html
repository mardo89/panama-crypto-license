Overview: What Is a Crypto License in Lithuania and Switzerland?

In Lithuania, a crypto license is a CASP (Crypto Asset Service Provider) registration under the EU Markets in Crypto-Assets Regulation (MiCA), fully in force since 2025. It is issued by the Bank of Lithuania and covers services like exchange, custody, and transfer of crypto assets. The license is recognised across all EU/EEA member states via passporting. For example, a Lithuanian CASP can serve clients in Germany, France, or Spain without additional local registrations.

Switzerland does not issue a single "crypto license" but rather a DLT (Distributed Ledger Technology) banking license or a simpler FINMA authorisation for certain activities. Most crypto businesses operate under a FINMA licence as a securities dealer or a payment system operator, or they use the DLT trading facility regime. The Swiss approach is principle-based and innovation-friendly, but it demands higher compliance standards. For instance, a DLT banking license allows the firm to take deposits and lend crypto, activities not permitted under a Lithuanian CASP.

Both jurisdictions are popular among crypto founders, but they serve different needs. Lithuania is ideal for startups seeking quick, cost-effective EU market access, while Switzerland appeals to established firms that value regulatory clarity and a favourable tax environment. Who Needs a Crypto License in Lithuania or Switzerland?

Any business offering crypto services to residents of Lithuania or Switzerland must hold the appropriate authorisation. This includes:

  • Cryptocurrency exchanges (centralised and decentralised)
  • Custodial wallet providers
  • Brokerage and trading platforms
  • Payment processors using crypto assets
  • Token issuers (if the token qualifies as a security in Switzerland)

In Lithuania, the CASP regime covers all services defined under MiCA, including transfer, exchange, custody, and advisory. In Switzerland, the scope depends on the licence type: a DLT banking licence allows full banking activities with crypto, while a FINMA authorisation for securities dealers covers trading and custody of tokens classified as securities. For example, a firm offering crypto derivatives would need a Swiss banking licence, as such activities fall outside the Lithuanian CASP scope.

Capital Requirement

Under MiCA, Lithuania applies tiered capital requirements based on the services offered:

  • EUR 50,000 for simple custody or exchange services
  • EUR 125,000 for combined services (e.g., exchange + custody)
  • EUR 150,000 if the business also provides transfer services or operates a trading platform

Switzerland's capital requirements are not fixed by law but are determined by FINMA based on the licence type and risk profile. For a DLT banking licence, minimum capital is typically CHF 10 million (approx. EUR 10.5 million). For a simpler FINMA authorisation (e.g., securities dealer), capital may range from CHF 500,000 to CHF 2 million. Higher capital reflects the broader scope of activities permitted under Swiss licences. For instance, a DLT banking licence allows taking deposits, which requires a higher capital buffer.

Both jurisdictions require the capital to be fully paid up and held in liquid assets. Consulting24 can help you structure the capital injection correctly. In contrast, Panama has no minimum capital requirement for its crypto license.

Tax Treatment

Lithuania: Corporate income tax is 15% (5% for small businesses under certain conditions). Crypto-to-crypto exchanges are not taxable events; only conversion to fiat triggers taxation. VAT is not applied to crypto transactions. However, mining and staking income may be treated as business income. For example, a Lithuanian CASP that earns fees in crypto would pay corporate tax on the fiat value of those fees at the time of receipt.

Switzerland: Corporate tax rates vary by canton, typically 12-20% (effective). Crypto assets are treated as assets for wealth tax; capital gains from private crypto trading are tax-free for individuals. For businesses, profits from crypto trading are subject to corporate income tax. VAT is generally not applied on crypto transactions. Switzerland also offers a favourable tax regime for DLT companies, including possible exemptions for tokens classified as payment tokens. For instance, a Swiss company issuing a utility token may not be subject to securities tax.

Switzerland is often considered more tax-friendly for long-term holders, while Lithuania offers a simpler system for active traders. Consulting24 can connect you with tax advisors in both jurisdictions.

Step-by-Step Application Process

Lithuania

  1. Prepare documentation: business plan, AML/KYC policies, risk assessment, IT security audit.
  2. Register a company in Lithuania (minimum share capital EUR 2,500).
  3. Submit application to Bank of Lithuania via the online portal.
  4. Pay state fee (EUR 1,500).
  5. Await review (typically 3-6 months). The regulator may request clarifications.
  6. Receive CASP registration and passporting rights.

Switzerland

  1. Pre-application meeting with FINMA (optional but recommended).
  2. Prepare comprehensive application: business model, governance, risk management, capital adequacy, AML policies.
  3. Submit application and pay FINMA fee (CHF 5,000-20,000).
  4. FINMA reviews (6-12 months). Extensive due diligence on shareholders and management.
  5. If approved, obtain licence and comply with ongoing reporting.

Banking and Payment Services

Lithuania: Access to EU banking is generally straightforward. Many Lithuanian banks (e.g., Swedbank, SEB) accept crypto businesses, though due diligence is rigorous. Payment services can be integrated via SEPA. Some crypto-friendly banks may require a minimum deposit or turnover. For example, a Lithuanian CASP may need to show a minimum monthly transaction volume of EUR 50,000 to open a corporate account.

Switzerland: Banking is more challenging due to FINMA's strict anti-money laundering rules. However, several cantonal banks (e.g., Zürcher Kantonalbank) and specialised fintech banks (e.g., Sygnum, SEBA) offer accounts to licensed crypto firms. Swiss banks are known for stability but impose high compliance costs. For instance, Sygnum may require a minimum deposit of CHF 100,000 and charge monthly fees of CHF 500-1,000.

Both jurisdictions require a local bank account for operational purposes. Consulting24 can introduce you to partner banks. If banking is a concern, Panama offers a more flexible banking environment with lower minimums.

Compliance and Ongoing Obligations

Both jurisdictions require ongoing compliance with AML/CFT rules, reporting, and audits.

Lithuania: CASP holders must submit annual reports to the Bank of Lithuania, maintain AML procedures, and undergo external audits. Transaction monitoring is mandatory. Non-compliance can result in fines up to EUR 500,000. For example, a Lithuanian CASP must report suspicious transactions within 24 hours to the Financial Crime Investigation Service.

Switzerland: FINMA imposes strict reporting requirements, including quarterly financial reports, AML audits, and capital adequacy monitoring. The regulator conducts on-site inspections. Penalties for non-compliance include licence revocation and criminal charges. For instance, a Swiss DLT bank must submit a quarterly report on its capital position and liquidity.

Common Mistakes When Applying

  • Incomplete AML policies: Both regulators require detailed, risk-based AML/KYC procedures. Generic templates are rejected. For example, a Lithuanian application must include a specific risk assessment for each service offered.
  • Underestimating capital: In Lithuania, ensure you meet the correct tier; in Switzerland, have sufficient capital for the licence type. A common mistake is applying for a DLT banking licence with only CHF 1 million in capital, which is insufficient.
  • Poor documentation: Business plans must be realistic and include financial projections. Switzerland expects a high level of detail, including a three-year business plan with profit and loss statements.
  • Ignoring passporting requirements: If you plan to operate in other EU countries, Lithuania's CASP passporting must be notified to the host regulator. Failure to do so can result in fines.
  • Not engaging a professional: Both regulators expect applicants to have professional advice. Consulting24 can help you avoid these pitfalls.

Frequently asked questions

What is the main difference between a Lithuania and Switzerland crypto license?

Lithuania offers a CASP license under MiCA, which is cheaper and faster (3-6 months, EUR 15k-25k) and allows passporting across the EU. Switzerland offers a DLT banking license or FINMA authorisation, which is more expensive and slower (6-12 months, CHF 50k+) but permits a wider range of activities, including derivatives and deposit-taking.

Which jurisdiction has lower capital requirements?

Lithuania has lower capital requirements: EUR 50,000 to 150,000 depending on services. Switzerland requires CHF 500,000 to 2 million for a securities dealer licence, and CHF 10 million for a DLT banking licence. Panama has no capital requirement.

Can I passport a Lithuanian CASP to other EU countries?

Yes. A Lithuanian CASP can provide services across the EU/EEA via passporting. You must notify the Bank of Lithuania and the host regulator. This is a key advantage over non-EU licenses like Panama.

Is Switzerland more tax-friendly than Lithuania for crypto businesses?

Switzerland can be more tax-friendly for long-term holders due to cantonal tax rates (12-20%) and tax-free capital gains for individuals. Lithuania has a 15% corporate tax (5% for small firms) and simpler rules for active traders. Your specific situation matters.

How long does it take to get a crypto license in Lithuania vs Switzerland?

Lithuania takes 3-6 months from application submission. Switzerland takes 6-12 months, sometimes longer if FINMA requests additional information. Both timelines depend on the completeness of your application.

What are the ongoing compliance costs for each jurisdiction?

Lithuania: annual audit (EUR 3,000-5,000), AML officer (EUR 1,000-2,000/month), and reporting. Switzerland: higher costs due to quarterly reporting, external audits (CHF 10,000-30,000/year), and compliance staff. Exact figures depend on business size.

Can I apply for both licenses simultaneously?

Yes, but it is complex and costly. Consulting24 advises on multi-jurisdictional strategies. You may consider a Lithuanian CASP for EU operations and a Swiss DLT licence for specific services like derivatives. Contact us for a tailored plan.

What happens if my application is rejected?

In Lithuania, you can reapply after addressing deficiencies. In Switzerland, rejection may require a new application with significant changes. Consulting24 helps ensure your application is complete and compliant the first time.

Do I need a physical office in Lithuania or Switzerland?

Lithuania requires a registered office (virtual offices are accepted). Switzerland may require a physical presence for DLT banking licenses, but FINMA authorisations for securities dealers may accept a registered address. Check specific requirements.

Which jurisdiction is better for a crypto exchange startup?

For a simple exchange startup, Lithuania is better due to lower cost and faster timeline. For a larger exchange offering derivatives or banking services, Switzerland is more suitable. Panama is a good alternative for a low-cost entry without EU passporting.
