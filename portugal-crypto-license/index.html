What Is the Portugal Crypto License?

The Portugal crypto license is a regulatory authorization required for crypto-asset service providers (CASPs) operating in or from Portugal. Under MiCA, which is fully in force in 2026, all EU member states including Portugal must license CASPs under a harmonized framework. The license is issued by the Banco de Portugal (the central bank) as the competent authority, with additional oversight from the Portuguese Securities Market Commission (CMVM) for certain activities.

This license replaces the previous regime where firms only registered with the Banco de Portugal without a full licensing process. Now, CASPs must meet strict capital, governance, and operational requirements to obtain and maintain the license. The license allows firms to offer services such as custody, exchange, and transfer of crypto-assets across the EU under passporting rights. For example, a Portuguese-licensed exchange can serve clients in Germany or France without needing separate licenses, as long as it complies with local consumer protection rules.

Portugal's approach under MiCA is seen as balanced, offering regulatory clarity while encouraging innovation. The Banco de Portugal has issued guidelines on how it interprets MiCA provisions, including specific expectations for risk management and cybersecurity. Firms must also adhere to the EU's Travel Rule for crypto transfers, requiring the collection and sharing of originator and beneficiary information for transactions above a certain threshold.

Who Needs a Portugal Crypto License?

Any legal entity that provides crypto-asset services to clients in Portugal or from Portugal needs this license. This includes:

  • Crypto exchanges (fiat-to-crypto and crypto-to-crypto)
  • Custodial wallet providers
  • Crypto-asset transfer services
  • Platforms for crypto-asset trading (order books, DEXs if they have control)
  • Advisors or portfolio managers dealing with crypto-assets
  • Issuers of asset-referenced tokens or e-money tokens (also subject to CMVM)

Foreign firms targeting Portuguese clients must also obtain a license or passport from another EU member state. License Type and Regulator

The Portugal crypto license is a CASP license under MiCA. The primary regulator is the Banco de Portugal, responsible for authorization and supervision of most crypto-asset services. The CMVM oversees services that involve asset-referenced tokens or e-money tokens. The application is submitted via the Banco de Portugal's online portal, which was updated in early 2026 to handle MiCA applications.

The license is valid throughout the EU once granted, thanks to the MiCA passport. Firms must also comply with anti-money laundering (AML) and counter-terrorist financing (CTF) obligations, which are enforced by the Banco de Portugal and the Financial Intelligence Unit. The regulator conducts ongoing supervision, including periodic reporting and on-site inspections.

Portugal has also established a sandbox for innovative fintech solutions, but for most commercial operations, the full CASP license is required. Capital Requirement

Under MiCA, Portugal applies tiered capital requirements based on the services offered:

  • EUR 50,000 for simple services like custody and transfer of crypto-assets
  • EUR 125,000 for exchange services (fiat-to-crypto or crypto-to-crypto)
  • EUR 150,000 for more complex services including trading platforms and portfolio management

These amounts must be maintained as own funds throughout the license period. Capital can be in cash or high-quality liquid assets, such as government bonds. The regulator may require additional capital if the firm's risk profile is higher, for example, if it handles large volumes or complex derivatives. Firms must also have professional indemnity insurance or a similar guarantee for at least EUR 1 million per claim, which is a separate cost.

Tax Treatment

Portugal offers a favorable tax regime for crypto businesses and investors. As of 2026, capital gains from crypto-assets held for more than one year are tax-exempt for individuals. For businesses, corporate income tax (IRC) applies at a standard rate of 21%, with reduced rates for small and medium enterprises (17% on the first EUR 50,000 of taxable income). VAT is generally not applied to crypto-asset transactions, as they are treated as exempt financial services.

However, tax treatment can vary based on the specific activity and entity structure. For example, mining income may be treated as business income, while staking rewards could be considered capital gains. Portugal also has a participation exemption regime that can apply to dividends and capital gains from subsidiaries. Allowed Activities

A Portugal crypto license permits the following activities under MiCA:

  • Custody and administration of crypto-assets on behalf of clients
  • Operation of a trading platform for crypto-assets
  • Exchange of crypto-assets for fiat currency or other crypto-assets
  • Execution of orders for crypto-assets on behalf of clients
  • Placement of crypto-assets
  • Receiving and transmitting orders for crypto-assets
  • Providing advice on crypto-assets
  • Portfolio management of crypto-assets
  • Transfer services for crypto-assets

Each activity may require additional permissions or compliance measures. For instance, operating a trading platform may require market abuse surveillance systems. Firms can apply for multiple service categories, but the capital requirement will be based on the highest tier among the services offered. Step-by-Step Application Process

The process to obtain a Portugal crypto license involves several stages:

  1. Pre-application preparation: Define your business model, prepare a detailed business plan, AML/CTF policies, risk assessment, and governance structure. This stage takes 2-4 months.
  2. Submission of application: Submit the application via the Banco de Portugal's online platform, including all required documentation and payment of the application fee. The regulator acknowledges receipt within 10 business days.
  3. Review and due diligence: The regulator reviews the application, conducts background checks on key personnel, and may request additional information. This phase typically takes 3-6 months.
  4. On-site inspection (if required): The Banco de Portugal may conduct an on-site visit to verify operational readiness, including IT systems and physical premises.
  5. Decision: The regulator issues a decision within 6-12 months of submission. If approved, the license is granted with conditions, such as reporting obligations.
  6. Post-licensing compliance: Ongoing reporting, AML/CTF compliance, and capital maintenance are required. Annual fees apply, typically EUR 1,000-3,000.

Firms must appoint a compliance officer, conduct employee training, and implement transaction monitoring systems. Non-compliance can lead to fines, suspension, or revocation of the license. The regulator has the power to impose penalties of up to EUR 5 million or 10% of annual turnover for serious breaches.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

When applying for a Portugal crypto license, avoid these common pitfalls:

  • Incomplete documentation: Failing to provide a comprehensive business plan or AML policies can cause delays. Ensure all documents are translated into Portuguese if required.
  • Underestimating capital requirements: Ensure you have the required capital in place before applying, and that it is held in a suitable form.
  • Ignoring tax obligations: Even with exemptions, proper tax reporting is mandatory. Portugal has a strict tax authority (Autoridade Tributária) that audits crypto firms.
  • Choosing the wrong service scope: Select the correct license category for your activities. Adding services later may require a new application.
  • Poor governance: Regulators scrutinize the fitness of directors and shareholders. Ensure all key personnel have clean backgrounds and relevant experience.
  • Not preparing for on-site inspections: The Banco de Portugal may visit your premises. Have your IT systems and physical security ready.
