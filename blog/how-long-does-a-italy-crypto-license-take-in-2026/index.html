Overview: What Is the Italy Crypto License?

The Italy crypto license, officially a registration as a Virtual Asset Service Provider (VASP) under the new MiCA framework, allows companies to legally offer crypto services such as exchange, custody, and wallet management to Italian residents. Since MiCA is fully in force in 2026, Italian CASPs must comply with EU-wide standards, including capital requirements, AML procedures, and governance rules. The license is issued by the Banca d'Italia in coordination with CONSOB. The process involves a pre-authorization phase, submission of detailed documentation, and ongoing supervision. The timeline is influenced by the applicant's readiness, the complexity of services, and the regulator's review capacity. For example, a simple wallet service may take 6 months, while a full exchange with custody could take 12 months. Consulting24 advises and coordinates applications for Italy, using our experience from 500+ licenses in other jurisdictions. The license is valid indefinitely, subject to annual compliance reviews and renewal of certain registrations.

Who Needs an Italy Crypto License?

Any business offering crypto-related services to Italian residents must obtain a license. This includes:

  • Crypto exchanges (fiat-to-crypto and crypto-to-crypto)
  • Cryptocurrency wallet providers
  • Custodians of crypto assets
  • Brokers and dealers in crypto assets
  • Platforms for initial coin offerings (ICOs) or security token offerings (STOs)
  • Automated teller machines (ATMs) for crypto

Foreign companies targeting the Italian market also need a local presence or a branch. If you are already licensed in another EU member state under MiCA, passporting may reduce the timeline, but full compliance with Italian specifics is still required. For example, a company holding a Lithuania crypto license can passport into Italy, but must notify the Italian regulator and adapt to local AML requirements. Even decentralized finance (DeFi) projects that have a central point of control may fall under the scope if they offer services to Italian residents.

License Type & Regulator

Italy issues a single license type for CASPs, but the scope of activities determines the applicable capital tier. The primary regulators are:

  • Banca d'Italia: Responsible for prudential supervision, AML compliance, and authorization of crypto asset services.
  • CONSOB: Handles market conduct, investor protection, and transparency requirements.

Under MiCA, capital requirements are tiered: EUR 50,000 for basic services (e.g., wallet provision), EUR 125,000 for exchange services, and EUR 150,000 for custodial services. These figures are minimums; higher capital may be required based on business volume. The application is submitted to Banca d'Italia, which coordinates with CONSOB for market conduct aspects. The regulator expects a detailed business plan, AML policies, and proof of capital. The regulators also conduct background checks on directors and beneficial owners, including criminal record checks and financial history reviews.

Capital Requirement

Under MiCA, Italy imposes minimum capital requirements based on the type of services offered:

  • EUR 50,000 for wallet and custody services without exchange functionality
  • EUR 125,000 for exchange services (fiat-to-crypto or crypto-to-crypto)
  • EUR 150,000 for combined services including custody and exchange

Capital must be held in liquid assets and maintained throughout the license period. Additional capital may be required based on the volume of transactions or risk profile. These requirements are strictly enforced by Banca d'Italia. For example, if you plan to handle over EUR 1 million in monthly transactions, the regulator may demand a higher capital buffer, sometimes up to EUR 250,000. The capital can be held in a bank account in Italy or another EU member state, but must be readily accessible. Consulting24 helps you calculate the appropriate capital level and structure the deposit. The capital cannot be used for operational expenses; it must remain as a reserve.

Tax Treatment

Italy taxes crypto gains as capital gains at a flat rate of 26% for individuals, with a threshold of EUR 2,000 per year. For corporate entities, crypto profits are subject to corporate income tax (IRES) at 24% plus regional tax (IRAP) at around 3.9%. VAT is not applicable on crypto-to-crypto transactions, but fiat conversions may be subject to standard VAT. Italy has specific reporting obligations for crypto holdings, and non-compliance can lead to penalties. Professional advice is recommended to structure tax efficiently. For example, holding crypto as inventory rather than investment may change tax treatment. Consulting24 can coordinate with local tax advisors to optimize your structure. Compare with Panama, which has no capital gains tax on crypto for offshore entities. In Italy, losses can be carried forward for five years to offset future gains. The tax year aligns with the calendar year, and annual returns must be filed by November 30.

Allowed Activities

The Italy crypto license permits the following activities under MiCA:

  • Exchange of crypto assets for fiat currency
  • Exchange of crypto assets for other crypto assets
  • Custody and administration of crypto assets on behalf of clients
  • Operation of a trading platform for crypto assets
  • Placement of crypto assets (including ICOs)
  • Receiving and transmitting orders for crypto assets
  • Providing advice on crypto assets

Certain activities, such as decentralized finance (DeFi) protocols or non-custodial wallets, may fall outside the scope. Always verify with the regulator. If your business model includes staking or lending, these may require additional authorization under MiCA or national law. Consulting24 can map your services to the correct license categories. For example, if you offer staking as a service where you control the private keys, that is considered custody and requires the higher capital tier. Non-custodial staking may not need a license, but legal clarity is still evolving.

Step-by-Step Process

  1. Pre-application preparation: Prepare business plan, AML policies, risk assessment, and governance documents. Engage a local legal advisor. This stage typically takes 1-2 months. You must also appoint a compliance officer and an AML officer who are resident in Italy or the EU.
  2. Company setup: Incorporate a legal entity in Italy (e.g., SRL) with a registered office and local directors. This can be done in parallel with step 1, taking 2-4 weeks. The company must have a physical address in Italy and at least one director who is an EU resident.
  3. Capital deposit: Deposit the required minimum capital in a blocked account. The bank may require a letter of intent from the regulator. The account must be with an Italian bank or an EU bank with a branch in Italy.
  4. Submit application: File the application with Banca d'Italia, including all documentation and fees. The regulator will acknowledge receipt within 10 working days. The application must be in Italian, with certified translations of foreign documents.
  5. Regulatory review: The regulator assesses the application, may request additional information, and conducts interviews. This phase takes 4-8 months. Banca d'Italia may also coordinate with CONSOB for market conduct review. Expect at least one round of questions and possibly a meeting with the regulator.
  6. Approval and registration: Upon approval, the company is registered as a CASP and can begin operations under supervision. The license is valid indefinitely, subject to ongoing compliance. You must then notify the regulator of any changes in business activities or ownership.

Banking & Payments

Opening a bank account for a crypto business in Italy can be challenging due to perceived risks. Many traditional banks are hesitant to serve CASPs. Options include:

  • Italian banks with dedicated fintech divisions (e.g., Banca Sella, Illimity)
  • EU-based payment institutions that support crypto businesses
  • Cryptocurrency-friendly banks in Lithuania or Estonia, which may be more accessible

For example, Banca Sella offers a specific account for regulated crypto firms, but requires a license in hand. The account opening process can take 1-3 months. Some banks require a minimum deposit of EUR 10,000 and charge monthly fees of EUR 50-200. Consulting24 can assist in identifying banking partners and preparing the necessary documentation. For a simpler banking environment, Panama offers a more open banking environment for crypto firms, with accounts available in 2-4 weeks. In Italy, you may also need a separate account for client funds segregation, which adds complexity.

Compliance & Trust

Compliance is critical. Italy requires strong AML/KYC procedures, regular audits, and reporting to Banca d'Italia. Non-compliance can result in fines, suspension, or revocation of the license. Key requirements include:

  • Appointment of a compliance officer and AML officer
  • Implementation of transaction monitoring systems
  • Annual external audits
  • Reporting of suspicious transactions within 30 days
  • Maintaining records for at least 10 years

Common Mistakes

Founders often make these errors when applying for an Italy crypto license:

  • Underestimating the documentation burden: Incomplete applications cause delays. For example, missing a risk assessment for each service type can halt the review.
  • Inadequate AML policies: Generic templates are not accepted. The AML program must be tailored to your specific business model and include transaction monitoring thresholds, customer due diligence procedures, and a clear reporting chain.
  • Choosing the wrong capital tier: Some applicants apply with EUR 50,000 but later need to offer exchange services, requiring a top-up to EUR 125,000 and a re-submission.
  • Lack of local presence: Not having a physical office or local director can lead to rejection. The regulator expects a substantive presence in Italy.
  • Ignoring tax obligations: Failing to register for VAT or report crypto holdings can result in fines and reputational damage.
