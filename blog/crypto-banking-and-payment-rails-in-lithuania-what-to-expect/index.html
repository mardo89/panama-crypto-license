What Are Crypto Banking and Payment Rails?

Crypto banking and payment rails refer to the infrastructure that enables the transfer of digital assets and fiat currency between accounts, exchanges, and merchants. In Lithuania, these rails are supported by a strong fintech ecosystem, including licensed electronic money institutions (EMIs) and payment institutions (PIs). For example, a crypto exchange can use these rails to offer instant SEPA deposits and withdrawals, or issue virtual IBANs to customers for easy fiat management.

Payment rails also include card processing networks, allowing crypto businesses to issue debit cards that spend crypto or fiat balances. In Lithuania, companies like PayrNet and ConnectPay provide API-driven banking services tailored to crypto firms. These rails are essential for any business that wants to bridge the gap between traditional finance and digital assets. Without them, you would need to rely on less efficient methods like manual bank transfers or third-party processors that add latency and cost.

The infrastructure is built on standard protocols like SEPA, SWIFT, and Faster Payments, ensuring interoperability with the broader financial system. For crypto companies, having reliable payment rails means you can offer services like fiat on-ramps, off-ramps, virtual IBANs, and card issuance. Lithuania's regulatory clarity allows businesses to integrate these services while staying compliant with anti-money laundering (AML) and counter-terrorist financing (CTF) rules. This makes it an attractive base for operations targeting the European market.

Who Needs Crypto Banking and Payment Rails in Lithuania?

Any crypto business that handles fiat currency alongside digital assets will benefit from Lithuanian payment rails. Typical use cases include:

  • Crypto exchanges that need to process customer deposits and withdrawals in EUR or USD.
  • Wallet providers offering fiat-to-crypto conversion and vice versa.
  • Payment processors enabling merchants to accept crypto and settle in fiat.
  • DeFi platforms bridging traditional finance with decentralized protocols.
  • OTC desks that require fast settlement in multiple currencies.

Even if your primary operations are elsewhere, having a Lithuanian entity can unlock access to SEPA and SWIFT networks, making cross-border transactions efficient. For instance, a crypto exchange based in Asia can use a Lithuanian subsidiary to offer European customers local IBANs, reducing transfer fees and processing times. Similarly, a payment processor serving global merchants can use Lithuania's banking relationships to settle in EUR with minimal friction.

Startups and established companies alike can benefit. If you are planning to launch a crypto debit card, you will need a partner that can issue cards and process transactions. Lithuania's EMIs are well-equipped for this. Even if you only operate a crypto-to-crypto exchange, having access to fiat rails can help you attract more users who want to enter or exit the market easily.

License Type and Regulator

In Lithuania, crypto activities are regulated under the Law on Prevention of Money Laundering and Terrorist Financing (PMLTF). The regulator is the Bank of Lithuania (Lietuvos bankas). Companies must register as a Virtual Currency Exchange Operator or a Virtual Currency Wallet Operator. Additionally, if you handle fiat, you may need an EMI or PI license from the same regulator.

The crypto license itself is a registration rather than a full authorization, but it comes with strict AML obligations. For payment services, the EMI license is the most common route, allowing you to issue e-money, provide payment accounts, and process transactions. The Bank of Lithuania is known for its thorough but fair review process. They expect a detailed business plan, AML policies, and proof of capital. The regulator also conducts on-site inspections and ongoing supervision.

It is important to note that the crypto registration and EMI license are separate. You can obtain the crypto registration first and later apply for an EMI license if needed. Capital Requirements

Lithuania does not impose a specific minimum capital for crypto license registration. However, if you also apply for an EMI license, the minimum initial capital is EUR 125,000 (for payment services) or EUR 350,000 (for e-money issuance). These are standard under EU regulations and are strictly enforced by the Bank of Lithuania.

For the crypto registration alone, the Bank of Lithuania expects that you have sufficient financial resources to operate soundly. Typically, a minimum of EUR 10,000 in share capital is recommended to demonstrate commitment. This capital must be paid up and held in a bank account. The regulator may ask for proof of funds and a business plan showing how the capital will be used.

If you plan to offer both crypto and payment services, you will need to meet the higher capital requirements for the EMI license. Some companies choose to set up a separate entity for payment services to isolate risk. Tax Treatment of Crypto Banking and Payment Activities

Lithuania offers a favorable tax environment for crypto businesses. Corporate income tax is 15% (or 5% for small companies with fewer than 10 employees and revenue under EUR 300,000). Capital gains from crypto trading are taxed as income, but VAT is not applied to crypto-to-crypto exchanges. For payment services, transaction fees are subject to standard VAT (21%).

It is essential to maintain proper accounting and tax records. The Lithuanian tax authority (VMI) has issued guidelines on crypto taxation, clarifying that mining, staking, and lending rewards are taxable as income. Losses can be offset against gains, but only within the same tax year. For companies holding crypto as assets, unrealized gains are not taxed until realization.

Allowed Activities Under a Lithuanian Crypto License

With a Lithuanian crypto registration, you can legally operate the following activities:

  • Exchange services between virtual currencies and fiat currencies.
  • Exchange between one or more virtual currencies.
  • Transfer of virtual currencies.
  • Custodial wallet services.
  • Participation in ICOs/STOs (with additional compliance).

If you hold an EMI license, you can also issue e-money, provide payment accounts, and process payments. This combination allows for a full-fledged crypto banking platform. For example, you could offer a multi-currency wallet that supports both crypto and fiat, with instant conversions and withdrawals to bank accounts.

Note that certain activities like crypto derivatives or margin trading may require additional licensing under MiFID II. The Bank of Lithuania provides a list of regulated activities and can offer guidance on whether your planned services fall within scope. It is better to clarify upfront than to risk operating outside the license.

Step-by-Step Process to Set Up Crypto Banking and Payment Rails

The process involves several stages. Here is a typical timeline:

  1. Company incorporation: Register a UAB (private limited liability company) in Lithuania. This takes 1-2 weeks. You will need a registered address and a local director or representative.
  2. Open a bank account: Apply with a crypto-friendly bank (e.g., PayrNet, Revolut Business). This can take 1-3 months. Some banks require the crypto license to be in place first, so plan accordingly.
  3. Prepare compliance documentation: Draft AML/KYC policies, risk assessment, and internal procedures. This is critical for the license application.
  4. Submit crypto license application: File with the Bank of Lithuania. Decision within 2-4 months. The regulator may request additional information or clarifications.
  5. Apply for EMI license (if needed): Submit a full application including business plan, governance, and capital proof. Timeline 6-12 months. This is a more intensive process.
  6. Integrate payment rails: Once licenses are obtained, you can integrate with banking partners via APIs. This typically takes 1-2 months.
  7. Go live: Start offering services to customers. Ensure ongoing compliance reporting.

Compliance and Trust: Meeting Regulatory Standards

Compliance is the backbone of a successful crypto banking operation. The Bank of Lithuania expects strong AML/CTF measures, including:

  • Customer due diligence (CDD) and enhanced due diligence (EDD) for high-risk clients.
  • Transaction monitoring and suspicious activity reporting.
  • Appointment of a compliance officer and a money laundering reporting officer (MLRO).
  • Regular audits and reporting to the regulator.
  • Record keeping for at least 5 years.

Failure to comply can result in fines or license revocation. For example, in 2023, the Bank of Lithuania fined several companies for AML breaches. Common Mistakes to Avoid

Founders often make these errors when setting up crypto banking in Lithuania:

  • Underestimating compliance costs: AML software and legal fees can be significant. Budget at least EUR 10,000 for initial setup.
  • Applying without a clear business plan: The regulator expects detailed documentation, including financial projections and risk assessments.
  • Choosing the wrong bank: Not all banks accept crypto clients; pre-approval is key. Some banks may close accounts without notice.
  • Ignoring tax obligations: Lithuania requires proper tax filings even for crypto transactions. Failure to report can lead to penalties.
  • Attempting to operate without a license: Unlicensed activity can lead to criminal penalties and reputational damage.
  • Overlooking ongoing reporting: The Bank of Lithuania requires regular reports on transactions and suspicious activities. Missing deadlines can result in fines.

Frequently asked questions

What is the minimum capital for a crypto license in Lithuania?

There is no specific minimum capital for the crypto registration itself, but typically EUR 10,000 is recommended. If you also apply for an EMI license, the minimum capital is EUR 125,000 for payment services or EUR 350,000 for e-money issuance.

How long does it take to get a crypto license in Lithuania?

The crypto registration takes 2-4 months from submission. If you also need an EMI license, expect 6-12 months. The total timeline can be 6-12 months if you apply for both simultaneously.

Can I use a Lithuanian crypto license to serve customers in other EU countries?

Yes, if you hold an EMI license, you can passport it to other EU member states. The crypto registration itself does not have passporting rights, but it is recognized across the EU for AML purposes.

What banks in Lithuania accept crypto companies?

Crypto-friendly banks include PayrNet, Revolut Business, MisterTango, and ConnectPay. Traditional banks like Swedbank and SEB may also open accounts for licensed entities, but with stricter due diligence.

Do I need a physical office in Lithuania?

You need a registered address in Lithuania, but a physical office is not mandatory. Many companies use virtual office services. However, the Bank of Lithuania may require a local presence for compliance purposes.

What are the tax rates for crypto businesses in Lithuania?

Corporate income tax is 15% (5% for small companies). Capital gains from crypto are taxed as income. VAT is 21% on payment service fees, but crypto-to-crypto exchanges are VAT-exempt.

Can I operate a crypto exchange with just a crypto registration?

Yes, the crypto registration allows you to operate a crypto exchange (fiat-to-crypto and crypto-to-crypto). However, if you want to hold customer fiat or issue e-money, you need an EMI license.

What are the ongoing compliance requirements?

You must conduct AML/KYC checks, monitor transactions, report suspicious activities, and submit annual reports to the Bank of Lithuania. You also need to appoint a compliance officer and MLRO.

Is Lithuania part of MiCA? How does that affect crypto licensing?

Yes, Lithuania is an EU member state and MiCA is fully in force in 2026. This means that crypto asset service providers (CASPs) must comply with MiCA regulations, including capital requirements of EUR 50,000 to 150,000 depending on services. Lithuania's existing regime has been updated to align with MiCA.
