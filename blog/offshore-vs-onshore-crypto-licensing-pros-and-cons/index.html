What Is Offshore vs Onshore Crypto Licensing?

Offshore crypto licensing refers to obtaining a license in a jurisdiction that offers lighter regulation, lower capital requirements, and often tax advantages, typically in a country where the company does not have a physical presence. Onshore licensing, by contrast, involves incorporating and licensing in a jurisdiction with full regulatory oversight, often where the company operates or has substance. The choice impacts compliance costs, investor confidence, and operational flexibility.

In 2026, onshore EU licenses under MiCA require minimum capital of EUR 50,000 to EUR 150,000 depending on services, while offshore hubs like Panama offer a flat fee of EUR 6,000 for a license with no minimum capital and zero tax on foreign-source income. The trade-off is between lower upfront cost and lighter ongoing compliance versus higher credibility and easier banking access in regulated markets.

For example, a startup exchange targeting global users might choose Panama to minimise initial outlay. An institutional-grade custody provider serving EU clients would likely prefer an onshore EU license to meet partner due diligence. The decision also affects fundraising: venture capitalists often prefer onshore regulated entities, while bootstrapped projects may prioritise cost savings.

Who Needs to Consider Offshore vs Onshore Licensing?

Founders of crypto exchanges, wallet providers, payment processors, and DeFi projects need to evaluate both routes. Startups with limited capital often lean offshore to conserve funds. Established firms seeking institutional partnerships or EU passporting rights prefer onshore EU licenses. Projects targeting retail users in regulated markets may need onshore credibility to secure bank accounts and payment partnerships.

Specifically, if you plan to serve EU customers directly, an onshore license in Lithuania or Estonia provides a clear regulatory framework under MiCA. If your target market is global or non-EU, an Panama license offers a cost-effective base with no corporate tax on offshore income. License Type and Regulator

Offshore example: Panama's crypto license is issued by the National Securities Commission (CNV) under Law 218 of 2021. It is a single license covering exchange, custody, and payment services. Onshore EU example: Under MiCA, each member state designates a national competent authority (NCA) to issue CASP licenses. In Lithuania, it is the Bank of Lithuania; in Estonia, the Financial Supervision Authority (FSA).

The EU license is a CASP (Crypto-Asset Service Provider) license, with categories for different services. Panama's license is simpler, with no distinction by service type. Both require AML compliance, but the EU imposes stricter governance, reporting, and capital rules. For instance, EU CASPs must have a board with at least two members, a compliance officer, and an AML officer, while Panama requires a local registered agent and a compliance officer but with less prescriptive governance.

Capital Requirements

Panama has no minimum capital requirement for a crypto license. You can start with as little as the incorporation capital (typically USD 10,000, but no regulatory minimum). In contrast, under MiCA, EU CASP licenses require minimum capital based on services offered: EUR 50,000 for custody and transfer services, EUR 125,000 for exchange services, and EUR 150,000 for trading platforms. These funds must be held as own funds and are subject to ongoing prudential requirements.

For startups, Panama's zero capital requirement is a major advantage. However, EU capital tiers provide a clear benchmark that can reassure partners and regulators. For example, a startup planning a simple wallet service could meet the EUR 50,000 capital requirement in Lithuania, while a full exchange would need EUR 125,000. In Panama, no such capital is needed, freeing up funds for operations.

Tax Treatment

Panama taxes only Panama-source income. Income from crypto services provided to non-resident clients (offshore) is tax-free. There is no capital gains tax, no VAT on crypto transactions, and no withholding tax on dividends. This makes Panama highly attractive for global operations.

EU jurisdictions apply corporate income tax (e.g., 15% in Lithuania, 20% in Estonia on distributed profits). VAT may apply to certain services, though crypto-to-fiat exchanges are often exempt. Transfer pricing and substance rules apply. Onshore licensing means full tax compliance, which can be a burden but also provides tax certainty for institutional investors.

For example, a Panama-licensed company earning EUR 1 million from non-Panama clients pays zero corporate tax. The same company in Lithuania would pay 15% corporate tax, i.e., EUR 150,000. However, EU licenses offer double tax treaty networks and credibility that can offset the tax cost for some business models.

Allowed Activities Under Each License

Panama's crypto license covers: exchange between crypto and fiat, crypto-to-crypto exchange, custody of private keys, payment processing, and issuance of tokens (subject to securities law). It does not allow derivatives trading or securities token offerings without additional registration.

Under MiCA, EU CASP licenses allow: custody and administration, operation of a trading platform, exchange (crypto/fiat and crypto/crypto), execution of orders, placement of crypto-assets, reception and transmission of orders, and advice. Each service must be specified in the license. DeFi and unbacked asset-referenced tokens have additional rules.

For example, a Panama license covers most common services, but if you plan to offer crypto derivatives, you would need a separate securities license. Banking and Payment Processing

Banking is a critical differentiator. Panama has a mature offshore banking sector, with banks willing to open accounts for licensed crypto companies, though due diligence is thorough. EU-licensed firms find it easier to open accounts with European banks, especially in Lithuania and Estonia, which have pro-fintech banking environments. However, many traditional banks still avoid crypto, so specialized payment processors are often used.

Onshore EU licenses provide access to SEPA payment systems and faster integration with fiat on-ramps. Offshore licenses may require alternative banking solutions. Benefits of Offshore vs Onshore Licensing

Offshore (Panama)

  • Low cost: EUR 6,000 flat fee
  • Zero corporate tax on offshore income
  • No minimum capital
  • Fast process (2-4 months)
  • Privacy and confidentiality

Onshore (EU under MiCA)

  • High credibility and trust
  • EU passporting rights (serve all EU markets)
  • Easier banking and payment integration
  • Clear regulatory framework
  • Access to institutional investors

For example, a Panama license is ideal for a small exchange targeting Latin America or Asia. An EU license is better for a custody provider serving European pension funds. The benefits are not mutually exclusive; some firms use a Panama holding company with an EU operating subsidiary.

Compliance and Trust Considerations

Offshore jurisdictions sometimes face stigma, but Panama has improved its AML framework significantly. The CNV requires strong AML/KYC procedures, regular reporting, and cooperation with international bodies. However, some partners may still perceive offshore licenses as less reputable.

Onshore EU licenses are recognized globally as gold-standard compliance. MiCA imposes strict governance, transparency, and consumer protection rules. This can be a burden for small startups but is a strong signal to banks, investors, and regulators. For example, an EU-licensed exchange must conduct regular audits, file quarterly reports, and maintain a complaints register. Panama requires annual audited financial statements and AML reports but with less frequency. The choice affects not only regulatory burden but also how partners perceive your business.

Common Mistakes When Choosing Offshore or Onshore

  • Ignoring substance requirements: Even offshore, you need local registered agent and director. EU requires real physical presence.
  • Underestimating ongoing costs: Compliance, audits, and legal fees add up, especially in EU.
  • Choosing solely on cost: Cheapest may not suit your target market or banking needs.
  • Neglecting tax planning: Offshore tax benefits require careful structuring to avoid unintended tax liabilities in home country.
  • Overlooking passporting: EU license allows serving all EU states; offshore license does not.

For example, a founder who chooses Panama solely for low cost may later struggle to open a bank account in the EU or face higher tax in their home country due to CFC rules. Consulting24 helps clients avoid these pitfalls by conducting a full feasibility analysis.

Alternatives: Comparing Panama with Other Jurisdictions

Besides Panama, other offshore options include the Cayman Islands (under SIBL) and Switzerland (though onshore, it offers a light-touch regime). The Cayman Islands provide a similar zero-tax structure but with higher costs (approx. EUR 15,000-20,000) and longer timelines. Switzerland's FINMA license is onshore but with lower capital than EU MiCA (as low as CHF 100,000 for limited services).

Onshore EU alternatives: Lithuania and Estonia are the most popular due to their efficient regulators and established fintech ecosystems. Frequently asked questions

What is the main difference between offshore and onshore crypto licensing?

Offshore licensing (e.g., Panama) offers lower costs, no minimum capital, and tax advantages but limited banking access and credibility. Onshore EU licensing under MiCA provides higher credibility, EU passporting, and easier banking but requires higher capital (EUR 50,000-150,000) and ongoing compliance costs.

Is Panama a good jurisdiction for crypto licensing in 2026?

Yes, Panama remains attractive for its flat EUR 6,000 fee, zero tax on offshore income, and no minimum capital. It is ideal for startups targeting non-EU markets. However, it lacks EU passporting and may face stigma with some partners.

What are the capital requirements for an EU crypto license under MiCA?

MiCA sets three tiers: EUR 50,000 for custody and transfer services, EUR 125,000 for exchange services, and EUR 150,000 for trading platforms. These must be held as own funds and are subject to ongoing prudential requirements.

Can I serve EU customers with a Panama crypto license?

Technically yes, but you would need to comply with each EU member state's local regulations, which may require registration or a physical presence. An EU license under MiCA provides passporting rights to serve all EU markets smoothly.

How long does it take to get a crypto license in Panama vs Lithuania?

Panama typically takes 2-4 months from submission to approval. Lithuania takes 3-6 months. Both timelines depend on the completeness of documentation and regulator workload.

What ongoing costs should I expect for a Panama crypto license?

Annual costs typically range from EUR 2,000 to EUR 3,000, including registered agent fees, government annual fees, and basic compliance. Audits and legal fees may add EUR 1,000-2,000 if required.

Does Panama require a physical office or local staff?

Panama requires a local registered agent and a local director (can be provided by the agent). No physical office or staff is mandated, but you must have a compliance officer (can be outsourced).

What services can I offer with a Panama crypto license?

The license covers exchange (crypto/fiat and crypto/crypto), custody, payment processing, and token issuance (subject to securities law). Derivatives and securities offerings require additional registration.

How does tax work for a Panama-licensed crypto company?

Panama taxes only Panama-source income. Income from services to non-resident clients is tax-free. No capital gains tax, no VAT on crypto, and no withholding tax on dividends. However, your home country may tax worldwide income.
