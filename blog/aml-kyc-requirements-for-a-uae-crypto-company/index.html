What Are AML/KYC Requirements for a UAE Crypto Company?

AML (Anti-Money Laundering) and KYC (Know Your Customer) requirements are mandatory procedures that crypto companies must implement to prevent financial crime. In the UAE, these are enforced by the Virtual Assets Regulatory Authority (VARA) in Dubai and the Securities and Commodities Authority (SCA) for mainland activities. The UAE Central Bank also issues guidelines. Essentially, you must verify customer identities, monitor transactions, and report suspicious activity.

For a UAE crypto company, AML/KYC is not optional. Failure to comply can result in fines, license revocation, or criminal liability. The requirements align with Financial Action Task Force (FATF) recommendations and apply to all virtual asset service providers (VASPs). The UAE also enforces the FATF Travel Rule, requiring VASPs to share originator and beneficiary information for transfers above a threshold. This adds a layer of operational complexity. Who Needs to Comply with UAE AML/KYC Rules?

Any entity that provides virtual asset services in or from the UAE must comply. This includes:

  • Crypto exchanges (centralized and decentralized)
  • Wallet providers
  • OTC desks
  • Token issuers
  • Brokerage and custody services
  • DeFi platforms (if they exert control over user funds)

Even if you are based in a free zone, you must register with VARA or the relevant regulator. The rules apply to both licensed and unlicensed operators, though unlicensed activity is illegal. License Type & Regulator in the UAE

The UAE does not have a single crypto license. Instead, you need specific approvals depending on your location and activity:

  • Dubai (VARA): Virtual Asset Service Provider (VASP) license. Covers exchanges, custody, and advisory. VARA has four categories: Exchange, Custody, Advisory, and Broker-Dealer.
  • Abu Dhabi (FSRA): Financial Services Permission (FSP) for virtual assets under ADGM. This is a more comprehensive license covering multiple activities under one umbrella.
  • Mainland (SCA): Securities license for tokenized securities and certain crypto activities. Less common for pure crypto businesses.

Each regulator has its own AML/KYC framework. VARA, for instance, requires a detailed AML policy, appointment of a compliance officer, and regular audits. The FSRA follows the ADGM AML rules, which are based on international standards. For a simpler regulatory environment, consider Panama, where Consulting24 handles the entire licensing process.

Capital Requirement for UAE Crypto Companies

The UAE imposes minimum capital requirements that depend on the license type and scope of activities. For a VARA license in Dubai, you typically need at least AED 500,000 (EUR 125,000) for a basic exchange. For custody or derivatives, the requirement can reach AED 2,000,000 (EUR 500,000). In ADGM, the FSRA requires a minimum of USD 500,000 (EUR 450,000) for a full VASP license. This capital must be deposited in a UAE bank account and cannot be withdrawn without approval.

These capital requirements are high compared to other jurisdictions. For example, Panama has no minimum capital requirement for a crypto license. This makes Panama attractive for startups. Tax Treatment for UAE Crypto Companies

The UAE offers a favorable tax environment: 0% corporate income tax for most free zone companies, no capital gains tax, and no VAT on crypto transactions (subject to certain conditions). However, as of 2026, the UAE has introduced a 9% corporate tax for mainland companies exceeding AED 375,000 profit. Free zone companies may still qualify for 0% if they meet substance requirements, such as having a physical office and local staff.

For crypto companies, the tax treatment depends on the nature of activities. Trading income may be taxable, while long-term holdings may be exempt. It is essential to get tax advice. In contrast, Panama taxes only local-source income, and crypto gains are generally tax-free. For a tax-efficient structure, Panama is worth considering.

Allowed Activities Under a UAE Crypto License

A UAE crypto license typically allows the following activities:

  • Exchange of virtual assets for fiat currency
  • Exchange of one virtual asset for another
  • Custody and wallet services
  • Token issuance (subject to approval)
  • Brokerage and advisory services
  • OTC trading

Each regulator has specific categories. VARA, for instance, has four license categories: Exchange, Custody, Advisory, and Broker-Dealer. You must apply for the category that matches your business model. Activities not covered require additional approvals. Panama's license is broader and covers most crypto activities without sub-categorization, allowing you to operate multiple services under one license.

Step-by-Step Process to Obtain a UAE Crypto License

  1. Business plan: Define your activities, target market, and compliance framework. Include financial projections and risk assessment.
  2. Entity formation: Incorporate a company in a free zone (e.g., DMCC, ADGM) or mainland. This involves registering with the free zone authority and obtaining a trade license.
  3. AML/KYC policy: Draft a comprehensive policy covering customer due diligence, transaction monitoring, and reporting. This must be tailored to your business model.
  4. Appoint a compliance officer: Must be a UAE resident with relevant experience (e.g., CAMS certification). The officer is personally liable for compliance failures.
  5. Submit application: File with VARA, FSRA, or SCA, including all supporting documents: business plan, AML policy, compliance officer CV, financial statements, and capital proof.
  6. Regulatory review: The regulator assesses your application, which may take 3-6 months. They may request additional information or an in-person interview.
  7. License issuance: Upon approval, you receive the license and must maintain ongoing compliance, including periodic audits and reporting.

Banking & Payment Solutions for UAE Crypto Companies

Banking in the UAE for crypto companies is challenging. Many traditional banks refuse to open accounts for crypto businesses due to perceived risk. However, some banks in free zones (e.g., RAKBANK, Mashreq) are more open. You will need to provide your AML/KYC policy, license, and proof of compliance. Expect a lengthy due diligence process that can take 2-3 months.

Compliance & Trust: AML/KYC Best Practices

To maintain compliance with UAE AML/KYC requirements, implement these best practices:

  • Customer due diligence (CDD): Verify identity using government-issued ID and proof of address. For high-risk customers, enhanced due diligence (EDD) is required, including source of funds and wealth.
  • Transaction monitoring: Use automated tools to flag suspicious transactions based on volume, frequency, and geography. Set thresholds for alerts.
  • Record keeping: Maintain records for at least 5 years, including transaction details and identification documents.
  • Suspicious activity reports (SARs): File with the UAE Financial Intelligence Unit (FIU) within 30 days of suspicion.
  • Training: Train staff on AML/KYC procedures regularly, at least annually.
  • Travel Rule compliance: Implement systems to share originator and beneficiary information for transfers over AED 10,000 (approx. EUR 2,500).

Note: This is general guidance, not legal advice. Always consult a qualified lawyer for your specific situation.

Common Mistakes in UAE AML/KYC Compliance

  1. Underestimating the compliance burden: Many startups think AML/KYC is just a checkbox. In reality, it requires ongoing resources, including a dedicated compliance team and software.
  2. Inadequate policies: Copying generic templates without customizing to your business model. Regulators expect a tailored approach.
  3. Poor record keeping: Failing to maintain proper audit trails can lead to fines during inspections.
  4. Ignoring travel rule: The UAE requires compliance with FATF's travel rule for crypto transfers. Many companies overlook this.
  5. Not appointing a qualified compliance officer: The officer must have relevant experience and be based in the UAE. Hiring a junior person can be a red flag.
  6. Delayed SAR filing: Missing the 30-day deadline can result in penalties.
