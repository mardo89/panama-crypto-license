What Is the Dubai vs Panama Comparison for Crypto Companies?

Dubai and Panama are both attractive jurisdictions for crypto businesses, but they cater to different needs. Dubai offers a sophisticated regulatory environment under VARA, with a clear licensing framework for virtual asset service providers (VASPs). Panama, on the other hand, provides a simpler, more cost-effective route with no specific crypto law, relying on existing corporate and financial regulations.

Dubai is ideal for companies targeting the Middle East and seeking regulatory clarity, while Panama appeals to those prioritising low costs and operational flexibility. Both jurisdictions allow crypto-related activities, but the compliance burden and ongoing costs differ significantly.

Who Needs a Crypto License in Dubai or Panama?

Any business that provides virtual asset services in or from Dubai must obtain a VARA license. This includes exchanges, custodians, brokers, and DeFi platforms. Panama does not have a dedicated crypto license; instead, crypto businesses operate under a standard corporate structure with a business license that covers their activities.

  • Dubai: Required for any entity offering virtual asset services within or from Dubai (including free zones).
  • Panama: Recommended for any company engaging in crypto trading, custody, or payment processing to ensure compliance with anti-money laundering (AML) regulations.

If you are a crypto founder looking to establish a presence in a regulated environment, Dubai is the stronger choice. For a lower-cost entry point with fewer regulatory hurdles, Panama may be more suitable. Consulting24 can guide you through both options, with direct delivery in Panama and advisory for Dubai.

License Type and Regulator: VARA vs Panama's Authorities

Dubai: The regulator is the Virtual Assets Regulatory Authority (VARA), established under Dubai Law No. 4 of 2022. VARA issues licenses for various virtual asset activities, including exchange, custody, and advisory services. The licensing framework is comprehensive and aligned with international standards, including FATF recommendations.

Panama: There is no single crypto regulator. Crypto companies are regulated under the general corporate law (Law 32 of 1927) and must comply with AML obligations overseen by the Superintendency of Banks of Panama (SBP) and the Financial Analysis Unit (UAF). The business license is issued by the Ministry of Commerce and Industries (MICI).

In contrast, EU member states under MiCA have a unified licensing regime with capital tiers of EUR 50,000, 125,000, or 150,000 depending on service class, which is fully in force in 2026.

Capital Requirements

Dubai: VARA does not prescribe a fixed minimum capital in law, but in practice, applicants must demonstrate sufficient financial resources. Typically, a minimum of AED 500,000 (approx. EUR 125,000) is expected, with higher amounts for larger operations.

Panama: No minimum capital requirement for a standard corporation. The authorised capital is usually USD 10,000, but no paid-up capital is required. This makes Panama extremely capital-light.

For comparison, EU member states under MiCA require capital of EUR 50,000 / 125,000 / 150,000 depending on service class. Panama's flexibility is a major advantage for startups.

For example, a crypto exchange with custody services in the EU would need EUR 150,000 capital, while in Panama the same activity can start with zero paid-up capital.

Tax Treatment

Dubai: No corporate income tax for most free zone companies. A 9% corporate tax applies to mainland companies with profits above AED 375,000, but free zone companies can maintain 0% tax if they comply with qualifying income rules. No VAT on crypto transactions (VAT only on services).

Panama: Territorial taxation - only income sourced in Panama is taxed. Offshore income (including crypto trading with non-Panamanian counterparties) is tax-free. No capital gains tax, no VAT on crypto. Panama is often considered a tax-neutral jurisdiction for international crypto businesses.

Both jurisdictions offer favourable tax treatment, but Panama's territorial system is simpler for pure international operations. For instance, a Panama company trading crypto with clients in Asia pays 0% Panamanian tax on that income.

Allowed Activities Under Each License

Dubai VARA license: Covers a wide range of virtual asset services, including:

  • Exchange (spot, derivatives)
  • Custody
  • Broker-dealer
  • Advisory and management
  • Payment services (issuing stablecoins, remittances)

Panama business license: No specific crypto license, but the following activities are commonly conducted under a standard corporate structure:

  • Cryptocurrency trading (proprietary and client)
  • Exchange operations (peer-to-peer, OTC)
  • Custody and wallet services
  • Payment processing
  • Token issuance (subject to securities law if applicable)

Dubai's license provides explicit regulatory approval for each activity, while Panama offers more flexibility but less regulatory certainty. For example, a DeFi platform may find Panama's open framework more accommodating, whereas a regulated exchange would benefit from Dubai's clarity.

Step-by-Step Process to Set Up in Dubai vs Panama

Dubai (VARA) Process

  1. Choose a free zone (e.g., DMCC, ADGM) or mainland setup.
  2. Register the company and obtain initial approval.
  3. Prepare VARA application (business plan, AML policy, compliance manual).
  4. Submit to VARA and undergo review (including background checks).
  5. Pay license fees and obtain final approval.
  6. Set up bank account and commence operations.

Panama Process

  4. Open a corporate bank account (Panama or international).
  5. Begin operations.

Banking and Payment Services

Dubai: Banks in Dubai are generally crypto-friendly, especially free zone banks like RAKBANK and Mashreq. However, compliance requirements are strict, and accounts may take 1-3 months to open. Some banks require a minimum deposit of AED 100,000.

Panama: Banking is more challenging. Local banks are cautious about crypto, but international banks (e.g., in Switzerland, Lithuania) are more open. Panama's corporate structure allows easy access to payment processors and EMI accounts in the EU.

  • Access to Middle East and Asian markets.

Your choice depends on your budget, target market, and need for regulatory clarity. Panama is ideal for cost-conscious startups; Dubai suits companies seeking a regulated hub.

Compliance and Trust: AML and Regulatory Obligations

Dubai: VARA requires a strong AML/CFT framework, including appointment of a compliance officer, transaction monitoring, and regular audits. Non-compliance can result in fines or license revocation. Dubai's regime is aligned with FATF standards, enhancing trust with partners and banks.

Panama: AML obligations exist under Law 23 of 2015, but enforcement is less stringent. Companies must register with the UAF if conducting certain activities, but the overall compliance burden is lower. However, this may reduce trust with counterparties in regulated markets.

Common Mistakes When Choosing Between Dubai and Panama

  • Underestimating costs: Dubai's total setup can exceed EUR 50,000; Panama's is EUR 6,000. Budget accordingly.
  • Ignoring regulatory requirements: Dubai's VARA process is rigorous; Panama's simplicity may lead to overlooking AML obligations.
  • Assuming banking is easy: Both jurisdictions require effort to open accounts; plan ahead.
  • Not considering tax residency: Panama's territorial tax only applies if you are not tax resident there; Dubai's 0% tax is conditional on free zone compliance.
  • Choosing based on hype: Evaluate your business model, target market, and long-term goals rather than following trends.

For example, a startup that chooses Dubai for prestige but cannot afford the ongoing compliance costs may struggle. Conversely, a company that chooses Panama for low cost but needs to partner with regulated EU entities may find trust issues.
