What is the Italy crypto license?

Italy's crypto license is a mandatory registration for virtual asset service providers (VASPs) operating in the country. As of 2026, Italy fully implements the EU's Markets in Crypto-Assets (MiCA) regulation, replacing the previous registration regime. The license is issued by the Organismo Agenti e Mediatori (OAM), which oversees compliance with anti-money laundering (AML) and counter-terrorism financing (CTF) rules. Companies must also register with the Italian Financial Intelligence Unit (UIF) for AML supervision.

The license covers activities such as exchange, custody, and transfer of crypto assets. It is essential for any business offering crypto services to Italian residents. Without it, firms face fines and legal action. The process involves a detailed application, capital requirements, and ongoing reporting obligations. Under MiCA, the license is harmonised across the EU, meaning that once obtained in Italy, you can passport services to other member states. However, Italy imposes additional local requirements such as a physical office and a local AML officer. Many founders mistakenly believe that a lighter registration from another EU country automatically covers Italy, but local registration is still required for certain services. Who needs an Italy crypto license?

Any entity providing crypto services to Italian residents must obtain the license. This includes:

  • Crypto exchanges (centralized and decentralized)
  • Custodial wallet providers
  • Brokers and dealers in crypto assets
  • Payment processors using crypto
  • ATM operators

Foreign companies offering services to Italian customers also need to register, unless they operate under a passport from another EU member state under MiCA. Many founders mistakenly believe that a license from another EU country automatically covers Italy, but local registration is still required. For example, a company licensed in Lithuania under the old regime may need to re-register in Italy if it wants to actively market to Italian clients. License type and regulator

The Italy crypto license is a VASP registration under MiCA, supervised by the Organismo Agenti e Mediatori (OAM). The regulator is responsible for maintaining the official register of crypto-asset service providers. Additionally, the Bank of Italy and the Italian Securities and Exchange Commission (CONSOB) have oversight roles in specific areas such as financial stability and investor protection.

Under MiCA, there are three license classes based on services offered: Class 1 (basic services like exchange and transfer) requires EUR 50,000 capital; Class 2 (custody and trading) requires EUR 125,000; Class 3 (full services including proprietary trading) requires EUR 150,000. The license is valid across the EU under passporting, but Italy requires a local legal presence and compliance infrastructure. It is a common mistake to apply for the wrong class, either underestimating or overestimating the capital needed. Capital requirement

Under MiCA, the minimum capital requirement for an Italy crypto license is determined by the service class. As of 2026, the tiers are:

  • Class 1: EUR 50,000
  • Class 2: EUR 125,000
  • Class 3: EUR 150,000

These amounts must be held as paid-up share capital and maintained throughout the license period. Many applicants fail to demonstrate that the capital is unencumbered and available for operational risks. Additionally, the capital must be deposited in an Italian bank account, which can be a hurdle for foreign founders. Consulting24 assists with bank account opening and capital verification. We have seen cases where founders tried to use crypto assets as proof of capital, which is not accepted. Only fiat currency in a bank account qualifies. A common mistake is not maintaining the capital after approval; the regulator conducts periodic checks, and a drop below the threshold can lead to license suspension.

Tax treatment

Italy imposes a 26% capital gains tax on crypto profits for individuals and a corporate tax rate of 24% (IRES) plus regional tax (IRAP) for companies. VAT is not applicable on crypto-to-crypto trades, but fiat-to-crypto transactions may be subject to VAT in some cases. The tax treatment is complex, and many founders make the mistake of not accounting for withholding taxes on dividends or interest from crypto activities.

Consulting24 recommends engaging a local tax advisor to structure your operations efficiently. Compared to Panama, which has no capital gains tax on crypto, Italy's tax burden is higher. However, Italy offers access to the EU single market, which can offset costs for some businesses. For example, a company generating EUR 1 million in revenue from EU clients may find the Italian tax cost acceptable given the market access. We advise clients to model their tax exposure before choosing a jurisdiction.

Allowed activities

An Italy crypto license permits the following activities under MiCA:

  • Exchange of crypto assets for fiat currency
  • Exchange of crypto assets for other crypto assets
  • Custody and administration of crypto assets
  • Transfer of crypto assets on behalf of clients
  • Execution of orders for crypto assets
  • Placing of crypto assets
  • Receiving and transmitting orders for crypto assets
  • Advising on crypto assets

Each activity requires specific compliance measures. A common mistake is applying for a license that does not cover all intended services, leading to the need for a supplementary application. Consulting24 helps map your business model to the correct license class. For instance, if you plan to offer both exchange and custody, you need at least Class 2 capital. Some founders try to avoid higher capital by splitting services into separate entities, but this can increase overall costs and complexity.

Step-by-step application process

The application process for an Italy crypto license involves several stages. Here is a step-by-step guide:

  1. Pre-application preparation: Define your business model, select the appropriate license class, and prepare a detailed business plan.
  2. Company incorporation: Register a legal entity in Italy with a registered office. This can take 2-4 weeks.
  3. AML/CTF program: Develop a compliant AML policy, risk assessment, and internal controls. This is a common area for mistakes.
  4. Capital deposit: Deposit the required capital in an Italian bank and obtain proof of funds.
  5. Submit application: File with OAM along with all supporting documents. Pay the application fee.
  6. Review and interview: OAM may request additional information or conduct an interview with the management team.
  7. Approval and registration: Once approved, you are registered in the OAM register and can start operations.

The entire process typically takes 6-12 months. Many applicants underestimate the time needed for document preparation. For example, translating corporate documents into Italian and having them notarised can take weeks. Consulting24 provides a checklist and timeline to keep you on track. Banking and payments

  • Italian banks like UniCredit or Intesa Sanpaolo (require strong compliance history)
  • Specialized crypto-friendly banks such as Fideuram or CheBanca.
  • EMI (Electronic Money Institution) accounts for faster setup

Frequently asked questions

What is the minimum capital for an Italy crypto license in 2026?

Under MiCA, the minimum capital depends on the service class: Class 1 (basic services) requires EUR 50,000, Class 2 (custody and trading) requires EUR 125,000, and Class 3 (full services) requires EUR 150,000. This capital must be held as paid-up share capital in an Italian bank account.

How long does it take to get an Italy crypto license?

The typical timeline is 6 to 12 months from application submission to approval. Delays often occur due to incomplete documentation or inadequate AML programs. With proper preparation, some clients have obtained approval in 7 months.

Can I use a license from another EU country to operate in Italy?

Under MiCA, you can passport your license from another EU member state to Italy. However, Italy may require additional local registrations, such as with OAM, and a physical presence. Consulting24 can advise on whether passporting or direct licensing is more efficient.

What are the tax implications for crypto companies in Italy?

Italy imposes a 26% capital gains tax on crypto profits for individuals, and corporate tax of 24% (IRES) plus regional tax (IRAP) for companies. VAT treatment varies. Compared to Panama's 0% capital gains tax, Italy's tax burden is higher, but EU market access may justify it.

Is it mandatory to have a local director in Italy?

Yes, Italy requires at least one local director who is an Italian resident or an EU resident with a physical presence in Italy. This director must be involved in the day-to-day management and compliance oversight.

What are the most common reasons for rejection of an Italy crypto license application?

Common reasons include incomplete or poorly translated documents, inadequate AML/CTF policies, failure to demonstrate sufficient capital, lack of a local office, and not covering all intended services in the application. Working with an experienced advisor reduces these risks.

Can I apply for an Italy crypto license remotely?

While the application can be prepared remotely, you must have a physical office in Italy and a local director. Some steps, such as notarisation of documents, may require in-person presence. Consulting24 can coordinate local representatives for you.

How does the Italy crypto license compare to Panama's crypto license?

Panama offers a flat fee of EUR 6,000, no capital requirement, 0% capital gains tax, and a timeline of 2-4 months. Italy costs EUR 20,000-55,000, requires EUR 50,000-150,000 capital, has a 26% CGT, and takes 6-12 months. Panama is faster and cheaper, but Italy provides EU market access.

What ongoing compliance obligations are there after obtaining the license?

Ongoing obligations include regular AML/CTF reporting, annual audits, maintaining the minimum capital, and updating the regulator on any changes in management or business model. Failure to comply can result in fines or license revocation.
