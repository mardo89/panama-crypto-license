Overview: Georgia vs Panama for a Crypto Company

Georgia and Panama are both attractive for crypto entrepreneurs, but for different reasons. Georgia has a dedicated virtual asset service provider (VASP) licensing regime under the NBG, which is aligned with the EU's MiCA framework. Panama, on the other hand, does not have a specific crypto license; crypto businesses operate under general corporate law. This fundamental difference shapes the entire comparison.

Georgia offers regulatory clarity and a path to compliance with international standards, which can be crucial for attracting institutional investors and forming banking relationships. Panama provides flexibility and lower upfront costs, making it ideal for startups and projects that want to launch quickly without heavy regulatory overhead. Your choice depends on your business model, target market, and need for regulatory approval.

For founders who prioritize a regulated status to signal credibility, Georgia's VASP license is a strong asset. Panama suits those who want to minimize initial expenses and operate with fewer bureaucratic constraints. Both have pros and cons, which we break down in this guide with concrete examples and data.

Who Needs a Crypto License in Georgia vs Panama?

In Georgia, any business offering virtual asset services such as exchange, custody, or transfer must obtain a VASP license from the NBG. This includes both fiat-to-crypto and crypto-to-crypto platforms, as well as OTC desks and custodial wallet providers. In Panama, there is no specific license; however, businesses must comply with anti-money laundering (AML) laws and register with the Financial Analysis Unit (UAF) if they engage in financial activities.

If you need a regulated status to partner with banks or attract institutional investors, Georgia is the better choice. For example, a Georgian licensed exchange can more easily open a corporate account with European correspondent banks. If you prefer a lighter regulatory touch and lower costs, Panama may suit you. Examples of businesses that typically need a license in Georgia include centralized exchanges, custodial wallet providers, and OTC desks. In Panama, even large exchanges operate without a specific license, but they must implement strong AML programs and register with the UAF if they handle fiat currency.

License Type & Regulator

Georgia: The NBG issues a VASP license under the Law on Virtual Asset Service Providers. The regime is based on the EU's MiCA framework, with capital tiers and operational requirements. The license allows activities like exchange, custody, and transfer of virtual assets. The NBG conducts thorough due diligence on applicants, including background checks on beneficial owners.

Panama: No specific crypto license. Crypto companies typically incorporate as a standard corporation (Sociedad Anónima) and register with the UAF for AML compliance if they engage in financial intermediation. This means less regulatory burden but also less formal recognition. However, Panama's legal system is stable and business-friendly.

Capital Requirement

Georgia: Minimum capital is EUR 50,000 for basic services (e.g., exchange), EUR 125,000 for custody services, and EUR 150,000 for more complex activities like operating a trading platform with margin. These are in line with MiCA tiers. The capital must be deposited in a Georgian bank account and cannot be withdrawn during the license period. It serves as a financial guarantee for the NBG.

Panama: No minimum capital for a standard corporation. You can start with as little as USD 10,000 in paid-in capital, but there is no legal minimum. This makes Panama attractive for startups with limited funds. However, some banks may require a minimum deposit to open a corporate account.

If you have limited capital, Panama is attractive. If you need to meet EU standards, Georgia's capital requirement is a barrier but also a sign of credibility. The capital must be maintained throughout the license period and can be used for operational expenses only with NBG approval.

Tax Treatment

Georgia: Corporate income tax is 15% on distributed profits. A territorial tax system applies: income from outside Georgia is not taxed if not repatriated. Crypto gains may be subject to capital gains tax at 15%. VAT is 18% on services, but crypto transactions may be exempt under certain conditions. Georgia has double tax treaties with many countries.

Panama: Territorial taxation: only income sourced in Panama is taxed. Offshore income is exempt. No capital gains tax on crypto. No VAT. Panama offers a more tax-efficient environment for international crypto businesses. For example, a crypto exchange serving global clients from Panama would pay zero tax on trading fees from non-Panamanian users. However, Panama does not have extensive double tax treaties.

Consulting24 recommends Panama for tax optimization, but always seek professional tax advice. For example, a DeFi protocol generating revenue from global users may benefit from Panama's territorial system, while a Georgian licensed exchange may benefit from treaty access.

Allowed Activities

Georgia: Licensed VASPs can operate crypto exchanges (fiat-to-crypto and crypto-to-crypto), custody services, transfer services, and wallet services. Activities must be pre-approved by the NBG. Additional activities like staking or lending may require separate approval. The license scope is defined in the application.

Panama: No restrictions on crypto activities under general corporate law. You can run an exchange, payment processor, mining, DeFi, or NFT marketplace. However, you must not violate AML laws or engage in unlicensed financial intermediation. Panama offers more flexibility, but Georgia provides a clear regulatory framework that may be required for certain partnerships.

For instance, a Georgian license may be necessary to integrate with EU payment processors or to list on certain exchanges. In Panama, you can experiment with innovative business models without prior approval, but you bear the risk of regulatory changes.

Banking & Payments

Georgia: Banks are cautious but some work with licensed VASPs. You may need to present your NBG license to open a corporate account. International payments are possible but may be slow due to correspondent banking restrictions. Examples of banks that accept crypto clients include TBC Bank and Bank of Georgia, but they require thorough due diligence.

Panama: Banking is more accessible. Many international banks operate in Panama, and crypto-friendly banks exist, such as Banco General, Credicorp Bank, and Multibank. However, compliance is strict, and you will need to provide a clear business plan and source of funds. Consulting24 can introduce you to banking partners who understand the crypto industry.

Panama generally offers easier banking for crypto companies, but Georgia's license can open doors with EU banks. For example, a Georgian licensed exchange may obtain an account with a European correspondent bank, while a Panama company might rely on local banks with higher fees.

Benefits of Each Jurisdiction

Georgia: Regulatory clarity, EU-aligned framework, growing crypto ecosystem, potential for passporting within the EU (via MiCA equivalence), and a skilled workforce. The NBG is proactive in engaging with the crypto industry.

Panama: Low cost, fast setup, no crypto-specific regulation, territorial tax, strong banking sector, no capital gains tax, and a stable legal environment. Panama also offers a strategic location for Latin American markets.

Compliance & Trust

Georgia's VASP license signals compliance with international AML/CFT standards. This builds trust with partners, banks, and regulators. The NBG conducts regular audits and requires ongoing reporting. Panama relies on general AML compliance, which may be less recognized by foreign regulators. However, Panama has a solid reputation as a financial center and is a member of FATF.

Consulting24 advises that for high-risk activities or institutional clients, Georgia may be more credible. For startups, Panama offers a lower barrier to entry. Always implement strong compliance measures regardless of jurisdiction, including KYC, transaction monitoring, and suspicious activity reporting. This is general guidance, not legal advice.

  • Assuming Panama needs no compliance. You must still register with UAF and follow AML rules if handling fiat or providing financial services.
  • Choosing based on tax alone without considering banking and partner requirements. For example, a Georgian license may be necessary to work with EU payment processors.
