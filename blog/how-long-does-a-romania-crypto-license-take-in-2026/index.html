What Is a Romania Crypto License?

A Romania crypto license, officially a CASP (Crypto Asset Service Provider) authorization under MiCA, allows companies to offer crypto services such as exchange, custody, and wallet management. Since MiCA is fully in force in 2026, Romania has transposed the regulation into national law, requiring all crypto service providers to obtain authorization from the National Bank of Romania (BNR) and the Financial Supervisory Authority (ASF). The license is essential for legal operation within Romania and across the EU under the single passport.

The process involves a thorough assessment of your business model, AML/CFT procedures, governance, and capital adequacy. Unlike pre-MiCA regimes that had minimal requirements, Romania now enforces strict standards aligned with EU directives. The application requires detailed documentation, including a business plan, risk assessment, and proof of own funds. The regulator also evaluates the fit and proper status of directors and shareholders, which can add time if background checks are extensive.

For example, a crypto exchange offering both fiat-to-crypto and crypto-to-crypto services must meet the EUR 125,000 capital tier and provide evidence of secure custody arrangements. The license is valid across the EU, but you must maintain ongoing compliance with reporting and audit obligations.

Who Needs a Romania Crypto License?

Any business offering crypto-related services in Romania or targeting Romanian customers must obtain a CASP license. This includes:

  • Cryptocurrency exchanges (fiat-to-crypto and crypto-to-crypto)
  • Custodial wallet providers
  • Brokerage services
  • Payment services using crypto assets
  • DeFi platforms that fall under MiCA scope, such as those with a central operator or governance token

If you are a startup or an established company looking to expand into the EU, the Romanian license can serve as a gateway. However, the timeline and cost may vary based on your service classes. For instance, a simple advisory service requires lower capital (EUR 50,000) and less documentation, while a full exchange with custody needs EUR 150,000 and more extensive AML procedures.

Companies that already hold a CASP license in another EU member state can passport into Romania without a separate application, but new entrants must go through the full authorization process. License Type and Regulator

Romania's crypto license is a single CASP authorization under MiCA, supervised jointly by the National Bank of Romania (BNR) for prudential oversight and the Financial Supervisory Authority (ASF) for market conduct and investor protection. The license covers multiple service categories, each with specific capital requirements.

The regulator assesses fit and proper criteria for management, operational resilience, and AML/CFT compliance. As of 2026, the application is submitted through a single electronic portal, with a preliminary review within 15 working days. The BNR and ASF coordinate to ensure consistency with EU standards, but this dual oversight can sometimes slow down the process if both agencies request additional information.

For example, the ASF may focus on consumer protection aspects, while the BNR examines financial stability and capital adequacy. Applicants must provide separate documentation for each regulator, though the portal aims to streamline submissions. Capital Requirement

Under MiCA, Romania applies tiered capital requirements based on the type of crypto services offered. As of 2026, the minimum capital is:

  • EUR 50,000 for simple services like order execution or advisory
  • EUR 125,000 for exchange services (fiat-to-crypto or crypto-to-crypto)
  • EUR 150,000 for custodial wallet services or combined services

These amounts must be maintained as own funds, and evidence of capital must be provided with the application. This is a significant increase from pre-MiCA regimes where no minimum capital was required. The capital must be held in a bank account in Romania and cannot be used for operational expenses. For example, a company offering both exchange and custody must hold EUR 150,000, not the sum of individual tiers.

In contrast, Panama has no minimum capital requirement, making it more accessible for startups. Consulting24 can help you structure your capital to meet Romanian requirements while optimising your balance sheet.

Tax Treatment for Crypto Businesses in Romania

Romania taxes corporate income at a standard rate of 16%. Crypto-related income is treated as ordinary business income, subject to the same rate. VAT is generally not applicable to crypto transactions, but advisory services may be subject to VAT at 19%. Additionally, Romania has a withholding tax of 16% on dividends distributed to non-residents, unless reduced by a tax treaty.

For example, if your Romanian entity earns EUR 100,000 from exchange fees, you pay 16% corporate tax, leaving EUR 84,000. Distributing dividends to a foreign parent may incur another 16% withholding, though treaties like the Romania-US tax treaty can reduce this to 10% or 5% depending on ownership. Romania also has a micro-enterprise tax regime for small companies (revenue under EUR 500,000) at 1% or 3%, but this may not apply to crypto businesses due to the nature of income.

For comparison, Panama offers territorial taxation (no tax on foreign-source income) and no capital gains tax, making it more tax-efficient for many crypto businesses. Consulting24 can help you structure your operations to optimize tax liability.

Allowed Activities Under the License

A Romania CASP license permits the following activities under MiCA:

  • Providing custody and administration of crypto assets on behalf of clients
  • Operating a trading platform for crypto assets
  • Exchanging crypto assets for fiat currency or other crypto assets
  • Executing orders for crypto assets on behalf of clients
  • Placing crypto assets (underwriting or placement)
  • Receiving and transmitting orders for crypto assets
  • Providing advice on crypto assets
  • Managing crypto asset portfolios

Each activity class may require separate authorization or notification. The license does not cover activities outside MiCA's scope, such as pure DeFi protocols without a central operator. For instance, if you operate a non-custodial wallet that does not hold client keys, you may not need a license, but offering custodial services requires the EUR 150,000 capital tier. Step-by-Step Application Process

  1. Pre-application preparation: Define your business model, select service classes, and prepare documentation including business plan, AML policy, risk assessment, and governance structure. This stage typically takes 2-4 weeks.
  2. Company incorporation: Register a legal entity in Romania (typically a SRL) with a minimum share capital of EUR 200 (not to be confused with regulatory capital). Incorporation takes 1-2 weeks.
  3. Capital deposit: Deposit the required own funds (EUR 50,000-150,000) in a Romanian bank account and obtain proof. This can take 1-2 weeks depending on bank processing.
  4. Submit application: File the application electronically via the BNR/ASF portal, including all supporting documents and payment of the application fee. The regulator has 15 working days to acknowledge receipt.
  5. Regulator review: The regulator has 3-6 months to issue a decision. They may request additional information, which can extend the timeline. Typical review time is 4-5 months for complete applications.
  6. Post-approval: Once approved, you must maintain ongoing compliance, submit regular reports, and undergo periodic audits. The license is valid indefinitely but subject to annual renewal fees.

Common Mistakes When Applying for a Romania Crypto License

  • Incomplete documentation: Missing business plan or AML policy leads to delays. Ensure all documents are translated into Romanian if required.
  • Underestimating capital: Not having the required own funds ready in a Romanian bank account before submission.
  • Ignoring fit and proper requirements: Management with criminal records or poor reputation can cause rejection. Conduct background checks early.
  • Choosing the wrong service class: Selecting classes that don't match your business model, leading to reapplication or additional capital needs.
  • Not engaging local advisors: Romanian language requirements and legal nuances can trip up foreign applicants. Work with local legal counsel.
  • Overlooking ongoing compliance: After approval, you must submit quarterly reports and maintain AML procedures. Non-compliance can lead to fines.
