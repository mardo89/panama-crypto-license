What Is the Lithuania CASP Licence Under MiCA?

The Lithuania CASP licence under MiCA is the new authorisation required for any entity offering crypto-asset services in Lithuania. It aligns with the EU-wide MiCA framework, which came into full effect in 2025-2026. The licence is issued by the Bank of Lithuania, which acts as the competent authority under MiCA. Key features include:

  • Harmonised passporting rights across all EU member states.
  • Three capital tiers based on service types: EUR 50,000 for simple services (e.g., custody), EUR 125,000 for exchange services, and EUR 150,000 for trading platforms.
  • Mandatory governance, AML, and consumer protection measures.
  • Transition period for existing VASPs ended in 2025; all new applicants must apply under MiCA.

This licence is essential for any crypto business targeting the European market from Lithuania. The Bank of Lithuania has a reputation for being thorough but fair, and the process is more predictable than in some other EU states. For example, the regulator publishes detailed guidance on application requirements, reducing uncertainty. The licence also allows firms to offer services across the EU without additional authorisation, making Lithuania a strategic hub for crypto operations.

Who Needs a Lithuania CASP Licence?

Any legal entity providing crypto-asset services in or from Lithuania must hold a CASP licence. This includes:

  • Crypto exchanges (fiat-to-crypto and crypto-to-crypto).
  • Custodial wallet providers.
  • Brokers dealing in crypto-assets.
  • Platforms offering crypto staking or lending services (if classified as crypto-asset services).
  • Market makers and OTC desks.

If you plan to serve EU clients, the Lithuania CASP licence under MiCA is a strategic choice due to its efficient regulatory process and relatively lower costs compared to other EU hubs like Malta or Cyprus. For non-EU operations, Panama offers a simpler, flat-fee structure at EUR 6,000. Even if your target market is global, having an EU licence adds credibility and access to the single market. For example, a crypto exchange based in Lithuania can onboard clients from Germany, France, and Spain without needing separate licences, saving significant time and cost.

Licence Type and Regulator

The licence is a single CASP authorisation under MiCA, administered by the Bank of Lithuania (Lietuvos bankas). Unlike the previous VASP registration, which was a lighter-touch regime, the CASP licence requires a full application with detailed documentation, including a business plan, risk assessment, governance framework, and capital proof. The Bank of Lithuania is known for its pragmatic approach but enforces strict compliance with MiCA standards. There is no separate licence for different service types; instead, the licence scope reflects the services applied for, with capital adjusted accordingly. The regulator also conducts ongoing supervision, including periodic reviews and audits. For instance, the Bank of Lithuania may request updated AML policies annually or conduct on-site inspections. This regulatory rigour enhances trust among clients and partners.

Capital Requirement

Under MiCA, the minimum capital requirement for a CASP depends on the services offered:

  • EUR 50,000 for custody and transfer services.
  • EUR 125,000 for exchange services (fiat-to-crypto or crypto-to-crypto).
  • EUR 150,000 for trading platforms (matching orders).

This capital must be held in liquid assets and demonstrated at application. The Bank of Lithuania may require additional capital based on risk assessment. Compared to Estonia, which also follows MiCA with similar tiers, Lithuania offers a slightly faster process. The capital must be maintained throughout the licence period and cannot be withdrawn without regulatory approval. For example, if you apply for a custody licence, you must show EUR 50,000 in a bank account or liquid securities. This capital serves as a buffer for operational risks and client protection. It is important to note that the capital requirement is separate from the company's share capital, which is typically EUR 2,500 for a UAB.

Tax Treatment

Lithuania applies a 15% corporate income tax on profits, with a reduced 5% rate for small companies (revenue under EUR 300,000, fewer than 10 employees). Crypto-to-crypto transactions are generally not taxable at the point of exchange; only realisation into fiat triggers a taxable event. VAT is not charged on crypto-asset services. Lithuania has a favourable tax environment for crypto businesses, especially compared to higher-tax EU jurisdictions. However, tax advice from a local specialist is essential. For example, if you hold crypto as an investment, capital gains tax may apply upon sale. Lithuania also has a participation exemption for dividends from subsidiaries, which can be beneficial for holding structures. Additionally, the country has a wide network of double tax treaties, reducing withholding taxes on cross-border payments. For a detailed analysis, see our cost page.

Allowed Activities

Under the Lithuania CASP licence, the following activities are permitted:

  • Execution of orders on behalf of clients.
  • Placing of crypto-assets.
  • Reception and transmission of orders.
  • Custody and administration of crypto-assets.
  • Exchange services (fiat-to-crypto, crypto-to-crypto).
  • Operation of a trading platform.
  • Advice on crypto-assets.

Activities not covered (e.g., crypto derivatives) may require additional licences under MiFID II. The licence allows passporting across the EU, making Lithuania a gateway to the European market. If you plan to offer services that are not explicitly listed, you should check with the regulator or seek legal advice. For instance, if your platform offers staking as a service, it may fall under custody or advice, depending on the structure. The Bank of Lithuania provides a list of classified activities in its guidance, which is updated periodically.

Frequently asked questions

What is the difference between the old VASP registration and the new CASP licence in Lithuania?

The old VASP registration was a lighter-touch regime with no minimum capital requirement and simpler AML obligations. The new CASP licence under MiCA requires minimum capital of EUR 50,000 to EUR 150,000, a full application with detailed governance and risk documentation, and ongoing compliance with EU-wide standards. The transition period ended in 2025, so all new applicants must apply for the CASP licence.

Can I passport the Lithuania CASP licence to other EU countries?

Yes, the CASP licence under MiCA allows passporting across all EU member states. Once licensed in Lithuania, you can offer services in any other EU country without needing additional authorisation. This is one of the main advantages of the licence, as it provides access to a market of over 450 million consumers.

What are the ongoing compliance requirements after obtaining the licence?

Ongoing compliance includes annual AML/CFT audits, regular reporting to the Bank of Lithuania on transaction volumes and client complaints, maintaining minimum capital, and updating policies as regulations change. The regulator may also conduct on-site inspections. Consulting24 offers ongoing compliance support to help you meet these obligations.

How long does the Lithuania CASP licence application process take?

The process typically takes 3 to 6 months from submission to approval. The timeline depends on the complexity of your business model and the completeness of your application. Consulting24 helps prepare a thorough application to minimise delays. For simple services like custody, approval can be closer to 3 months.

Is there a physical presence requirement for the Lithuania CASP licence?

Yes, you need a registered office in Lithuania and a local director or AML officer. The Bank of Lithuania requires that the company has a real presence, including staff and operational capacity. Consulting24 can assist with finding a registered office and recruiting local personnel if needed.

Can I use the Lithuania CASP licence to offer crypto derivatives?

No, crypto derivatives (such as futures and options) are not covered under MiCA and require a MiFID II licence. The CASP licence covers only crypto-asset services as defined in MiCA. If you plan to offer derivatives, you may need a separate investment firm licence under MiFID II.

What happens if I fail to maintain the minimum capital requirement?

If you fail to maintain the minimum capital, the Bank of Lithuania may impose fines, suspend your licence, or revoke it. It is critical to monitor capital levels and report any changes to the regulator. Consulting24 can help set up systems to track capital adequacy.

Is the Lithuania CASP licence recognised outside the EU?

While the licence is an EU authorisation, it is recognised globally as a sign of regulatory compliance. Many non-EU jurisdictions and financial institutions view an EU CASP licence as a mark of credibility, which can facilitate partnerships and banking relationships outside the EU.

Can I apply for the Lithuania CASP licence if my company is already registered in another EU country?

Yes, you can establish a Lithuanian subsidiary and apply for the CASP licence. Alternatively, if your existing company is already licensed as a CASP in another EU member state, you can passport into Lithuania without a separate application. However, if you want to be regulated in Lithuania, you must apply through the Bank of Lithuania.

What are the tax implications for crypto-to-crypto transactions in Lithuania?

Crypto-to-crypto transactions are generally not taxable at the point of exchange. Tax is triggered only when crypto is converted to fiat currency or used to purchase goods and services. Corporate income tax of 15% (or 5% for small companies) applies on profits. It is advisable to consult a local tax advisor for your specific situation.
