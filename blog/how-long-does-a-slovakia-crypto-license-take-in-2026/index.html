What Is a Slovakia Crypto License?

A Slovakia crypto license is an authorisation from the National Bank of Slovakia (NBS) to provide virtual asset services as a CASP (Crypto Asset Service Provider) under MiCA. It replaced the previous VASP registration system in 2025. The license covers activities such as custody, exchange, transfer, and advisory services for crypto assets.

Key features of the Slovak regime include:

  • Full compliance with EU MiCA regulation, ensuring passporting rights across the EEA.
  • Minimum capital requirements from EUR 50,000 to EUR 150,000 depending on service type.
  • Mandatory AML/CFT policies, including a local AML officer and transaction monitoring.
  • Regular reporting to the NBS on financial and operational metrics.

As of 2026, Slovakia is one of the more accessible EU jurisdictions for crypto licensing, with a clear regulatory framework and a relatively efficient application process compared to some other member states. For example, the NBS has published detailed guidelines on AML policies, which helps applicants prepare correctly. The regulator also offers a pre-application consultation to clarify requirements, reducing the risk of rejection. However, the timeline can still stretch if your business model is complex or if you apply for multiple service classes.

Who Needs a Slovakia Crypto License?

Any entity offering crypto asset services to Slovak residents or operating from Slovakia must obtain a license. This includes:

  • Centralised and decentralised exchanges (spot and derivatives).
  • Custodial wallet providers.
  • Brokers and dealers in crypto assets.
  • Platforms facilitating crypto-to-crypto or crypto-to-fiat trading.
  • Advisory firms offering investment advice on crypto assets.

If your business model involves any of these activities and you target the EU market, a Slovak license can serve as your gateway. However, if you are looking for a faster and more cost-effective option outside the EU, consider Panama, where we can set up a licensed entity for a flat EUR 6,000. For startups with limited capital, Panama avoids the EUR 50,000 minimum capital requirement, making it an attractive alternative. Additionally, if your services are purely non-custodial or you operate a decentralised exchange without a central operator, you may fall outside the licensing scope, but legal advice is essential to confirm.

License Type and Regulator

The regulator for crypto licenses in Slovakia is the National Bank of Slovakia (Národná banka Slovenska, NBS). It operates under the authority of the European Central Bank and enforces MiCA standards.

There is one main license category: the CASP license, which covers all virtual asset services. However, the specific permissions depend on the services you apply for. The NBS classifies services into several categories, each with its own capital requirement:

  • Class 1: Custody and administration of crypto assets on behalf of clients (EUR 125,000 minimum capital).
  • Class 2: Operation of a trading platform (EUR 150,000).
  • Class 3: Exchange of crypto assets for fiat or other crypto assets (EUR 50,000).
  • Class 4: Execution of orders on behalf of clients (EUR 50,000).
  • Class 5: Investment advice (EUR 50,000).

You can apply for multiple classes in one application. The NBS also requires that the company's management team has sufficient experience and is fit and proper. For example, directors must provide CVs, police clearance certificates, and proof of relevant qualifications. The NBS may interview key personnel during the review process. Compared to other EU regulators, Slovakia's approach is pragmatic but thorough, with a focus on substance over form.

Capital Requirement

Under MiCA, the capital requirement for a Slovakia crypto license depends on the type of services offered. The minimum capital must be paid up and held in a Slovak bank account. It cannot be used for operational expenses-it serves as a safeguard for clients.

  • For custody services: EUR 125,000.
  • For trading platforms: EUR 150,000.
  • For exchange and order execution: EUR 50,000.
  • For advisory services: EUR 50,000.

If you apply for multiple classes, the highest capital requirement applies. The NBS may also require additional capital based on the volume of transactions. Note that this capital is separate from the company's registered capital (which is typically EUR 5,000 for an s.r.o.). The capital must be deposited in a blocked account and released only with NBS approval. For example, if you plan to offer both custody and exchange services, you need EUR 125,000 (the higher of the two). Some applicants choose to apply for a single class initially to reduce capital outlay, then expand later. Compared to Lithuania, which has a similar capital structure under MiCA, Slovakia's requirements are identical, but the application process differs.

Tax Treatment

Slovakia has a standard corporate income tax rate of 21% (15% for companies with turnover below EUR 100,000). Crypto asset transactions are generally treated as taxable events, subject to capital gains tax. VAT does not apply to crypto-to-fiat exchanges, but advisory fees may be subject to VAT.

Key tax points:

  • Profits from crypto trading are taxed as business income.
  • Losses can be offset against gains within the same tax year.
  • Withholding tax on dividends is 7% (or lower under tax treaties).
  • Transfer pricing rules apply to related-party transactions.

Slovakia does not offer specific crypto tax incentives. For a more tax-efficient structure, some founders choose Panama, which has no corporate tax on offshore income. However, if your customers are in the EU, you may still have VAT or withholding tax obligations. Slovakia has a double tax treaty network that can reduce withholding taxes on dividends and interest. It is advisable to structure your operations with a tax advisor, especially if you plan to repatriate profits. The NBS does not tax crypto directly, but the Financial Administration (Finančná správa) enforces reporting requirements for crypto transactions.

Allowed Activities

A Slovakia CASP license permits the following activities under MiCA:

  • Custody and administration of crypto assets on behalf of clients.
  • Operation of a crypto trading platform.
  • Exchange of crypto assets for fiat currency or other crypto assets.
  • Execution of orders for clients.
  • Placing of crypto assets.
  • Receiving and transmitting orders for crypto assets.
  • Providing advice on crypto assets.

Activities not covered include issuing stablecoins (e-money license required) or operating as a crypto ATM (separate license needed). The license allows passporting to other EU member states without additional authorisation. For example, if you obtain a Slovak CASP license, you can offer services in Germany or France by simply notifying the local regulator. This is a major advantage over non-EU jurisdictions like Panama, where you would need separate licenses for each country. However, passporting requires compliance with local consumer protection laws and language requirements. The NBS maintains a public register of licensed CASPs, which adds credibility for clients.

Step-by-Step Process

Obtaining a Slovakia crypto license involves several stages. Here is a typical timeline:

  1. Pre-application preparation (1-2 months): Company incorporation in Slovakia, preparation of AML policies, business plan, risk assessment, and governance documents. You will need a local registered office and a local AML officer.
  2. Submission of application (1 week): Submit the complete application to the NBS, including all supporting documents and payment of the fee.
  3. Review and clarifications (2-4 months): The NBS reviews the application and may request additional information. This is the most variable phase.
  4. Approval and licensing (1 month): Once satisfied, the NBS issues the license. You must then deposit the minimum capital and set up operational infrastructure.

Compliance and Trust

Maintaining a Slovak crypto license requires ongoing compliance. The NBS expects:

  • Annual AML audits and reporting.
  • Transaction monitoring and suspicious activity reporting.
  • Regular financial reporting (quarterly or annually).
  • Fit and proper assessments of management.
  • Compliance with travel rule requirements (transfer of crypto assets).

Failure to comply can result in fines or license revocation. We recommend engaging a local compliance officer and using automated monitoring tools. This is general guidance, not legal advice; consult a Slovak lawyer for your specific situation. The NBS may conduct thematic reviews or on-site inspections. For example, in 2025, the NBS fined several CASPs for inadequate AML controls. To avoid this, implement strong KYC procedures and report suspicious transactions promptly.   • Insufficient capital: Not having the required minimum capital deposited before application.
  • Poorly prepared management: Lack of relevant experience or criminal records among directors.
  • Ignoring local presence: The NBS expects a physical office and local staff (at least an AML officer).
