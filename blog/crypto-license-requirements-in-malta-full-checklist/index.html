What Is a Crypto License in Malta?

Malta's crypto license, officially a Virtual Financial Assets (VFA) license, is a regulatory authorisation issued by the Malta Financial Services Authority (MFSA). It allows companies to operate a crypto exchange, wallet service, or other VFA-related business. The framework is based on the Virtual Financial Assets Act (VFAA) and the Innovative Technology Arrangements and Services Act (ITAS).

Malta aims to be a blockchain island, but its regime is demanding. The MFSA requires a detailed business plan, fit and proper management, and strong AML/CFT controls. Unlike some EU countries that offer a lighter registration, Malta's full license is a Class 4 financial services authorisation. This means the same rigorous standards apply to all VFA service providers, regardless of size. For example, even a small wallet provider must meet the same capital and compliance requirements as a major exchange. The MFSA conducts thorough background checks on all beneficial owners and key personnel, including interviews and on-site inspections. This level of scrutiny can be a barrier for startups but adds credibility for established firms.

Who Needs a Crypto License in Malta?

Any entity providing VFA services in or from Malta needs a license. This includes:

  • Virtual financial asset exchanges (centralised and peer-to-peer)
  • VFA wallet providers (custodial and non-custodial)
  • VFA investment services (brokerage, advisory, portfolio management)
  • Issuers of VFAs (including ICOs and STOs)
  • Custodians of VFAs

If you plan to serve EU clients, Malta offers a MiCA-compliant regime. However, many startups find the process lengthy and costly. For example, a DeFi project that wants to offer a non-custodial wallet may not need a license if they do not hold client funds, but if they provide custody or exchange services, they must apply. The MFSA also regulates VFA agents, who must be licensed separately. It is important to assess your specific activities against the VFAA definitions.

License Type and Regulator

The regulator is the Malta Financial Services Authority (MFSA). The license is a Class 4 VFA license, which is the highest tier. There is no separate lighter license for smaller firms. All VFA service providers must meet the same standards. The MFSA also oversees ITAS for innovative technology arrangements (like DLT registries) and the Malta Digital Innovation Authority (MDIA) for certification of technology. But for crypto exchange or wallet, the VFA license from MFSA is the main requirement.

The MFSA is known for its thoroughness. They conduct interviews with key personnel, review business plans in detail, and perform on-site inspections. The application process is rigorous and can take 6-12 months. In contrast, the Lithuania crypto license under MiCA is a CASP license with a similar capital requirement but a faster timeline of 3-6 months. Capital Requirement

Malta imposes a minimum capital requirement of EUR 125,000 for VFA license applicants. This is a hard requirement and must be maintained at all times. The capital can be in cash or liquid assets. For comparison, under MiCA in 2026, EU member states apply capital tiers of EUR 50,000 / 125,000 / 150,000 by service class. Malta's EUR 125,000 aligns with the mid-tier, covering most exchange and wallet services. For example, a simple wallet provider may need EUR 50,000 under MiCA, but Malta's VFA regime requires EUR 125,000 regardless. This is a key difference.

Some founders find this barrier high. Panama has no capital requirement, making it more accessible for early-stage startups. Tax Treatment

Malta offers a favourable tax regime for crypto companies. Corporate income tax is 35%, but through Malta's full imputation system and tax refunds, the effective rate can be as low as 5% for non-resident shareholders. Crypto transactions are generally treated as trading income, not subject to VAT. However, the MFSA requires substance: you must have a physical office and at least one director residing in Malta. This means you cannot be a shell company; you need real operations.

For comparison, Panama taxes only local-source income, and offshore crypto companies pay 0% on foreign income. Many founders pair a Panama company with a Malta license for substance. For example, a trading firm might have its trading entity in Panama and a regulated entity in Malta for EU clients. For more on tax planning, read our blog on crypto tax strategies.

Allowed Activities

With a VFA license, you can:

  • Operate a VFA exchange (order book, OTC, P2P)
  • Provide VFA wallet services (custodial and non-custodial)
  • Offer VFA investment advice and portfolio management
  • Issue VFAs (subject to a white paper approved by MFSA)
  • Provide VFA custody and administration

You cannot engage in activities not covered by the license, such as derivatives or margin trading without additional authorisation. The MFSA also restricts certain activities for retail clients. For instance, offering use above 2:1 to retail investors may require a MiFID license. For a full list, see our requirements page. If your business model includes multiple services, you may need a broader license scope, which could increase capital requirements.

Step-by-Step Process

  1. Pre-application: Engage a local compliance advisor. Prepare a detailed business plan, AML/CFT policies, and risk assessment. This stage typically takes 1-2 months.
  2. Submit application: File with MFSA including all documents, fees, and proof of capital. The MFSA will acknowledge receipt and begin review.
  3. MFSA review: The MFSA assesses fitness and propriety of directors, shareholders, and key personnel. This includes background checks and interviews. Expect 3-6 months.
  4. On-site inspection: MFSA may visit your office to verify substance and systems. This can take 1-2 days.
  5. Decision: If approved, you receive a conditional or full license. Conditions may include additional reporting or capital requirements. The entire process takes 6-12 months.
  6. Post-license: Ongoing compliance, annual audits, and supervisory fees. You must submit periodic reports to MFSA.

Banking and Payments

Opening a bank account for a Malta VFA company is challenging. Many traditional banks avoid crypto. Some options include:

  • Malta-based banks like Bank of Valletta (BOV) and HSBC Malta, but they have strict AML policies and often require a minimum deposit of EUR 50,000.
  • EMIs like Revolut Business, Monese, or Payoneer (limited crypto acceptance).
  • Specialist crypto-friendly banks in other EU jurisdictions, such as those in Lithuania or Estonia.

Benefits of a Malta Crypto License

Malta offers several advantages:

  • EU passporting: A Malta license allows you to serve clients across the EU under MiCA.
  • Strong reputation: Malta is a respected financial centre, adding credibility with partners and clients.
  • Tax efficiency: Effective tax rate as low as 5% for non-residents.
  • Regulatory clarity: The VFAA is well-established and understood, with a track record of approvals.

These benefits come at a cost. For many startups, Panama offers a better cost-benefit ratio. Compare more jurisdictions on our jurisdictions page. For example, a company that needs EU passporting for B2B services may find Malta worthwhile, while a B2C exchange may prefer Panama for speed.

Compliance and Trust

Compliance is rigorous. You must appoint a Money Laundering Reporting Officer (MLRO), conduct ongoing transaction monitoring, and submit regular reports to MFSA. The MFSA also requires annual audits by a recognised auditor. Non-compliance can result in fines or license revocation. For instance, failure to report suspicious transactions can lead to penalties of up to EUR 500,000.

Common Mistakes

  • Insufficient substance: Malta requires a physical office and resident directors. Remote setups are not accepted. You need at least one director residing in Malta and a local office with staff.
  • Poor AML documentation: The MFSA expects detailed, customised AML policies, not templates. Generic policies are rejected.
  • Underestimating timeline: Many applications take 9-12 months. Plan accordingly. Rushing the process leads to errors.
  • Ignoring capital requirements: The EUR 125,000 capital must be maintained at all times. If you use it for operating expenses, you may fall below the minimum.
  • Lack of local expertise: Attempting to apply without local advisors often leads to delays. Engage a Malta-based law firm or compliance consultant.
