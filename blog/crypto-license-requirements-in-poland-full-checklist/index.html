What is a Crypto License in Poland?

A crypto license in Poland, officially a 'CASP registration' under MiCA, is the mandatory authorization for any entity offering virtual asset services. Poland's Financial Supervision Authority (KNF) acts as the competent authority, enforcing the EU-wide CASP regime. As of 2026, all crypto service providers must hold this license to operate legally in Poland.

The license covers a range of activities, from exchange and custody to transfer services. It is not a simple registration; it requires a strong compliance framework, capital reserves, and ongoing reporting. The KNF has the power to impose fines, suspend operations, or revoke licenses for non-compliance. For example, in 2025, KNF fined several unregistered entities for operating without a license, highlighting the importance of proper authorization.

Unlike some jurisdictions where a simple registration suffices, Poland demands a full-fledged authorization process. This includes a detailed business plan, AML/KYC policies, and proof of capital. The license is valid across the EU under the MiCA passport, making it attractive for firms targeting the European market.

Who Needs a Crypto License in Poland?

Any company providing one or more of the following services to Polish residents needs a license:

  • Exchange of crypto for fiat or other crypto
  • Custody and administration of crypto assets
  • Operation of a trading platform (order book)
  • Transfer services (sending crypto on behalf of clients)
  • Advice on crypto investments (if regulated)

If your business involves any of these, you must apply. Even foreign entities targeting Polish users may need a Polish branch or a passported license from another EU state. For instance, a company based in Estonia that actively markets to Polish customers may need to register in Poland unless it holds a MiCA license elsewhere. The KNF has been proactive in monitoring cross-border activities.

Exemptions exist for entities that only provide non-custodial software or hardware wallets, or for those acting solely as miners or node operators. However, if you hold private keys for clients or facilitate exchanges, you are likely within scope. Consulting24 can help assess whether your business model requires a license.

License Type and Regulator

The Polish regulator for crypto licenses is the Polish Financial Supervision Authority (KNF). Under MiCA, there is a single license type: the CASP (Crypto Asset Service Provider) authorization. However, the capital requirement varies by the services offered.

Service ClassMinimum Capital (EUR)
Only exchange and/or transfer50,000
Exchange + custody125,000
All services including platform150,000

These figures are set by MiCA and are strictly enforced by KNF. The regulator also assesses the suitability of directors and shareholders, requiring them to have clean criminal records and relevant experience. KNF may impose additional conditions, such as higher capital for high-risk activities or mandatory insurance.

Poland does not have a separate 'crypto exchange license' or 'custody license'; the CASP covers all. However, you must specify which services you intend to offer, and the capital requirement is based on the highest tier of service. For example, if you offer exchange and custody, you need EUR 125,000, not the sum of both.

Capital Requirement

As shown above, the minimum capital is EUR 50,000 for basic services, EUR 125,000 for custody, and EUR 150,000 for full-service platforms. This capital must be fully paid up and maintained at all times. It can be in cash or liquid assets, and must be held in a Polish bank account or an EU account with a branch in Poland.

KNF may require additional capital based on the volume of transactions or risk profile. For example, if your projected monthly trading volume exceeds EUR 1 million, the regulator may ask for a capital buffer of up to 25% of operational costs. Unlike some jurisdictions, Poland does not allow capital to be in crypto. The capital must be in fiat currency (EUR or PLN) and cannot be borrowed.

Proof of capital must be submitted with the application, typically in the form of a bank statement or a letter from the bank. The capital must remain untouched during the application process. Once licensed, you must maintain the capital at all times, and any reduction must be reported to KNF.

Tax Treatment of Crypto in Poland

Poland treats crypto as a financial asset. Corporate income tax (CIT) is 19% (or 9% for small taxpayers). VAT is not applied to crypto exchange transactions, but services like custody may be subject to VAT at 23%. The tax treatment depends on the nature of the service. For example, exchange fees are VAT-exempt, while custody fees are taxable.

Capital gains from crypto trading are taxed at 19% for corporations. Individuals pay 19% flat tax on crypto gains. There is no special crypto tax regime; standard rules apply. It is crucial to maintain detailed records of all transactions for tax reporting. The Polish tax authority (KAS) has been increasing audits of crypto firms, so proper accounting is essential.

Losses can be offset against gains within the same tax year, but carryforward is limited to 5 years. Transfer pricing rules apply if transactions with related parties exceed certain thresholds. Consulting24 recommends engaging a local tax advisor to ensure compliance.

Allowed Activities Under the License

A Polish CASP license permits the following activities, as per MiCA:

  • Exchange of crypto for fiat currency
  • Exchange of crypto for other crypto
  • Provision of custody and administration of crypto assets
  • Operation of a trading platform
  • Execution of orders on behalf of clients
  • Placing of crypto assets
  • Transfer services for crypto assets
  • Advice on crypto assets
  • Portfolio management

Each activity requires specific compliance procedures. The license does not cover security tokens or other financial instruments (those fall under MiFID II). If your business involves both crypto and securities, you may need dual licensing. For example, a platform offering tokenized stocks would need both a CASP and an investment firm license.

Activities not covered include mining, node operation, and software development (unless you hold keys). The KNF has issued guidance clarifying that mere software provision is not a regulated activity. However, if you provide a wallet with key management, that is custody and requires a license.

Step-by-Step Application Process

  1. Pre-application preparation: Draft business plan, AML/KYC policies, risk assessment, and internal procedures. Appoint a compliance officer and a board member with relevant experience. The compliance officer must be based in Poland or the EU and have at least 3 years of experience in AML.
  2. Company incorporation: Register a Polish limited liability company (sp. z o.o.) or a branch. Minimum share capital is PLN 5,000 (approx. EUR 1,100), but higher for the license. The company must have a registered office in Poland.
  3. Open a bank account in Poland or an EU bank with a Polish branch. Deposit the required capital. Many banks require a license application before opening an account, creating a chicken-and-egg problem. Consulting24 can help with banking introductions.
  4. Submit application to KNF via the electronic portal. Include all documents: business plan, AML policies, organizational structure, financial projections, and personal questionnaires for directors. The application fee is around EUR 3,000.
  5. KNF review: KNF has 3 months to assess completeness. They may request additional information. Once complete, they issue a decision within 3 months. In practice, the total review period is 6-9 months.
  6. Post-approval: Pay the license fee, implement ongoing reporting, and begin operations. KNF conducts periodic audits. You must submit quarterly reports on transaction volumes and capital adequacy.

Banking and Payment Services

Opening a bank account for a crypto company in Poland is challenging but possible. Major banks like PKO BP, mBank, and Santander may accept crypto clients, but they require the license and a proven compliance framework. Some banks have internal policies that restrict crypto-related accounts, so you may need to approach multiple banks.

Alternative payment providers include:

  • PSP (Payment Service Providers) like Paydo or Billon
  • EMI (Electronic Money Institutions) that offer IBAN accounts
  • Crypto-friendly banks in Lithuania or Estonia (via passporting)

Many Polish crypto firms use EMI accounts for daily operations, as they are easier to open. However, EMIs may have lower transaction limits. For larger volumes, a traditional bank account is preferable. Consulting24 can help connect you with banking partners. For a more straightforward banking environment, many founders choose Lithuania or Panama.

It is important to note that Polish banks often require a minimum deposit of EUR 10,000 to open a corporate account, and they may charge monthly fees of EUR 50-100. Some banks also require a personal guarantee from the director.

Benefits of a Polish Crypto License

  • EU passporting: Once licensed in Poland, you can serve clients across the EU without additional licenses, thanks to MiCA. This gives you access to a market of over 450 million people.
  • Regulatory clarity: Poland has fully implemented MiCA, so rules are clear and stable. There is no ambiguity about what is required.
  • Access to EU market: Poland is a large market with a growing crypto adoption rate. According to a 2025 survey, 12% of Poles own crypto, up from 8% in 2023.
  • Reputation: A Polish license signals credibility to partners and investors. It shows that you have passed a rigorous regulatory process.
  • No crypto tax on exchange: VAT exemption on exchange fees reduces operational costs.

However, the high capital requirement and long timeline are drawbacks. For startups with limited budgets, Panama or Estonia may be more suitable.

Compliance and Trust Considerations

Compliance is the backbone of a Polish crypto license. You must maintain a strong AML/CFT program, including customer due diligence (CDD), transaction monitoring, and suspicious activity reporting. KNF expects a dedicated compliance officer with relevant experience, preferably a certified AML specialist.

Annual audits by an external auditor are mandatory. You must also submit periodic reports to KNF on transaction volumes, capital adequacy, and compliance incidents. Failure to comply can result in fines of up to EUR 5 million or 10% of annual turnover, suspension, or revocation of the license.

KNF also conducts on-site inspections. In 2025, they inspected 15 CASPs and found deficiencies in 12, leading to corrective actions. Common issues include inadequate transaction monitoring and lack of independent audits. To avoid these, implement strong systems from day one.

Common Mistakes When Applying

  • Incomplete documentation: KNF is strict about document completeness. Missing even a single form can delay the process by months. Use a checklist provided by KNF.
  • Underestimating capital: Some applicants try to use lower capital than required. KNF checks this and will reject applications with insufficient capital.
  • Weak AML policies: Generic policies copied from other jurisdictions will be rejected. They must be tailored to Polish law, including specific risk factors for crypto.
  • Ignoring tax obligations: Not registering for CIT/VAT can lead to penalties. Ensure you register with the tax office before starting operations.
  • Choosing the wrong legal form: A branch of a foreign company may have different requirements. A subsidiary is often simpler.
  • Not appointing a local compliance officer: KNF requires a compliance officer based in Poland. Remote officers are not accepted.

Frequently asked questions

What is the minimum capital for a crypto license in Poland?

The minimum capital is EUR 50,000 for exchange and transfer services, EUR 125,000 if custody is included, and EUR 150,000 for a full trading platform. These are set by MiCA and enforced by KNF.

How long does it take to get a Polish crypto license?

The process typically takes 6 to 12 months. KNF has a 3-month preliminary review, but full approval often takes longer due to additional document requests. Some applicants report 8-10 months from submission to final decision.

Can a foreign company apply for a Polish crypto license?

Yes, a foreign company can apply by setting up a Polish subsidiary (sp. z o.o.) or a branch. The subsidiary is more common. The company must have a registered office in Poland and comply with all local requirements.

What are the tax implications for crypto companies in Poland?

Corporate income tax is 19% (9% for small taxpayers). VAT is not applied to crypto exchange transactions, but custody services are subject to 23% VAT. Capital gains are taxed at 19% for both corporations and individuals.

Is a Polish crypto license valid across the EU?

Yes, under MiCA, a Polish CASP license allows you to passport services to other EU member states without additional licensing. You must notify KNF of your intention to operate cross-border.

What are the ongoing compliance requirements?

You must maintain AML/KYC procedures, submit quarterly reports to KNF on transaction volumes and capital adequacy, undergo annual external audits, and report any suspicious activities. Non-compliance can lead to fines or license revocation.

Can I use a Polish license for a crypto exchange?

Yes, operating a crypto exchange is a permitted activity under the CASP license. You need to specify this in your application and meet the capital requirement for exchange services (EUR 50,000).

What happens if I operate without a license in Poland?

Operating without a license is illegal and can result in fines of up to EUR 5 million or 10% of annual turnover, imprisonment for up to 5 years, and seizure of assets. KNF actively monitors and penalizes unlicensed operators.

How does Poland compare to Panama for crypto licensing?

Poland offers EU passporting but has high capital requirements (EUR 50,000+) and a long timeline (6-12 months). Panama costs a flat EUR 6,000, takes 4-6 weeks, and has no capital requirement, but does not provide EU market access.

Do I need a local director for a Polish crypto company?

Yes, at least one director must be a resident of Poland or the EU. KNF requires that directors have a clean criminal record and relevant experience. The compliance officer must also be based in Poland.
