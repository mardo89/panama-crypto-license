Overview: Georgia vs Panama for a Crypto Company

Georgia and Panama represent two distinct models for crypto licensing. Georgia, a Eurasian country, has developed a specific regulatory framework for virtual asset service providers (VASPs) under the National Bank of Georgia (NBG). Panama, a Central American hub, offers a more flexible approach with no dedicated crypto license but allows crypto activities under a standard corporate structure, often combined with a payment processor license. For founders comparing Georgia vs Panama for a crypto company, the choice often comes down to regulatory clarity versus cost efficiency.

Georgia's framework is more structured, requiring compliance with AML/CFT rules, capital thresholds, and ongoing reporting. Panama, on the other hand, is lighter-touch but still requires substance and banking relationships. Consulting24's experience shows that Panama is often preferred for cost-sensitive startups, while Georgia attracts those seeking a regulated environment with EU alignment. However, Panama's simplicity and low cost make it a strong contender for many crypto businesses.

Consider your target market: if you plan to serve EU clients, Georgia's alignment with FATF may be beneficial, but Panama's territorial tax system can offer significant savings for global operations. Both jurisdictions have their merits, and the right choice depends on your specific circumstances.

Who Needs a Crypto License in Georgia or Panama?

Founders typically consider Georgia vs Panama for a crypto company based on their target market and operational scale. You might need a license if you are:

  • Operating a cryptocurrency exchange (centralized or decentralized).
  • Providing custodial wallet services.
  • Offering crypto-to-fiat conversion services.
  • Running a payment processor handling digital assets.
  • Engaging in crypto trading on behalf of clients (brokerage).
  • Launching a crypto ATM network.

In Georgia, any entity providing virtual asset services must register with the NBG. This includes exchanges, wallets, and transfer services. Panama does not have a specific crypto license, but activities like exchange or payment processing may require a commercial license and compliance with anti-money laundering laws. For example, if you operate a crypto-to-fiat exchange in Panama, you will likely need a payment processor license from the Superintendencia de Bancos. License Type and Regulator

Georgia: The regulator is the National Bank of Georgia (NBG). The license is a VASP registration under the Law on Combating Money Laundering and Terrorism Financing. It is not a full-fledged license but a mandatory registration with ongoing compliance obligations. As of 2026, Georgia is not an EU member, but its framework aligns with FATF recommendations. The NBG conducts inspections and can impose sanctions for non-compliance.

Panama: There is no dedicated crypto regulator. Instead, crypto companies operate under a general corporate license, often with a payment processor license from the Superintendencia de Bancos de Panama (SBP) if handling fiat. Panama's approach is less prescriptive, making it easier to start but requiring self-regulation and strong banking relationships. The SBP oversees payment processors, while the Superintendencia del Mercado de Valores may regulate crypto securities. For a detailed comparison of other jurisdictions, see our hub page.

Capital Requirement

Capital requirements differ significantly between the two. Georgia requires a minimum share capital of GEL 500,000 (approximately EUR 160,000) for VASP registration, which must be paid up and maintained. This is a substantial barrier for smaller startups and can tie up funds that could be used for operations. Panama has no statutory minimum capital for a standard corporation; however, banks may require a minimum deposit (often USD 10,000) to open an account. For payment processing, a higher capital may be expected, typically USD 50,000 or more. If capital is tight, Panama may be the more accessible option. Tax Treatment

Georgia: Corporate income tax is 15%, but there is a preferential regime for international companies: a 5% withholding tax on dividends distributed to non-residents, and no tax on retained earnings. VAT is 18% but crypto transactions may be exempt. Georgia has a territorial tax system, meaning foreign-sourced income is generally not taxed. However, careful structuring is needed to ensure foreign income is not deemed Georgian-source.

Panama: Panama taxes only income sourced within Panama (territorial system). Offshore companies (with operations outside Panama) pay no corporate tax. For local activities, the rate is 25%. No capital gains tax on crypto trades if sourced abroad. Panama also has no VAT, making it attractive for crypto businesses. Both jurisdictions offer tax advantages, but Panama's territorial system is simpler for international crypto operations. For example, a Panama company trading crypto on international exchanges would pay zero tax on trading profits.

Allowed Activities

In Georgia, registered VASPs can engage in: exchange between virtual and fiat currencies, exchange between virtual assets, transfer of virtual assets, safekeeping/administration, and participation in financial services related to virtual assets. The NBG may restrict certain activities like derivatives or margin trading. In Panama, allowed activities are broader due to the lack of specific restrictions. You can operate a crypto exchange, wallet, payment processor, or even a crypto ATM network, provided you comply with AML laws. However, activities involving securities (e.g., ICOs, security tokens) may fall under securities regulation and require a license from the Superintendencia del Mercado de Valores. Step-by-Step Application Process

Georgia:

  1. Incorporate a company in Georgia (LLC or JSC) with minimum capital of GEL 500,000.
  2. Prepare AML/KYC policies, business plan, risk assessment, and internal controls.
  3. Submit application to NBG with required documents (certified translations into Georgian).
  4. NBG reviews within 60 days; may request additional info or clarifications.
  5. Upon approval, register with the Financial Monitoring Service of Georgia.
  6. Open a corporate bank account in Georgia or abroad (may require physical presence).
  7. Implement ongoing reporting: quarterly transaction reports, annual AML audit.

Panama:

  1. Incorporate a Panama corporation (Sociedad Anonima) with a resident agent and registered address.
  2. Obtain a commercial license (Aviso de Operacion) from the municipality (cost ~USD 200).
  3. If handling fiat, apply for a payment processor license from SBP (requires capital, AML program, and physical office).
  4. Open a corporate bank account (requires due diligence, often a video call or visit).
  5. Implement AML compliance program: customer due diligence, transaction monitoring, suspicious activity reporting.
  6. File annual reports and maintain corporate records.

Banking and Payment Services

Banking is a common challenge for crypto companies. Georgia has a few banks willing to work with registered VASPs, but they often require a local presence and high minimum deposits (e.g., USD 50,000). Banks like TBC Bank and Bank of Georgia have crypto-friendly policies but conduct thorough due diligence. Panama has a more developed offshore banking sector, with several banks accepting crypto-related businesses, though due diligence is strict. Panama also offers access to USD-based accounts, which is advantageous for global operations. Banks in Panama may require a minimum deposit of USD 10,000 to USD 50,000.   • Strategic location between Europe and Asia, with access to Eurasian markets.
  • Growing crypto ecosystem and talent pool.
  • No tax on foreign-sourced income under territorial system.

Panama:

  • Low setup cost (EUR 6,000 fixed) and fast incorporation (4-6 weeks).
  • No minimum capital requirement for standard corporation.
  • Territorial tax system (no tax on foreign income, no capital gains tax on offshore trades).
  • USD-based economy, eliminating currency risk.
  • Strong banking sector with crypto-friendly options.

Compliance and Trust

Compliance is non-negotiable in both jurisdictions. Georgia requires a detailed AML program, regular reporting to NBG, and external audits. The NBG has the power to conduct on-site inspections and impose fines for non-compliance. Panama requires AML compliance under Law 23 of 2015, including customer due diligence, record keeping, and suspicious transaction reporting. Failure to comply can result in fines or license revocation. In both jurisdictions, you must appoint a compliance officer and maintain a register of beneficial owners. Common Mistakes When Choosing a Jurisdiction

Founders often make these errors:

  • Underestimating capital requirements (especially in Georgia, where EUR 160,000 is required).
  • Ignoring substance requirements (physical office, local staff, board meetings). Both jurisdictions require substance.
  • Choosing based on tax alone without considering banking access or regulatory clarity.
  • Not preparing AML documentation in advance, causing delays.
  • Assuming Panama has no regulation - it does, and non-compliance is risky (fines, account closure).
  • Overlooking ongoing compliance costs (annual reports, AML audits, resident agent fees).

Frequently asked questions

What is the main difference between Georgia and Panama for a crypto company?

Georgia has a dedicated VASP registration with the National Bank of Georgia, requiring a minimum capital of GEL 500,000 and ongoing compliance. Panama has no specific crypto license; companies operate under a general corporate license, often with a payment processor license. Panama is cheaper (EUR 6,000 fixed) and faster (4-6 weeks), while Georgia offers more regulatory clarity.

Which jurisdiction is cheaper for crypto licensing?

Panama is significantly cheaper. The fixed cost for company setup and licensing coordination is EUR 6,000. Georgia's costs are estimated at EUR 8,000-15,000 plus a capital requirement of EUR 160,000. Panama also has lower annual compliance costs (EUR 1,500-2,500 vs EUR 3,000-5,000).

Do I need a physical office in Georgia or Panama?

Both jurisdictions require substance. Georgia expects a local registered address and may require a physical office for larger operations. Panama requires a registered agent and a physical address; for payment processor licenses, a physical office is mandatory. Consulting24 can help you set up compliant substance arrangements.

Can I use Panama for an EU-focused crypto business?

Panama is not an EU member, so it does not offer MiCA passporting. However, you can serve EU clients under Panama's framework, but you must comply with EU AML regulations and may need to register in each member state. For EU passporting, consider Lithuania or Estonia instead.

What are the tax benefits of Panama for crypto companies?

Panama has a territorial tax system: income sourced outside Panama is tax-free. Crypto trading on international exchanges is typically tax-free. No capital gains tax on offshore trades, no VAT, and no withholding tax on dividends paid to non-residents. This makes Panama highly attractive for global crypto operations.

How long does it take to get a crypto license in Georgia?

The NBG review process takes up to 60 days, but the entire process including company incorporation and document preparation typically takes 8-12 weeks. Delays can occur if additional information is requested. Consulting24 streamlines the process to minimize delays.

What are the ongoing compliance requirements in Panama?

Panama requires annual corporate fees (resident agent, registered address), filing of financial statements, and AML compliance under Law 23. This includes customer due diligence, transaction monitoring, and suspicious activity reporting. Payment processor license holders have additional reporting to the SBP.

Yes. Consulting24 has established relationships with banks in both jurisdictions. We facilitate introductions and guide you through the due diligence process. Banking is a critical step, and we ensure you meet the requirements for account opening.

Is Georgia's crypto framework recognized by the EU?

Georgia is not an EU member, but its framework aligns with FATF recommendations. The EU does not automatically recognize Georgian licenses, but compliance with FATF standards may facilitate cooperation. For EU passporting, a MiCA license from an EU member state is required.

What activities are prohibited in Georgia under the VASP registration?

The NBG may prohibit activities involving derivatives, margin trading, or securities. Activities must be limited to those defined in the law: exchange, transfer, safekeeping, and administration of virtual assets. ICOs and security tokens may fall under securities regulation. How does Panama's payment processor license work for crypto?

If you handle fiat currency (e.g., crypto-to-fiat exchange), you may need a payment processor license from the Superintendencia de Bancos. This requires a minimum capital (typically USD 50,000), a physical office, and an AML program. The license allows you to process payments, including crypto-to-fiat conversions.
