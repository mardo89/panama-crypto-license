What Is Crypto Banking in Malta?

Crypto banking in Malta refers to the ability of crypto businesses to open and maintain corporate bank accounts with Maltese banks, and to access payment services such as fiat on/off ramps, custody, and merchant services. Unlike some jurisdictions where crypto-native banks have emerged, Malta's traditional banks-such as Bank of Valletta, HSBC Malta, and APS Bank-have been cautious, often requiring a full MFSA VFA license before opening an account.

In 2026, the situation has improved slightly. The MFSA's regulatory clarity, combined with EU-wide Anti-Money Laundering (AML) standards, means that licensed VFA service providers can approach banks with a credible compliance framework. However, many founders still face long onboarding processes and high minimum deposit requirements. For those who find Malta's banking environment too restrictive, jurisdictions like Lithuania or Panama offer more accessible banking solutions.

Who Needs Crypto Banking in Malta?

Any crypto business that intends to operate in or from Malta, or that targets Maltese customers, will need a local bank account for operational purposes. Typical entities include:

  • VFA exchanges and trading platforms
  • Custodian wallet providers
  • Payment processors handling fiat-crypto conversions
  • Token issuers conducting STOs or ICOs
  • Fund managers dealing with crypto assets

Even if your business is registered elsewhere, having a Maltese bank account can facilitate transactions with local partners or clients. However, many founders find that the strict compliance requirements make it challenging. License Type and Regulator

The primary regulator for crypto activities in Malta is the Malta Financial Services Authority (MFSA). Under the Virtual Financial Assets Act (VFAA), any entity providing VFA services-such as exchange, custody, or advisory-must obtain a VFA Service Provider license. There are four classes of licenses (Class 1 to 4), with Class 4 being the most comprehensive (allowing all services including dealing on own account).

In 2026, Malta is fully compliant with MiCA, meaning that VFA licenses are aligned with EU CASP requirements. The MFSA also oversees the Innovative Technology Arrangements and Services (ITAS) framework for DLT-based services. For founders, the key takeaway is that a VFA license is mandatory for banking, and the application process is rigorous, taking 6-12 months.

Capital Requirement

Under MiCA, which is fully in force in 2026, capital requirements for crypto asset service providers (CASPs) in Malta are tiered by service class:

  • EUR 50,000 for certain services (e.g., advisory, custody of client crypto assets)
  • EUR 125,000 for exchange services (fiat-crypto and crypto-crypto)
  • EUR 150,000 for dealing on own account or underwriting

These minimums must be maintained as own funds, and additional capital may be required based on operational risk. Malta's VFA law also requires a minimum of EUR 125,000 for Class 3 and 4 licenses. For comparison, Panama has no minimum capital requirement, making it a more accessible option for startups.

Tax Treatment

Malta offers a favourable corporate tax regime with a standard rate of 35%, but effective rates can be as low as 5% through imputation and refund systems. Crypto transactions are treated as taxable events, and profits from trading or mining are subject to corporate income tax. VAT is generally not applied to crypto-to-crypto trades, but fiat conversions may attract VAT.

For founders, Malta's tax treaties with over 70 countries can reduce withholding taxes. However, the tax treatment of DeFi and staking rewards remains under review. Consulting24 recommends consulting a local tax advisor for specific structuring. In contrast, Panama taxes only locally sourced income, and crypto gains are generally tax-free, which is a significant advantage for many founders.

Allowed Activities Under a Malta Crypto License

A VFA license in Malta permits a range of activities, depending on the class:

  • Class 1: Receiving and transmitting orders, portfolio management (non-discretionary)
  • Class 2: Custody and administration of VFAs, exchange services (including fiat-crypto)
  • Class 3: Dealing on own account, underwriting, and placing of VFAs
  • Class 4: All Class 1-3 activities plus operating a multilateral trading facility (MTF)

Additionally, the MFSA allows staking and lending under certain conditions. Founders should note that operating without a license is illegal and can result in fines of up to EUR 5 million. For a simpler regulatory environment, many founders choose Panama, where a single license covers all crypto activities.

Step-by-Step Process to Obtain a Malta Crypto License

  1. Pre-application assessment: Engage a local compliance advisor to review your business model and determine the appropriate license class.
  2. Company incorporation: Register a Malta company with a minimum share capital (typically EUR 1,200 for private companies).
  3. Draft compliance documentation: Prepare AML/CFT policies, risk assessment, business plan, and governance arrangements.
  4. Submit application to MFSA: File the application with all supporting documents and pay the application fee.
  5. Review and due diligence: The MFSA conducts a thorough review, including background checks on directors and shareholders.
  6. On-site inspection (optional): The MFSA may visit your premises to verify operational readiness.
  7. License issuance: Upon approval, you receive the VFA license, typically valid for one year and renewable.

Banking and Payment Solutions in Malta

Once licensed, opening a corporate bank account in Malta remains challenging. Major banks like Bank of Valletta and HSBC Malta require extensive due diligence, including proof of license, source of funds, and a detailed business plan. Minimum deposits often range from EUR 10,000 to EUR 50,000. Some banks may also require a physical presence in Malta.

Alternative payment providers, such as electronic money institutions (EMIs) and payment service providers (PSPs), offer more flexible onboarding. Examples include Revolut Business, Monese, and local EMIs like PagoPay. However, these may have lower transaction limits. The typical timeline is 6 to 12 months from application to issuance. Delays often occur due to incomplete documentation or additional due diligence requests from the MFSA.

Yes, but it is challenging. Licensed VFA providers can approach banks like Bank of Valletta or HSBC, but they require extensive due diligence. Many founders opt for EMIs or consider Panama for easier banking.
