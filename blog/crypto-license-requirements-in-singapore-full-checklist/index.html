What Is the Crypto License in Singapore?

The Monetary Authority of Singapore (MAS) does not issue a standalone 'crypto license'. Instead, firms providing digital payment token (DPT) services must obtain a Major Payment Institution (MPI) licence under the Payment Services Act (PSA). This licence covers activities such as dealing in DPTs, facilitating their exchange, and providing custodial services. The regime is fully operational as of 2026, with strict anti-money laundering (AML) and counter-terrorism financing (CFT) obligations.

Singapore’s approach is principles-based but rigorously enforced. The MAS expects licensees to have strong risk management, consumer protection measures, and a physical presence in the country. The application process is detailed and can take 6 to 12 months or longer, depending on completeness and regulatory scrutiny. The MPI licence is the most common route for crypto businesses, though a Standard Payment Institution (SPI) licence exists for smaller operators with lower transaction volumes. As of 2026, the MAS has not introduced a separate crypto-specific licence; DPT services remain under the PSA umbrella.

Who Needs a Singapore Crypto License?

Any business that carries out any of the following activities in Singapore or provides services to Singapore residents must apply for an MPI licence:

  • Buying or selling digital payment tokens (DPTs) against fiat or other DPTs
  • Operating a digital payment token exchange
  • Providing custodial wallet services for DPTs
  • Facilitating the transfer of DPTs
  • Cross-border money transfer involving DPTs

Smaller operators may qualify for a Standard Payment Institution (SPI) licence if their monthly transaction volume is below SGD 3 million (or SGD 6 million for e-money), but the MPI licence is the typical route for crypto firms. Even businesses based outside Singapore that target Singapore residents may trigger licensing requirements, so it is important to assess your customer base carefully.

License Type and Regulator

The regulator is the Monetary Authority of Singapore (MAS), which oversees all payment services under the PSA. The relevant licence type for crypto businesses is the Major Payment Institution (MPI) licence. There is also a Standard Payment Institution (SPI) licence for smaller players, but most crypto firms will exceed the thresholds and require the MPI.

The MAS does not have a separate 'crypto licence' category; instead, DPT services fall under the 'digital payment token service' class within the PSA. This means compliance with the full suite of MAS regulations, including the Payment Services Regulations and the AML/CFT Notice PSOA-N02. The MAS also issues guidelines on technology risk management and outsourcing, which apply to licensees. As of 2026, the MAS continues to refine its approach, with increased focus on consumer protection and market integrity.

Capital Requirement

The MAS requires an MPI licence applicant to maintain a minimum base capital of SGD 250,000. This capital must be held in Singapore and be readily available to cover operational risks. There is no tiered structure based on service type; the SGD 250,000 applies uniformly to all MPI licensees offering DPT services.

Capital must be maintained at all times and can be in the form of cash or liquid assets acceptable to MAS. Proof of capital is required at the application stage. For comparison, under the EU’s MiCA regime (fully in force in 2026), capital tiers are EUR 50,000 / 125,000 / 150,000 depending on service class. Panama has no minimum capital requirement. The SGD 250,000 threshold is a significant barrier for startups, but it ensures that licensees have financial substance. Some firms may need additional capital if they engage in higher-risk activities, as MAS may impose conditions.

Tax Treatment

Singapore offers a favourable tax regime for crypto businesses. Corporate income tax is a flat 17% on chargeable income. Capital gains are not taxed, which can apply to long-term holdings of digital assets. However, trading income from DPT activities is taxable.

The Inland Revenue Authority of Singapore (IRAS) has issued guidance that DPT transactions are generally treated as a supply of services for GST purposes, but there are exemptions for certain transactions. As of 2026, the GST rate is 9%. Crypto-to-crypto exchanges are not subject to GST if both tokens are digital payment tokens. It is essential to get professional tax advice to structure your operations correctly. Additionally, Singapore has a network of double taxation agreements that can benefit international firms. For offshore operations, Panama offers a 0% tax rate on foreign-sourced income, which may be more attractive for some business models.

Allowed Activities Under the MPI Licence

An MPI licence for DPT services permits the following activities:

  • Dealing in digital payment tokens (buying, selling, exchanging)
  • Operating a DPT exchange platform
  • Providing custodial wallet services
  • Facilitating the transfer of DPTs
  • Cross-border money transfer using DPTs

The licence does not cover activities such as issuing DPTs (which may fall under securities regulation), derivatives, or lending/borrowing of crypto assets. Those may require separate licences under the Securities and Futures Act (SFA) or the Financial Advisers Act (FAA). Always confirm the scope with MAS or a qualified advisor. For example, if your platform offers staking or yield products, you may need additional approvals. The MAS has also indicated that stablecoin issuance may be regulated under a separate framework, so it is important to monitor regulatory updates.

Step-by-Step Application Process

The application process for an MPI licence involves several stages. Below is a typical timeline:

  1. Pre-application preparation (2-3 months): Incorporate a Singapore company, prepare business plan, AML/CFT policies, risk assessment, and governance framework.
  2. Submission (1 month): File the application via MAS’s online portal (Cosmic) with all supporting documents, including financial statements, shareholder details, and compliance manual.
  3. Review and queries (3-6 months): MAS reviews the application, may request additional information, and conducts background checks on directors and shareholders.
  4. In-principle approval (IPA) (1-2 months): If satisfied, MAS issues an IPA with conditions (e.g., capital injection, operational readiness).
  5. Final approval (1-3 months): After meeting all conditions, MAS grants the full MPI licence.

Opening a corporate bank account in Singapore for a crypto business is challenging but possible. Major banks like DBS, OCBC, and UOB have strict compliance policies and often require a track record, a local director, and a detailed business plan. Some digital banks and fintech-friendly institutions are more open.

MAS requires licensees to maintain a settlement account with a bank in Singapore for fiat transactions. Payment service providers may also need to integrate with the local payment infrastructure (e.g., FAST, PayNow). Consulting24 can introduce you to banking partners that understand the crypto sector. In practice, many crypto firms use a combination of a traditional bank account and a digital payment platform for fiat on-ramps. Benefits of a Singapore Crypto License

An MPI licence from MAS carries significant advantages:

  • Global reputation: Singapore is a trusted financial centre, and MAS regulation signals credibility to partners and customers.
  • Tax efficiency: Low corporate tax rate (17%) and no capital gains tax.
  • Access to Asia-Pacific markets: Singapore serves as a gateway to ASEAN and other Asian economies.
  • Stable regulatory environment: Clear rules and consistent enforcement reduce uncertainty.

However, the cost and timeline are high. Singapore’s regulatory reputation can also open doors to partnerships with traditional financial institutions and investors, which is a key benefit for scaling businesses.

Compliance and Ongoing Obligations

Once licensed, MPI holders must comply with ongoing requirements under the PSA and MAS regulations. Key obligations include:

  • Annual AML/CFT audits and reporting
  • Submission of periodic financial and transaction reports
  • Maintaining minimum capital at all times
  • Appointing a compliance officer and a money laundering reporting officer (MLRO)
  • Conducting regular risk assessments and staff training

Non-compliance can result in fines, suspension, or revocation of the licence. This is general guidance, not legal advice. Always engage a local compliance consultant to ensure full adherence. The MAS also conducts on-site inspections and may require licensees to submit to thematic reviews. Ongoing costs for compliance can be substantial, but they are necessary to maintain the licence. Firms should budget for annual audits, which typically cost SGD 20,000 to 50,000 depending on transaction volume.

Common Mistakes When Applying

Many applications are delayed or rejected due to avoidable errors. Common mistakes include:

  • Incomplete or inconsistent documentation (e.g., business model not aligned with AML policies)
  • Insufficient detail on source of funds and beneficial ownership
  • Lack of a local director or physical office (MAS expects substance)
  • Underestimating the timeline and budget for compliance setup
  • Failing to address MAS’s queries promptly and thoroughly

Frequently asked questions

What is the difference between an MPI and an SPI licence in Singapore?

The Major Payment Institution (MPI) licence is for firms exceeding monthly transaction thresholds of SGD 3 million (or SGD 6 million for e-money). The Standard Payment Institution (SPI) licence is for smaller operators. Most crypto businesses require the MPI due to higher volumes.

Can a foreign company apply for a Singapore crypto license?

Yes, but the company must be incorporated in Singapore as a local entity. Foreign directors are allowed, but at least one director must be a Singapore resident (typically a local director). The MAS also expects a physical office in Singapore.

How long does the MAS take to process an MPI licence application?

The typical timeline is 6 to 12 months, but it can be longer if the application is incomplete or raises concerns. The MAS aims to process applications within 6 months for straightforward cases, but complex applications may take over a year.

Is the SGD 250,000 capital requirement refundable?

No, the capital must be maintained as part of the company’s base capital. It is not a deposit; it is a regulatory requirement to ensure financial stability. The funds can be used for operational expenses but must always meet the minimum threshold.

What are the ongoing compliance costs for an MPI licence?

Annual costs include compliance officer salary, AML audits, regulatory filing fees, and external audit. These typically range from SGD 30,000 to 80,000 per year, depending on transaction volume and complexity. Additional costs may arise from MAS inspections.

Does Singapore tax crypto-to-crypto trades?

Crypto-to-crypto trades are generally not subject to GST if both tokens are digital payment tokens as defined by IRAS. However, income from trading is subject to corporate income tax at 17%. Capital gains on long-term holdings are not taxed.

Can I use a Singapore MPI licence to operate in other Asian countries?

The MPI licence only covers activities in Singapore or with Singapore residents. To operate in other countries, you may need additional licences. However, Singapore’s reputation can facilitate partnerships and credibility in the region.

What happens if my application is rejected?

If MAS rejects the application, it will provide reasons. You can address the issues and reapply, but there is no guarantee of approval. Common reasons include insufficient AML controls, lack of substance, or adverse background checks. Consulting with an expert can reduce rejection risk.

Is Panama a good alternative to Singapore for crypto licensing?

Panama offers a flat EUR 6,000 fee, no minimum capital, and a 1-2 month timeline. It is ideal for startups or projects with limited budgets. However, Panama’s regulatory reputation is lower than Singapore’s, which may affect partnerships with traditional financial institutions.
