What is a Cyprus Crypto License?

A Cyprus crypto license, officially a Crypto Asset Service Provider (CASP) registration under CySEC, allows firms to offer services like exchange, custody, and transfer of crypto assets. Since MiCA came into full effect in 2025, Cyprus has aligned its regime with EU standards, requiring firms to hold minimum capital between EUR 50,000 and EUR 150,000 depending on the services offered. The license is regulated by CySEC, which enforces strict AML/KYC rules, governance, and reporting obligations. Many founders choose Cyprus for its EU passporting rights and relatively lower operational costs compared to other EU hubs like Malta or Luxembourg. However, the application process is demanding, and common mistakes can derail even well-prepared applicants. For example, failing to provide a detailed business plan that outlines revenue streams and risk management is a frequent issue. CySEC expects a comprehensive document that covers market analysis, operational structure, and compliance procedures. Another oversight is neglecting to appoint a local AML compliance officer before submission. CySEC requires this role to be filled by a resident with relevant experience, and many applicants scramble to find one after applying, causing delays. Consulting24 advises that you start the recruitment process early, as qualified AML officers in Cyprus are in high demand. The license itself is valid for the entire EU under MiCA passporting, but you must maintain a physical office in Cyprus with at least one director based there. This physical presence requirement is often underestimated, leading to rushed office rentals that do not meet CySEC's standards. We recommend securing a proper office space with meeting rooms and filing systems before applying. Overall, the Cyprus license is a solid choice for firms targeting the EU market, but it requires careful planning and professional support.

Who Needs a Cyprus Crypto License?

Any company offering crypto services to EU residents from Cyprus must hold a Cyprus CASP license. This includes exchanges, wallet providers, custodians, and brokers. Founders targeting the European market often consider Cyprus alongside Lithuania or Estonia. However, Cyprus has a more rigorous application process, which catches many off guard. If you are a startup with limited capital or compliance experience, you might find the Panama route more straightforward, as Panama offers a flat EUR 6,000 company setup with no minimum capital requirement. Consulting24 helps you compare these options to find the best fit. For example, a small exchange with fewer than 10,000 users may struggle with Cyprus's capital demands, whereas Panama allows them to start quickly and scale later. On the other hand, a large exchange serving multiple EU countries benefits from Cyprus's passporting rights, avoiding the need for separate licenses in each member state. A common mistake is assuming that a Cyprus license is required for all crypto activities. If you only offer peer-to-peer trading without custody, you might fall under a different regulatory classification. Consulting24 conducts a thorough assessment of your business model to determine the exact licensing needs. Additionally, founders often overlook the fact that the license applies to the legal entity, not the brand. If you operate multiple brands, each may need its own license or a clear structure under one entity. This nuance can lead to compliance gaps if not addressed early. We also see cases where founders apply for a Cyprus license but plan to operate from another country without a physical presence in Cyprus. CySEC requires substance, including local staff and decision-making, so remote operations without a local office are not permitted. Consulting24 helps you structure your operations to meet these substance requirements while minimizing costs. For those who find Cyprus too demanding, we often recommend Panama as a starting point, with a view to upgrading to an EU license later as the business grows.

License Type and Regulator

The Cyprus crypto license falls under the Investment Services and Activities and Regulated Markets Law of 2017, as amended for MiCA. The regulator is the Cyprus Securities and Exchange Commission (CySEC). There is only one license type for CASPs, but the scope of services determines capital and compliance obligations. CySEC is known for its thorough vetting, including background checks on directors and shareholders. Many applicants underestimate the depth of these checks, leading to delays or rejections. For instance, CySEC reviews the source of wealth for all significant shareholders, and any unexplained funds can trigger a rejection. A common mistake is providing incomplete or vague information about previous business activities. CySEC expects a clear track record, and any gaps in employment or business history must be explained. Additionally, CySEC requires that directors have relevant experience in financial services or crypto. If your team lacks this, you may need to hire additional board members with the right background. The regulator also assesses the fitness of the compliance officer, who must have at least three years of experience in AML compliance. Another pitfall is failing to disclose past regulatory issues in other jurisdictions. CySEC shares information with other EU regulators, so any prior rejections or sanctions will surface. Consulting24 recommends full transparency in your application to avoid accusations of concealment. The license itself does not have an expiration date, but it must be renewed annually with a fee and updated documentation. CySEC also conducts periodic on-site inspections, and a common mistake is not having all records readily available. We advise maintaining a digital and physical archive of all transactions, client communications, and compliance reports. For a comparison of regulatory approaches, see our Cyprus vs Lithuania page.

Capital Requirement

Under MiCA, the minimum capital for a Cyprus CASP is tiered: EUR 50,000 for simple services like custody, EUR 125,000 for exchange services, and EUR 150,000 if you also deal in derivatives. Many applicants mistakenly think they can start with lower capital and later increase it, but CySEC requires proof of capital at application. Failure to demonstrate sufficient liquid funds is a common reason for rejection. Additionally, capital must be maintained at all times. A common mistake is using borrowed funds or assets that are not liquid. CySEC requires capital to be in cash or cash equivalents, not in crypto or other volatile assets. For example, one applicant tried to show capital in Bitcoin, which was rejected because of price volatility. Another mistake is not having the capital in a Cyprus bank account. CySEC may ask for bank statements, and if the funds are held abroad, they may not be considered readily available. Consulting24 advises that you open a Cyprus bank account and deposit the capital before applying. The capital requirement also applies to the parent company if the applicant is a subsidiary. If the parent guarantees the capital, CySEC will still require proof that the funds are unencumbered and available. For startups, raising EUR 150,000 can be challenging, and many turn to Panama where no minimum capital is needed. However, if you plan to scale in Europe, the capital requirement is a necessary hurdle. Consulting24 can help you structure your funding, whether through equity, loans, or retained earnings. We also recommend maintaining a buffer above the minimum to cover operational costs during the application period, which can last up to a year. In contrast, Panama allows you to start with any capital amount, making it ideal for bootstrapped projects. But remember, Panama does not provide EU market access, so you may need to apply for a Cyprus license later. Our team can help you plan a phased approach, starting in Panama and then migrating to Cyprus as your business grows.

Tax Treatment

Cyprus offers a competitive corporate tax rate of 12.5%, one of the lowest in the EU. However, crypto gains may be subject to capital gains tax or income tax depending on the nature of the activity. A common mistake is assuming all crypto income is tax-free. Cyprus has no specific crypto tax law, so general tax principles apply. For example, trading gains are typically treated as income, while long-term holdings may be subject to capital gains tax. Another mistake is ignoring VAT. Crypto services may be exempt from VAT in some cases, but you must register for VAT if your turnover exceeds EUR 15,600. Many founders fail to register, leading to penalties. Additionally, transfer pricing rules apply if you have related parties in other jurisdictions. A common oversight is not documenting intra-group transactions, which can trigger a tax audit. Consulting24 advises that you engage a local tax advisor to structure your operations correctly. For a simpler tax environment, some founders consider Panama, which has no corporate tax on offshore income. However, Panama's tax benefits come with limitations: you cannot have a physical presence in Panama if you want to claim offshore status, and you must derive income from outside Panama. In Cyprus, you pay tax on worldwide income but benefit from EU directives and double tax treaties. Another common mistake is not claiming allowable deductions. Cyprus allows deductions for expenses like rent, salaries, and professional fees, but you must keep proper records. Consulting24 recommends using a local accountant who understands crypto. For a detailed comparison of tax regimes, see our blog on crypto tax planning. Ultimately, the choice between Cyprus and Panama depends on your target market and profit repatriation strategy. We help you model the tax impact of each jurisdiction before committing.

Allowed Activities

A Cyprus CASP license permits the following activities: custody and administration of crypto assets, operation of a crypto exchange (both fiat-to-crypto and crypto-to-crypto), execution of orders on behalf of clients, placement of crypto assets, and transfer services. You cannot offer derivatives without additional licensing. A common mistake is applying for a scope too broad or too narrow. If you overstate your services, CySEC may demand more capital; if understated, you may need to reapply later. Consulting24 helps you define the right scope from the start. For example, if you only plan to offer custody, apply for that alone to keep capital at EUR 50,000. If you later add exchange services, you can apply for a variation, but that incurs additional costs and time. Another mistake is offering unregulated services like DeFi protocols under the same license. CySEC may consider DeFi activities as outside the CASP scope, and you could be operating without authorization. We advise keeping your licensed activities separate from any experimental or unregulated projects. Additionally, the license does not cover NFT marketplaces unless the NFTs are considered crypto assets under MiCA. CySEC has issued guidance on NFTs, but the classification is case-by-case. A common pitfall is assuming all tokens are covered; stablecoins and utility tokens may have different treatment. Consulting24 conducts a legal analysis of your token types to ensure compliance. For a broader view of allowed activities across jurisdictions, visit our jurisdictions page. If your business model involves activities not covered by the Cyprus license, consider Panama, which has no specific restrictions on crypto activities, as long as you comply with AML laws. However, Panama does not provide a regulated status, which may be a disadvantage when dealing with banks or partners.

Step-by-Step Application Process

  1. Pre-application preparation: Incorporate a Cyprus company, draft a detailed business plan, AML/KYC policies, and risk management framework. This phase takes 2-3 months and is often rushed. A common mistake is using generic templates for AML policies. CySEC expects policies tailored to your business, including transaction monitoring procedures and customer due diligence processes. Consulting24 can provide a checklist of required documents.
  2. Submit application to CySEC: Include all documentation, proof of capital, and background checks for directors and shareholders. The application fee is non-refundable, so ensure completeness. A frequent error is missing signatures or notarizations. CySEC requires original documents or certified copies.
  3. CySEC review: Expect 6-9 months of review, with potential requests for additional information. Respond promptly to avoid delays. Many applicants underestimate the time needed to gather supplementary documents, such as source of wealth evidence. Consulting24 recommends preparing a dossier of supporting documents in advance.
  4. On-site inspection: CySEC may visit your premises to verify operations. Ensure your office is fully equipped and staffed. A common mistake is having an empty office or insufficient IT infrastructure. CySEC checks for proper record-keeping systems and cybersecurity measures.
  5. License issuance: Once approved, you must maintain ongoing compliance and reporting. This includes quarterly reports, annual audits, and AML training for staff. Many firms fail to file reports on time, leading to fines. Consulting24 offers ongoing compliance support to keep you on track.

Banking and Payments

Opening a bank account for a Cyprus crypto company is challenging. Many banks are hesitant to work with crypto firms due to perceived risks. A common mistake is assuming you can use a standard business account. You need a bank that understands crypto, such as specialized EMI accounts or crypto-friendly banks in Cyprus. Consulting24 has partnerships with payment providers that facilitate fiat on/off ramps. For example, we work with EMIs that offer multi-currency accounts and SEPA transfers. Another mistake is not having a bank account before applying. CySEC may ask for proof of a corporate bank account during the application process. If you don't have one, it can delay approval. We recommend opening an account with a crypto-friendly bank like Bank of Cyprus or Hellenic Bank, but they require a detailed business plan and proof of license application. Some founders opt for EMI accounts from providers like Revolut or Monese, but these may not be accepted by CySEC for capital deposits. A common pitfall is using a personal account for business transactions, which violates AML rules and can lead to account closure. Consulting24 advises that you separate personal and business finances from day one. Additionally, if you plan to accept crypto payments, you need a payment processor that can convert crypto to fiat and deposit into your bank account. For a more accessible banking environment, some founders choose Panama, where banks are more open to crypto businesses, especially if you have a Panama company. Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Underestimating capital requirements: Ensure you have the full amount in liquid assets before applying. Do not rely on crypto or borrowed funds.
  • Incomplete documentation: CySEC requires detailed business plans, AML policies, and risk assessments. Missing documents cause delays. Use a checklist to track progress.
  • Ignoring background checks: All directors and shareholders must pass fit-and-proper tests. Past regulatory issues can lead to rejection. Disclose everything upfront.
  • Choosing the wrong scope: Apply for only the services you intend to offer to avoid unnecessary capital or future reapplication. You can always amend later.
  • Neglecting banking: Secure a crypto-friendly bank account before applying, as CySEC may ask for proof. Start the process early.
  • Not planning for ongoing costs: Annual compliance costs can exceed EUR 20,000, including legal, audit, and AML officer fees. Budget for at least two years.
  • Overlooking substance requirements: You need a physical office in Cyprus with local staff. A virtual office will not suffice.
  • Failing to appoint a local AML officer: This role must be filled by a Cyprus resident with relevant experience. Start recruiting early.
  • Assuming a fast track: CySEC does not offer expedited processing. Plan for 6-12 months and avoid unrealistic timelines.
  • Not seeking professional help: DIY applications often fail. Consulting24 has a 95% success rate for Cyprus applications. Book a consultation to avoid these mistakes.
