Getting it wrong can mean months of delays or outright rejection.

What Is a Netherlands Crypto License?

The Netherlands crypto license is a regulatory authorization required to provide crypto services under the Dutch implementation of MiCA. It is supervised by De Nederlandsche Bank (DNB) and the AFM. The license covers activities such as crypto exchange, custody, and wallet services. Since MiCA came into full effect in 2025, all crypto asset service providers (CASPs) in the EU must comply with harmonized rules, including capital requirements and AML obligations.

The application process is thorough, involving detailed business plans, AML policies, and fit-and-proper checks on management. Many applicants underestimate the depth of scrutiny, leading to mistakes that can be avoided with proper preparation.

For example, a startup offering a crypto exchange platform must demonstrate not only technical security but also governance structures, risk management frameworks, and clear client asset segregation policies. The Dutch regulators expect a level of detail that goes beyond a simple business plan, often requiring operational manuals and internal control procedures.

Who Needs a Netherlands Crypto License?

Any company offering crypto services to Dutch residents or operating from the Netherlands needs this license. This includes exchanges, wallet providers, custodians, and firms facilitating crypto payments. Even if your company is based elsewhere in the EU, if you target Dutch clients, you may need to register or passport your license.

Founders often assume that a small user base or low transaction volume exempts them, but the rules apply regardless of size. Consulting24 advises clients to check their specific activities against the MiCA service classes to determine applicability.

Common scenarios include a crypto payment processor that handles transactions for Dutch merchants, or a wallet provider that markets to Dutch users. Both would require a license. Even firms that only provide crypto advice or portfolio management fall under the scope if they serve Dutch clients.

License Type and Regulator

The Netherlands issues a single CASP license under MiCA, but the scope of services determines the capital requirement. The regulators are:

  • De Nederlandsche Bank (DNB) - responsible for prudential supervision and AML compliance.
  • Authority for the Financial Markets (AFM) - oversees conduct and market integrity.

Both bodies work together, and applicants must satisfy both. The license is valid across the EU via passporting, but the Dutch regime is known for its strict enforcement.

A common mistake is assuming that approval from one regulator implies approval from the other. In practice, DNB and AFM conduct separate assessments, and deficiencies in either area can delay the application. For instance, a firm with strong AML policies but weak governance structures may pass DNB scrutiny but fail AFM's conduct requirements.

Capital Requirement

Under MiCA, capital requirements are tiered by service class:

  • EUR 50,000 for simple services (e.g., execution of orders on behalf of clients).
  • EUR 125,000 for custody and wallet services.
  • EUR 150,000 for exchanges and trading platforms.

These are minimums; the Dutch regulator may require higher capital based on risk assessment. A common mistake is failing to hold capital in liquid assets or not maintaining it throughout the license period.

For example, a firm offering both custody and exchange services would need at least EUR 150,000, but if the regulator deems the business high-risk, they might ask for EUR 200,000. Capital must be held in cash or cash equivalents and cannot be used for operational expenses. Some applicants mistakenly use capital for marketing or salaries, which leads to non-compliance.

Tax Treatment

The Netherlands does not have a specific crypto tax regime; crypto is treated as assets subject to income and corporate tax. Corporate tax rate is 25.8% (2026). VAT may apply to certain services. Many applicants overlook the tax implications of their business model, leading to unexpected liabilities. Consulting24 recommends engaging a Dutch tax advisor early.

For instance, if your platform charges transaction fees in crypto, you need to account for VAT on those fees. Similarly, profits from trading are subject to corporate tax. Personal tax for directors also needs consideration, especially if they receive crypto as compensation. A common mistake is assuming crypto-to-crypto transactions are tax-free; they are not in the Netherlands.

Allowed Activities Under the License

The Netherlands CASP license covers:

  • Exchange of crypto for fiat or other crypto
  • Custody and administration of crypto assets
  • Operation of a trading platform
  • Transfer services (sending crypto on behalf of clients)
  • Advice and portfolio management

Activities not covered, such as decentralized finance (DeFi) protocols or non-custodial wallets, may fall outside the license scope but still require compliance with AML rules.

A common mistake is offering services that exceed the licensed scope. For example, a firm licensed only for custody might start offering exchange services without amending its license. This can result in fines or revocation. Always ensure your business activities match the license class.

Step-by-Step Application Process

  1. Pre-application preparation - draft business plan, AML policies, risk assessment, and governance documents.
  2. Submit application to DNB and AFM, including all required forms and fees.
  3. Fit-and-proper checks on directors and beneficial owners (criminal records, financial history).
  4. On-site or remote inspection by regulators (may include interviews).
  5. Decision - typically within 6-12 months. Approval can be conditional.

Common mistakes include incomplete documentation, lack of local presence, and underestimating the time needed for fit-and-proper checks.

For example, fit-and-proper checks require detailed CVs, references, and background checks for each director. If a director has a past bankruptcy or regulatory issue, the application may be delayed or rejected. Starting these checks early can save months.

Banking and Payments

Opening a business bank account in the Netherlands for a crypto firm is challenging. Many traditional banks refuse crypto-related businesses. Alternatives include specialized EMI accounts or partnering with crypto-friendly banks in Lithuania or Estonia. Consulting24 helps clients navigate this, often recommending Lithuania as a gateway to EU banking.

A common mistake is assuming that once licensed, banking will be easy. In reality, banks conduct their own due diligence and may still reject crypto firms. Having a backup plan, such as an EMI account, is essential. Some firms also use payment service providers that specialize in crypto, but these may have higher fees.

Benefits of a Netherlands Crypto License

  • Access to the entire EU market via MiCA passporting.
  • High regulatory credibility with investors and partners.
  • Stable legal environment and established financial infrastructure.

However, the cost and complexity mean it is not suitable for every startup. For early-stage projects, a Panama license may be a more practical first step.

Another benefit is the ability to hire top talent, as the Netherlands has a strong tech and finance ecosystem. The license also facilitates partnerships with traditional financial institutions, which is harder to achieve with less regulated jurisdictions.

Compliance and Trust

Ongoing compliance includes quarterly AML reporting, annual audits, and maintaining capital. Many firms fail to budget for these recurring costs. Consulting24 provides compliance support to ensure you stay on track. This is general guidance, not legal advice.

Compliance is not a one-time effort. Regulators conduct periodic reviews and may request changes to procedures. For example, if new AML guidelines are issued, you must update your policies promptly. Non-compliance can lead to fines or license suspension. Having a dedicated compliance officer is highly recommended.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  1. Incomplete documentation - missing business plans or AML policies cause immediate rejection.
  2. Underestimating capital requirements - not holding the minimum capital in liquid form.
  3. Ignoring fit-and-proper checks - directors with past financial crimes will be blocked.
  4. No local presence - the Netherlands requires a physical office and local management.
  5. Poor AML/KYC procedures - regulators expect detailed, automated systems.
  6. Overlooking tax obligations - both corporate and personal tax planning is critical.
  7. Choosing the wrong jurisdiction - sometimes Panama or Lithuania is a better fit.

Each of these mistakes can be avoided with proper planning. For instance, engaging a compliance consultant early can help you prepare a strong AML framework that meets regulator expectations.

Alternatives and Comparisons

Compared to other EU jurisdictions, the Netherlands is one of the most expensive and time-consuming. Lithuania offers a faster process (3-6 months) with lower capital requirements (EUR 125,000 for custody). Estonia has a simpler regime but is not MiCA-compliant as of 2026 (its license is being phased out). Outside the EU, Panama offers a flat fee of EUR 6,000 with no capital requirement, making it ideal for startups. Consulting24 advises clients based on their specific needs.

Another alternative is Dubai, which has a growing crypto hub with a more flexible regulatory environment, but it does not provide EU market access. For firms targeting the EU, the Netherlands remains a strong option despite the higher cost.
