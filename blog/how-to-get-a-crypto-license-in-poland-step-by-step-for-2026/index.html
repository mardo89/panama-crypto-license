What is the Polish crypto license?

The Polish crypto license is a mandatory registration for businesses offering virtual asset services. Under MiCA, which is fully in force in 2026, Poland requires all VASPs to register with the KNF. The license covers activities such as crypto exchange, custody, and wallet services. Unlike some EU member states that had light-touch regimes, Poland now enforces strict AML/CFT obligations, capital requirements, and operational standards. The license is valid across the EU under the MiCA passport, making Poland a strategic entry point for the European market.

Poland's approach is aligned with the EU's harmonised framework, meaning that once registered, your firm can serve clients in other EU countries without additional licensing. However, you must maintain a physical presence in Poland, including a registered office and a local compliance officer. The KNF conducts ongoing supervision, including on-site inspections and reporting requirements.

Who needs a Polish crypto license?

Any business that provides one or more of the following services in Poland or to Polish residents must obtain a crypto license:

  • Exchange of crypto assets for fiat currency or other crypto assets
  • Operation of a crypto trading platform
  • Custody and administration of crypto assets on behalf of clients
  • Transfer services for crypto assets
  • Advice on crypto investments

This includes startups, established exchanges, payment processors, and fintech firms. Even if your company is based outside Poland, if you actively target Polish clients, you must register. The KNF has been proactive in issuing warnings against unregistered entities. For example, in 2025, the regulator fined several foreign exchanges for operating without a license. License type and regulator

The Polish crypto license is a single registration for all VASP activities, but the capital requirement varies by service class. The regulator is the Polish Financial Supervision Authority (Komisja Nadzoru Finansowego, KNF). The KNF is responsible for licensing, supervision, and enforcement. Under MiCA, the license is classified as a CASP (Crypto Asset Service Provider) license. The KNF has published detailed guidelines on the application process, which include a mandatory pre-application meeting. The license does not expire but requires annual renewal and ongoing compliance.

Poland does not have a separate license for different activities; instead, the scope of services determines the capital tier. For example, providing custody alone requires lower capital than operating a trading platform. The KNF also requires that at least one director or manager is a Polish resident with relevant experience. The regulator is known for thorough background checks on beneficial owners and management.

Capital requirement

Under MiCA, the minimum capital requirement for Polish VASPs depends on the services offered:

  • EUR 50,000 for custody and administration of crypto assets
  • EUR 125,000 for exchange services (crypto-to-fiat or crypto-to-crypto)
  • EUR 150,000 for operating a trading platform

These amounts must be held in a Polish bank account or as a bank guarantee. The capital must be fully paid up before application. The KNF may require additional capital if the business model involves higher risk, such as high transaction volumes or complex products. For example, a platform offering margin trading may need to demonstrate higher capital adequacy. The capital requirement is a key differentiator from other jurisdictions like Estonia, which has no minimum capital for certain activities, or Lithuania, which requires EUR 125,000 for exchange services. Poland's tiered approach aligns with MiCA and provides regulatory clarity.

Tax treatment

Poland has a favourable tax regime for crypto businesses. Corporate income tax (CIT) is 19% (9% for small taxpayers with revenue under EUR 2 million). Capital gains from crypto trading for businesses are taxed as regular income. VAT is exempt on crypto exchange transactions, following the EU Court of Justice ruling. However, advisory services may be subject to 23% VAT. Personal income tax (PIT) for crypto gains by individuals is 19% flat, but only if trading is not part of a business activity. Poland also has a withholding tax of 19% on dividends and interest paid to non-residents, subject to double tax treaties. It is essential to structure your operations with local tax advice to optimise your tax position. Consulting24 can coordinate with Polish tax advisors to ensure compliance.

Allowed activities

Under the Polish crypto license, you are permitted to offer the following services:

  • Exchange of crypto assets for fiat currency
  • Exchange of crypto assets for other crypto assets
  • Operation of a crypto trading platform (order book, AMM, etc.)
  • Custody and administration of crypto assets
  • Transfer services for crypto assets
  • Advice on crypto assets and investments

Activities that are not allowed include issuing stablecoins (unless authorised under MiCA), operating as a central securities depository, or providing banking services. The KNF also prohibits mixing client funds with company funds. All client assets must be held in segregated accounts. If you plan to offer derivatives or margin trading, you may need additional authorisation under MiFID II. Poland is a member of the EU, so all activities must comply with AML directives and the Travel Rule for crypto transfers above EUR 1,000.

Step-by-step application process

  1. Pre-application consultation: Schedule a meeting with the KNF to discuss your business model and requirements. This is mandatory and helps identify potential issues early.
  2. Company incorporation: Set up a Polish limited liability company (sp. z o.o.) with a registered office in Poland. Minimum share capital is PLN 5,000 (approx. EUR 1,100).
  3. Prepare documentation: Draft a business plan, AML/CFT policies, risk assessment, internal procedures, and governance framework. Include details on IT security and data protection.
  4. Capital deposit: Open a corporate bank account in Poland and deposit the required minimum capital. Obtain a bank confirmation letter.
  5. Submit application: File the application with the KNF, including all supporting documents and the application fee. The KNF will acknowledge receipt within 30 days.
  6. Review and interview: The KNF may request additional information or schedule an interview with management. Respond promptly to avoid delays.
  7. Approval and registration: Once approved, your company is added to the KNF's register of VASPs. You can then commence operations.

Banking and payment solutions

Opening a corporate bank account in Poland is a critical step. Many traditional banks are cautious with crypto businesses, but some are more open. Recommended banks include mBank, ING Bank Śląski, and Alior Bank, which have dedicated fintech teams. You will need to provide your license (or proof of application), AML policies, and source of funds. Some banks may require a minimum deposit or turnover threshold. Alternatively, you can use payment institutions like PayDo or Modulr for fiat on/off ramps. For crypto-to-crypto operations, you may not need a full bank account. Benefits of the Polish crypto license

  • EU passporting: Once registered, you can serve clients across the EU without additional licensing, saving time and cost.
  • Regulatory clarity: MiCA provides a clear legal framework, reducing uncertainty for businesses and investors.
  • Competitive tax rates: Corporate tax as low as 9% for small businesses, and VAT exemption on crypto exchanges.
  • Skilled workforce: Poland has a large pool of IT and finance professionals, making it easier to hire local talent.
  • Growing crypto ecosystem: Poland has a vibrant crypto community and increasing adoption, providing a ready market.

Compared to other EU jurisdictions, Poland offers a balanced mix of regulatory rigour and business-friendly conditions. For example, Malta has higher capital requirements and a more complex application process, while Cyprus has a slower timeline. Poland strikes a good balance for most mid-sized crypto firms.

Compliance and ongoing obligations

After obtaining the license, you must maintain ongoing compliance with Polish and EU regulations. Key obligations include:

  • AML/CFT: Implement and update AML policies, conduct customer due diligence (CDD), and report suspicious transactions to the General Inspector of Financial Information (GIIF).
  • Travel Rule: For transfers above EUR 1,000, collect and transmit sender and beneficiary information.
  • Reporting: Submit annual financial statements and compliance reports to the KNF. Notify the regulator of any material changes in management or ownership.
  • Audit: Some activities may require an external audit of AML controls and financial statements.
  • Data protection: Comply with GDPR requirements for handling personal data of clients.

Non-compliance can result in fines, suspension, or revocation of the license. The KNF has the power to conduct on-site inspections. Consulting24 recommends engaging a local compliance officer and using automated monitoring tools. Common mistakes to avoid

  1. Incomplete documentation: Many applications are rejected due to missing or poorly prepared AML policies. Use templates but tailor them to your business.
  2. Underestimating capital: Ensure you have the exact capital amount in a Polish bank account before applying. The KNF will verify this.
  3. Ignoring the pre-application meeting: This is mandatory and skipping it can delay your application. Use it to clarify requirements.
  4. Choosing the wrong legal structure: A Polish sp. z o.o. is the most common, but other forms may have different tax implications. Get professional advice.
  5. Not planning for banking: Start the bank account opening process early, as it can take weeks. Some banks require a license before opening an account, creating a chicken-and-egg problem.

Frequently asked questions

How long does it take to get a crypto license in Poland?

The typical timeline is 3 to 6 months from application submission to approval. The KNF has up to 3 months to decide, but delays can occur if additional information is needed. Pre-application preparation takes 2-4 weeks.

What is the minimum capital for a Polish crypto license?

Under MiCA, the minimum capital is EUR 50,000 for custody services, EUR 125,000 for exchange services, and EUR 150,000 for operating a trading platform. The capital must be deposited in a Polish bank account.

Can I use the Polish license to serve clients in other EU countries?

Yes. Under MiCA, once registered in Poland, you can passport your services to any EU member state without additional licensing. You must notify the KNF of your intent to operate cross-border.

What are the tax rates for crypto businesses in Poland?

Corporate income tax is 19% (9% for small taxpayers with revenue under EUR 2 million). VAT is exempt on crypto exchange transactions. Personal capital gains tax for individuals is 19% flat.

Is a physical office required in Poland?

Yes. You must have a registered office in Poland, which can be a virtual office for compliance purposes. The KNF may conduct on-site inspections, so a physical address is necessary.

What documents are needed for the application?

Key documents include a business plan, AML/CFT policies, risk assessment, internal procedures, proof of capital, company incorporation documents, and background checks on directors and beneficial owners.

Can I apply for a Polish crypto license if my company is already registered in another EU country?

If you already hold a CASP license in another EU member state, you can passport into Poland without a full application. However, you must notify the KNF and comply with local AML requirements.

What happens if I operate without a license in Poland?

Operating without a license is illegal and can result in fines, criminal penalties, and forced cessation of activities. The KNF actively monitors and takes enforcement actions against unregistered entities.

Does Poland allow crypto derivatives or margin trading?

Crypto derivatives and margin trading may require additional authorisation under MiFID II. The KNF treats these as financial instruments, so a separate investment firm license may be needed.
