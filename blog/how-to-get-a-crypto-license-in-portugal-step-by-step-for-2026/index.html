What Is a Crypto License in Portugal?

A crypto license in Portugal is an authorization granted by the Bank of Portugal (Banco de Portugal) and the Portuguese Securities Market Commission (CMVM) to provide crypto-asset services legally. Under MiCA, which is fully in force in 2026, all crypto-asset service providers (CASPs) must be registered and comply with EU-wide standards. The license covers activities such as exchange, custody, wallet services, and more.

Portugal's regulatory framework is aligned with MiCA, ensuring a high level of investor protection and market integrity. The license is essential for any business offering crypto services to Portuguese residents or operating from Portugal. The regime replaces the previous voluntary registration system, making licensing mandatory for all CASPs. This means that even small operations must obtain authorization or face penalties. The license is passportable across the EU, allowing you to serve clients in any member state without additional registration.

Portugal also requires that the licensed entity have a physical presence in the country, including a registered office and local management. This ensures that the regulator can effectively supervise activities. The application process involves a thorough assessment of the business model, governance, and compliance systems.

Who Needs a Crypto License in Portugal?

Any entity that provides any of the following services in or from Portugal must obtain a crypto license:

  • Exchange of crypto-assets for fiat currency
  • Exchange of crypto-assets for other crypto-assets
  • Custody and administration of crypto-assets on behalf of clients
  • Operation of a trading platform for crypto-assets
  • Transfer services for crypto-assets
  • Advising on crypto-asset investments
  • Portfolio management of crypto-assets

If you are a startup, an established exchange, or a fintech company planning to offer these services, you need a license. Even if you are based outside Portugal but target Portuguese clients, you may need to register locally. The definition of "targeting" includes marketing in Portuguese language or offering services specifically to Portuguese residents. For example, a crypto exchange that advertises in Portuguese media or accepts Portuguese bank transfers would likely need a license. Non-compliance can result in fines up to EUR 5 million or 10% of annual turnover, as per EU regulations.

Additionally, if you are a decentralized finance (DeFi) protocol, you may still fall under the scope if you have a certain level of control over the service. It is advisable to seek legal advice to determine if your specific business model requires a license.

License Type & Regulator

The primary regulator for crypto-asset services in Portugal is the Bank of Portugal (Banco de Portugal). However, the CMVM also plays a role if the services involve securities or investment advice. Under MiCA, the license is a single EU-wide passportable authorization, known as a CASP license. There are different classes of licenses depending on the services offered, with varying capital requirements (see below). The license is valid across all EU member states once obtained in Portugal.

The Bank of Portugal is responsible for registering CASPs and overseeing their compliance with AML/CFT rules. The CMVM handles cases where crypto-assets qualify as financial instruments, such as security tokens. For most standard crypto services (e.g., exchange, custody), the Bank of Portugal is the main point of contact. The application process involves submitting detailed documentation, including a business plan, AML policies, and proof of capital. The regulator conducts background checks on key personnel and may require interviews.

Once licensed, the entity must comply with ongoing reporting obligations, including annual audits and transaction monitoring. The regulator may conduct on-site inspections to verify compliance.

Capital Requirement

Under MiCA, the minimum capital requirements for CASPs in Portugal are tiered based on the services provided:

  • EUR 50,000 for simple services like custody or transfer
  • EUR 125,000 for exchange services (fiat-to-crypto or crypto-to-crypto)
  • EUR 150,000 for operating a trading platform

These amounts must be maintained as own funds and cannot be used for operational expenses. Portugal does not impose additional local capital requirements beyond MiCA. The capital must be held in liquid assets, such as cash or government bonds, and cannot be borrowed. For example, if you plan to offer both custody and exchange services, you need at least EUR 125,000. If you also operate a trading platform, the requirement increases to EUR 150,000. The regulator may require higher capital if your business volume is large or if you have a higher risk profile.

It is important to note that the capital requirement is a minimum; you may need additional funds for operational costs, such as salaries, rent, and technology infrastructure. A common mistake is underestimating the total funding needed, leading to delays in licensing.

Tax Treatment

Portugal has a favorable tax regime for crypto investors and businesses. As of 2026, capital gains from crypto-assets held for more than one year are tax-exempt for individuals. For businesses, corporate income tax applies at a standard rate of 21%, with possible reductions for small and medium enterprises. Value-added tax (VAT) is generally not applicable to crypto transactions under EU VAT rules, but advisory services may be subject to VAT.

For crypto businesses, the main tax considerations include corporate income tax on profits, stamp duty on certain transactions, and potential withholding taxes on dividends. Portugal also offers a participation exemption regime for dividends and capital gains from subsidiaries, which can be beneficial for holding structures. However, the tax treatment of specific crypto activities, such as staking or lending, may vary. It is advisable to consult a local tax specialist to structure your operations optimally. For example, a company that provides crypto exchange services may be able to deduct expenses such as technology costs and salaries, reducing its taxable income.

Portugal's tax authorities have issued guidelines on the treatment of crypto-assets, but the landscape is evolving. Engaging a tax advisor with crypto expertise is essential to ensure compliance and optimize your tax position.

Allowed Activities

A Portuguese crypto license permits the following activities:

  • Exchange of crypto-assets for fiat or other crypto-assets
  • Custody and wallet services
  • Operation of a crypto trading platform
  • Transfer services (sending/receiving crypto on behalf of clients)
  • Advisory services on crypto-assets
  • Portfolio management of crypto-assets

Activities not covered include issuing stablecoins or security tokens, which require separate licenses under other EU regulations. For example, issuing an asset-referenced token (ART) or an e-money token (EMT) is regulated under MiCA but requires a different authorization from the European Banking Authority (EBA) or national competent authority. Additionally, activities that involve derivatives or securities are regulated by the CMVM under MiFID II.

The license allows you to offer services to both retail and institutional clients, subject to suitability and appropriateness assessments. You must also comply with marketing rules, including clear risk warnings. If you plan to offer services that are not explicitly listed, you should seek regulatory guidance to determine if they fall within the scope of the license.

Step-by-Step Process

Obtaining a crypto license in Portugal involves several stages. Here is a step-by-step outline:

  1. Pre-application assessment: Evaluate your business model and determine the license class needed. This includes identifying the services you will offer and the corresponding capital requirement.
  2. Company incorporation: Register a legal entity in Portugal (e.g., Lda or SA) with a registered office. You will need a local address and a Portuguese bank account.
  3. AML/CFT program: Develop a comprehensive anti-money laundering policy, risk assessment, and procedures. This must include customer due diligence (CDD), transaction monitoring, and suspicious activity reporting.
  4. Appoint compliance officers: Designate a board member or senior manager responsible for compliance. The compliance officer must be based in Portugal and have relevant experience.
  5. Prepare documentation: Gather business plan, financial projections, organizational structure, IT security policies, and business continuity plan. All documents must be in Portuguese or translated by a certified translator.
  6. Submit application: File with the Bank of Portugal and/or CMVM, including all required documents and fees. The application is submitted online through the regulator's portal.
  7. Regulatory review: The regulator assesses the application, may request additional information, and conducts background checks on key personnel. This stage typically takes 3-6 months.
  8. On-site inspection (if required): Regulators may visit your premises to verify operational readiness, including IT systems and physical security.
  9. License issuance: Upon approval, you receive the license and can commence operations. You must then comply with ongoing reporting obligations.

Banking & Payments

Opening a bank account for a crypto business in Portugal can be challenging due to the perceived risk. However, several Portuguese banks are open to crypto companies with proper licensing. You will need to provide your license, AML policy, and business plan. Alternatively, you can use specialized payment service providers or e-money institutions that support crypto businesses. Examples include Clear Junction, Railsbank, and Modulr, which offer dedicated accounts for crypto firms.

Portugal has a modern payment infrastructure, and once your account is open, you can accept SEPA transfers, credit cards, and other methods. For international payments, consider multi-currency accounts offered by banks like Banco BPI or Santander Totta. Some banks may require a minimum deposit or charge higher fees for crypto-related accounts. It is advisable to approach multiple banks to compare terms.

Additionally, you may need to integrate with payment gateways that support crypto-to-fiat conversion for your clients. Services like MoonPay or Simplex can be integrated into your platform. Common Mistakes to Avoid

Founders often make these errors when applying for a Portuguese crypto license:

  • Incomplete documentation: Missing business plans or inadequate AML policies can delay the process. Ensure all documents are complete and translated into Portuguese.
  • Underestimating capital requirements: Ensure you have the required own funds before applying. The capital must be fully paid up and held in a Portuguese bank account.
  • Ignoring local presence: You need a registered office and local management. Virtual offices may not be accepted; you need a physical address where records are kept.
  • Choosing the wrong license class: Applying for a license that does not match your services can lead to rejection. For example, if you offer exchange services but apply for a custody-only license, your application will be rejected.
  • Not engaging experienced advisors: The regulatory landscape is complex; professional guidance saves time and reduces risk. Many applicants underestimate the level of detail required in the AML program.
  • Failing to plan for ongoing compliance: After licensing, you must submit regular reports and undergo audits. Failure to do so can result in license revocation.

Avoiding these mistakes can significantly increase your chances of a smooth application process.

Frequently asked questions

What is the minimum capital requirement for a crypto license in Portugal?

Under MiCA, the minimum capital depends on services: EUR 50,000 for custody/transfer, EUR 125,000 for exchange, and EUR 150,000 for trading platforms. These must be maintained as own funds.

How long does it take to get a crypto license in Portugal?

The process typically takes 6 to 12 months from application submission to license issuance. Delays can occur if documentation is incomplete or if the regulator requests additional information.

Can I use a Portuguese crypto license to operate in other EU countries?

Yes, under MiCA, a CASP license obtained in Portugal is passportable across all EU member states, allowing you to serve clients throughout the EU without additional registration.

What are the tax benefits for crypto businesses in Portugal?

Portugal offers no capital gains tax for individuals holding crypto for over one year. Corporate tax is 21%, with possible reductions for SMEs. VAT is generally not applicable to crypto transactions.

Do I need a physical office in Portugal to get a license?

Yes, you need a registered office and local management. The regulator requires a physical presence where records are kept and where inspections can be conducted.

What activities are allowed under a Portuguese crypto license?

Allowed activities include exchange, custody, wallet services, trading platform operation, transfer services, advisory, and portfolio management. Issuing stablecoins or security tokens requires separate authorization.

Is it difficult to open a bank account for a crypto business in Portugal?

It can be challenging, but several Portuguese banks accept licensed crypto firms. You will need to provide your license, AML policy, and business plan. Specialized payment providers are also an option.

What happens if I operate without a license in Portugal?

Operating without a license can result in fines up to EUR 5 million or 10% of annual turnover, as well as criminal liability for directors. The regulator may also issue cease-and-desist orders.

How does Portugal compare to Panama for crypto licensing?

Panama offers a flat fee of EUR 6,000, no capital requirement, and a 2-4 month timeline, with 0% tax on foreign-source income. Portugal is more expensive and time-consuming but provides EU passporting and a favorable tax regime.
