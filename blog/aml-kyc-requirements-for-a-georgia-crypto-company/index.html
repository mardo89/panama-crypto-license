What Are AML/KYC Requirements for a Georgia Crypto Company?

AML (Anti-Money Laundering) and KYC (Know Your Customer) requirements are regulatory obligations that crypto companies must implement to prevent financial crime. In Georgia, these requirements are mandated by the National Bank of Georgia (NBG) under the Law on Combating Money Laundering and Terrorism Financing. The NBG oversees virtual asset service providers (VASPs) and enforces compliance with FATF recommendations.

Key components include customer due diligence (CDD), ongoing monitoring, record-keeping, and reporting suspicious transactions. Georgia's framework aligns with international standards, making it a credible jurisdiction for crypto operations. Companies must appoint a compliance officer, conduct risk assessments, and maintain AML policies. For example, a crypto exchange must verify customer identities for transactions above EUR 1,000 and monitor for unusual patterns. Record-keeping requires storing transaction data for at least five years. The NBG also mandates regular reporting of suspicious activities to the Financial Monitoring Service of Georgia.

Who Needs AML/KYC Compliance in Georgia?

Any legal entity providing virtual asset services in Georgia must comply with AML/KYC requirements. This includes:

  • Crypto exchanges (fiat-to-crypto and crypto-to-crypto)
  • Wallet providers (custodial and non-custodial)
  • Payment processors using crypto
  • Brokerage services
  • ATMs and kiosks
  • OTC trading desks

Even if your company is registered abroad but operates in Georgia, you may fall under NBG jurisdiction. Consulting24 advises founders to assess their activities and register early to avoid penalties. For a full list of regulated activities, see our jurisdictions page. Startups often overlook that even non-custodial wallet providers may need to register if they facilitate transfers. The NBG has clarified that any service that holds or controls virtual assets on behalf of customers is a VASP.

License Type & Regulator

The regulator for crypto licensing in Georgia is the National Bank of Georgia (NBG). The license type is a Virtual Asset Service Provider (VASP) license. Unlike some EU member states under MiCA, Georgia maintains its own regulatory framework, which is not yet fully harmonized with MiCA. The NBG requires VASPs to register and comply with AML/CFT obligations.

There is no specific minimum capital requirement for a VASP license in Georgia, but applicants must demonstrate sufficient financial resources to operate. The NBG also requires a physical presence in Georgia, including a registered office and a local director. Consulting24 can coordinate with local legal partners to facilitate the application. The NBG conducts background checks on all beneficial owners and directors, including criminal record checks and financial history reviews. Capital Requirement

Georgia does not impose a statutory minimum capital requirement for VASPs. However, the NBG expects companies to have sufficient capital to cover operational expenses and compliance costs for at least six months. Typically, founders allocate EUR 10,000 - 20,000 as initial capital, though this is not a fixed rule. In contrast, under MiCA, EU member states require EUR 50,000 - 150,000 depending on services. Consulting24 recommends preparing a detailed business plan and budget to demonstrate financial stability. The NBG may ask for proof of funds, such as bank statements or investor commitments. Tax Treatment for Crypto Companies in Georgia

Georgia offers a favorable tax regime for crypto companies. The corporate income tax rate is 15%, but there is no VAT on crypto transactions. Additionally, Georgia has a territorial tax system, meaning only income sourced in Georgia is taxed. Crypto-to-crypto trades are generally tax-exempt, while fiat conversions may be subject to tax. Founders should consult a local tax advisor for specific obligations. Consulting24 can introduce you to trusted tax partners in Georgia. For example, if your company earns revenue from trading fees on a global platform, only the portion attributable to Georgian users may be taxed. Panama offers even more favorable tax treatment with 0% tax on foreign income, making it a strong alternative for international operations.

Allowed Activities Under a Georgia Crypto License

The Georgia VASP license permits the following activities:

  • Exchange between virtual assets and fiat currencies
  • Exchange between one or more forms of virtual assets
  • Transfer of virtual assets
  • Custodial wallet services
  • Participation in and provision of financial services related to an issuer’s offer or sale of a virtual asset
  • OTC trading

Activities not covered include issuing securities tokens or operating as a central bank digital currency (CBDC) node. For a broader scope, consider Estonia or Lithuania, which allow more activities under MiCA. The NBG may also restrict certain high-risk activities, such as anonymity-enhanced cryptocurrencies. Step-by-Step Process to Obtain a Georgia Crypto License

  1. Company Registration: Incorporate a legal entity in Georgia (LLC recommended). This takes 1-2 weeks and costs around EUR 500.
  2. AML/KYC Policy: Draft policies covering CDD, risk assessment, transaction monitoring, and reporting. Engage a compliance expert to ensure alignment with NBG requirements.
  3. Compliance Officer: Appoint a local compliance officer with relevant experience. The officer must be a resident of Georgia and have at least three years of AML experience.
  4. Register with NBG: Submit the application with required documents (business plan, AML policy, shareholder details, proof of office address). The NBG charges a fee of approximately EUR 500.
  5. Due Diligence: NBG conducts background checks on directors and beneficial owners. This can take 2-4 weeks.
  6. License Issuance: Upon approval, you receive a VASP license. The total process averages 2-3 months.

Common Mistakes When Applying for a Georgia Crypto License

  • Incomplete AML Policy: Not covering all FATF recommendations, such as travel rule compliance or sanctions screening.
  • Lack of Local Presence: No physical office or local director. The NBG requires a registered address and a resident director.
  • Underestimating Timelines: Expecting approval in weeks. The average is 2-3 months, and delays are common if documents are incomplete.
  • Ignoring Tax Obligations: Not registering for corporate tax or VAT. Even if no tax is due, registration is mandatory.
  • Poor Record-Keeping: Failing to maintain transaction records for five years as required by law.

Frequently asked questions

What is the minimum capital requirement for a Georgia crypto license?

Georgia does not have a statutory minimum capital requirement for VASPs. However, the NBG expects sufficient funds to cover operational and compliance costs for at least six months. Typically, founders allocate EUR 10,000 - 20,000.

How long does it take to get a Georgia crypto license?

The process typically takes 2-3 months from company registration to license issuance. Delays can occur if the application is incomplete or if background checks take longer.

What are the ongoing AML/KYC obligations for a Georgia crypto company?

Ongoing obligations include customer due diligence, transaction monitoring, suspicious activity reporting, annual AML audits, and employee training. Companies must also submit regular reports to the NBG.

Can a foreigner own a Georgia crypto company?

Yes, foreigners can own 100% of a Georgia company. However, at least one director must be a resident of Georgia. Consulting24 can assist in finding a local director.

No, Georgia is not an EU member, so its license does not provide EU passporting. For EU-wide operations, consider a MiCA-compliant license in Lithuania or Estonia.

Do I need a physical office in Georgia?

Yes, the NBG requires a registered office in Georgia. This can be a virtual office or a co-working space, but it must be a physical address.
