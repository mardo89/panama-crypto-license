Applying for a crypto license in Georgia can be a strategic move for many crypto founders, but the process is fraught with pitfalls. The National Bank of Georgia (NBG) regulates virtual asset service providers (VASPs) under the Law on Combating Money Laundering and Terrorist Financing. Many applicants stumble on documentation, capital, or compliance requirements. This guide highlights the most frequent errors and how to avoid them.

1. What Is a Georgia Crypto License?

The Georgia crypto license is a regulatory authorization issued by the National Bank of Georgia (NBG) for companies offering virtual asset services. It is required for exchanges, wallet providers, and other VASPs operating in or from Georgia. The license is part of Georgia's anti-money laundering (AML) framework, aligned with FATF recommendations.

As of 2026, Georgia is not an EU member, so MiCA does not apply. However, the NBG has adopted similar standards, including minimum capital requirements and mandatory internal controls. The license allows firms to operate legally and access banking services within Georgia's growing fintech ecosystem. Unlike some jurisdictions, Georgia does not have a tiered license system; all VASPs apply under the same regime, though the NBG may impose specific conditions based on the scope of services. For example, a simple exchange may have lighter conditions than a custodial wallet provider handling large volumes.

The license is valid indefinitely, subject to ongoing compliance. Renewal is automatic as long as annual reports and fees are submitted on time. However, the NBG conducts periodic reviews, and any material change in ownership, management, or business model requires prior approval. Failure to notify the NBG of such changes is a common mistake that can lead to penalties.

2. Who Needs a Georgia Crypto License?

Any legal entity providing virtual asset services in Georgia must obtain a license. This includes:

  • Cryptocurrency exchanges (fiat-to-crypto and crypto-to-crypto)
  • Wallet providers (custodial and non-custodial)
  • Brokers and dealers in virtual assets
  • Payment processors using crypto
  • ATMs and kiosks for virtual assets

Foreign companies targeting Georgian clients or establishing a physical presence also need the license. A typical example is a foreign exchange that allows Georgian residents to trade crypto. Even if the company is registered abroad, if it actively solicits Georgian clients, it must register with the NBG. Another common scenario is a startup offering crypto payment processing for Georgian merchants; this too requires a license.

Entities that only develop blockchain software or provide non-custodial wallets (where the provider never holds private keys) may be exempt, but the NBG assesses each case individually. Mistakenly assuming an exemption is a frequent error.

3. License Type & Regulator

The sole regulator for crypto licensing in Georgia is the National Bank of Georgia (NBG). The license is a single type covering all VASP activities, but the NBG may impose conditions based on the service scope. There is no tiered system like in some EU jurisdictions.

Applicants must register as a legal entity in Georgia (typically a LLC) and pass a fit-and-proper test for beneficial owners and management. The NBG also requires a physical office in Georgia and at least one local director. This is a common stumbling block for foreign founders who try to operate remotely without local substance. The fit-and-proper test examines criminal records, financial soundness, and professional experience. Any prior regulatory infractions or bankruptcies can disqualify an applicant. Additionally, the NBG requires that at least one director be a resident of Georgia, which often forces founders to hire local personnel or engage a nominee director service.

The NBG also mandates that the company's AML compliance officer be based in Georgia and have relevant certification. Many applicants overlook this requirement and later face delays.

5. Capital Requirement

As of 2026, the NBG requires a minimum capital of GEL 500,000 (approx. EUR 160,000) for VASP licenses. This must be fully paid up before application and maintained throughout the license period. The capital can be in cash or liquid assets, held in a Georgian bank account.

A common mistake is underestimating this requirement or not having the funds readily available. Some applicants try to use crypto assets, but the NBG only accepts fiat currency. Ensure your capital is in a Georgian bank account before applying. The capital must be unencumbered and cannot be borrowed from related parties without proper documentation. The NBG may ask for proof of source of funds, so be prepared to show bank statements or sale of assets.

Another mistake is using the capital for operating expenses after license issuance. The NBG requires that the minimum capital be maintained at all times. If the company's net equity falls below this threshold, the NBG may suspend the license. Therefore, founders should plan for additional working capital beyond the minimum.

6. Tax Treatment for Crypto in Georgia

Georgia offers a favorable tax regime for crypto businesses. Corporate income tax is 15%, but there is a preferential rate for IT companies (including crypto) of 5% on distributed profits. VAT is 18% but crypto transactions are generally exempt. Personal income tax on crypto gains is 20% for residents.

However, many applicants neglect to register for taxes properly or fail to maintain transparent accounting. The NBG and Revenue Service share information, so any discrepancy can lead to license denial. Always engage a local tax advisor. For example, if your company earns revenue from trading fees, you must issue electronic invoices and file monthly VAT returns (even if exempt). Failure to do so can result in fines.

Transfer pricing rules also apply if you have related-party transactions, such as paying a foreign parent company for software licenses. The Revenue Service may scrutinize such payments to ensure they are at arm's length. A common mistake is not documenting transfer pricing policies, leading to tax adjustments.

7. Allowed Activities Under the License

The Georgia crypto license permits the following activities:

  • Exchange of virtual assets for fiat currency
  • Exchange of one virtual asset for another
  • Transfer of virtual assets (on behalf of clients)
  • Custodial wallet services
  • Participation in ICOs/STOs (with additional approval)

Not allowed: operating a crypto derivatives exchange, lending/borrowing platforms, or any activity that constitutes a security under Georgian law. If your business model includes such services, you may need additional licensing or consider other jurisdictions like Panama or Lithuania. For instance, offering staking services where the platform pools client assets and earns rewards may be considered a collective investment scheme, requiring a separate license from the Georgian Securities Commission.

Another restricted activity is operating a crypto ATM network without a separate license. The NBG treats each ATM as a separate place of business, so you must register each location. Many entrepreneurs overlook this and face enforcement actions.

8. Step-by-Step Application Process

  1. Company incorporation in Georgia (LLC) with a local registered address and director.
  2. Prepare documentation: business plan, AML/KYC policy, risk assessment, internal controls, and financial projections.
  3. Open a bank account in Georgia and deposit the minimum capital.
  4. Submit application to the NBG with all supporting documents and pay the fee.
  5. NBG review (2-4 months) may include interviews and requests for additional information.
  6. License issuance upon approval, followed by ongoing reporting obligations.

Common mistakes at each stage: incomplete business plan, lack of local substance, and poor AML documentation. Consulting24 provides end-to-end support for the entire process. For example, the business plan must include detailed financial projections for at least three years, with realistic assumptions about transaction volumes and revenue. Many applicants submit overly optimistic projections that the NBG rejects as implausible.

The AML policy must be tailored to Georgia's specific legal framework, not copied from another jurisdiction. It should cover customer due diligence, transaction monitoring, and suspicious activity reporting. The NBG provides guidelines, but many applicants fail to incorporate them fully.

9. Banking & Payments for Crypto Firms

Opening a bank account in Georgia for a crypto company is challenging but possible. Major banks like TBC Bank and Bank of Georgia accept VASPs with proper licensing. However, many applicants fail to prepare a strong compliance file for the bank's due diligence.

Tips: Have your license or application proof ready, provide a detailed business model, and be transparent about your source of funds. Some firms use payment processors or EMI accounts as alternatives. Consulting24 can introduce you to banking partners who understand the crypto space. For example, TBC Bank requires a minimum deposit of GEL 50,000 and a detailed AML questionnaire. They may also request a personal guarantee from the director.

Another option is to use a Georgian EMI like Payze or CityPay, which offer IBAN accounts and are more crypto-friendly. However, these may have lower transaction limits. A common mistake is not starting the banking process early; account opening can take 4-8 weeks, and without a bank account, you cannot deposit the minimum capital, stalling the license application.

10. Benefits of a Georgia Crypto License

Georgia offers several advantages for crypto businesses:

  • Low corporate tax (5% for IT companies)
  • Fast-growing fintech ecosystem
  • Strategic location at the crossroads of Europe and Asia
  • No VAT on crypto transactions
  • Relatively low setup costs compared to EU jurisdictions

However, the license is not a passport to the EU. If you need EU-wide access, consider Lithuania or Estonia. For a simpler, tax-neutral alternative, Panama offers a flat fee of EUR 6,000 with no capital requirement. Georgia also offers a relatively fast timeline compared to some EU countries, where MiCA implementation has led to longer processing times. The NBG is known for being pragmatic and accessible, often providing pre-application guidance.

Another benefit is the ability to obtain a residency permit for company directors, which can be attractive for founders seeking to relocate. Georgia has a straightforward residency-by-investment program for business owners.

11. Compliance & Trust: Ongoing Obligations

After obtaining the license, VASPs must comply with ongoing AML/CFT obligations, including:

  • Transaction monitoring and reporting suspicious activities to the Financial Monitoring Service of Georgia.
  • Annual audits by an independent auditor.
  • Regular submission of financial statements and compliance reports to the NBG.
  • Maintaining minimum capital at all times.

Failure to comply can result in fines, license suspension, or revocation. Many licensees underestimate the administrative burden. Consulting24 offers ongoing compliance support to ensure you remain in good standing. This is general guidance, not legal advice. For example, the NBG requires quarterly reporting on transaction volumes, number of clients, and any suspicious transactions. These reports must be submitted within 15 days of the quarter end. Missing a deadline can trigger a warning or fine.

Additionally, the NBG conducts on-site inspections every two years. During these inspections, they review client files, transaction records, and AML procedures. A common finding is incomplete customer due diligence (CDD) files, such as missing proof of address or source of wealth. To avoid this, maintain a strong CDD process and retain records for at least five years after the business relationship ends.

Frequently asked questions

What is the minimum capital requirement for a Georgia crypto license?

The NBG requires a minimum capital of GEL 500,000 (approximately EUR 160,000) as of 2026. This must be held in a Georgian bank account in fiat currency and maintained throughout the license period.

How long does it take to get a Georgia crypto license?

The typical timeline is 3-6 months from application submission. Delays often occur due to incomplete documentation or failure to meet capital requirements. Consulting24 can help expedite the process.

Can a foreign company apply for a Georgia crypto license?

Yes, but the company must first register a legal entity in Georgia (usually an LLC) with a local registered address and at least one local director. The NBG requires local substance.

What activities are allowed under a Georgia crypto license?

Allowed activities include exchange of virtual assets for fiat or other crypto, custodial wallet services, and transfers on behalf of clients. Derivatives, lending, and securities activities are not permitted.

Is Georgia part of the EU? Does MiCA apply?

No, Georgia is not an EU member. MiCA does not apply. However, the NBG has aligned its regulations with FATF recommendations and EU standards, including minimum capital and AML requirements.

What are the tax benefits for crypto companies in Georgia?

Corporate income tax is 15%, but IT companies (including crypto) can benefit from a 5% rate on distributed profits. VAT is 18% but crypto transactions are generally exempt. Personal income tax on crypto gains is 20% for residents.

Do I need a physical office in Georgia for the license?

Yes, the NBG requires a physical office in Georgia. A virtual office is not sufficient. You also need at least one local director and a compliance officer based in Georgia.

Can I use crypto assets to meet the capital requirement?

No, the NBG only accepts fiat currency (GEL or major foreign currencies) for the minimum capital. Crypto assets are not considered acceptable.

What happens if I fail to maintain ongoing compliance?

Non-compliance can result in fines, license suspension, or revocation. The NBG conducts periodic reviews and on-site inspections. It is essential to have a compliance officer and maintain proper records.

How does Georgia compare to Panama for crypto licensing?

Panama offers a flat fee of EUR 6,000 with no capital requirement and a faster timeline (2-3 months). Georgia requires EUR 160,000 capital and takes 3-6 months. Panama is tax-neutral, while Georgia offers low tax (5% for IT companies).
