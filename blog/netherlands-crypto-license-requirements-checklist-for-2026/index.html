What Is the Netherlands Crypto License?

The Netherlands crypto license is a registration with DNB under the Anti-Money Laundering and Anti-Terrorist Financing Act (Wwft). Since MiCA came into full force in 2026, Dutch CASPs must also comply with EU-wide capital requirements and passporting rules. The license allows you to operate legally within the Netherlands and, with proper notification, across other EU member states.

Key features include:

  • Registration with DNB as a crypto service provider
  • Ongoing AML/CFT compliance
  • Minimum capital based on service type (EUR 50,000 to EUR 150,000)
  • Fit and proper requirements for management
  • Annual audits and reporting

Unlike some EU countries that previously operated lighter regimes, the Netherlands now enforces the full MiCA tiered capital approach. This means a simple exchange must hold at least EUR 50,000, while a full-service platform needs EUR 150,000. DNB also requires a detailed business plan showing how you will maintain these funds.

Who Needs a Netherlands Crypto License?

Any business that provides crypto-related services to Dutch residents must register with DNB. This includes:

  • Crypto exchanges (fiat-to-crypto and crypto-to-crypto)
  • Custodial wallet providers
  • Brokers and dealers in crypto assets
  • ATMs and kiosks
  • Payment processors using crypto

If your company is based outside the Netherlands but targets Dutch clients, you still need registration unless you passport from another EU member state under MiCA. Non-compliance can lead to fines, forced closure, or criminal liability. For example, in 2023 DNB fined a foreign exchange EUR 2 million for operating without registration. Even if you only offer services via a website accessible in the Netherlands, you may fall under DNB's scope.

License Type and Regulator

The Netherlands does not issue a separate "crypto license" but requires registration under the Wwft. The regulator is De Nederlandsche Bank (DNB), which supervises all financial institutions including crypto service providers. DNB is known for its rigorous approach, especially regarding AML controls and management integrity.

Under MiCA, the registration is harmonised with the EU CASP regime. This means that once registered, you can apply for a passport to offer services in other EU countries without additional local registrations. However, DNB remains the lead supervisor for your Dutch entity. DNB also coordinates with the Dutch Authority for the Financial Markets (AFM) on certain matters, but DNB is the primary contact for crypto licensing.

Capital Requirement

Under MiCA, the Netherlands applies tiered capital requirements based on the type of crypto services offered:

  • EUR 50,000 for simple services (e.g., non-custodial exchange)
  • EUR 125,000 for custodial wallet services
  • EUR 150,000 for full-service platforms combining exchange, custody, and brokerage

These amounts must be held as own funds and maintained at all times. DNB may require additional capital if your business volume exceeds certain thresholds. For comparison, Panama has no minimum capital requirement, making it a cost-effective alternative for startups. The capital must be in liquid form, such as cash or government bonds, and cannot be crypto assets themselves.

Tax Treatment

The Netherlands taxes crypto assets as property (box 3) for individuals, meaning a flat tax on deemed investment returns. For businesses, crypto income is subject to corporate income tax at the standard rate of 25.8% (2026). VAT is not charged on crypto transactions themselves, but advisory and management fees may be subject to 21% VAT.

Key tax considerations:

  • Capital gains on crypto are generally not taxed separately for corporations; they are part of taxable profit.
  • Mining and staking rewards are taxable as income at the time of receipt.
  • Cross-border transactions may trigger withholding tax issues; professional advice is essential.

The Dutch tax authority has issued specific guidance on crypto valuation, requiring fair market value at each reporting date. This can create volatility in tax liabilities, so proper accounting is critical.

Allowed Activities Under the License

With a Netherlands crypto license, you can conduct the following activities:

  • Exchange between crypto and fiat currency
  • Exchange between one or more crypto assets
  • Custody and administration of crypto assets on behalf of clients
  • Operation of a crypto trading platform
  • Transfer services for crypto assets
  • Advising on crypto investments (if ancillary to other services)

Activities not covered include issuing new tokens (security token offerings) or operating a crypto fund, which require separate regulatory approvals. Also, if you plan to offer derivatives or used products, you may need a MiFID license instead. DNB expects you to clearly define your service scope in the application.

Step-by-Step Application Process

Applying for a Netherlands crypto license involves several stages. Follow this checklist:

  1. Pre-application preparation: Draft your business plan, AML policy, risk assessment, and governance documents. Ensure management meets fit and proper criteria.
  2. Submit application to DNB: Complete the online form and upload all required documents. Pay the application fee.
  3. DNB review: DNB will assess your application for completeness and may request additional information. This stage takes 4-8 weeks.
  4. On-site or remote inspection: DNB may conduct a compliance audit of your systems and procedures.
  5. Decision: If approved, you receive a registration number and are added to the public register. If rejected, you can appeal.
  6. Post-registration: Implement ongoing reporting, annual audits, and AML training. Notify DNB of any material changes.

Banking and Payment Services

Opening a business bank account in the Netherlands for a crypto firm is challenging. Many traditional banks are reluctant to serve crypto businesses due to perceived AML risks. Options include:

  • Specialised fintech banks (e.g., ClearBank, Railsbank)
  • Dutch banks with dedicated crypto teams (e.g., ABN AMRO has a selective policy)
  • EMI accounts from Lithuania or Estonia (with passporting)

We recommend securing a banking relationship before applying for the license, as DNB will ask for proof of a bank account. For payment processing, consider integrating with licensed payment institutions. Some crypto-friendly banks require a minimum deposit of EUR 50,000 and charge monthly fees of EUR 200-500. Without a bank account, DNB may reject your application.

Benefits of the Netherlands Crypto License

Despite the high costs, the Netherlands license offers several advantages:

  • EU passporting: Once registered, you can serve clients across all EU/EEA states without additional licenses.
  • Strong reputation: DNB supervision signals trustworthiness to partners and customers.
  • Stable regulatory environment: The Netherlands is a founding EU member with clear, consistent rules.
  • Access to talent: Amsterdam is a major fintech hub with skilled professionals.
  • No maximum capital requirement: Unlike some jurisdictions, you can scale without additional capital demands.

Additionally, the Netherlands has a strong legal framework for digital assets, and DNB actively engages with the industry through consultations. This can be valuable for long-term planning.

Compliance and Ongoing Obligations

Once licensed, you must maintain strict compliance. Key obligations include:

  • Continuous AML/CFT monitoring and reporting of suspicious transactions
  • Annual audit by a registered auditor
  • Submission of quarterly financial reports to DNB
  • Maintenance of minimum capital at all times
  • Fit and proper reassessment of management every two years
  • Data protection under GDPR

Failure to comply can result in fines up to EUR 5 million or 10% of annual turnover. This is general guidance, not legal advice. Always consult a qualified lawyer. DNB also conducts thematic reviews, for example on crypto custody practices, and may require remedial actions.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Many applicants face delays or rejection due to these pitfalls:

  • Incomplete AML documentation: DNB requires a detailed, business-specific AML policy, not a generic template.
  • Unfit management: Any criminal record or past regulatory issues will likely disqualify directors.
  • Underestimating capital: Ensure you have the required funds in liquid assets before applying.
  • Ignoring tax planning: The Dutch tax system is complex; plan for corporate and personal tax liabilities.
  • No banking relationship: DNB expects you to have a bank account ready; start early.

Another common mistake is failing to translate documents into Dutch. DNB accepts English but may request Dutch translations for certain forms. Also, some applicants underestimate the time needed for management background checks; these can take weeks.

Alternatives to the Netherlands License

If the Netherlands route seems too costly or time-consuming, consider these alternatives:

  • Panama: No minimum capital, flat fee of EUR 6,000, and no corporate tax on foreign-source income. Ideal for startups.
  • Lithuania: Lower costs (approx. EUR 10,000 total) and faster processing (2-4 months). Full MiCA compliance.
  • Estonia: Established e-residency program, transparent licensing, and EU passporting. Costs around EUR 15,000.
  • Czech Republic: Another EU option with lower capital requirements (EUR 50,000) and a straightforward process. See our Czech Republic crypto license page.

Frequently asked questions

What is the minimum capital for a Netherlands crypto license?

Under MiCA, the minimum capital ranges from EUR 50,000 to EUR 150,000 depending on the services offered. For simple exchange services, EUR 50,000 is required. For custodial wallets, EUR 125,000. For full-service platforms, EUR 150,000. These amounts must be held as own funds and maintained at all times.

How long does it take to get a Netherlands crypto license?

Typically 3 to 6 months from application to registration. DNB's initial review takes 4-8 weeks, followed by a deeper assessment. Delays often occur if documents are incomplete or if management background checks take longer. Consulting24 can help streamline the process.

Can I passport my Netherlands license to other EU countries?

Yes, under MiCA, once registered with DNB, you can notify DNB and passport your license to offer services in other EU/EEA member states without additional local registrations. This is a key benefit of the Netherlands license.

What are the ongoing compliance costs?

Annual compliance costs typically range from EUR 10,000 to EUR 25,000, including audit fees, AML monitoring, reporting, and legal updates. These costs can vary based on transaction volume and complexity.

Is a Netherlands crypto license worth it for a startup?

It depends on your budget and target market. The Netherlands license is expensive (EUR 27,000-54,000 upfront) but offers strong reputation and EU passporting. For startups with limited funds, Panama (EUR 6,000 flat) or Lithuania may be more suitable.

What happens if I operate without a license?

Operating without registration is illegal and can result in fines up to EUR 5 million or 10% of annual turnover, forced closure, and criminal liability for directors. DNB actively monitors the market and has imposed significant penalties.

Does the Netherlands require a physical office?

Yes, you need a registered office in the Netherlands. DNB expects a physical presence with local management. Virtual offices are generally not accepted. You must also have a local bank account.

Can I use a Netherlands license for DeFi services?

DeFi services are not explicitly covered under the current framework. If you operate a decentralised exchange or protocol, you may still need registration if you have control over customer funds or act as an intermediary. DNB assesses on a case-by-case basis.

What documents are needed for the application?

Key documents include a business plan, AML policy, risk assessment, governance structure, management CVs and background checks, financial projections, and proof of capital. All documents must be in Dutch or English.

How does the Netherlands license compare to Panama?

Panama has no minimum capital, a flat fee of EUR 6,000, and no corporate tax on foreign-source income. It is faster and cheaper but does not offer EU passporting. The Netherlands provides EU access but at higher cost and complexity.
