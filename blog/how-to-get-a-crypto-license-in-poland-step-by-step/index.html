What Is a Crypto License in Poland?

A crypto license in Poland is an authorization granted by the KNF to operate as a CASP under MiCA. It allows companies to provide services such as exchanging crypto for fiat, operating trading platforms, and safeguarding client assets. Poland transposed MiCA into national law in 2025, replacing the previous VASP registration system. The license is valid across all EU member states under the passporting regime.

Key features include a minimum capital requirement of EUR 125,000 for most services (with lower tiers for simple services), mandatory AML/CFT programs, and regular reporting to the KNF. The license is essential for any crypto business targeting Polish or EU customers. Unlike the old VASP register, which had no minimum capital and lighter oversight, the MiCA regime imposes rigorous standards on governance, IT security, and client asset protection. For example, a crypto exchange must segregate client funds and hold them in a qualified custodian bank. The license also requires a physical presence in Poland, including a registered office and local management, which adds to operational costs but enhances credibility.

Who Needs a Crypto License in Poland?

Any entity providing crypto-asset services to Polish residents or operating from Poland must obtain a license. This includes:

  • Crypto exchanges (crypto-to-fiat and crypto-to-crypto)
  • Wallet providers (custodial and non-custodial with certain features)
  • Custodians of client crypto assets
  • Brokerage and advisory services
  • Operators of trading platforms

Foreign companies serving Polish customers without a license risk penalties, including fines up to EUR 5 million or 5% of annual turnover, and potential criminal liability for directors. If you are already licensed in another EU state, you may passport into Poland under MiCA's single license regime, but you must notify the KNF and comply with local AML rules. License Type and Regulator

The regulator is the Polish Financial Supervision Authority (Komisja Nadzoru Finansowego, KNF). Under MiCA, there is a single CASP license with different service classes. The license is categorised by the services offered, each with its own capital requirement:

Service ClassMinimum Capital (EUR)
Simple services (e.g., advisory, non-custodial wallet)50,000
Exchange, custody, trading platform125,000
Full service (including proprietary trading)150,000

Poland does not issue separate licenses for different activities; instead, the license scope is defined by the services listed in the application. The KNF also assesses the fitness and propriety of directors and beneficial owners, requiring detailed personal questionnaires and background checks. This is similar to the approach in Lithuania, where the central bank oversees licensing. Capital Requirement

Under MiCA, Poland applies capital tiers based on the services provided. The minimum capital is EUR 50,000 for simple services, EUR 125,000 for core services like exchange and custody, and EUR 150,000 for full-service operators. This capital must be held as equity and cannot be borrowed. It must be maintained at all times during the license period. The KNF may also require additional capital based on the volume of transactions or risk profile, as per MiCA's internal capital adequacy requirements.

For comparison, Lithuania requires EUR 125,000 for most licenses, while Estonia has no minimum capital but higher operational costs due to mandatory AML officer outsourcing. Tax Treatment

Poland taxes crypto income as capital gains at a flat rate of 19% for individuals and corporations. VAT is not applicable on crypto-to-fiat exchanges, but advisory services may be subject to 23% VAT. Crypto-to-crypto trades are generally exempt. Poland has a favourable tax regime for long-term holdings (held over one year) with a reduced rate of 15% for individuals. However, mining income is treated as business income and taxed at the standard CIT rate of 19%.

Companies must register for corporate income tax (CIT) and value-added tax (VAT) if applicable. Transfer pricing rules apply for related-party transactions, requiring documentation for cross-border payments to affiliates. Poland also has a withholding tax on dividends (19%) but reduced rates under EU directives. Consulting24 recommends a tax review as part of the licensing process. For example, a Polish subsidiary of a US parent should consider the US-Poland tax treaty to avoid double taxation.

Allowed Activities

A Polish CASP license permits the following activities under MiCA:

  • Exchange of crypto assets for fiat currency
  • Exchange of crypto assets for other crypto assets
  • Operation of a trading platform
  • Custody and administration of crypto assets on behalf of clients
  • Placement of crypto assets (underwriting)
  • Receiving and transmitting orders for crypto assets
  • Providing advice on crypto assets
  • Portfolio management of crypto assets

Activities not covered include issuing crypto assets (which requires a separate prospectus under MiCA) and operating a crypto ATM (which is regulated separately under Polish law). The license does not cover traditional financial services like lending or deposit-taking, which require a banking license. For example, a company offering crypto-backed loans would need both a CASP license and a credit institution license, which is complex. Step-by-Step Process

  1. Company setup: Incorporate a Polish limited liability company (sp. z o.o.) with a registered office in Poland. Minimum share capital is PLN 5,000 (approx. EUR 1,100). You will need at least one director, but two are recommended, with at least one Polish resident for regulatory communication.
  2. Prepare documentation: Draft a business plan, AML/CFT policies, risk assessment, and internal controls. The AML program must include transaction monitoring, customer due diligence (CDD), and suspicious transaction reporting (STR) procedures. Appoint a board of at least two directors (one Polish resident recommended).
  3. Capital deposit: Deposit the required minimum capital in a Polish bank account and obtain a certificate from the bank. The account must be in the company's name and the capital must be unencumbered.
  4. Submit application: File the application with the KNF, including all documents, proof of capital, and personal questionnaires for directors and beneficial owners. The KNF charges an application fee of approximately EUR 5,000.
  5. Review and interview: The KNF reviews the application within 3 months, may request additional information, and may interview management. Interviews typically focus on the business model, AML knowledge, and operational readiness.
  6. Approval: If approved, the license is issued. You must begin operations within 6 months or the license may lapse. Post-approval, you must submit quarterly compliance reports and undergo annual audits.

Banking and Payments

Opening a bank account for a crypto business in Poland can be challenging. Most major banks (PKO BP, Santander, mBank) accept crypto CASPs but require a license and thorough due diligence. Some banks may refuse service due to risk appetite, especially for smaller startups. Alternative options include payment institutions like PPRO or specialised crypto-friendly banks in Lithuania or Estonia, such as PayrNet or LHV Bank.

Poland has a well-developed payment infrastructure including instant transfers (Express Elixir) and BLIK for mobile payments. For fiat on-ramps, you can integrate with local payment processors like PayU or Tpay. However, banks often require a minimum deposit balance (e.g., EUR 50,000) and charge monthly fees of EUR 200-500. Benefits of a Polish Crypto License

  • EU passporting: Serve all 27 EU member states from one license, avoiding multiple applications.
  • Clear regulation: MiCA provides legal certainty and a single rulebook, reducing compliance complexity.
  • Tax advantages: Competitive 19% capital gains tax, lower for long-term holdings (15%). No VAT on crypto-to-fiat exchanges.
  • Skilled workforce: Access to a large pool of IT and finance professionals at lower salaries than Western Europe.
  • Cost-effective: Lower setup and operational costs compared to Germany or France, with total license costs around EUR 50,000-80,000.

Poland is also a member of the European Banking Authority (EBA) and follows EU AML directives, enhancing credibility with partners and investors. For instance, a Polish-licensed exchange can easily partner with EU banks and payment processors, unlike a Panama-licensed entity which may face higher due diligence requirements.

Compliance and Trust

Ongoing compliance is mandatory. License holders must:

  • Conduct ongoing AML/CFT monitoring and report suspicious transactions to the General Inspector of Financial Information (GIIF).
  • Submit annual financial statements and compliance reports to the KNF within 6 months of year-end.
  • Maintain minimum capital at all times, with quarterly attestation.
  • Appoint an AML officer and a compliance officer, who must be Polish residents or EU nationals with relevant experience.
  • Undergo periodic audits by external auditors, including IT security audits under MiCA's operational resilience requirements.

Non-compliance can result in fines up to EUR 5 million or 5% of annual turnover, suspension of operations, or revocation of the license. The KNF conducts on-site inspections and may request ad-hoc reports. Consulting24 provides post-license compliance support, including AML training and reporting templates. Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Incomplete documentation: Missing business plans or AML policies cause delays. The KNF expects a detailed risk assessment and transaction monitoring procedures.
  • Underestimating capital: Failing to maintain the required capital can lead to rejection or license revocation. Some applicants apply for too many services, increasing the capital requirement unnecessarily.
  • Ignoring beneficial ownership: All ultimate owners must be disclosed and pass fit-and-proper tests. Hidden ownership structures are a red flag for the KNF.
  • Choosing the wrong service scope: Applying for too many services increases capital requirements unnecessarily. For example, a simple exchange does not need a custody license.
  • Banking issues: Not securing a bank account before applying can stall operations. Some applicants assume they can open an account post-license, but banks may refuse without an active license.

Frequently asked questions

What is the minimum capital for a crypto license in Poland?

Under MiCA, the minimum capital is EUR 50,000 for simple services (e.g., advisory), EUR 125,000 for core services (exchange, custody, trading platform), and EUR 150,000 for full-service operators. This capital must be held as equity and maintained at all times.

How long does it take to get a Polish crypto license?

The typical timeline is 6 to 12 months from preparation to approval. The KNF has 3 months to review the application, but delays can occur if additional information is needed. Consulting24 helps streamline the process to minimise delays.

Can I passport a Polish crypto license to other EU countries?

Yes, under MiCA, a Polish CASP license allows you to serve clients in all 27 EU member states without additional licensing. You must notify the KNF of your intent to passport and comply with local AML requirements.

What are the tax rates for crypto in Poland?

Crypto income is taxed as capital gains at 19% for individuals and corporations. Long-term holdings (over one year) qualify for a reduced rate of 15% for individuals. VAT is not applicable on crypto-to-fiat exchanges.

Do I need a physical office in Poland?

Yes, you must have a registered office in Poland, which can be a virtual office for licensing purposes. However, the KNF expects a physical presence for management and compliance functions. Consulting24 can help arrange a registered address.

Can I apply for a Polish crypto license if my company is outside the EU?

Yes, non-EU companies can establish a Polish subsidiary and apply for a license. The subsidiary must have local directors and meet all capital and compliance requirements. Consulting24 advises on the optimal corporate structure.

What are the ongoing compliance requirements?

License holders must conduct AML/CFT monitoring, submit annual reports to the KNF, maintain minimum capital, appoint an AML officer, and undergo external audits. Non-compliance can result in fines or license revocation.

How does Poland compare to Lithuania for crypto licensing?

Lithuania has a faster timeline (3-6 months) and lower costs (EUR 30,000-50,000), but requires EUR 125,000 capital for most services. Poland offers lower capital for simple services and a larger domestic market. Both allow EU passporting.

Is Panama a good alternative to Poland?

Panama is faster (2-4 weeks) and cheaper (EUR 6,000 flat) with no minimum capital, but it does not offer EU passporting. It is ideal for non-EU markets or as a holding company. Consulting24 delivers Panama licenses directly.

Can I operate a crypto ATM under a Polish CASP license?

No, crypto ATMs are regulated separately under Polish law and require a different authorization. The CASP license covers exchange, custody, and advisory services, but not ATM operations.
