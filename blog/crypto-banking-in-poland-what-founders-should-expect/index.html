What Is Crypto Banking in Poland?

Crypto banking in Poland refers to the provision of banking services specifically tailored to crypto asset businesses. This includes opening corporate accounts, processing fiat and crypto transactions, and offering payment services compliant with Polish and EU regulations. Under MiCA, Poland's Financial Supervision Authority (KNF) oversees crypto asset service providers, ensuring they meet capital, AML, and operational requirements.

For founders, crypto banking in Poland means access to a regulated environment with a growing ecosystem of banks and payment institutions that understand the crypto sector. Unlike some jurisdictions where banks are hesitant to serve crypto firms, Poland has a more open approach, especially for licensed CASPs. This makes it a viable option for companies looking to establish a European presence.

Who Needs a Crypto Banking Licence in Poland?

Any business that offers crypto-related services in Poland or to Polish residents needs a licence. This includes exchanges, wallet providers, custody services, and payment processors dealing with crypto. The licence is mandatory under MiCA for CASPs operating in the EU.

Founders launching a crypto exchange, a crypto payment gateway, or a DeFi platform that interacts with fiat will require this licence. Even if your company is based elsewhere, if you target Polish customers, you must comply. Consulting24 helps you assess whether you need a Polish licence or if a licence from another EU member state (passported) suffices. For most, a Polish licence offers local credibility and direct access to the Polish banking system.

Licence Type and Regulator

In Poland, the relevant licence is the Crypto Asset Service Provider (CASP) authorisation, regulated by the Polish Financial Supervision Authority (Komisja Nadzoru Finansowego, KNF). The KNF is responsible for licensing, supervision, and enforcement under MiCA. The CASP licence covers multiple service classes, each with specific capital requirements.

The licence types include: (1) exchange between crypto and fiat, (2) exchange between crypto assets, (3) custody and administration of crypto assets, (4) operation of a trading platform, (5) transfer services, and (6) advisory services. Each class may require a different capital tier. The KNF is known for thorough due diligence, so preparation is key.

Capital Requirement

Under MiCA, capital requirements for CASPs in Poland are tiered by service class. For custody and exchange services, the minimum capital is EUR 125,000. For trading platforms, it is EUR 150,000. For simpler services like advisory, EUR 50,000 may suffice. These figures are subject to the final classification of services.

The capital must be held in liquid assets, such as cash or government bonds, and maintained throughout the licence period. Poland does not impose additional local capital buffers beyond MiCA, but the KNF may require higher amounts if the business model poses higher risks. Consulting24 recommends preparing at least EUR 150,000 to cover the most common service classes.

Tax Treatment of Crypto in Poland

Poland has a relatively clear tax regime for crypto assets. Corporate income tax (CIT) is 19% (9% for small taxpayers). Capital gains from crypto trading are taxed at 19% CIT, while mining and staking income is treated as business revenue. VAT is generally not applicable on crypto-to-fiat exchanges, but advisory services may be subject to 23% VAT.

For individuals, crypto gains are taxed at 19% flat rate (PIT). Losses can be offset against gains within the same year. Poland has no wealth tax on crypto holdings. However, tax reporting requires detailed records of each transaction. Consulting24 recommends working with a Polish tax advisor to ensure compliance with local tax laws, especially for cross-border operations.

Allowed Activities Under the Polish Crypto Licence

The Polish CASP licence permits a wide range of activities, as defined by MiCA. These include:

  • Exchange of crypto assets for fiat currency
  • Exchange of crypto assets for other crypto assets
  • Custody and administration of crypto assets on behalf of clients
  • Operation of a crypto trading platform
  • Transfer of crypto assets (as a service)
  • Provision of crypto advisory services

Additionally, the licence allows for staking and lending services if they fall under custody or exchange definitions. Poland does not restrict DeFi activities per se, but any service that involves holding client assets requires authorisation. The KNF has indicated that stablecoin issuance may require a separate e-money licence.

Step-by-Step Process to Obtain a Polish Crypto Licence

Obtaining a Polish crypto licence involves several stages. Consulting24 coordinates with local partners to streamline the process:

  1. Pre-assessment: Review your business model, service classes, and eligibility. Determine if a Polish licence is the best fit or if another jurisdiction like Lithuania or Estonia suits you better.
  2. Company Setup: Register a Polish company (usually a limited liability company, Sp. z o.o.) with a minimum share capital of PLN 5,000 (approx. EUR 1,100). You need a registered office in Poland.
  3. Documentation: Prepare AML/KYC policies, business plan, risk assessment, capital proof, and organisational structure. These must comply with MiCA and KNF guidelines.
  4. Application Submission: Submit the application to the KNF along with the fee. The KNF has up to 3 months to review, but this can extend if additional information is requested.
  5. Operational Setup: Once approved, you must implement the policies, hire a compliance officer, and connect with Polish banks for fiat services.
  6. Ongoing Compliance: Submit periodic reports to the KNF, conduct AML audits, and maintain capital requirements.

Banking and Payment Integration

One of the main challenges for crypto firms in Poland is finding a bank willing to open accounts. However, several Polish banks now accept licensed CASPs, including major players like PKO BP, Santander Bank Polska, and mBank. These banks require proof of licence, AML policies, and often a minimum deposit.

For payment processing, Polish fintechs like Autopay and PayU offer crypto-friendly solutions. Additionally, EMI licences from Lithuania or other EU countries can be used for SEPA payments. Compliance and Trust Requirements

Compliance is critical for maintaining a Polish crypto licence. The KNF expects strong AML/CFT measures, including customer due diligence, transaction monitoring, and suspicious activity reporting. You must appoint a compliance officer residing in Poland or the EU.

Additionally, you need to implement security protocols for safeguarding client assets, such as cold storage and insurance. The KNF conducts periodic inspections and can impose fines or revoke licences for non-compliance. Common Mistakes When Applying for a Polish Crypto Licence

Founders often make these errors:

  • Incomplete Documentation: Missing business plans or risk assessments delay the process.
  • Underestimating Capital: Applying with insufficient capital for the intended service classes.
  • Ignoring Tax Obligations: Failing to register for CIT or VAT properly.
  • Choosing the Wrong Service Classes: Over- or under-scoping the licence, leading to regulatory friction.
  • Neglecting Banking Relationships: Not securing a bank account before applying, which can hinder operational readiness.
