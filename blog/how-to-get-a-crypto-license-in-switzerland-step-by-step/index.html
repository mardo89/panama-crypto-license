What Is a Crypto License in Switzerland?

A crypto license in Switzerland is not a single permit but a regulatory authorisation required to operate certain activities involving cryptocurrencies, such as exchange, custody, trading, or issuance. The Swiss Financial Market Supervisory Authority (FINMA) oversees these activities under the Anti-Money Laundering Act (AMLA) and the Financial Market Infrastructure Act (FMIA). Depending on the scope, businesses may need a fintech license, a banking license, or simply be registered as a financial intermediary with a self-regulatory organisation (SRO).

Switzerland’s “Crypto Valley” in Zug has attracted hundreds of blockchain companies due to its clear legal environment and supportive government. The regulatory framework distinguishes between payment tokens, utility tokens, and asset tokens, each with different requirements. For most crypto exchanges and custodians, the key authorisation is either a fintech license (for limited deposits up to CHF 100 million) or a full banking license (for larger operations).

Importantly, Switzerland is not part of the European Economic Area, so MiCA does not apply. Instead, FINMA applies its own principles, which are often seen as pragmatic and innovation-friendly. However, the application process is thorough and can take 6-12 months.

Who Needs a Crypto License in Switzerland?

Any legal entity that professionally provides services related to cryptocurrencies in or from Switzerland must obtain the appropriate authorisation. This includes:

  • Cryptocurrency exchanges (centralised and peer-to-peer platforms)
  • Custodial wallet providers
  • Brokers and dealers in crypto assets
  • Issuers of new tokens (ICOs/STOs) depending on the token classification
  • Operators of crypto ATMs
  • Asset managers dealing with crypto assets

If you are a foreign company offering services to Swiss residents, you may also trigger licensing requirements. The threshold for “professional” activity is low: even a single client in Switzerland can be enough if the service is offered repeatedly. Consult with a specialist to determine your exact obligations.

The classification depends on the token type and the business model. It is essential to engage with FINMA early via a “no-action letter” or guidance process.

Capital Requirement

Switzerland imposes minimum capital requirements based on the license type. For a fintech license, the minimum capital is CHF 3 million (approximately EUR 3.1 million). For a full banking license, it is CHF 10 million (approx. EUR 10.3 million). These amounts must be fully paid-up in cash or liquid assets and maintained at all times.

For businesses that only need SRO registration under AMLA, there is no statutory minimum capital. However, FINMA expects the company to have adequate financial resources relative to its activities. In practice, this means having sufficient funds to cover operational costs for at least 6-12 months and to implement proper AML controls.

Compared to other jurisdictions, Switzerland’s capital requirements are high. For example, Lithuania requires EUR 125,000 for a CASP license under MiCA, and Panama has no minimum capital at all. If capital is a constraint, Panama or Lithuania may be more suitable. See our Lithuania crypto license page for details.

Tax Treatment of Crypto in Switzerland

Switzerland has a favourable tax regime for crypto businesses. Income from trading and exchange fees is subject to corporate income tax at the federal level (effective rate approx. 11-21% depending on canton). Capital gains from the sale of crypto assets held as part of the business are treated as ordinary income. There is no capital gains tax for private investors, but businesses are taxed on all profits.

Value-added tax (VAT) applies to most services at the standard rate of 8.1%. However, certain crypto-related services may be exempt if they qualify as financial services. The Swiss Federal Tax Administration (FTA) provides guidelines on the tax treatment of tokens, distinguishing between payment, utility, and asset tokens. Utility tokens are generally not subject to VAT when used for their intended purpose.

Switzerland does not have a withholding tax on dividends or interest paid to foreign shareholders, making it attractive for international founders. However, stamp duties may apply to the issuance of tokens if they qualify as securities. Always consult a Swiss tax advisor for your specific situation.

Allowed Activities Under a Swiss Crypto License

The specific activities permitted depend on the license type. Under a fintech license, you can:

  • Accept deposits from the public up to CHF 100 million
  • Provide custody of crypto assets
  • Operate a trading platform for crypto assets
  • Execute orders in crypto assets
  • Offer payment services using crypto

With a full banking license, you can additionally:

  • Lend crypto assets
  • Issue your own tokens as securities
  • Underwrite token offerings
  • Provide asset management services

SRO registration only covers activities under AMLA: exchange, custody, and transfer of virtual currencies. You cannot take deposits or lend. It is important to match your business model to the correct license. FINMA provides a “Fintech Desk” for preliminary inquiries.

Step-by-Step Process to Get a Crypto License in Switzerland

Here is the typical step-by-step process for obtaining a crypto license in Switzerland, based on our advisory experience:

  1. Initial consultation: Contact Consulting24 or a local Swiss law firm to assess your business model and determine the appropriate license type.
  2. Company incorporation: Register a Swiss company (usually an AG or GmbH) with a registered office in Switzerland. Minimum share capital for an AG is CHF 100,000 (EUR 104,000) with at least 20% paid up.
  3. Business plan preparation: Draft a detailed business plan including financial projections, risk management, AML/CFT policies, and IT security measures.
  4. Engage with FINMA: Submit a preliminary application or request a “no-action letter” to clarify the regulatory perimeter. This step is optional but recommended.
  5. Submit formal application: Prepare and submit the full application to FINMA with all required documents, including organisational regulations, internal controls, and a clean criminal record for all board members.
  6. Review and audit: FINMA reviews the application and may request additional information. An external audit may be required to verify compliance.
  7. Decision: FINMA issues a decision. If approved, you receive the license with conditions. The entire process typically takes 6-12 months.
  8. Ongoing compliance: Submit periodic reports, maintain AML procedures, and undergo annual audits.

Banking and Payment Partners in Switzerland

Switzerland has a well-developed banking sector that is generally open to crypto businesses, especially after FINMA authorisation. Major banks such as UBS, Credit Suisse (now part of UBS), and cantonal banks offer accounts to licensed crypto firms. However, the onboarding process can be rigorous and may take several months.

For unlicensed or SRO-registered companies, finding a bank can be challenging. Some smaller private banks and fintech-friendly institutions like SEBA Bank, Sygnum, and FlowBank are more accommodating. These banks specialise in digital assets and offer integrated services such as custody and trading.

Payment processing is also available through Swiss payment providers like Worldline and SIX Payment Services. For international payments, many firms use a combination of Swiss bank accounts and crypto-friendly payment gateways. Compliance and Trust: Ongoing Obligations

Once licensed, you must maintain strict compliance with Swiss regulations. Key ongoing obligations include:

  • AML/CFT program: Implement and maintain a risk-based anti-money laundering program, including KYC, transaction monitoring, and reporting to the Money Laundering Reporting Office (MROS).
  • Annual audit: Appoint an external auditor approved by FINMA to review your financial statements and compliance annually.
  • Capital maintenance: Keep the minimum capital at all times and notify FINMA of any significant changes.
  • Reporting: Submit periodic reports to FINMA on financial condition, business developments, and material incidents.
  • Data protection: Comply with the Swiss Federal Act on Data Protection (FADP) for handling personal data.

Failure to comply can result in fines, license revocation, or criminal charges. This is general guidance, not legal advice. Always work with a qualified Swiss compliance advisor.

Common Mistakes When Applying for a Swiss Crypto License

Many applicants make avoidable errors that delay or derail their application. Common mistakes include:

  • Underestimating the timeline: Expect 6-12 months; rushing the process leads to incomplete applications.
  • Inadequate business plan: FINMA requires a detailed, realistic business plan with financial projections for at least three years.
  • Weak AML controls: Switzerland has strict AML standards. Your policies must be strong and documented.
  • Choosing the wrong license type: Misclassifying your activities can lead to rejection. Seek expert advice early.
  • Ignoring local presence: You need a registered office and local directors. Remote management is not accepted.
  • Not engaging with FINMA early: A preliminary discussion can clarify requirements and save time.

Frequently asked questions

What is the difference between a fintech license and an SRO registration in Switzerland?

A fintech license (Art. 1b Banking Act) allows deposit-taking up to CHF 100 million and requires CHF 3 million capital. SRO registration under AMLA is for exchange and custody without deposit-taking and has no minimum capital. Most crypto startups start with SRO, but if you plan to hold client funds, you need a fintech license.

How long does it take to get a crypto license in Switzerland?

The process typically takes 6 to 12 months from application to approval. This includes company incorporation, documentation, and FINMA review. Preliminary discussions can add 1-2 months. For a faster alternative, Panama can be done in 4-6 weeks.

Can a foreign company apply for a Swiss crypto license?

Yes, but you must incorporate a Swiss subsidiary (AG or GmbH) with a registered office in Switzerland. The company must have local directors and comply with Swiss corporate law. FINMA will assess the parent company's reputation and financial standing.

What are the capital requirements for a crypto license in Switzerland?

For a fintech license, minimum capital is CHF 3 million (approx. EUR 3.1 million). For a full banking license, it is CHF 10 million (approx. EUR 10.3 million). For SRO registration, there is no statutory minimum, but you must have adequate financial resources.

Is Switzerland part of MiCA?

No, Switzerland is not in the European Union, so MiCA does not apply. FINMA has its own regulatory framework, which is considered pragmatic and innovation-friendly. However, Swiss rules are not harmonised with EU standards, which can create complexity for cross-border operations.

What activities are allowed under a Swiss SRO registration?

SRO registration under AMLA allows exchange, custody, and transfer of virtual currencies. You cannot take deposits, lend, or issue tokens as securities. For those activities, you need a fintech or banking license.

How much does it cost to apply for a Swiss crypto license?

Total costs typically range from EUR 60,000 to 150,000 for a fintech license, including legal fees, application fees, and compliance setup. SRO registration is cheaper, around EUR 10,000 to 30,000. Panama is a fixed EUR 6,000.

Do I need a Swiss bank account for the license?

Yes, you need a Swiss bank account to deposit the minimum capital and for ongoing operations. Licensed firms can open accounts with major banks like UBS or cantonal banks. Unlicensed firms may face difficulties; crypto-friendly banks like SEBA or Sygnum are options.

Operating without the required authorisation is illegal and can lead to criminal prosecution, fines, and imprisonment. FINMA actively monitors the market and can order the cessation of activities. Always ensure you have the correct license before offering services.

What ongoing compliance is required after obtaining a Swiss crypto license?

You must maintain AML/CFT programs, undergo annual audits, submit reports to FINMA, and keep minimum capital. Data protection and record-keeping are also required. Non-compliance can lead to fines or license revocation.
