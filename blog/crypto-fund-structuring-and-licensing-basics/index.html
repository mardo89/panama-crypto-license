What Is Crypto Fund Structuring and Licensing?

Crypto fund structuring and licensing refers to the legal and regulatory framework under which a pooled investment vehicle that invests in digital assets is established and authorised. The structure can be a limited partnership, a variable capital company, or a contractual fund, depending on the jurisdiction. Licensing means obtaining the necessary permission from the financial regulator to operate as a fund manager or alternative investment fund manager (AIFM) that deals in crypto assets.

In 2026, the key distinction is between EU member states applying MiCA (which sets a harmonised regime for crypto-asset service providers, including fund managers) and non-EU jurisdictions like Panama, which offer lighter regulation. A proper structure ensures investor protection, tax efficiency, and compliance with anti-money laundering (AML) rules. For example, a fund that holds assets directly on behalf of investors without a licence may be deemed an unregulated collective investment scheme, exposing the manager to regulatory penalties and investor lawsuits. Proper licensing also enables the fund to open bank accounts, onboard institutional investors, and market itself legally across borders.

The process involves selecting a legal form (e.g., limited partnership, investment company with variable capital), drafting constitutional documents, appointing a fund administrator and custodian, and applying for the appropriate licence. In EU jurisdictions, the licence is typically a Crypto-Asset Service Provider (CASP) authorisation under MiCA, which covers portfolio management of crypto assets. In Panama, no specific crypto fund licence exists; instead, a standard corporate structure is used, with the fund's activities governed by general corporate and securities laws.

Who Needs a Crypto Fund Licence?

Any entity that pools capital from multiple investors to invest in crypto assets and manages those assets on a discretionary basis typically needs a licence. This includes:

  • Crypto hedge funds and venture capital funds that actively trade or invest in digital assets
  • Family offices that externalise asset management to a professional fund manager
  • Tokenised fund structures where investors hold tokens representing fund units
  • Decentralised autonomous organisations (DAOs) that fall under fund definitions if they pool capital and distribute profits

Exemptions may apply for small funds (below certain thresholds, e.g., under EUR 100 million assets under management in some EU jurisdictions) or for fully passive holdings where no active management is performed. However, most professional fund structures require authorisation. For example, a crypto venture capital fund with 20 limited partners investing a total of EUR 50 million would almost certainly need a licence in the EU. Licence Types and Regulators

The licence type depends on the jurisdiction. Under MiCA, a Crypto-Asset Service Provider (CASP) licence covers fund management activities if the fund deals in crypto assets. EU member states like Lithuania and Estonia issue CASP licences under MiCA, with capital tiers of EUR 50,000, 125,000, or 150,000 depending on the services offered. The regulator in Lithuania is the Bank of Lithuania; in Estonia, it is the Financial Supervision Authority (FSA). These regulators assess the fund's business plan, AML policies, and the fitness and propriety of management.

In Panama, there is no dedicated crypto fund licence. Instead, fund managers typically use a standard corporate structure (Sociedad Anónima) and operate under the general regulatory framework of the Superintendencia de Bancos de Panamá (SBP) or the Superintendencia del Mercado de Valores (SMV) for securities-related activities. Panama's approach is lighter but may limit access to EU investors. For funds that issue securities to the public, a licence from the SMV may be required, which involves a more rigorous process. Capital Requirements

Under MiCA, minimum capital requirements are tiered by service class: EUR 50,000 for simple custody/transfer services, EUR 125,000 for exchange services, and EUR 150,000 for more complex activities including fund management. These funds must be held as own funds and cannot be used for operational expenses. They serve as a buffer to cover potential losses and demonstrate financial stability.

Panama has no statutory minimum capital for a standard corporation, but a practical minimum of around EUR 10,000 is recommended to demonstrate substance and to cover initial setup costs. For fund structures, the capital requirement is typically set by the fund's prospectus and investor agreements; there is no regulatory minimum. However, if the fund engages in securities activities regulated by the SMV, a minimum capital may be required depending on the licence type. Tax Treatment

Tax treatment varies significantly. Panama operates a territorial tax system: income sourced outside Panama is not taxed, and there is no capital gains tax on crypto. This makes Panama attractive for funds that invest globally. However, investors may be subject to tax in their home countries. For example, a US investor in a Panama fund would still need to report and pay US taxes on their share of the fund's income.

In Lithuania and Estonia, corporate income tax is generally 15-20%, with Estonia offering a deferred tax system (profits are taxed only when distributed). Crypto gains are treated as business income. MiCA does not harmonise tax rules, so local tax advice is essential. For instance, a Lithuania-based fund that trades crypto frequently may be subject to corporate income tax on realised gains, while a Panama fund with the same activities would pay no local tax. Allowed Activities Under Each Licence

A MiCA CASP licence allows a wide range of activities: custody and administration of crypto assets, operation of a trading platform, exchange of crypto for fiat or other crypto, execution of orders, placing of crypto assets, reception and transmission of orders, and portfolio management. Fund management is covered under portfolio management. This means a MiCA-licensed fund can actively manage a portfolio of crypto assets on behalf of investors, including trading, staking, and lending.

In Panama, a standard corporate structure allows any lawful activity not specifically regulated. However, if the fund engages in securities offerings or takes deposits, additional licences from the SMV or SBP may be required. Most crypto fund activities (trading, holding, staking) are permissible without a specific licence. For example, a Panama fund can hold Bitcoin, trade on exchanges, and distribute profits to investors without needing a financial services licence, as long as it does not offer securities to the public or act as a bank.   2. Company setup: Incorporate the fund vehicle (e.g., in Panama as a Sociedad Anónima, in Lithuania as a UAB). This includes drafting the memorandum and articles of association.
  3. Documentation: Prepare a business plan, AML/KYC policies, risk management procedures, and fund prospectus. For MiCA applications, the business plan must detail the fund's investment strategy, custody arrangements, and target market.
  4. Regulatory application: Submit the application to the relevant regulator (e.g., Bank of Lithuania for MiCA CASP). The regulator will review the application and may request additional information or interviews with management.
  5. Review and approval: The regulator assesses the application; timeline varies from 3 to 6 months for MiCA, 4 to 8 weeks for Panama.
  6. Ongoing compliance: Implement reporting, audit, and AML obligations. For MiCA, this includes quarterly reporting on transactions and annual audits. In Panama, annual filings with the Public Registry and a resident agent are required.

Benefits of Proper Structuring

  • Investor confidence: A regulated fund attracts institutional investors who require compliance with regulatory standards, such as pension funds and endowments.
  • Tax efficiency: Choosing the right jurisdiction can minimise tax leakage. For example, Panama's territorial tax system can result in zero local tax on global crypto trading gains.
  • Asset protection: Legal separation of fund assets from manager assets protects investors in case of manager insolvency.
  • Passporting: MiCA licences allow fund marketing across the EU without additional authorisation, expanding the investor base.
  • Operational flexibility: Clear rules on custody, delegation, and valuation reduce legal uncertainty. For instance, MiCA sets out detailed requirements for custody of crypto assets, giving investors confidence that their assets are safe.

Compliance and Trust Considerations

Compliance is not optional. Under MiCA, funds must appoint a compliance officer, conduct regular AML training, and submit periodic reports. In Lithuania, the Bank of Lithuania requires quarterly reports on transaction volumes and customer due diligence. In Estonia, the FSA requires annual audits and ongoing capital adequacy monitoring. Failure to comply can result in fines, licence revocation, or criminal liability. For example, in 2025, a Lithuanian CASP had its licence suspended for failing to report suspicious transactions.

Panama requires a resident agent and annual filings with the Public Registry. While the regulatory burden is lighter, funds must still comply with AML laws and maintain proper records. Common Mistakes in Fund Structuring

  • Ignoring substance requirements: Many founders try to register a fund in a jurisdiction where they have no physical presence, leading to rejection. For MiCA licences, regulators expect a local office and at least one local director.
  • Underestimating capital needs: Choosing a jurisdiction with low capital but high ongoing costs can be misleading. Panama's low setup cost is attractive, but ongoing compliance costs for a MiCA fund can be EUR 10,000 per year.
  • Mixing fund and manager assets: Proper segregation is critical. A common mistake is using the same bank account for fund and manager expenses, which can lead to legal liability.
  • Overlooking tax implications for investors: A fund's structure can create tax liabilities for investors in their home countries. For example, a Panama fund may be considered a passive foreign investment company (PFIC) for US investors, resulting in punitive tax treatment.
  • Rushing the process: Licensing can take months; plan ahead. Some founders start fundraising before obtaining the licence, which can lead to regulatory sanctions.

Alternatives and Comparisons

Besides Panama, Lithuania, and Estonia, other popular jurisdictions for crypto fund structuring include Switzerland, Cayman Islands, and Dubai. Switzerland offers a mature regulatory framework with FINMA oversight, but costs are higher (EUR 50,000+ for a fund licence). The Cayman Islands are a traditional fund hub with no direct crypto licence, but funds can be structured as exempted limited partnerships, with costs around EUR 20,000 for setup. Dubai (VARA) has a dedicated crypto regime but requires a local presence and substantial capital (minimum EUR 100,000).

Panama remains the most cost-effective option at EUR 6,000 flat, with no capital gains tax and a fast setup. For EU market access, Lithuania or Estonia under MiCA is the logical choice, despite higher costs. Frequently asked questions

What is the minimum capital for a crypto fund under MiCA?

Under MiCA, capital requirements are tiered: EUR 50,000 for custody/transfer services, EUR 125,000 for exchange services, and EUR 150,000 for portfolio management (including fund management). These amounts must be held as own funds and cannot be used for operational expenses.

Can I set up a crypto fund in Panama without a licence?

Yes, Panama does not require a specific crypto fund licence. A standard corporation (Sociedad Anónima) can operate a crypto fund, provided it does not engage in regulated activities like securities offerings or taking deposits. This makes Panama a cost-effective option at EUR 6,000 flat.

How long does it take to get a crypto fund licence in Lithuania?

The timeline for a MiCA CASP licence in Lithuania is typically 3 to 6 months, depending on the complexity of the application and the regulator's workload. Consulting24 can expedite the process by preparing a thorough application upfront.

What are the ongoing compliance costs for a Panama crypto fund?

Ongoing costs for a Panama fund are relatively low, around EUR 1,000 to 2,000 per year. This includes the resident agent fee, annual government fees, and basic accounting. No regulatory reporting is required beyond annual filings.

Is a Panama crypto fund suitable for EU investors?

A Panama fund can accept EU investors, but it cannot be marketed as a regulated fund in the EU. EU investors may be subject to local tax and reporting obligations. For EU-wide marketing, a MiCA licence in Lithuania or Estonia is preferable.

What activities are allowed under a MiCA CASP licence for fund management?

A MiCA CASP licence allows portfolio management of crypto assets, including trading, custody, staking, and lending. It also covers reception and transmission of orders, execution of orders, and placement of crypto assets. Fund managers must comply with conduct of business rules.

Do I need a physical office in Lithuania for a crypto fund licence?

Yes, the Bank of Lithuania requires a registered office in Lithuania and expects substance, including a local director or compliance officer. Consulting24 can help arrange a virtual office and local representation.

What are the tax benefits of a Panama crypto fund?

Panama has a territorial tax system: income sourced outside Panama is not taxed, and there is no capital gains tax on crypto. This can result in zero local tax on global trading profits. However, investors must comply with their home country tax laws.

What is the difference between a Panama corporation and a MiCA-licensed fund?

A Panama corporation is a simple legal entity with no specific regulatory oversight, making it fast and cheap (EUR 6,000). A MiCA-licensed fund is a regulated investment vehicle that can market across the EU but requires higher capital (EUR 150,000) and ongoing compliance (EUR 5,000-10,000/year).
