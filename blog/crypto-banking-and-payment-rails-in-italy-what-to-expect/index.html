What Are Crypto Banking and Payment Rails in Italy?

Crypto banking and payment rails refer to the infrastructure that allows digital assets to be used for payments, transfers, and banking-like services. In Italy, this includes services such as crypto-to-fiat conversion, merchant payment processing, and custodial wallet provision. Under MiCA, these activities fall under the Crypto-Asset Service Provider (CASP) regime, which replaced the previous Italian virtual currency register.

Key features of the Italian framework:

  • Full alignment with MiCA, meaning a single license is valid across the EU.
  • Supervision by the Bank of Italy and CONSOB (the securities regulator).
  • Strict anti-money laundering (AML) and know-your-customer (KYC) requirements.
  • Capital requirements based on the type of services offered (see below).

For founders, this means you can operate in Italy and passport your services to other EU countries. However, the application process and ongoing compliance can be more demanding than in smaller jurisdictions.

Who Needs a Crypto Payment License in Italy?

Any business offering crypto-asset services to Italian residents, including crypto payment processing, exchange, or custody, must be registered as a CASP. This applies to both Italian companies and foreign firms targeting Italian clients. Specifically, you need a license if you:

  • Provide crypto-to-fiat or crypto-to-crypto exchange services.
  • Operate a crypto wallet (custodial or non-custodial if you hold private keys).
  • Facilitate merchant payments in crypto.
  • Offer crypto lending or staking as a service.

Even if you are based outside Italy, if you actively market to Italian customers, you must comply. Many founders choose to establish a legal entity in Italy or in another EU country with a lighter regime, such as Lithuania, and then passport into Italy. License Type and Regulator

The competent authority for crypto licensing in Italy is the Bank of Italy (Banca d'Italia), in coordination with CONSOB (the Italian securities regulator). Under MiCA, the license is called a Crypto-Asset Service Provider (CASP) authorization. This is a single license covering all crypto services, but the capital requirement varies by service class.

Regulatory framework:

  • Primary legislation: MiCA (EU Regulation 2023/1114) and Italian implementing decrees.
  • AML supervision: Bank of Italy and the Financial Intelligence Unit (UIF).
  • Ongoing reporting: Quarterly AML reports, annual financial statements, and transaction monitoring.

Compared to Estonia or Lithuania, Italy has a more rigorous review process, often taking 6-12 months. However, the license is highly respected and provides full EU passporting.

Capital Requirement Under MiCA

MiCA introduces tiered capital requirements based on the type of crypto-asset services provided. For Italy, the minimum capital must be held as equity and cannot be borrowed. The tiers are:

  • EUR 50,000 for simple exchange services (crypto-to-crypto only, no fiat).
  • EUR 125,000 for exchange services involving fiat currency (crypto-to-fiat) and/or custody of client assets.
  • EUR 150,000 for services that include trading on own account, underwriting, or placing crypto-assets.

If your business offers multiple service classes, the highest capital requirement applies. Additionally, you must maintain professional indemnity insurance or a similar guarantee covering at least EUR 1 million per claim. This capital must be deposited in a bank account in Italy or another EU member state and cannot be used for operational expenses.

Tax Treatment of Crypto Payments in Italy

Italy has a relatively clear tax framework for crypto assets. As of 2026, the key rules are:

  • Capital gains tax: 26% on profits from crypto trading (if held for less than 12 months). Gains from long-term holdings (over 12 months) are tax-free up to EUR 2,000 per year; above that, 26% applies.
  • Income tax: Crypto payments received as income (e.g., salary, merchant payments) are taxed as ordinary income at progressive rates up to 43%.
  • VAT: Crypto-to-fiat exchanges are exempt from VAT. Crypto-to-crypto exchanges are also VAT-exempt. Merchant payments in crypto are treated as barter transactions for VAT purposes.
  • Corporate tax: Corporate income tax (IRES) at 24% plus regional tax (IRAP) at 3.9% on business income from crypto activities.

It is essential to maintain proper accounting records and report all crypto transactions. The Italian tax authority (Agenzia delle Entrate) has increased scrutiny on crypto. We recommend engaging a local tax advisor. For a more tax-friendly environment, some founders consider Malta or Panama, which have no capital gains tax on crypto.

Allowed Activities Under the Italian Crypto License

With a CASP license in Italy, you can offer the following services to EU clients:

  • Exchange services: Crypto-to-fiat and crypto-to-crypto trading.
  • Custody and wallet services: Holding private keys on behalf of clients.
  • Payment processing: Enabling merchants to accept crypto payments and convert to fiat.
  • Transfer services: Sending crypto on behalf of clients.
  • Order execution: Executing buy/sell orders on behalf of clients.
  • Portfolio management: Managing crypto assets for clients (discretionary).
  • Advice: Providing personalized recommendations on crypto investments.

Activities not covered by the CASP license include issuing stablecoins (requires a separate e-money license) and operating a crypto ATM (may require additional registration). For a broader scope, you might consider a Switzerland license, which covers a wider range of services under a single framework.

Step-by-Step Application Process

Obtaining a CASP license in Italy involves several stages. Here is a typical timeline:

  1. Pre-application (1-2 months): Define your business model, prepare AML/KYC policies, and engage a local legal representative. Appoint a compliance officer and board members.
  2. Submit application (2-3 months): File the application with the Bank of Italy, including business plan, governance structure, capital proof, and AML procedures. The regulator may request additional information.
  3. Regulatory review (3-6 months): The Bank of Italy and CONSOB review the application. They may conduct interviews with management and request changes. This is the longest phase.
  4. Approval and registration (1 month): Once approved, you are registered in the CASP register and can start operations. You must notify the regulator of any changes.
  5. Ongoing compliance: Submit quarterly AML reports, annual audits, and maintain capital requirements.

Banking and Payment Partnerships in Italy

One of the biggest challenges for crypto firms in Italy is securing banking relationships. Many traditional banks are hesitant to work with crypto companies due to perceived risks. However, the situation has improved under MiCA, as regulated CASPs are seen as legitimate.

Options for banking and payment rails:

  • Italian banks: A few banks, such as Banca Sella and Intesa Sanpaolo, have started offering accounts to crypto firms. Expect strict due diligence and possibly higher fees.
  • EMIs (Electronic Money Institutions): Companies like Satispay or Revolut Business may provide payment processing, but they often require a CASP license themselves.
  • Foreign banks: Some EU banks based in Lithuania or Estonia are more crypto-friendly and can accept Italian CASPs as clients.
  • Payment processors: Specialized crypto payment gateways like Coinbase Commerce or BitPay can handle merchant settlements.

We recommend opening a corporate bank account before applying for the license, as it demonstrates financial stability. Consulting24 can introduce you to banking partners in Lithuania and Estonia if Italian banks are too restrictive.

Benefits of an Italian Crypto License

Despite the higher cost and longer timeline, an Italian CASP license offers several advantages:

  • EU passporting: Serve clients across all 27 EU member states without additional licenses.
  • Regulatory credibility: Italy's rigorous process signals trustworthiness to partners and customers.
  • Access to the Italian market: Italy is the third-largest economy in the EU, with a growing crypto-savvy population.
  • MiCA compliance: You are future-proofed against regulatory changes, as MiCA is the gold standard.
  • Clear tax rules: The tax framework is relatively stable, reducing uncertainty.

For founders who prioritize speed and cost, a Czech Republic or Panama license might be more suitable. However, if you plan to scale across Europe, Italy is a strong choice.

Compliance and Trust Requirements

Operating a crypto payment service in Italy requires a strong compliance framework. Key obligations include:

  • AML/KYC: Verify customer identity, monitor transactions, and report suspicious activities to the UIF. Implement risk-based procedures.
  • Travel Rule: For transfers over EUR 1,000, you must collect and share sender and receiver information with counterparties.
  • Data protection: Comply with GDPR, including data minimization and breach notification.
  • Audit and reporting: Annual financial audits and quarterly AML reports to the Bank of Italy.
  • Insurance: Professional indemnity insurance or equivalent coverage of at least EUR 1 million.

Failure to comply can result in fines up to EUR 5 million or 10% of annual turnover, and possible license revocation. This is general guidance, not legal advice. Always consult with a qualified Italian attorney. For a lighter compliance burden, consider Poland or Panama, which have less stringent requirements.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Founders often make these errors when applying for an Italian crypto license:

  • Incomplete AML policies: The Bank of Italy expects detailed, risk-based AML procedures. Generic templates are rejected.
  • Underestimating capital requirements: Ensure you have the required capital in a bank account before applying. Borrowed capital is not accepted.
  • Ignoring local representation: You must have a legal representative in Italy (a person or a registered office). Many founders skip this.
  • Overlooking tax obligations: Even if your company is non-Italian, selling to Italian clients creates a tax presence. Seek local tax advice.
  • Choosing the wrong service class: If you plan to offer custody, you need the higher capital tier. Misclassification delays approval.

Frequently asked questions

What is the minimum capital for a crypto payment license in Italy?

Under MiCA, the minimum capital is EUR 50,000 for basic exchange services, EUR 125,000 for services involving fiat or custody, and EUR 150,000 for trading on own account. The highest tier applies if you offer multiple services.

How long does it take to get an Italian crypto license?

The typical timeline is 6 to 12 months from submission to approval. This includes regulatory review and possible requests for additional information. Pre-application preparation can add 1-2 months.

Can I passport my Italian license to other EU countries?

Yes, a CASP license issued by the Bank of Italy is valid across all EU member states under the MiCA passporting regime. You must notify the regulator of your intention to operate in other countries.

What taxes apply to crypto payments in Italy?

Capital gains are taxed at 26% (with a EUR 2,000 annual exemption for long-term gains). Income from crypto payments is taxed as ordinary income (up to 43%). Crypto-to-fiat exchanges are VAT-exempt.

Do I need a local office in Italy to apply?

Yes, you must have a registered office or a legal representative in Italy. This can be a physical office or a service address provided by a local agent.

Is it possible to operate a crypto payment service without a license in Italy?

No, any business offering crypto-asset services to Italian residents must be registered as a CASP. Unlicensed operation can lead to fines and criminal penalties.

What are the main differences between Italy and Lithuania for crypto licensing?

Lithuania has a lower capital requirement (EUR 125,000 for most services), faster processing (3-6 months), and lower overall costs. Italy offers a larger domestic market but with higher costs and longer timelines.

Can I use a Panama company to serve Italian clients?

If you target Italian clients, you must comply with Italian regulations, which typically require an EU license. A Panama company alone is not sufficient. However, you can use a Panama entity for non-EU operations alongside an EU license.

What banking options are available for crypto firms in Italy?

Some Italian banks like Banca Sella and Intesa Sanpaolo offer accounts to regulated CASPs. Alternatively, you can use EMIs or banks in other EU countries that are more crypto-friendly.
