Overview: Why Banks and EMIs Reject Crypto Companies

Banks and EMIs assess risk carefully. Crypto companies are often seen as high-risk due to money laundering concerns, volatility, and regulatory uncertainty. Even with a license, if your business model involves anonymous transactions, high-risk jurisdictions, or unclear source of funds, rejection is likely. In 2026, with MiCA fully in force in the EU, licensed CASPs (Crypto Asset Service Providers) have a clearer regulatory status, but banks still apply their own risk appetite. For example, a bank might reject a crypto exchange that allows trading of privacy coins like Monero, even if the exchange is licensed. Similarly, if your company has directors from jurisdictions with weak AML enforcement, banks may flag the application. Key reasons for rejection include:

  • Lack of a clear business plan and compliance framework
  • Unclear source of funds or wealth of directors
  • High-risk client base (e.g., unregulated exchanges or P2P platforms)
  • Previous adverse media or sanctions links
  • Incomplete documentation or poor communication during due diligence

Understanding these factors helps you prepare a stronger application. Consulting24 often reviews a client's compliance documentation before submission to identify gaps.

Who Needs a Bank or EMI Account?

Any licensed crypto company that handles fiat currency needs a bank or EMI account. This includes crypto exchanges, custodial wallet providers, OTC desks, and payment processors. Even if you operate a purely crypto-to-crypto exchange, you may still need an account for operational expenses, salaries, and tax payments. In 2026, most regulators require licensed entities to have a segregated client funds account with a regulated financial institution. For instance, under MiCA, a CASP must safeguard client funds in a separate account with a credit institution or an authorised EMI. Typical users include:

  • Startups with a new license seeking their first banking partner
  • Established firms looking to expand to new jurisdictions
  • Companies needing multi-currency accounts for international operations
  • Firms that have been rejected by traditional banks and need alternative EMI solutions

For example, a Panama-licensed OTC desk handling USD transactions for Latin American clients would benefit from a Panama bank account that offers SWIFT and local clearing.

License Type and Regulator Impact on Banking

The type of crypto license you hold significantly affects your banking options. For example, a crypto exchange license from a reputable jurisdiction like Estonia or Lithuania is more widely accepted than a license from a less regulated jurisdiction. In the EU, MiCA introduces a passportable CASP license, which banks recognize more readily. Panama's license is also well-regarded for its strong regulatory framework under the Superintendencia de Bancos de Panama. Regulators that are FATF-compliant and have strong AML/CFT frameworks give banks more confidence. Consulting24 advises clients to choose a license that balances cost, speed, and banking acceptance. For instance, a Panama license at EUR 6,000 flat is cost-effective and opens doors to many EMIs, while an Estonian license may be preferred for EU passporting but requires higher capital (EUR 50,000 under MiCA).

Capital Requirement for Bank/EMI Accounts

Banks and EMIs do not impose a regulatory capital requirement, but they may ask for a minimum deposit or balance to open an account. This is typically between EUR 1,000 and EUR 10,000, depending on the provider and the type of account. For corporate accounts, some banks require a minimum average balance to avoid fees. For licensed crypto companies, having sufficient capital to cover operational costs for 6-12 months is often a sign of stability. Note that the crypto license itself may have capital requirements (e.g., EUR 50,000 for a CASP under MiCA), but that is separate from banking requirements. For example, a Panama-licensed company might need to deposit USD 5,000 to open a corporate account with a Panama bank, while an EU EMI might require EUR 2,000 initial deposit.

Tax Treatment of Crypto Banking Accounts

The tax treatment of funds held in a bank or EMI account depends on your jurisdiction and business structure. In Panama, there is no capital gains tax on crypto, and corporate income tax is low. In the EU, MiCA does not harmonise tax, so you must comply with local tax laws. Generally, interest earned on fiat deposits is taxable as income. For crypto companies, proper accounting and segregation of client funds are critical to avoid tax complications. Consulting24 recommends working with a tax advisor familiar with crypto to ensure compliance. For example, a company holding client funds in a segregated account must report those funds separately from its own capital. In Lithuania, corporate income tax is 15%, but crypto gains may be taxed differently depending on the activity.

Allowed Activities with a Crypto-Friendly Account

Not all accounts allow the same activities. Typical allowed transactions include:

  • Receiving fiat from clients for crypto purchases
  • Paying out fiat to clients for crypto sales
  • Paying operational expenses (rent, salaries, fees)
  • Converting fiat to crypto and vice versa (via integrated partners)
  • Holding client funds in segregated accounts

Prohibited activities often include: transacting with unregulated entities, high-risk jurisdictions, or anonymous crypto transactions. Some EMIs restrict certain crypto assets (e.g., privacy coins). Always check the provider's terms. For example, Bank Frick may allow transactions with major coins like Bitcoin and Ethereum but prohibit Monero. Similarly, some EMIs may not allow transfers to unregulated exchanges in jurisdictions like Iran or North Korea.

Step-by-Step Process to Open an Account

  1. Choose a provider: Research crypto-friendly banks or EMIs. Consulting24 can provide a shortlist based on your license and business model.
  2. Prepare documentation: Business license, certificate of incorporation, proof of registered address, AML/KYC policies, source of funds, business plan, director IDs, and sometimes a legal opinion.
  3. Submit application: Complete the provider's application form and upload documents via their secure portal.
  4. Due diligence: The provider will conduct background checks on directors and beneficial owners, including sanctions screening and adverse media checks.
  5. Interview: Some providers require a video call to discuss your business model, compliance framework, and expected transaction volumes.
  6. Approval and account setup: Once approved, sign the agreement and fund the account with the minimum deposit.
  7. Ongoing compliance: Submit periodic reports (e.g., transaction summaries, AML audits) and respond to any queries promptly.

Timelines vary; having a professional advisor can speed up the process. Consulting24 often handles document preparation and coordinates with providers to reduce delays.

Examples include Bank Frick (Liechtenstein), Clear Junction, and some Lithuanian EMIs. Traditional banks like LHV (Estonia) and Revolut Business have crypto-friendly policies but are selective. Panama offers a range of banks that accept licensed crypto firms, often with lower compliance hurdles. For instance, Banco General and Towerbank in Panama have been known to open accounts for licensed crypto companies. Compliance and Trust: How to Pass Due Diligence

Banks and EMIs will scrutinise your AML/CFT policies, transaction monitoring, and source of funds. To pass due diligence:

  • Have a clear, written AML policy that meets FATF standards
  • Conduct thorough KYC on all clients, including beneficial ownership verification
  • Maintain transaction records and be ready to explain unusual activity
  • Ensure directors have clean backgrounds and can articulate the business model
  • Provide references from other regulated entities if possible

Note: This is general guidance, not legal advice. Consulting24 can review your compliance framework before you apply. For example, we helped a Panama-licensed exchange prepare a detailed AML manual and transaction monitoring procedures, which led to approval from a Panama bank within 3 weeks. Common red flags include directors with previous bankruptcies or unexplained large deposits.

Common Mistakes That Lead to Rejection

Avoid these pitfalls:

  • Applying without a clear business plan or compliance documentation
  • Using a license from a jurisdiction with weak regulation (e.g., some unregulated zones)
  • Having directors with adverse media or sanctions links
  • Failing to disclose all beneficial owners
  • Submitting incomplete or inconsistent information
  • Choosing a provider that is not crypto-friendly

Consulting24 helps clients avoid these mistakes by preparing a comprehensive application package. For instance, one client was rejected by three EMIs because their business plan did not specify transaction limits. After we revised the plan to include expected volumes and risk controls, the fourth application succeeded. Another common mistake is using a personal bank account for business transactions, which can lead to account closure.

Alternatives and Comparison: Panama vs Other Jurisdictions

When choosing a jurisdiction for your crypto license, consider banking access. Panama offers a flat EUR 6,000 license fee and a straightforward process. Its banks are generally open to licensed crypto companies. In comparison, Estonia and Lithuania require higher capital (EUR 50,000+ under MiCA) and have longer timelines but offer EU passporting. Dubai (VARA) is another option but with higher costs and a different regulatory approach. For most startups, Panama provides the best balance of cost, speed, and banking acceptance. For example, a company targeting EU clients might prefer an Estonian CASP license despite the EUR 50,000 capital, because it allows passporting across the EU. However, a company focused on Latin America might choose Panama for lower costs and easier banking.

Frequently asked questions

Can I open a bank account with a Panama crypto license?

Yes, many banks in Panama and international EMIs accept Panama-licensed crypto companies. The license is well-regarded for its strong AML framework. Consulting24 can recommend specific providers like Banco General or Towerbank. Typically, you need to provide the license, company documents, and a business plan.

What documents do I need to open an EMI account?

Typically: certified copy of the crypto license, certificate of incorporation, proof of registered address, AML policy, source of funds, business plan, director IDs, and sometimes a legal opinion. Some EMIs also require a personal guarantee from directors. Consulting24 can help prepare a complete package.

How long does it take to open a crypto-friendly bank account?

Timelines vary: EMIs often take 1-3 months, while traditional banks can take 2-6 months. Panama banks are usually faster, around 1-2 months. Factors include the completeness of your application and the provider's due diligence workload. Consulting24 can expedite the process by pre-vetting documentation.

What is the minimum deposit required for a crypto company account?

Minimum deposits range from EUR 1,000 to EUR 10,000, depending on the provider. Some EMIs require no minimum deposit but charge higher monthly fees. For Panama banks, USD 5,000 is common. Consulting24 can advise on specific requirements for your chosen provider.

Can I use a personal bank account for my crypto business?

No, using a personal account for business transactions violates most banks' terms and can lead to account closure. Licensed crypto companies must use a corporate account. Mixing personal and business funds also creates tax and liability issues.

What are the ongoing compliance requirements after opening an account?

You must submit periodic transaction reports, update KYC information annually, and respond to any due diligence queries. Some providers require quarterly AML audits. Failure to comply can result in account suspension. Consulting24 can help set up compliance procedures.

Are there EMIs that support crypto-to-fiat conversion?

Yes, several EMIs offer integrated crypto-to-fiat conversion, such as Clear Junction, Bank Frick, and some Lithuanian EMIs. They typically support major coins like Bitcoin and Ethereum. Conversion fees vary; some charge a spread of 0.5-1%.

What happens if my bank account application is rejected?

If rejected, you can apply to another provider. Common reasons for rejection include incomplete documentation or adverse media on directors. Consulting24 can help identify the issue and improve your application for the next try. We have a network of alternative providers.

Can I open an account before obtaining my crypto license?

Some EMIs allow pre-approval based on your application in progress, but most require the license to be issued first. Panama banks typically require the final license. Consulting24 can advise on providers that offer conditional approvals.

What is the difference between a bank and an EMI for crypto companies?

Banks are fully regulated credit institutions with deposit insurance, while EMIs are licensed to issue electronic money but not to lend. EMIs are often more crypto-friendly and faster to onboard. Banks may offer more services like loans but have stricter compliance.
