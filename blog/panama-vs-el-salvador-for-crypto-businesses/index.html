Overview: Panama vs El Salvador for Crypto

Panama and El Salvador represent two distinct paths for crypto entrepreneurs. Panama operates under a clear licensing regime supervised by the Superintendencia de Bancos de Panama (SBP) and the Superintendencia del Mercado de Valores (SMV). The country has no central bank, uses the US dollar as legal tender, and imposes no capital gains tax on cryptocurrency transactions. This makes it a tax-efficient destination for crypto businesses.

El Salvador, on the other hand, became the first country to adopt Bitcoin as legal tender in 2021. While this created a crypto-friendly image, the regulatory environment for crypto service providers remains less defined. The Comision Nacional de Activos Digitales (CNAD) oversees digital asset issuances, but a comprehensive licensing framework for exchanges and wallets is still evolving. As of 2026, Panama offers a more predictable and cost-effective route for most crypto startups.

Key differences include regulatory clarity, cost, and speed. Panama's license is well-defined, with a fixed fee and clear requirements. El Salvador's approach is more experimental, which can lead to uncertainty. For founders prioritizing speed and certainty, Panama is the stronger choice.

Who Needs a Crypto License in These Jurisdictions?

Any business providing cryptocurrency services such as exchange, custody, wallet management, or payment processing requires a license in Panama. The law covers both fiat-to-crypto and crypto-to-crypto transactions. In El Salvador, the need for a license depends on the activity; Bitcoin-related services may fall under existing financial regulations, but a dedicated crypto license is not yet mandatory for all operators. However, to operate legally and access banking, licensing is advisable in both countries.

Typical clients include fintech startups, crypto exchanges, OTC desks, and blockchain payment processors. Consulting24 helps founders assess whether Panama's flat EUR 6,000 license or El Salvador's evolving regime better suits their business model. For example, a startup focused on Bitcoin payments might find El Salvador's environment appealing, but an exchange offering multiple tokens would benefit from Panama's clear regulatory scope.

Other factors include target market and investor expectations. Many investors prefer regulated jurisdictions like Panama, which provide a clear legal framework. El Salvador's regime may be seen as riskier by some institutional partners.

License Type and Regulator

Panama: The license is issued by the Superintendencia de Bancos de Panama (SBP) for activities involving virtual assets. It is a single license covering exchange, custody, and transfer services. The regulator requires a physical presence in Panama, including a local office and compliance officer. The license is valid for two years and renewable.

El Salvador: The Comision Nacional de Activos Digitales (CNAD) regulates digital asset issuances and service providers. However, a formal crypto license for exchanges is not yet fully implemented. Businesses currently operate under a registration or sandbox regime. The CNAD may issue authorizations for specific activities, but the process is less standardized.

Capital Requirement

Panama: The minimum capital requirement for a crypto license is USD 10,000 (approximately EUR 9,000), which can be used for operational expenses. No paid-up capital is required upfront; the company can be incorporated with a minimum of one share. This low barrier makes Panama accessible to startups.

El Salvador: There is no official minimum capital requirement for crypto businesses, but banks may require a deposit of $50,000 or more to open accounts. Consulting24 advises clients to budget for working capital rather than a fixed regulatory minimum. In practice, having at least $50,000 in liquid funds is recommended for banking and operational stability.

Compare this to EU jurisdictions like Lithuania, where capital requirements under MiCA range from EUR 50,000 to EUR 150,000 depending on services. Panama's requirement is significantly lower.

Tax Treatment

Tax is a major differentiator. Panama has a territorial tax system: income earned outside Panama is not taxed. There is no capital gains tax on cryptocurrency profits, no VAT on crypto transactions, and no withholding tax on dividends paid to non-residents. Corporate income tax is 25% on locally sourced income, but most crypto businesses can structure to minimize local tax exposure.

El Salvador imposes a 30% corporate income tax on worldwide income for residents. Capital gains on crypto are taxed as ordinary income. However, there is no VAT on crypto transactions. For tax efficiency, Panama is generally more favorable. A crypto exchange structured in Panama can avoid capital gains tax entirely, while the same business in El Salvador would face a 30% tax on profits.

Allowed Activities

Panama: The license permits exchange between virtual assets and fiat currency, exchange between virtual assets, custody, and transfer of virtual assets. It does not allow derivative trading or securities activities unless a separate securities license is obtained. The scope is clearly defined, which helps businesses plan their operations.

El Salvador: There is no explicit list of allowed activities. Bitcoin-related services (payments, remittances) are permitted, but other crypto activities may fall under existing financial laws. This ambiguity can be a risk for businesses offering multiple services.

Banking and Payments

Access to banking is crucial. Panama has a mature banking sector with several banks accepting crypto-related businesses, especially those with a license. The US dollar is the official currency, simplifying international transactions. Banks typically require a minimum deposit of $10,000-$50,000, a physical presence, and a clear business plan.

El Salvador uses the US dollar as well, but banks are more cautious due to the country's Bitcoin adoption. Many international banks avoid exposure. Local banks may require higher deposits and stricter due diligence. Panama offers more reliable banking options. Consulting24 assists clients in opening accounts with partner banks in Panama, ensuring a smoother process.

For businesses that need payment processing, Panama's banking infrastructure supports fiat on-ramps and off-ramps efficiently. El Salvador's banking sector is less developed for crypto firms.

Compliance and Trust

Both jurisdictions require AML/CFT compliance. Panama has a strong AML framework aligned with FATF recommendations. License holders must submit regular reports, conduct customer due diligence, and undergo audits. The SBP supervises compliance and can impose sanctions for non-compliance.

El Salvador's compliance requirements are less formalized but still expected. The CNAD may issue guidelines, but enforcement is less consistent. Panama's established compliance regime builds trust with partners and regulators. Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Choosing a jurisdiction based solely on tax benefits without considering regulatory stability.
  • Underestimating the importance of local presence (office, compliance officer).
  • Failing to prepare a comprehensive AML program.
  • Ignoring banking requirements; some banks require a minimum deposit of $50,000.
  • Assuming El Salvador's Bitcoin law simplifies licensing; it does not.
  • Not budgeting for ongoing compliance costs, such as audit fees and compliance officer salary.
  • Delaying the appointment of a local compliance officer until after application.

Alternatives: Panama vs Other Jurisdictions

Beyond El Salvador, Panama is often compared to Estonia and Lithuania. Estonia offers a crypto license under the Financial Intelligence Unit (FIU) with a minimum capital of EUR 12,000 and a 20% corporate tax on distributed profits. Lithuania, under MiCA, requires EUR 125,000 capital for exchange services and has a 15% corporate tax. Panama's EUR 6,000 flat fee and zero capital gains tax make it highly competitive.
