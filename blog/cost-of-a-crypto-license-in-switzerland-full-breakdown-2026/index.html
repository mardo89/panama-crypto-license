What Is a Crypto License in Switzerland?

A crypto license in Switzerland is a regulatory authorisation from the Swiss Financial Market Supervisory Authority (FINMA) to operate a virtual asset service provider (VASP). Switzerland does not use the term 'crypto license' formally; instead, firms apply for a FinTech license or a banking license under the Swiss Banking Act, or they register as a self-regulatory organisation (SRO) member under the Anti-Money Laundering Act (AMLA). Most crypto businesses operate under the AMLA regime with a FINMA-recognised SRO, which is simpler and cheaper than a full banking license.

The cost of a crypto license in Switzerland varies depending on the chosen structure. For 2026, FINMA continues to apply its 2019 guidelines on ICOs and blockchain, and the DLT Act (effective 2021) provides a legal basis for tokenised securities. The Swiss approach is principle-based, not prescriptive like MiCA. This means lower direct regulatory costs but higher legal and compliance advisory fees to ensure alignment with FINMA expectations. For example, a typical SRO application requires a detailed business plan, AML policies, and risk assessment, which can cost CHF 20,000-50,000 in legal fees alone. Who Needs a Swiss Crypto License?

Any entity that, in or from Switzerland, provides services such as exchange between cryptocurrencies and fiat, transfer of virtual currencies, custody of private keys, or operation of a trading platform must comply with Swiss AML laws and obtain SRO membership or a license. This includes:

  • Crypto exchanges (centralised and decentralised if operated from Switzerland)
  • Wallet providers (custodial and non-custodial if they hold keys)
  • OTC desks
  • Token issuers (if tokens are deemed securities)
  • Asset managers dealing in crypto assets

If you plan to serve Swiss residents or base your operations in Switzerland, you need at least SRO membership. The cost of a crypto license in Switzerland for a simple AML-registered firm is lower than a full FinTech license, but you must still meet capital and reporting obligations. For example, a small exchange with fewer than 100 transactions per month might pay CHF 5,000 in SRO fees, while a large platform handling millions in volume would need a FinTech license with CHF 300,000 capital.

License Type and Regulator

The primary regulator is FINMA. The main license types relevant to crypto are:

  • SRO Membership (AMLA registration): For firms that only exchange, transfer, or store crypto. No minimum capital required by law, but SROs may set their own. This is the most common route for startups.
  • FinTech License (Banking Act art. 1b): For firms taking public deposits up to CHF 100 million but not engaging in traditional lending. Minimum capital CHF 300,000. Suitable for larger platforms.
  • Banking License: Full banking license for deposit-taking and lending. Minimum capital CHF 10 million. Rare for crypto firms.

For 2026, the cost of a crypto license in Switzerland for an SRO membership is typically CHF 5,000-20,000 in application fees plus annual SRO fees of CHF 2,000-10,000. FINMA itself does not charge for SRO registration directly; the SROs do. For a FinTech license, FINMA charges a processing fee of CHF 10,000-50,000 depending on complexity. Capital Requirement

For SRO membership, Swiss law does not impose a statutory minimum capital. However, most SROs require at least CHF 50,000-100,000 in paid-up capital as a risk buffer. The FinTech license requires CHF 300,000 minimum capital. Full banking license requires CHF 10 million. The cost of a crypto license in Switzerland thus includes the opportunity cost of locking up capital. Compare this to Panama, where no minimum capital is required and our flat fee is EUR 6,000. For EU jurisdictions under MiCA, capital tiers are EUR 50,000 / 125,000 / 150,000 depending on services. For example, a simple exchange service in Lithuania would need EUR 50,000 capital, while a full-service platform might need EUR 150,000.

Tax Treatment

Switzerland has a favourable tax regime for crypto. Private investors are not subject to capital gains tax. Professional traders and businesses pay corporate income tax (federal + cantonal, effective rate 12-20% depending on canton). VAT is not levied on crypto transactions (Swiss Federal Tax Administration ruling). Wealth tax applies to crypto holdings at cantonal level. Stamp duty may apply to issuance of tokens if deemed securities. The cost of a crypto license in Switzerland should include tax advisory fees, typically CHF 5,000-15,000 for initial structuring. For example, a company based in Zug (Crypto Valley) might pay an effective corporate tax rate of 14.6%, while one in Geneva could pay 18.5%. In contrast, Panama has territorial taxation, meaning no tax on foreign-source income, which can be more advantageous for international operations.

Allowed Activities

Under SRO membership, allowed activities include:

  • Exchange between crypto and fiat
  • Crypto-to-crypto exchange
  • Transfer of virtual currencies
  • Custodial wallet services
  • Operation of a trading platform (if not taking deposits)

Under a FinTech license, you can also take public deposits up to CHF 100 million and offer payment services. The DLT Act allows for dematerialised securities and trading of tokenised assets. The cost of a crypto license in Switzerland influences which activities you can pursue; a FinTech license opens more doors but at higher cost. Step-by-Step Application Process

  1. Choose structure: Decide between SRO membership or FinTech license based on your business model and budget.
  2. Select an SRO: FINMA-recognised SROs include VQF, SRO PolyReg, and ARIF. Each has different fee schedules and requirements.
  3. Prepare documentation: Business plan, AML policies, risk assessment, organisational chart, financial projections, and background checks on directors.
  4. Submit application: To the chosen SRO (for AMLA) or directly to FINMA (for FinTech license).
  5. Review and interview: SRO or FINMA may request additional information and conduct interviews with management.
  6. Approval and registration: Once approved, you are registered in the FINMA public register (for FinTech) or SRO register.
  7. Ongoing compliance: Submit regular AML reports, undergo annual audit (if FinTech), and pay annual fees.

The process typically takes 2-4 months for SRO and 6-12 months for FinTech. The cost of a crypto license in Switzerland includes advisory fees to navigate this process efficiently. For a faster and cheaper alternative, consider Panama where our process takes 2-4 weeks for EUR 6,000.

For SRO members, banks may require a minimum deposit of CHF 50,000-100,000 and charge monthly fees of CHF 500-2,000. The cost of a crypto license in Switzerland should include a banking setup budget of CHF 5,000-15,000 for legal support. Compliance and Trust

Swiss compliance requirements are rigorous. You must implement AML policies, conduct customer due diligence, screen for sanctions, report suspicious transactions, and maintain records for 10 years. For FinTech licensees, additional prudential requirements apply, including capital adequacy and liquidity. The cost of a crypto license in Switzerland includes ongoing compliance costs: hiring a compliance officer (CHF 80,000-120,000 per year) or outsourcing to a compliance firm (CHF 20,000-50,000 per year). This is general guidance, not legal advice. Always consult with a Swiss attorney for your specific situation. For example, a small SRO member might outsource compliance for CHF 15,000 per year, while a FinTech licensee might need an in-house officer costing CHF 100,000 plus benefits.

Common Mistakes When Applying

  • Underestimating timeline: Many founders expect SRO approval in weeks; reality is 2-4 months.
  • Insufficient AML documentation: SROs require detailed policies; generic templates are rejected.
  • Choosing the wrong SRO: Some SROs have higher capital requirements or stricter oversight.
  • Ignoring tax structuring: Not planning for cantonal taxes can lead to higher effective rates.
  • Neglecting banking: Assuming account opening is easy; it often takes months.

Frequently asked questions

What is the total cost of a crypto license in Switzerland for a startup?

For a startup using the SRO route, total first-year costs range from CHF 27,000 to CHF 110,000, including application fees, legal advisory, and capital. This is significantly higher than Panama's flat EUR 6,000. Consulting24 recommends comparing your budget and timeline before choosing.

Do I need a Swiss crypto license if I only serve non-Swiss clients?

If your company is based in Switzerland or you have a branch there, you likely need SRO membership even if clients are abroad. FINMA applies a territorial principle. However, if you operate remotely from Panama, no Swiss license is needed.

How long does it take to get a crypto license in Switzerland?

SRO membership takes 2-4 months, while a FinTech license takes 6-12 months. Delays often occur due to incomplete documentation. Consulting24 can help streamline the process, but Panama remains faster at 2-4 weeks.

Can I use a Swiss SRO membership to passport services into the EU?

No. Switzerland is not an EU member, so SRO membership does not provide MiCA passporting. If you need EU-wide access, consider a license in Lithuania or Estonia, which are EU members and follow MiCA.

What is the minimum capital for a Swiss crypto license?

For SRO membership, no statutory minimum, but SROs typically require CHF 50,000-100,000. For FinTech license, CHF 300,000. For banking license, CHF 10 million. Panama has no capital requirement.

Is Switzerland tax-friendly for crypto businesses?

Yes, Switzerland offers no capital gains tax for individuals and low corporate tax (12-20%). However, wealth tax and stamp duty may apply. Panama's territorial tax system may be more favourable for international income.

What activities are allowed under a Swiss SRO membership?

Exchange, transfer, custody, and trading platform operation (without deposit-taking). For deposit-taking or lending, you need a FinTech or banking license. Consulting24 can advise on the right scope.

Can I open a bank account for a Swiss crypto company?

Yes, but it is challenging. Banks like Sygnum and SEBA serve crypto firms, but require minimum deposits and compliance checks. Budget CHF 5,000-15,000 for legal support. Panama offers easier banking.

What are the ongoing compliance costs for a Swiss crypto license?

Annual SRO fees of CHF 2,000-10,000, plus compliance officer costs (CHF 20,000-120,000 per year). For FinTech, additional audit costs of CHF 10,000-30,000. These are higher than Panama's minimal ongoing costs.

How does Switzerland compare to Panama for crypto licensing?

Switzerland offers prestige and regulatory clarity but at high cost (EUR 25,000+ first year) and longer timeline. Panama costs a flat EUR 6,000, no capital, and 2-4 weeks. For most startups, Panama is more cost-effective.
