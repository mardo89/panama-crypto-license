What is a Spain crypto license?

A Spain crypto license, officially required under the EU Markets in Crypto-Assets Regulation (MiCA), authorises companies to provide crypto-asset services such as exchange, custody, and wallet services. Spain's regulator, the Comisión Nacional del Mercado de Valores (CNMV), oversees compliance with MiCA. The license is mandatory for any entity offering crypto services to Spanish residents, whether based in Spain or elsewhere in the EU via passporting.

Unlike the old registration system, MiCA imposes strict capital requirements, governance standards, and anti-money laundering (AML) obligations. The license is not a simple registration; it requires a full application demonstrating operational readiness, financial soundness, and compliance with EU consumer protection rules. For example, you must submit a detailed business plan, risk assessment, and AML policies. The CNMV also conducts background checks on all beneficial owners and management. Failure to meet these standards can lead to rejection or long delays. Many founders mistakenly think the process is similar to the pre-MiCA VASP register, but the bar is much higher now.

Who needs a Spain crypto license?

Any company that provides crypto-asset services to clients in Spain must hold a license. This includes crypto exchanges (both fiat-to-crypto and crypto-to-crypto), custodial wallet providers, platforms for token issuance, and brokers. If you are a startup or an established firm planning to offer these services, you need the license.

Foreign companies can also apply, but they must establish a physical presence in Spain or passport their license from another EU member state. Many founders mistakenly believe they can operate without a license if they are based outside Spain, but the territorial scope of MiCA captures any service directed at Spanish residents. For instance, if your website targets Spanish users or you advertise in Spain, you are likely caught. A common mistake is assuming that using a Spanish agent or a simple registration is enough; it is not. You need a fully licensed entity with substance in Spain, including a registered office, local management, and operational staff.

License type and regulator

The license is a MiCA Crypto-Asset Service Provider (CASP) license, regulated by the CNMV. Spain has not opted for additional national requirements beyond MiCA, but the CNMV has issued guidance on application procedures. The license covers several service classes, each with specific capital requirements:

  • Class 1: Exchange services (EUR 50,000)
  • Class 2: Custody and wallet services (EUR 125,000)
  • Class 3: Combined services or those with higher risk (EUR 150,000)

It is crucial to identify which classes apply to your business model, as applying for the wrong class can lead to rejection or additional costs. For example, if you offer both exchange and custody, you must apply for Class 3 with EUR 150,000 capital. A common mistake is applying for Class 1 and later adding custody, which forces a new application. The CNMV also requires that the license covers all services you intend to offer; you cannot add services later without approval. Additionally, the regulator expects that your business model is clearly defined and that you have the operational capacity to deliver each service.

Capital requirement

Under MiCA, the minimum capital requirement for a Spain crypto license is EUR 50,000 for exchange services, EUR 125,000 for custody, and EUR 150,000 for combined services. This capital must be maintained at all times and cannot be used for operational expenses. It is a common mistake to treat this as a deposit that can be withdrawn after approval; it is a permanent requirement.

Founders often miscalculate the amount needed, especially if they plan to offer multiple services. For example, offering both exchange and custody requires the higher tier. Additionally, the capital must be in liquid assets, typically cash or equivalents, and must be held in a Spanish bank account or an EU institution. Some founders try to use cryptocurrency as capital, but that is not accepted. The CNMV also requires that the capital is unencumbered and freely available. A mistake is to borrow the capital temporarily; the regulator will check the source of funds and may require proof that the capital is your own. If you plan to use the capital for operational expenses after approval, you cannot; it must remain as a reserve.

Tax treatment

Spain has a complex tax regime for crypto assets. Corporate income tax is 25%, and capital gains from crypto trading are taxed as income. VAT is not applicable on crypto transactions, but services like wallet provision may be subject to VAT. Personal income tax for individuals can be up to 47% for high earners.

For licensed companies, it is essential to register with the Spanish tax authorities and comply with reporting obligations. Many founders overlook the need for a local tax advisor, leading to penalties. Spain also has a wealth tax on crypto holdings above EUR 700,000, which can affect founders personally. A common mistake is not understanding the tax implications of token rewards or staking income. For example, if your company earns staking rewards, those are taxable as income at the corporate rate. Additionally, Spain has strict transfer pricing rules for related-party transactions, so if you have a parent company abroad, you must document arm's length pricing. Consulting with a Spanish tax specialist is highly recommended.

Allowed activities

A Spain crypto license permits the following activities under MiCA:

  • Exchange of crypto assets for fiat currency
  • Exchange of crypto assets for other crypto assets
  • Custody and administration of crypto assets on behalf of clients
  • Operation of a trading platform
  • Execution of orders on behalf of clients
  • Placement of crypto assets
  • Transfer services for crypto assets

Activities not covered include issuing stablecoins (which require a separate e-money license) and decentralised finance (DeFi) protocols that are fully automated. Founders often assume DeFi is exempt, but if you have control or profit from the protocol, you may need a license. For example, if you develop a DeFi platform that charges fees or has admin keys, the CNMV may consider it a regulated service. Another common mistake is offering unlicensed services like crypto lending or yield farming without a proper license. Always check with a legal expert whether your specific activities fall under MiCA.

Step-by-step application process

  1. Pre-application preparation: Define your business model, identify service classes, and prepare documentation (business plan, AML policies, risk assessment).
  2. Company incorporation: Register a Spanish company (S.L. or S.A.) with a registered office in Spain.
  3. Capital deposit: Open a Spanish bank account and deposit the required capital.
  4. Submit application: File with the CNMV, including all required documents. The CNMV has 3 months to review.
  5. Review and interview: The CNMV may request additional information or an interview with management.
  6. Approval: If successful, you receive the license. The entire process takes 4-9 months.

Common mistakes include incomplete documentation and underestimating the time needed for bank account opening. For example, many founders submit a business plan that is too generic; the CNMV expects a detailed plan with financial projections, market analysis, and operational processes. Another mistake is not having a local compliance officer in place before applying. The CNMV may request an interview with your compliance officer, and if they are not prepared, it can delay the process. Also, ensure that all documents are translated into Spanish by a certified translator.

Banking and payments

Opening a bank account for a crypto company in Spain is notoriously difficult. Many banks refuse to serve crypto businesses due to perceived risk. Founders often fail to secure a bank account before applying, which is a critical requirement. Options include traditional banks like BBVA or Santander, but they require extensive compliance checks. Alternatively, payment institutions or electronic money institutions may provide services.

It is advisable to start the banking process early, sometimes 2-3 months before the application. Consulting24 can assist with introductions to crypto-friendly banks. For comparison, in Panama, banking is more accessible, and the flat fee includes company setup with banking support. A common mistake is assuming that a bank account from another EU country is sufficient; the CNMV prefers a Spanish account to demonstrate local presence. Also, some founders try to use a digital bank like N26, but these may not meet the CNMV's requirements for a corporate account. It is best to use a bank with a physical branch in Spain.

Benefits of a Spain crypto license

Despite the complexity, a Spain crypto license offers significant advantages. It provides passporting rights across the entire EU, allowing you to serve 450 million consumers without additional licenses. Spain has a growing crypto ecosystem, with access to talent and a supportive regulatory environment. The CNMV is considered pragmatic and transparent.

Additionally, being licensed enhances credibility with investors, partners, and customers. It also ensures compliance with EU anti-money laundering directives, reducing legal risk. For companies targeting the European market, Spain is a strong choice, though the cost is higher than some alternatives like Panama. However, if you need EU passporting, Spain is one of the more affordable options compared to other EU jurisdictions like Malta or Cyprus. The license also allows you to apply for a Spanish fintech sandbox, which can provide regulatory relief for innovative products.

Compliance and trust

Ongoing compliance is mandatory. You must appoint a compliance officer, conduct regular AML audits, and report suspicious transactions. The CNMV conducts periodic inspections. Failure to comply can result in fines up to EUR 5 million or 5% of annual turnover, and potential license revocation.

Many founders underestimate the compliance burden. It is not a one-time effort; you need continuous monitoring and updates to policies. Consulting24 recommends engaging a local compliance consultant. This is general guidance, not legal advice; consult a qualified lawyer for your specific situation. Common compliance mistakes include not conducting regular staff training on AML, failing to update risk assessments, and not reporting suspicious transactions promptly. The CNMV also expects that you have a strong system for record-keeping, including transaction monitoring and customer due diligence. Some founders try to cut costs by outsourcing compliance to a third party without proper oversight, but the regulator holds the licensee ultimately responsible.

Common mistakes to avoid

  • Incomplete documentation: Missing business plans, AML policies, or risk assessments are the top reasons for rejection.
  • Wrong service class: Applying for the wrong capital tier leads to delays or denial.
  • Ignoring tax obligations: Failing to register for Spanish taxes can result in penalties.
  • Not securing a bank account: Without a local account, you cannot prove capital maintenance.
  • Underestimating timeline: Many founders expect 2-3 months, but 6-9 months is realistic.
  • Lack of substance: The CNMV requires a physical office and local management, not just a mailbox.
  • Inadequate AML procedures: Not having a proper AML framework or using generic templates.
  • Ignoring ongoing compliance: Thinking the license is a one-time effort.

Frequently asked questions

What is the most common mistake when applying for a Spain crypto license?

The most common mistake is submitting incomplete documentation, especially the AML policy and business plan. Founders often use generic templates that do not reflect their specific business model. The CNMV expects detailed, tailored documents that demonstrate operational readiness. Another frequent error is underestimating the time needed for bank account opening, which can delay the entire application.

Can I apply for a Spain crypto license without a local bank account?

No, you must have a Spanish bank account to deposit the required capital and demonstrate financial substance. Without it, the CNMV will reject your application. Opening a bank account for a crypto company in Spain can take 1-3 months, so start early. Some founders try to use an EU account, but the CNMV prefers a Spanish account.

What happens if I apply for the wrong service class?

Applying for the wrong service class can lead to rejection or a request to amend your application, causing delays. For example, if you apply for Class 1 (exchange) but also plan to offer custody, you need Class 3 with higher capital. It is essential to correctly identify all services you intend to offer and apply for the appropriate class from the start.

Is the capital requirement a one-time deposit?

No, the capital must be maintained permanently as a reserve. It cannot be used for operational expenses. Many founders mistakenly think they can withdraw the capital after approval, but the CNMV monitors compliance. If the capital falls below the minimum, you risk license revocation. The capital must be held in liquid assets in a Spanish bank account.

How long does the Spain crypto license application take?

The typical timeline is 4-9 months, but it can extend to 12 months if there are issues with documentation or bank account opening. The CNMV has up to 3 months to review the application, but additional time may be needed for resubmissions. It is important to plan accordingly and not expect a quick process.

Do I need a physical office in Spain?

Yes, the CNMV requires a physical registered office and local management. A virtual office or mailbox is not sufficient. You need a lease agreement and evidence that you have staff in Spain. This is part of the substance requirements under MiCA. Some founders try to use a shared office, but it must be a dedicated space for your business.

Can I passport my Spain license to other EU countries?

Yes, a Spain crypto license allows passporting across the EU under MiCA. You can serve clients in any EU member state without needing additional licenses. However, you must notify the CNMV of your intention to passport and comply with local marketing rules. This is a major advantage of choosing Spain over non-EU jurisdictions like Panama.

What are the ongoing compliance requirements?

Ongoing compliance includes appointing a compliance officer, conducting regular AML audits, reporting suspicious transactions, and updating policies. The CNMV conducts periodic inspections. You must also maintain records of all transactions for at least 5 years. Failure to comply can result in fines up to EUR 5 million or 5% of annual turnover. It is a continuous obligation, not a one-time effort.

Is Spain cheaper than other EU jurisdictions for a crypto license?

Spain is moderately priced compared to other EU jurisdictions. For example, Lithuania has lower upfront costs but higher capital requirements (EUR 125,000). Estonia is also cheaper but has a capital requirement of EUR 100,000. Spain's total cost is higher, but it offers a balanced option with a pragmatic regulator. Panama is much cheaper but does not offer EU passporting.

Can I use a Spanish crypto license for DeFi activities?

It depends. If your DeFi protocol is fully automated and you have no control or profit, it may be exempt. However, if you have admin keys, charge fees, or profit from the protocol, you likely need a license. The CNMV assesses each case individually. It is best to consult with a legal expert to determine if your DeFi activities fall under MiCA.

What happens if my application is rejected?

If your application is rejected, you can appeal the decision or resubmit after addressing the deficiencies. Common reasons for rejection include incomplete documentation, insufficient capital, or lack of substance.
