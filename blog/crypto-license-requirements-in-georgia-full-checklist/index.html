What Is a Crypto License in Georgia?

A crypto license in Georgia is an authorization from the National Bank of Georgia (NBG) to operate as a Virtual Asset Service Provider (VASP). The regime was introduced in 2023 and fully implemented by 2026, replacing earlier ambiguous rules. It covers exchanges, custodial wallets, transfer services, and crypto-to-fiat conversion.

Unlike some jurisdictions that require a physical office, Georgia allows remote management to a degree, but you must have a local registered agent and comply with AML/CFT obligations. The license is valid for an indefinite period, subject to annual compliance reviews.

Key features include a moderate capital requirement (EUR 50,000 for most activities), a clear application timeline of 3-6 months, and a corporate income tax rate of 15% (with a 5% dividend tax). Georgia is not an EU member, so it is not bound by MiCA, but its rules are largely harmonized with FATF recommendations.

Who Needs a Crypto License in Georgia?

Any business offering services related to virtual assets in or from Georgia must obtain a license. This includes:

  • Crypto exchanges (centralized and decentralized platforms operating in Georgia)
  • Wallet providers (custodial and non-custodial if offering key management)
  • Payment processors facilitating crypto-fiat conversions
  • OTC desks and brokerage services
  • Token issuers (if tokens are classified as virtual assets)

If you are a foreign company targeting Georgian clients, you may also need a license unless you operate under a reciprocal recognition agreement (rare). The NBG has indicated it will enforce against unlicensed operators, so compliance is essential.

License Type and Regulator

The sole regulator for crypto activities in Georgia is the National Bank of Georgia (NBG). There is one license type for VASPs, but the scope of permitted activities is defined in your application. The NBG does not issue separate licenses for exchanges vs. wallets; instead, you apply for a VASP license covering specific services.

The legal basis is the Law of Georgia on Combating Money Laundering and Terrorist Financing (as amended in 2023) and NBG regulations. The NBG also supervises compliance with AML/CFT rules, including customer due diligence and transaction monitoring.

Unlike some jurisdictions, Georgia does not have a tiered license system (e.g., Class A/B). However, the capital requirement varies by activity: EUR 50,000 for standard services, EUR 125,000 for services involving higher risk (e.g., custody of large amounts).

Capital Requirement

The minimum capital requirement for a crypto license in Georgia is EUR 50,000 for most VASP activities. If you plan to offer custodial services or handle large transaction volumes, the NBG may require up to EUR 125,000. This capital must be fully paid up before licensing and held in a Georgian bank account.

Capital can be in the form of cash or liquid assets. The NBG does not accept cryptocurrency as capital. You must provide proof of source of funds. For comparison, Lithuania requires EUR 125,000 for exchanges, while Panama has no minimum capital requirement (only EUR 6,000 flat fee).

Tax Treatment

Georgia offers a competitive tax regime for crypto businesses. Corporate income tax is 15% on distributed profits (i.e., you pay tax only when dividends are paid out). Retained earnings are tax-free. Additionally, a 5% dividend withholding tax applies to distributions to non-residents.

VAT is not applicable to crypto transactions (exempt). Personal income tax for employees is 20%. There are no specific crypto taxes beyond standard corporate rules. However, the tax treatment of crypto gains for individuals is still evolving; as of 2026, capital gains from crypto are taxed at 20% for residents.

For comparison, Panama has a territorial tax system (no tax on foreign-source income) and no capital gains tax, making it attractive for international operations. Lithuania applies a 15% corporate tax but with higher capital requirements.

Allowed Activities

Under the Georgian VASP license, you are permitted to:

  • Exchange virtual assets for fiat currency or other virtual assets
  • Provide custodial wallet services
  • Transfer virtual assets on behalf of clients
  • Participate in and provide financial services related to an issuer’s offer or sale of virtual assets
  • Provide advisory services on virtual assets (if included in scope)

Activities not allowed include operating a crypto ATM network without additional licensing, and offering derivatives or margin trading (these may fall under securities regulation). The NBG may also restrict certain tokens (e.g., privacy coins) on a case-by-case basis.

Step-by-Step Application Process

  1. Pre-application preparation: Prepare business plan, AML/KYC policies, risk assessment, and source of funds documentation.
  2. Company incorporation: Register a Georgian limited liability company (LLC) with minimum share capital (usually EUR 1,000). You need a local registered address and director (can be foreign).
  3. Open a bank account: Deposit the required capital (EUR 50,000+) in a Georgian bank and obtain a confirmation letter.
  4. Submit application: File with the NBG including all documents, pay the fee, and appoint a compliance officer.
  5. NBG review: The NBG will assess your application, conduct background checks on beneficial owners, and may request additional information.
  6. Approval and post-licensing: Once approved, you must maintain compliance, submit periodic reports, and undergo annual audits.

Banking and Payment Processing

Opening a bank account in Georgia for a crypto business is moderately challenging but feasible. Major banks like TBC Bank and Bank of Georgia accept VASPs, but they require the license and a detailed business plan. Some banks may also require a minimum deposit of EUR 10,000-20,000.

For payment processing, you can integrate with local payment gateways or use international providers like Stripe (if they support Georgia). Crypto-to-fiat conversion can be done via licensed exchanges or directly through partner banks.

If banking proves difficult, Panama offers a more established banking environment for crypto firms, with no capital requirement and a simpler setup.

Benefits of a Georgia Crypto License

  • Low capital requirement: EUR 50,000 is lower than many EU jurisdictions (e.g., Lithuania EUR 125,000, Malta EUR 730,000).
  • Competitive tax: No tax on retained earnings, only 15% on distributed profits.
  • Fast processing: 3-6 months is average for the region.
  • Favorable location: Georgia is a gateway between Europe and Asia, with a growing crypto-friendly ecosystem.
  • No MiCA constraints: Not subject to EU rules, offering more flexibility in token listing and operations.

However, Georgia's regime is relatively new, and regulatory interpretation may evolve. For a more established framework, consider Lithuania or Estonia, though with higher costs.

Compliance and Ongoing Obligations

Once licensed, you must comply with ongoing AML/CFT obligations, including:

  • Customer due diligence (CDD) for all clients
  • Transaction monitoring and suspicious activity reporting
  • Annual AML audit by an external auditor
  • Reporting to the NBG on a quarterly basis (transaction volumes, client numbers, etc.)
  • Maintaining records for at least 5 years

Failure to comply can result in fines, suspension, or revocation of the license. The NBG conducts periodic inspections. Common Mistakes to Avoid

  1. Underestimating capital requirements: The EUR 50,000 minimum is often not enough to cover operational costs; budget for at least EUR 60,000.
  2. Poor AML documentation: The NBG scrutinizes AML policies; generic templates are often rejected.
  3. Ignoring beneficial ownership rules: All ultimate beneficial owners (UBOs) must be disclosed, even if they hold shares via nominees.
  4. Not securing banking early: Bank account opening can take 2-3 months; start the process in parallel with the license application.
  5. Assuming no tax: Even with retained earnings tax exemption, you must file annual returns; non-compliance leads to penalties.

Frequently asked questions

What is the minimum capital for a crypto license in Georgia?

The minimum capital is EUR 50,000 for most VASP activities. For custodial services or high-volume operations, the NBG may require up to EUR 125,000. Capital must be deposited in a Georgian bank account before licensing.

How long does it take to get a Georgia crypto license?

The typical timeline is 3 to 6 months from application submission. The NBG aims to process within 90 days, but delays can occur if documents are incomplete. Pre-incorporation and bank account opening take additional time.

Can a foreign company apply for a Georgia crypto license?

Yes, but you must incorporate a Georgian LLC first. Foreign directors and shareholders are allowed. You need a local registered address and agent. The beneficial owners must be disclosed.

What are the tax rates for crypto businesses in Georgia?

Corporate income tax is 15% on distributed profits (retained earnings are tax-free). Dividend withholding tax is 5% for non-residents. VAT is exempt for crypto transactions. Personal income tax is 20%.

Is a Georgia crypto license recognized in the EU?

No, Georgia is not an EU member. The license does not automatically grant passporting rights under MiCA. However, it may be accepted by some EU member states for equivalence, but this is not guaranteed.

What activities are allowed under the Georgia VASP license?

Allowed activities include exchange of virtual assets for fiat or other virtual assets, custodial wallet services, transfer services, and participation in token offerings. Derivatives and margin trading are not permitted under this license.

Do I need a physical office in Georgia?

You need a registered office address in Georgia, but a full physical office is not required for the license. However, you must have a local agent and comply with record-keeping requirements.

How does Georgia compare to Panama for crypto licensing?

Panama has no capital requirement, a flat fee of EUR 6,000, and territorial tax (no tax on foreign income). Georgia requires EUR 50,000 capital and taxes distributed profits at 15%. Panama is faster and cheaper, but Georgia offers a more regulated environment.

What are the ongoing compliance obligations?

You must conduct customer due diligence, monitor transactions, report suspicious activities, submit quarterly reports to the NBG, and undergo an annual AML audit. Records must be kept for 5 years.
