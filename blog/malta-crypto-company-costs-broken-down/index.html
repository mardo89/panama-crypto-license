What Is a Malta Crypto Company?

A Malta crypto company is a legal entity registered in Malta that engages in activities related to virtual financial assets (VFAs), such as exchange, wallet services, or investment advice. Malta was an early adopter of crypto regulation through the VFAA, which established three categories: Virtual Financial Assets (VFAs), Electronic Money, and other digital assets. Since 2026, Malta has fully implemented MiCA, meaning that crypto asset service providers (CASPs) must comply with EU-wide rules. However, Malta retains its own national framework for certain activities not covered by MiCA, such as VFA agents and whitepaper approval.

The Malta Financial Services Authority (MFSA) is the regulator. Companies must be licensed as a VFA service provider or a CASP under MiCA. The costs vary depending on the services offered and the chosen license class. For example, a company offering only advisory services will have lower capital and fee requirements than one operating a full exchange with custody. The MFSA also requires the appointment of a VFA Agent, a licensed professional firm that acts as a bridge between the applicant and the regulator. This agent must be engaged before the application is submitted and remains involved throughout the licensing process.

Malta's regime is known for its thoroughness, which translates into higher costs and longer timelines compared to other jurisdictions. For founders who prioritize speed and cost efficiency, alternatives like Panama or Lithuania may be more suitable. License Type & Regulator

The primary regulator is the Malta Financial Services Authority (MFSA). Under the VFAA, the main license is the VFA Service Provider license. With MiCA in force, new applicants can also apply for a CASP license under the EU framework, which is passportable across the EEA. The MFSA has indicated that existing VFA license holders will need to transition to MiCA by a set deadline.

There are three classes of license under MiCA, based on the services provided:

  • Class 1: Services that do not involve holding client funds or assets (e.g., advice, portfolio management). Minimum capital: EUR 50,000.
  • Class 2: Services that involve holding client funds or assets (e.g., exchange, wallet). Minimum capital: EUR 125,000.
  • Class 3: Services that involve dealing on own account or underwriting. Minimum capital: EUR 150,000.

Malta also requires the appointment of a VFA Agent (a licensed professional firm) to assist with the application and ongoing compliance. Agent fees are a significant cost element, typically ranging from EUR 10,000 to EUR 25,000. The VFA Agent conducts due diligence, prepares the application, and liaises with the MFSA. Without an agent, the application cannot proceed. This requirement adds both cost and time but ensures a high standard of compliance.

Capital Requirement

Under MiCA, the minimum capital requirement for a Malta crypto company depends on the services offered. As of 2026, the tiers are:

  • EUR 50,000 for Class 1 (advisory, portfolio management)
  • EUR 125,000 for Class 2 (exchange, wallet, custody)
  • EUR 150,000 for Class 3 (dealing on own account, underwriting)

This capital must be held in liquid assets and is subject to ongoing solvency requirements. The capital is not a fee; it remains on the company's balance sheet. However, it is a significant upfront cost that must be considered. For example, a Class 2 exchange must have EUR 125,000 in liquid assets from day one, which can be a barrier for early-stage startups. In contrast, Panama has no minimum capital requirement for its crypto license. Additionally, Malta requires that the capital be maintained at all times; if the company incurs losses that reduce capital below the threshold, the MFSA may intervene. This can be a concern for volatile crypto businesses.

Tax Treatment

Malta offers a competitive corporate tax system with a standard rate of 35%, but through a full imputation system and tax refunds, the effective rate can be as low as 5% for non-resident shareholders. For crypto companies, the tax treatment of VFA transactions is subject to guidance from the Malta tax authorities. Generally, trading profits are taxable, but capital gains on certain disposals may be exempt. VAT is not applicable to most crypto transactions under current rules.

However, Malta's tax regime is less straightforward than Panama's territorial system, which taxes only locally sourced income and offers 0% tax on offshore profits. Panama also has no capital gains tax. For many crypto founders, Panama is more tax-efficient. For instance, a Malta-based crypto exchange earning profits from global clients would pay an effective rate of 5% after refunds, whereas a Panama-based company would pay 0% on that same income. The Malta system requires annual tax filings and refund applications, which adds administrative overhead. Allowed Activities

A Malta crypto company can engage in a range of activities under the VFA license or MiCA CASP license. These include:

  • Operating a VFA exchange (order book or matching)
  • Providing custody and wallet services
  • Brokering VFA transactions
  • Portfolio management and investment advice
  • Underwriting and placing VFA issuances
  • Transfer services for VFAs

Activities not covered by MiCA, such as decentralized finance (DeFi) protocols or non-custodial services, may still fall under Malta's VFAA. The MFSA has issued guidance on what is regulated. Always check the latest rules. For example, a company offering a non-custodial wallet may not need a license if it does not hold private keys, but if it provides any additional services like exchange or advice, a license may be required. The MFSA also regulates VFA whitepapers for token issuances, which must be approved by a VFA Agent. This adds another layer of cost and compliance for token projects.

Step-by-Step Process

Setting up a Malta crypto company involves several stages. Consulting24 coordinates with local partners for Malta. The typical process is:

  1. Pre-application: Engage a VFA Agent and legal advisor. Prepare business plan, compliance manuals, and risk assessments.
  2. Company formation: Register a Malta company with a minimum share capital (usually EUR 1,200). Appoint directors and a company secretary.
  3. Submit application: File with the MFSA through the VFA Agent. Include all documentation and pay the application fee.
  4. Review and due diligence: MFSA reviews the application, conducts background checks on directors and shareholders. This can take 3-6 months.
  5. License approval: If approved, pay the annual supervision fee and meet capital requirements. License is issued.
  6. Ongoing compliance: Submit regular reports, maintain capital, and comply with AML/CFT obligations.

Total timeline: 6-12 months. For a faster process, consider Lithuania or Panama. The pre-application stage is critical: the VFA Agent will help you draft a detailed business plan that includes financial projections, risk management policies, and AML procedures. Any gaps in documentation can delay the application. The MFSA also requires that key personnel, such as the MLRO and compliance officer, be based in Malta or have a substantive presence there. This may require hiring local staff or contracting with a compliance service provider.

Banking & Payments

Opening a bank account for a Malta crypto company is challenging due to the perceived risk. Many traditional banks are reluctant to serve crypto businesses. Options include:

  • Malta-based banks (e.g., Bank of Valletta, HSBC Malta) but they have strict due diligence.
  • EMI (Electronic Money Institutions) like Revolut Business, Monese, or Wirex.
  • Specialist crypto-friendly banks in other EU jurisdictions (e.g., Lithuania or Estonia).

Consulting24 can assist with introductions to payment partners. For Panama-licensed companies, banking is generally easier, and many international banks accept Panama entities. In Malta, the banking process can take 2-4 months and requires extensive documentation, including the license approval, business plan, and source of funds. Some EMIs offer faster onboarding but may have lower transaction limits. It is advisable to open a bank account in parallel with the license application to avoid delays. For example, a Malta-based exchange may need a EUR-denominated account for fiat on-ramps, which can be obtained through a Lithuanian EMI that is passportable in Malta.

Benefits

Malta offers several advantages for crypto companies:

  • EU passporting: Under MiCA, a Malta license allows you to serve clients across the EEA.
  • Reputation: Malta is a well-regulated jurisdiction with a strong fintech ecosystem.
  • Skilled workforce: Access to English-speaking professionals and a growing crypto talent pool.
  • Tax efficiency: Effective corporate tax rate as low as 5% for non-residents.

However, these benefits come at a high cost and long timeline. For many startups, Panama offers similar operational benefits at a fraction of the cost. For instance, a Panama-licensed company can also serve global clients without EU passporting, and the tax savings can be reinvested into growth. The choice depends on your target market: if you need to operate within the EU, Malta or Lithuania may be necessary; if you are serving non-EU clients, Panama is more cost-effective.

Compliance & Trust

Malta has a strong AML/CFT framework. Licensees must appoint a Money Laundering Reporting Officer (MLRO), conduct customer due diligence, and report suspicious transactions. The MFSA conducts regular inspections. Non-compliance can result in fines or license revocation.

Common Mistakes

Founders often make these errors when setting up a Malta crypto company:

  • Underestimating timeline: Expect 6-12 months, not 3.
  • Choosing the wrong license class: Picking a higher capital class than needed increases costs.
  • Ignoring VFA agent costs: Agent fees can be EUR 20,000+.
  • Poor documentation: Business plans and compliance manuals must be detailed.
  • Not planning for banking: Start the banking process early.

Avoid these by working with experienced advisors. Consulting24 can guide you through the process. Some founders also fail to budget for the annual supervision fee and VFA agent renewal fees, which can add EUR 10,000-15,000 per year. Finally, not understanding the tax implications can lead to unexpected liabilities. Always consult with a tax advisor familiar with Malta's imputation system.

Yes, under MiCA, a Malta CASP license is passportable across the EEA. This means you can serve clients in other EU member states without needing additional licenses. However, you must comply with local marketing rules and notify the MFSA of your passporting intentions.
