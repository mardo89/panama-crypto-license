What Is the Estonia Crypto License?

The Estonia crypto license is a regulatory authorisation under the Virtual Asset Service Provider (VASP) regime, now aligned with MiCA. It allows companies to offer crypto-to-fiat exchange, crypto-to-crypto exchange, wallet custody, and transfer services. The license is issued by the Financial Intelligence Unit (FIU) and, under MiCA, by the Financial Supervision Authority (FSA) for certain activities.

Estonia's e-Residency program makes it possible to apply remotely, but you must have a physical presence in Estonia (a registered office and a local contact). The license is valid across the EU under the MiCA passport. For example, a company licensed in Estonia can serve customers in Germany, France, or Spain without needing additional local authorisation, provided it notifies the host regulator. This passporting right is a key advantage over non-EU jurisdictions like Panama, which does not offer EU-wide recognition.

Under MiCA, the license is formally called a Crypto-Asset Service Provider (CASP) authorization. It covers both AML/CTF supervision and prudential requirements. The dual-regulator system means you must satisfy the FIU on AML grounds and the FSA on capital, governance, and consumer protection. This structure is similar to Lithuania but with a slightly different split of responsibilities. Estonia's digital infrastructure, including e-Residency and X-Road, streamlines many administrative steps, making it one of the most efficient EU jurisdictions for licensing.

Who Needs an Estonia Crypto License?

Any business that provides virtual asset services to customers in Estonia or the EU generally needs a license. This includes:

  • Cryptocurrency exchanges (centralised and decentralised if offering custody)
  • Wallet providers (custodial)
  • Payment processors using crypto
  • Brokers and dealers in virtual assets
  • Any entity that transfers virtual assets on behalf of clients

If you are a startup, a fintech expanding into crypto, or an established exchange seeking EU passporting, Estonia is a strong choice. For a comparison with other options, see our jurisdictions page. Even if your target market is outside the EU, Estonia can serve as a regulatory anchor for credibility. For example, a crypto exchange based in Asia may choose Estonia to demonstrate compliance with EU standards, which can help attract European investors and partners.

Entities that do not need a license include those offering non-custodial wallets (where the provider never holds private keys), pure software developers, and miners. However, if you offer any service that involves holding or transferring client assets, you likely fall under the VASP definition. Always confirm with a compliance expert, as the line can be blurry. For instance, a staking service that takes custody of tokens would require a license, while a non-custodial staking pool may not.

License Type and Regulator

The license is a Virtual Asset Service Provider (VASP) license. Under MiCA, it is categorised as a CASP (Crypto-Asset Service Provider). The primary regulator is the Financial Intelligence Unit (FIU) for AML/CTF supervision, while the Financial Supervision Authority (FSA) oversees prudential requirements and market conduct under MiCA.

Key points:

  • Single license for all crypto services
  • Passportable to other EU member states
  • Subject to ongoing AML and reporting obligations
  • The FIU conducts on-site inspections and can impose fines for non-compliance

The dual-regulator structure means you must satisfy both AML and prudential standards. The FIU focuses on customer due diligence, transaction monitoring, and suspicious activity reporting. The FSA focuses on capital adequacy, governance, and consumer protection. This is similar to Lithuania but with a slightly different split of responsibilities. In practice, you will submit one application that is reviewed by both bodies. The FIU typically takes the lead on initial assessment, while the FSA may conduct additional checks on financial soundness.

Under MiCA, the FSA also has the power to approve white papers for asset-referenced tokens and e-money tokens. If you plan to issue such tokens, you will need a separate approval process. The FSA can also impose sanctions, including fines up to 5% of annual turnover or EUR 5 million, whichever is higher. This underscores the importance of strong compliance from day one.

Capital Requirement

Under MiCA, the minimum capital requirement for CASPs in Estonia is tiered by service type:

  • EUR 50,000 for exchange and wallet services (basic)
  • EUR 125,000 for custody of client assets
  • EUR 150,000 for dealing on own account or underwriting

These amounts must be fully paid up and maintained. The capital must be in liquid assets (cash or equivalents) and cannot be borrowed. For comparison, Panama has no minimum capital requirement, making it a lower-cost alternative. However, the capital in Estonia serves as a buffer for operational risks and is a sign of seriousness to regulators and partners.

If your business model includes multiple service types, you must meet the highest applicable tier. For example, if you offer both exchange and custody services, you need EUR 125,000. The capital can be held in a bank account or in highly liquid securities, but it must be unencumbered. You must also maintain the capital at all times; if it falls below the threshold, you must notify the FSA immediately. Some companies choose to hold excess capital to demonstrate financial strength, which can help with banking relationships.

Tax Treatment

Estonia has a unique corporate tax system: 0% tax on retained profits, and 20% (or 14% for regular dividends) on distributed profits. This applies to crypto companies as well. VAT is generally 20%, but crypto transactions may be exempt depending on the service (e.g., exchange of crypto for fiat is VAT-exempt in many cases).

Personal income tax for employees is 20%. There are no withholding taxes on dividends paid to non-residents. This makes Estonia tax-efficient for growth-stage companies that reinvest profits. For example, a crypto exchange that earns EUR 1 million in profit and reinvests it pays 0% corporate tax. Only when dividends are distributed does tax apply. This is more favourable than Lithuania's 15% corporate tax or the 12.5% in some other EU countries.

However, the tax treatment of crypto-to-crypto trades can be complex. The Estonian Tax and Customs Board generally treats crypto as a means of payment, so gains from crypto-to-crypto exchanges may be taxable as business income. It is advisable to consult a tax advisor for your specific activities. Estonia also has a thin capitalisation rule that limits interest deductions, but this rarely affects crypto startups. Allowed Activities

A VASP license in Estonia covers:

  • Exchange between virtual assets and fiat currencies
  • Exchange between one or more virtual assets
  • Transfer of virtual assets
  • Custody and administration of virtual assets
  • Participation in and provision of financial services related to an issuer's offer or sale of virtual assets

Activities not covered: operating a crypto mining pool, developing software, or providing non-custodial wallets (unless they involve transfer services). If you plan to offer staking or lending, these may require additional authorisation under MiCA or national laws. Always check with a compliance expert.

Under MiCA, the definition of virtual assets is broad and includes all crypto-assets except those that qualify as financial instruments (e.g., security tokens) or e-money. If your token is deemed a security, you may need a MiFID license instead. The FSA can provide guidance on classification. For example, a stablecoin pegged to a fiat currency may be considered e-money and require an e-money license. Estonia's FIU has a pre-screening service where you can submit your business model for a preliminary opinion on whether a license is needed.

Step-by-Step Process

  1. Pre-incorporation: Choose a company name, prepare documents (business plan, AML policy, risk assessment). Engage a local compliance officer. The business plan should detail your target market, revenue model, and risk management framework.
  2. Company incorporation: Register a private limited company (OÜ) with a minimum share capital of EUR 2,500 (or higher if required by capital tier). Obtain an e-Residency card if applying remotely. The registration can be done online via the e-Business Register.
  3. Open a bank account: In Estonia or another EU country. Many banks require a license before opening, so a payment institution account may be needed initially. LHV Bank is known to be crypto-friendly. You may also consider EMI licenses for fiat services.
  4. Prepare the application: Draft AML procedures, internal controls, business continuity plan, and capital proof. Engage a local compliance officer. The AML policy must include customer due diligence procedures, transaction monitoring thresholds, and suspicious activity reporting protocols.
  5. Submit to FIU: File the application via the FIU portal, pay the state fee, and provide all supporting documents. The FIU may request additional information or conduct an interview. Ensure all documents are in English or Estonian.
  6. Review and interview: Typical review time is 1-3 months. Be prepared to explain your business model and AML controls. The FIU may ask for clarifications on your risk assessment or source of funds.
  7. Approval and ongoing compliance: Once approved, you must submit annual reports, maintain AML training, and undergo periodic audits. The FSA may also conduct on-site inspections. You must also register with the FSA for prudential supervision.

Banking and Payments

Opening a bank account for a crypto company in Estonia can be challenging. Traditional banks like Swedbank or SEB often require a license and a track record. Alternative options include:

  • Payment institutions (e.g., Monese, PaySera)
  • EMI licenses (if you also offer fiat services)
  • Specialised crypto-friendly banks (e.g., LHV Bank in Estonia)
  • Neobanks like Revolut Business (though they may restrict crypto-related transactions)

Compliance is critical. You must appoint a local AML officer, conduct ongoing monitoring, and file suspicious transaction reports. Under MiCA, additional requirements include safeguarding client assets and maintaining insurance (e.g., professional indemnity or cyber insurance). The FIU and FSA have the power to impose fines, revoke licenses, or even refer cases for criminal prosecution.

Common Mistakes

  • Underestimating capital: Ensure you have the required capital ready before applying. Some applicants try to use borrowed funds, which is not allowed. The capital must be in the company's bank account and fully paid up.
  • Poor AML documentation: The FIU scrutinises AML policies; use templates from a compliance expert. Generic policies from the internet are often rejected. Your AML policy must be tailored to your business model and risk profile.
  • No local presence: You need a registered office and a local contact person. Virtual offices may not satisfy the requirement. The local contact must be an employee or director, not just a service provider.
  • Ignoring MiCA: The regime is fully in force; follow the new rules. Some older guides still reference the old VASP regime. Ensure your application complies with MiCA's capital, governance, and consumer protection requirements.
  • Choosing the wrong jurisdiction: Compare with Panama if you want a lower-cost option, or with Lithuania if you prefer a faster timeline. Also consider Czech Republic or Poland for alternative EU bases.
  • Delaying bank account opening: Start the bank account process early, as it can take weeks. Some banks require the license to be approved first, creating a chicken-and-egg problem. A payment institution account can serve as a bridge.

By avoiding these pitfalls, you can streamline the application process and reduce the risk of rejection. Consulting24 has seen many applicants fail due to these issues; we help you prepare a complete and compliant application.

Frequently asked questions

What is the minimum capital for a crypto license in Estonia under MiCA?

Under MiCA, the minimum capital is tiered: EUR 50,000 for exchange and wallet services, EUR 125,000 for custody, and EUR 150,000 for dealing on own account. You must meet the highest tier applicable to your services. The capital must be fully paid up in liquid assets and cannot be borrowed.

How long does it take to get an Estonia crypto license in 2026?

The typical timeline is 3-6 months from application submission to approval. This includes company incorporation (1-2 weeks), document preparation (1-2 months), FIU review (1-3 months), and bank account opening (2-4 weeks). Complex cases or incomplete applications can take longer.

Can I apply for an Estonia crypto license remotely?

Yes, through Estonia's e-Residency program you can apply remotely. However, you must have a physical presence in Estonia, including a registered office and a local contact person (e.g., a director or employee). Consulting24 can help you set up the local presence.

What is the tax rate for crypto companies in Estonia?

Estonia has a 0% corporate tax on retained profits. Tax is only due when profits are distributed as dividends (20% or 14% for regular dividends). VAT is 20%, but crypto-to-fiat exchanges are often VAT-exempt. Personal income tax is 20%.

Is the Estonia crypto license valid across the EU?

Yes, under MiCA, the license is passportable to all EU member states. You can serve customers in other EU countries without needing additional licenses, but you must notify the host regulator. This is a key advantage over non-EU jurisdictions like Panama.

What activities are covered by the Estonia crypto license?

The license covers exchange between crypto and fiat, crypto-to-crypto exchange, transfer of virtual assets, custody, and participation in token offerings. It does not cover mining, software development, or non-custodial wallets. Staking and lending may require additional authorisation.

How much does the Estonia crypto license cost in total?

What are the main compliance requirements after obtaining the license?

You must appoint a local AML officer, conduct ongoing transaction monitoring, file suspicious activity reports, and submit annual AML audits. Under MiCA, you also need to safeguard client assets and maintain insurance. The FIU and FSA conduct periodic inspections.

Can I use a virtual office for the Estonia license?

No, the FIU requires a physical registered office in Estonia. Virtual offices that do not provide a physical address are not accepted. You also need a local contact person who is available during business hours. Consulting24 can arrange a physical office space.

How does Estonia compare to Panama for crypto licensing?

Estonia offers EU passporting and 0% tax on retained profits but requires EUR 50,000+ capital and costs EUR 10,000-15,000. Panama has no capital requirement, a flat fee of EUR 6,000, and faster processing (2-4 months), but no EU passport. Choose based on your target market.
