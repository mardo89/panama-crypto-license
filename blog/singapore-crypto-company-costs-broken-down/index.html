What Is a Singapore Crypto Company?

A Singapore crypto company is typically a business that provides digital payment token (DPT) services, such as trading, exchange, or custody, and is regulated under the Payment Services Act (PSA). The Monetary Authority of Singapore (MAS) requires such firms to hold a Major Payment Institution (MPI) license. This license covers activities like buying, selling, and facilitating the exchange of DPTs. The costs involved include application fees, compliance setup, and ongoing regulatory costs. For a detailed comparison of licensing costs across jurisdictions, see our jurisdictions page.

Singapore's regulatory environment is considered one of the most stringent in Asia, but it also offers credibility and access to a sophisticated financial market. The MPI license is designed for firms that exceed certain transaction thresholds, making it the standard for most crypto businesses. Smaller players may consider the Standard Payment Institution (SPI) license, but its lower thresholds (e.g., monthly transaction volume under SGD 3 million) often make it unsuitable for growth-oriented crypto firms. As of 2026, MAS has further tightened requirements, including enhanced AML/CFT controls and mandatory cybersecurity audits, adding to the cost burden.

Who Needs a Singapore Crypto License?

Any business that provides any of the following services in Singapore must apply for an MPI license under the PSA:

  • Digital payment token exchange (e.g., crypto-to-crypto or fiat-to-crypto trading)
  • Digital payment token custody
  • Cross-border money transfer involving DPTs
  • Merchant acquisition for DPTs

If you are a small startup with low transaction volumes, you may qualify for a smaller license, but the MPI license is the standard for most crypto firms. Exemptions exist for limited operations, but the MAS is tightening requirements. For example, firms processing less than SGD 3 million per month in DPT transactions may apply for an SPI license, but this still requires compliance with AML/CFT rules and a minimum capital of SGD 100,000. However, most crypto exchanges and custodians quickly exceed these thresholds, making the MPI license inevitable. If your target market is outside Asia, a jurisdiction like Panama may be more cost-effective, with no minimum capital and a flat EUR 6,000 setup fee.

License Type and Regulator

The regulator is the Monetary Authority of Singapore (MAS). The relevant license is the Major Payment Institution (MPI) license under the Payment Services Act (PSA). There is also a smaller Standard Payment Institution (SPI) license, but it has lower thresholds and is rarely used for crypto. The MPI license allows firms to provide multiple payment services, including DPT services, without individual approvals for each service. MAS is known for its rigorous due diligence and high compliance standards.

MAS requires that all MPI license holders have a physical office in Singapore, at least one executive director who is a Singapore resident, and a compliance officer with relevant experience. The regulator also conducts on-site inspections and may request additional information during the application process. Compared to other jurisdictions, Singapore's regulatory approach is more hands-on, which can be both a benefit (for credibility) and a drawback (for cost and time). Capital Requirement

For an MPI license that includes digital payment token services, the base capital requirement is SGD 250,000 (approximately EUR 170,000). This must be held in liquid assets and cannot be used for operational expenses. If the firm handles more than SGD 5 million in monthly transaction volume, higher capital may be required. This is a significant barrier for startups compared to jurisdictions like Estonia (EUR 12,000) or Panama (no minimum capital). For more on capital requirements across jurisdictions, see our requirements page.

MAS also requires that the capital be maintained as a safeguard against operational risks. In practice, many applicants choose to hold the capital in a fixed deposit or government bonds. The capital requirement is one of the main reasons why Singapore is not suitable for early-stage crypto ventures with limited funding. In contrast, Panama's zero capital requirement allows founders to allocate more resources to product development and marketing. If you are considering Singapore but are concerned about capital, we recommend exploring alternatives like Lithuania or Panama first.

Tax Treatment for Crypto Companies in Singapore

Singapore has a territorial tax system. Corporate income tax is a flat 17% on profits. There is no capital gains tax, which is favourable for crypto trading profits. However, the Inland Revenue Authority of Singapore (IRAS) treats crypto as intangible property, so transactions may be subject to goods and services tax (GST) if they involve fiat or other goods. For most crypto exchanges, the main tax is on net trading income. Startups may qualify for tax exemptions on the first SGD 100,000 of chargeable income for the first three years. In contrast, Panama has no corporate income tax on offshore income and no capital gains tax, making it a zero-tax jurisdiction for many crypto businesses.

It is important to note that Singapore's tax treaties and its reputation as a compliant jurisdiction can be advantageous for firms dealing with international partners. However, the effective tax rate after exemptions can still be around 8-10% for profitable startups. For a detailed comparison of tax treatments, see our blog on crypto taxation. Allowed Activities Under the MPI License

With an MPI license, a Singapore crypto company can:

  • Operate a digital payment token exchange (buying, selling, swapping)
  • Provide custodial services for DPTs
  • Facilitate cross-border money transfers involving DPTs
  • Accept DPTs as payment for goods and services (merchant acquisition)

Activities not allowed include offering derivatives or securities tokens without additional licensing under the Securities and Futures Act. The MPI license does not cover decentralised finance (DeFi) protocols unless they are operated by a central entity. For a broader view of allowed activities in other jurisdictions, see our exchange license page.

MAS has also clarified that stablecoin issuance may require a separate license under the proposed stablecoin framework. As of 2026, the regulatory landscape for stablecoins is still evolving, but firms dealing with payment stablecoins must comply with additional reserve and disclosure requirements. If your business involves stablecoins, you should factor in these extra compliance costs. For most crypto exchanges, the MPI license provides sufficient scope to operate legally in Singapore.

Step-by-Step Application Process

The MAS application process is rigorous. Here are the key steps:

  1. Pre-incorporation planning: Define business model, services, and target market. Engage a local corporate secretarial firm.
  2. Company incorporation: Register a private limited company with ACRA (Accounting and Corporate Regulatory Authority).
  3. Prepare compliance documents: Draft AML/CFT policies, risk assessment, business continuity plan, and audit framework. These must align with MAS guidelines.
  4. Appoint key personnel: Hire a compliance officer, MLRO, and at least one director who is a Singapore resident (or a locally registered branch).
  5. Submit application to MAS: File the MPI license application via the MAS portal, including all supporting documents and the SGD 10,000 fee.
  6. MAS review and interview: MAS will review the application, may request clarifications, and conduct interviews with management. This can take 6-9 months.
  7. Post-approval: Once approved, the firm must maintain ongoing compliance, submit regular reports, and undergo annual audits.

Banking and Payment Services

Opening a corporate bank account for a Singapore crypto company is challenging. Major banks like DBS, OCBC, and UOB are cautious and often require a strong relationship with the bank, a local director, and a proven track record. Many crypto firms opt for digital banks or payment service providers like Xfers or Liquid Group. Alternatively, some use multi-currency accounts from fintechs like Revolut Business or Wise. It is common for crypto companies to maintain accounts with multiple providers to ensure redundancy. The cost of banking services can range from SGD 500 to SGD 2,000 per month, depending on transaction volumes. For firms with a global client base, having a Singapore bank account can facilitate faster settlement in Asian markets, but the banking hurdles should not be underestimated. Benefits of a Singapore Crypto License

Singapore offers several advantages: a stable political and economic environment, strong rule of law, a sophisticated financial ecosystem, and a strategic location in Asia. The MAS license is recognised globally and can facilitate partnerships with banks and exchanges. Additionally, Singapore has a large pool of talent in blockchain and finance. However, these benefits come at a high cost. For founders with limited budgets, jurisdictions like Panama (EUR 6,000 flat) or Lithuania (EUR 2,500 setup) offer a more affordable entry point. Another key benefit is the ability to access Singapore's extensive network of double tax treaties, which can reduce withholding taxes on cross-border payments. The city-state also has a strong intellectual property protection framework, which is important for crypto firms developing proprietary technology. However, these benefits must be weighed against the high operational costs. Many founders find that a Singapore license is only justified if they have significant revenue or funding to support the ongoing compliance burden. For early-stage startups, Panama or Estonia may be more practical.

Compliance and Ongoing Obligations

Ongoing compliance is a major cost driver. MPI license holders must:

  • Conduct regular AML/CFT training for staff
  • File suspicious transaction reports (STRs) to the Commercial Affairs Department
  • Submit annual audited financial statements to ACRA and MAS
  • Maintain a compliance officer and MLRO
  • Undergo periodic MAS inspections

These obligations require a dedicated compliance team or outsourced service, costing SGD 50,000-100,000 annually. This is general guidance, not legal advice. Always consult a local lawyer for your specific situation.

In addition, MAS requires licensees to implement transaction monitoring systems that can detect unusual patterns. The cost of such software can range from SGD 10,000 to SGD 50,000 per year, depending on the complexity of the business. Failure to comply with AML/CFT requirements can result in fines or license revocation. For a jurisdiction with lower compliance costs, consider Panama, where the ongoing obligations are minimal. Common Mistakes When Applying for a Singapore Crypto License

Founders often make these errors:

  • Underestimating the capital requirement: Many assume SGD 250,000 is enough, but MAS may require more based on transaction volume.
  • Poorly drafted AML policies: MAS expects detailed, customised policies, not generic templates.
  • Lack of local presence: MAS requires at least one local director and a physical office. Using a virtual office may be rejected.
  • Ignoring tax implications: Not accounting for GST on fees can lead to penalties.
  • Choosing Singapore when a smaller jurisdiction suffices: If your target market is not Asia, Panama or Estonia may be more cost-effective.

Frequently asked questions

What is the total cost of setting up a crypto company in Singapore in 2026?

The total first-year cost, excluding the SGD 250,000 capital requirement, is approximately SGD 350,000 to SGD 500,000. This includes incorporation (SGD 1,000-3,000), MAS application fee (SGD 10,000), legal and compliance setup (SGD 30,000-80,000), and annual compliance costs (SGD 50,000-100,000). Exact figures depend on the complexity of your business.

What is the minimum capital requirement for an MPI license in Singapore?

The base capital requirement is SGD 250,000 (approximately EUR 170,000) for an MPI license that includes digital payment token services. This must be held in liquid assets and cannot be used for operational expenses. Higher capital may be required if monthly transaction volume exceeds SGD 5 million.

How long does it take to get a Singapore crypto license?

The MAS application process typically takes 6 to 12 months. This includes company incorporation, compliance documentation, and MAS review. Delays are common if the application is incomplete or if MAS requests additional information. Planning for at least 8 months is advisable.

Can I use a virtual office for my Singapore crypto company?

No. MAS requires MPI license holders to have a physical office in Singapore. A virtual office or shared workspace without a dedicated space is generally not accepted. You must have a registered address and a physical presence where records are kept and staff work.

What are the tax rates for crypto companies in Singapore?

Corporate income tax is a flat 17% on profits. There is no capital gains tax. Startups may qualify for tax exemptions on the first SGD 100,000 of chargeable income for the first three years. GST may apply on fees charged to customers, depending on the nature of the transaction.

Is Singapore a good jurisdiction for a crypto startup with limited funding?

Generally no. The high capital requirement (SGD 250,000) and ongoing compliance costs (SGD 50,000-100,000 annually) make Singapore more suitable for well-funded firms. Startups with limited budgets should consider Panama (EUR 6,000 flat, no capital) or Estonia (EUR 12,000 capital) as alternatives.

What activities are allowed under the Singapore MPI license?

The MPI license allows digital payment token exchange (buying, selling, swapping), custodial services for DPTs, cross-border money transfers involving DPTs, and merchant acquisition for DPTs. It does not cover securities tokens or derivatives without additional licensing.

Do I need a local director for my Singapore crypto company?

Yes. MAS requires at least one executive director who is a Singapore resident (citizen, permanent resident, or Employment Pass holder). The director must be actively involved in the management of the company and pass MAS's fit and proper criteria.

How does Singapore compare to Panama for crypto licensing?

Singapore is more expensive (SGD 350,000+ vs EUR 6,000 flat) and slower (6-12 months vs 2-4 weeks). Panama has no minimum capital and zero corporate tax on offshore income. Singapore offers greater credibility in Asia, but Panama is more cost-effective for most startups.
