What Is a Crypto License in Georgia?

A crypto license in Georgia is an authorization issued by the National Bank of Georgia (NBG) that allows companies to provide virtual asset services. These services include exchanging cryptocurrencies for fiat or other digital assets, operating a crypto exchange, and custodial wallet services. The license is mandatory for any business offering such services to Georgian residents or from Georgian territory.

The NBG introduced the licensing regime in 2023, and by 2026 it is fully enforced. The regime aligns with international standards, particularly the Financial Action Task Force (FATF) recommendations on AML/CFT. Georgia's approach is similar to the EU's MiCA framework but tailored to its local market, making it a viable option for companies seeking a regulated environment without the higher costs of some EU jurisdictions.

Key features include a relatively low minimum capital requirement (compared to EU states), a transparent application process, and a tax-friendly corporate income tax system. The license is valid for an indefinite period, subject to ongoing compliance.

Who Needs a Crypto License in Georgia?

Any legal entity that intends to provide virtual asset services in or from Georgia must obtain a license from the NBG. This includes:

  • Crypto exchanges (centralized and decentralized platforms operating as a business)
  • Peer-to-peer trading platforms that match buyers and sellers
  • Cryptocurrency wallet providers (custodial wallets)
  • Fiat-to-crypto and crypto-to-fiat conversion services
  • Payment processors that facilitate crypto transactions
  • ATMs and kiosks that exchange virtual assets

The requirement applies to both domestic companies and foreign entities that target Georgian customers. If you are a crypto founder looking to serve the Georgian market or use Georgia as a base for regional operations, you fall under this requirement. For businesses that only offer services to non-residents and have no physical presence in Georgia, it is advisable to seek legal advice, as the NBG may still assert jurisdiction if the company is managed from Georgia.

License Type and Regulator

The regulator is the National Bank of Georgia (NBG), which acts as the central bank and financial supervisor. The NBG issues a single type of license for virtual asset service providers (VASPs). Unlike some jurisdictions that have multiple tiers (e.g., exchange license vs. custodian wallet license), Georgia's license covers all VASP activities under one authorization. However, the license may include conditions specific to the services you intend to offer.

The NBG also supervises compliance with AML/CFT regulations, including customer due diligence (CDD), transaction monitoring, and reporting suspicious activities. The legal basis is the Law of Georgia on Combating Money Laundering and Terrorist Financing, as amended in 2023 to include virtual assets. Additionally, the NBG has issued secondary regulations detailing technical and organizational requirements.

Minimum Capital Requirement

The National Bank of Georgia has set a minimum capital requirement of GEL 500,000 (approximately EUR 150,000 as of 2026 exchange rates). This capital must be paid up in cash and held in a Georgian bank account. The requirement is higher than some other jurisdictions but lower than many EU member states under MiCA, where capital can reach EUR 150,000 for certain services.

The capital must be maintained throughout the license period and cannot be withdrawn without NBG approval. It serves as a buffer to cover operational risks and potential liabilities. For companies offering only limited services (e.g., wallet services without exchange), the NBG may consider a lower amount on a case-by-case basis, but the standard is GEL 500,000.

If you are comparing with other jurisdictions, note that Panama has no minimum capital requirement, making it a popular alternative for cost-conscious founders.

Tax Treatment for Crypto Businesses in Georgia

Georgia offers a favorable tax regime for businesses. The corporate income tax rate is 15%, but with a significant feature: retained earnings are not taxed until distributed. This means that if you reinvest profits, you defer tax indefinitely. This is known as the "Estonian-style" tax system, which Georgia adopted in 2017.

For crypto businesses, the tax treatment of virtual assets is subject to guidance from the Revenue Service of Georgia. Generally, gains from crypto trading are treated as ordinary income, and VAT does not apply to crypto-to-crypto exchanges. However, fiat-to-crypto transactions may be subject to VAT. It is essential to consult a local tax advisor to structure your operations optimally.

Compared to other jurisdictions, Georgia's tax system is competitive. For example, Lithuania imposes a 15% corporate tax on all profits, while Panama has no corporate tax on offshore income. You can read more about tax differences on our cost page.

Allowed Activities Under the Georgia Crypto License

The Georgia crypto license permits the following activities:

  • Exchange between virtual assets and fiat currencies
  • Exchange between one or more virtual assets
  • Transfer of virtual assets (on behalf of customers)
  • Custodial wallet services (holding private keys)
  • Participation in and provision of financial services related to an issuer's offer of virtual assets (e.g., ICOs, STOs, with additional requirements)

The license does not cover activities such as operating a virtual asset mining pool (unless you also offer exchange services) or providing non-custodial wallet software as a service (unless you hold customer funds). If you plan to offer services beyond the standard list, you must seek clarification from the NBG. The license is comprehensive enough for most crypto business models, including exchanges, OTC desks, and payment processors.

Step-by-Step Application Process

Here is the step-by-step process to obtain a crypto license in Georgia:

  1. Company Incorporation: Register a legal entity in Georgia (typically a Limited Liability Company - LLC). You will need a local registered address and a director (can be foreign). The registration takes 1-2 weeks.
  2. Open a Bank Account: Open a corporate bank account with a Georgian bank to deposit the minimum capital. This is a prerequisite for the license application.
  3. Prepare Documentation: Gather required documents: business plan, AML/KYC policies, risk assessment, organizational structure, financial projections, and proof of capital. These must be in Georgian or translated.
  4. Submit Application: File the application with the NBG along with the fee. The NBG will acknowledge receipt and begin the review.
  5. Review and Interview: The NBG reviews the application, may request additional information, and may interview the beneficial owners and management. This stage takes 2-4 months.
  6. License Issuance: If approved, you receive the license. You must then implement your AML program and begin operations under NBG supervision.

Banking and Payment Services for Crypto License Holders

Opening a bank account for a crypto business in Georgia is challenging but feasible. Major Georgian banks, such as Bank of Georgia and TBC Bank, have policies regarding VASPs. They require proof of the license and a strong AML framework. Some banks may refuse service to crypto companies due to perceived risks.

Alternative options include specialized payment service providers (PSPs) that work with crypto firms, such as Modulr or Railsbank, though they may require a European entity. Benefits of a Georgia Crypto License

Georgia offers several advantages for crypto founders:

  • Low cost: Overall setup and licensing costs are lower than in most EU countries.
  • Fast timeline: 3-6 months is achievable with proper preparation.
  • Tax efficiency: Deferred taxation on retained profits; 15% corporate tax only on distributions.
  • Growing ecosystem: Georgia has a pro-innovation government and a developing fintech scene.
  • Strategic location: Bridge between Europe and Asia, with access to regional markets.

However, the minimum capital of EUR 150,000 may be a barrier for some startups. If capital is a concern, Panama (EUR 6,000 flat, no minimum capital) is a compelling alternative. See our Panama page for details.

Compliance and Ongoing Obligations

Once licensed, you must comply with ongoing obligations:

  • Appoint a compliance officer (AML/CFT) who is a resident of Georgia or can be reached during business hours.
  • Conduct customer due diligence (CDD) for all customers, including enhanced due diligence for high-risk customers.
  • Maintain records of transactions for at least 5 years.
  • Report suspicious transactions to the Financial Monitoring Service of Georgia.
  • Submit annual financial statements and compliance reports to the NBG.
  • Undergo periodic audits by the NBG or an external auditor.

Failure to comply can result in fines, suspension, or revocation of the license. This is general guidance, not legal advice. We recommend engaging a local compliance expert. For a broader view, see our requirements page.

Common Mistakes to Avoid When Applying

Founders often make these mistakes:

  • Incomplete documentation: Submitting a business plan that lacks specifics on AML procedures or risk assessment. The NBG expects detailed policies.
  • Underestimating capital: Not having the full GEL 500,000 available in cash before application.
  • Ignoring local language requirements: All documents must be in Georgian or accompanied by a certified translation.
  • Choosing the wrong legal structure: Some structures (e.g., sole proprietorship) are not eligible. An LLC is standard.
  • Rushing the process: Trying to cut corners leads to rejection. Allow 6 months for a thorough application.

Frequently asked questions

Can a foreigner apply for a Georgia crypto license?

Yes, foreigners can own and manage a Georgian company. There is no residency requirement for directors or shareholders, though a local registered address is needed. The beneficial owners must pass fit and proper checks by the NBG.

What is the minimum capital for a Georgia crypto license?

The minimum capital is GEL 500,000 (approximately EUR 150,000). This must be paid up in cash and deposited in a Georgian bank account before the license is issued.

How long does the application process take?

Typically 3 to 6 months from company incorporation to license issuance. The NBG reviews the application within 2-4 months, but preparation time can add 1-2 months.

What are the ongoing compliance requirements?

You must appoint a compliance officer, perform CDD on all customers, maintain transaction records for 5 years, report suspicious transactions, and submit annual reports to the NBG.

Is the Georgia crypto license recognized in the EU?

No, Georgia is not an EU member. The license is valid only in Georgia. However, it may facilitate business with non-EU partners. For EU passporting, consider Lithuania or Estonia under MiCA.

Can I use the license to operate a crypto exchange?

Yes, the license covers exchange services between virtual assets and fiat, as well as between virtual assets. You can operate a centralized exchange, OTC desk, or P2P platform.

What taxes apply to crypto businesses in Georgia?

Corporate income tax is 15% only on distributed profits (retained earnings are tax-free). Crypto gains are treated as ordinary income. VAT may apply to fiat conversions. Consult a tax advisor.

Do I need to travel to Georgia to apply?

Not necessarily. You can incorporate and apply remotely through a local agent. However, the NBG may require an interview, which can be done via video call. Some banks may require physical presence for account opening.

What happens if my application is rejected?

The NBG will provide reasons for rejection. You can address the issues and resubmit. Common reasons include insufficient AML policies or concerns about beneficial owners. We can help you strengthen your application.

How does Georgia compare to Panama for crypto licensing?

Panama has no minimum capital, lower cost (EUR 6,000 flat), and faster setup (2-3 months). Georgia requires EUR 150,000 capital and takes longer. Panama is better for startups; Georgia offers a regulated environment with tax benefits.
