What Is a Crypto License in Czech Republic?

A crypto license in Czech Republic is a regulatory authorization that allows businesses to legally offer crypto-asset services such as exchange, custody, and wallet services. The license is issued by the Czech National Bank (CNB) under the Act on Selected Services of the Information Society (Act No. 253/2008 Coll., as amended). Since the full implementation of MiCA in 2026, the Czech regime aligns with EU-wide standards, requiring CASPs to hold a license and comply with capital, AML, and governance requirements.

The license is not a single document but a registration process that includes a fit and proper assessment of the management team, a detailed business plan, and proof of operational readiness. The CNB maintains a public register of licensed entities, providing transparency for clients and partners. For founders, this license is a gateway to operating legally across the EU under the MiCA passport. The Czech regime is considered one of the more straightforward EU frameworks, but it still demands rigorous preparation. Consulting24 recommends starting the process at least six months before your planned launch date to account for potential delays.

Who Needs a Crypto License in Czech Republic?

Any business offering crypto-asset services in or from the Czech Republic must obtain a license. This includes:

  • Cryptocurrency exchanges (fiat-to-crypto and crypto-to-crypto)
  • Custodial wallet providers
  • Brokerage services for crypto assets
  • DeFi platforms that offer exchange or custody functions
  • ATMs operators (if they provide exchange services)

If your business involves any of these activities, you must register with the CNB. Failure to do so can result in fines up to CZK 5 million (approx. EUR 200,000) and potential criminal liability. Even if you are based outside the Czech Republic but target Czech clients, MiCA may require you to establish a presence or obtain a license. Consulting24 recommends early assessment of your service scope to determine the exact license type needed. For example, a pure peer-to-peer platform may not require a license, but any intermediary role likely does.

License Type and Regulator

The primary regulator for crypto activities in Czech Republic is the Czech National Bank (CNB). The license is formally a registration under the Act on Selected Services of the Information Society, but MiCA has introduced a more comprehensive licensing regime for CASPs. The license type is a CASP license (Crypto-Asset Service Provider), which covers multiple service classes.

The CNB supervises compliance with AML/CFT rules, capital requirements, and consumer protection. The Financial Analytical Office (FAÚ) oversees AML enforcement. The license is valid indefinitely, subject to ongoing compliance and annual reporting. For a detailed comparison with other jurisdictions, see our alternatives section. The CNB also conducts on-site inspections and may request additional information at any time. Capital Requirement

Under MiCA, capital requirements for CASPs in Czech Republic are tiered based on service class:

  • EUR 50,000 for simple exchange services (no custody)
  • EUR 125,000 for custody or wallet services
  • EUR 150,000 for combined services (exchange + custody)

These amounts must be held as own funds and maintained throughout the license period. The CNB may require additional capital based on risk assessment. For example, if your business handles high transaction volumes, the CNB might impose a higher requirement. For comparison, Panama has no minimum capital requirement, making it a cost-effective option for startups. Tax Treatment

Czech Republic taxes crypto profits as capital gains for individuals (15% flat rate) and as corporate income for businesses (19% flat rate). VAT is not applied to crypto-to-crypto exchanges, but fiat-to-crypto transactions may be subject to VAT in some cases. The tax treatment is relatively favorable compared to other EU states, but proper accounting and reporting are essential. Consulting24 recommends engaging a local tax advisor to ensure compliance with Czech tax laws and double tax treaties. For instance, if your company is tax resident in another country, you may avoid double taxation. The Czech tax authority (Financial Administration) has issued specific guidance on crypto taxation, which should be reviewed annually.

Allowed Activities

A Czech crypto license permits the following activities under MiCA:

  • Exchange of crypto assets for fiat currency
  • Exchange of crypto assets for other crypto assets
  • Custody and administration of crypto assets on behalf of clients
  • Operation of a trading platform
  • Transfer services for crypto assets
  • Advice on crypto assets

Activities not covered include issuing stablecoins (e-money license required) or operating as a central securities depository. The license does not cover DeFi protocols that are fully decentralized, but hybrid models may require registration. For example, a platform that offers non-custodial exchange but also provides liquidity pools may need a license. Consulting24 can help classify your activities correctly to avoid regulatory gaps.

Step-by-Step Process

Follow these steps to obtain a crypto license in Czech Republic:

  1. Pre-application preparation: Define your service scope, prepare a business plan, and appoint a local AML officer. The business plan should include financial projections, risk management, and operational details.
  2. Company registration: Incorporate a Czech entity (s.r.o. or a.s.) with a registered office in the country. The minimum share capital for an s.r.o. is CZK 1 (approx. EUR 0.04), but for licensing purposes, you need to meet the capital requirement.
  3. Documentation: Compile AML/KYC policies, risk assessment, IT security measures, and audited financial statements. The AML policy must cover customer due diligence, transaction monitoring, and suspicious activity reporting.
  4. Application submission: Submit the application to the CNB via the electronic system, including all supporting documents and the application fee. The CNB will acknowledge receipt within 30 days.
  5. CNB review: The CNB reviews the application, may request additional information, and conducts a fit and proper test of management. This includes background checks on directors and beneficial owners for criminal records and financial integrity.
  6. Approval and registration: Upon approval, the entity is registered in the CNB's public register and can commence operations. The CNB issues a certificate of registration.
  7. Ongoing compliance: Submit annual reports, maintain capital, and update AML policies as required. The first annual report is due 12 months after approval.

Compliance and Trust

Compliance is critical for maintaining your Czech crypto license. Key obligations include:

  • Implementing AML/KYC procedures aligned with EU directives
  • Appointing a local AML officer who is a resident of the Czech Republic
  • Conducting regular risk assessments at least annually
  • Reporting suspicious transactions to the FAÚ within 24 hours
  • Maintaining capital requirements at all times
  • Submitting annual financial and operational reports to the CNB

Failure to comply can result in fines up to CZK 10 million (approx. EUR 400,000), suspension, or revocation of the license. Common Mistakes

Avoid these pitfalls when applying for a Czech crypto license:

  • Incomplete documentation: Missing AML policies or business plans cause delays. Ensure all documents are translated into Czech if required.
  • Insufficient capital: Not meeting the minimum capital requirement leads to rejection. Verify that your own funds are unencumbered.
  • Poor management fit: The CNB scrutinizes the background of directors and beneficial owners. Any criminal record or financial misconduct can disqualify applicants.
  • Ignoring local presence: A physical office and local staff are often required. Virtual offices may not be accepted.
  • Overlooking ongoing costs: Annual compliance and audit fees can be significant, ranging from EUR 5,000 to 15,000 per year.

Frequently asked questions

What is the minimum capital requirement for a crypto license in Czech Republic?

Under MiCA, the minimum capital requirement depends on the services offered: EUR 50,000 for exchange only, EUR 125,000 for custody, and EUR 150,000 for combined services. These funds must be maintained as own funds throughout the license period.

How long does it take to get a crypto license in Czech Republic?

The process typically takes 3 to 6 months from application to approval. Delays can occur if documentation is incomplete or if the CNB requests additional information. Consulting24 recommends starting early to allow for potential setbacks.

Can I operate across the EU with a Czech crypto license?

Yes, under MiCA passporting, a Czech CASP license allows you to provide services in any EU member state without additional licensing. You must notify the CNB of your intention to passport and comply with local consumer protection rules.

What are the ongoing compliance obligations?

Ongoing obligations include maintaining capital requirements, submitting annual reports to the CNB, conducting regular AML risk assessments, reporting suspicious transactions to FAÚ, and updating policies as needed. Non-compliance can lead to fines or license revocation.

Is a physical office required in Czech Republic?

Yes, you need a registered office and typically a physical presence, such as a local office or co-working space. The CNB may require evidence of operational capacity, including local staff. Virtual offices are generally not sufficient.

What activities are not covered by the Czech crypto license?

The license does not cover issuing stablecoins (requires an e-money license), operating as a central securities depository, or fully decentralized DeFi protocols. If your business involves these activities, additional licensing may be needed.

How does Panama compare to Czech Republic for a crypto license?

Panama offers a flat fee of EUR 6,000, no minimum capital requirement, and a faster timeline of 2-3 months. However, it does not provide EU passporting. Czech Republic has higher costs and capital requirements but allows access to the entire EU market.

What is the tax rate for crypto businesses in Czech Republic?

Corporate income tax is 19% flat. Capital gains for individuals are taxed at 15%. Crypto-to-crypto exchanges are generally VAT-exempt, but fiat-to-crypto transactions may be subject to VAT. Consult a local tax advisor for your specific situation.

Are there any restrictions on foreign ownership?

No, there are no restrictions on foreign ownership for crypto license applicants. However, beneficial owners and directors must pass a fit and proper test, which includes background checks. Foreign entities can own 100% of the Czech company.
