MiCA CASP Capital Requirements Explained: The 50k / 125k / 150k Tiers by Activity

The Markets in Crypto-Assets Regulation (MiCA) has fully entered into force across the European Union in 2026, establishing a harmonised framework for crypto-asset service providers (CASPs). One of the most critical compliance obligations is the minimum capital requirement, which varies depending on the type of services offered. Under MiCA, CASPs must hold at least EUR 50,000, EUR 125,000, or EUR 150,000 in own funds, based on their activity classification. What Are MiCA CASP Capital Requirements?

MiCA (Regulation (EU) 2023/1114) introduces a mandatory minimum capital requirement for all CASPs authorised in the EU. The requirement is based on the principle that a CASP must at all times hold own funds equal to the higher of: (a) a fixed minimum amount depending on the services provided, or (b) a variable amount calculated as 25% of the previous year's fixed overheads. The fixed minimum amounts are set at three tiers: EUR 50,000, EUR 125,000, and EUR 150,000. These are not optional; they are a condition for authorisation and ongoing compliance.

The capital must be held in liquid assets, typically cash or cash equivalents, and cannot be used for operational expenses. It serves as a buffer to protect clients and ensure business continuity. Unlike some pre-MiCA regimes that had no minimum capital (e.g., the old UK FCA temporary registration), MiCA imposes real financial substance requirements. For founders, this means budgeting for locked-up capital from day one.

Who Needs to Comply with MiCA CASP Capital Requirements?

Any legal entity providing crypto-asset services in the EU must comply with MiCA. This includes exchanges, wallet providers, custodians, brokers, and platforms offering crypto lending or staking. The capital requirement applies regardless of the company's size or transaction volume. Even small startups must meet the relevant tier. Exemptions exist only for very limited cases, such as providers of fully decentralised services that do not hold client assets.

Founders planning to serve EU clients must either establish a licensed entity in an EU member state or rely on a third-country regime that is deemed equivalent (which is rare as of 2026). For many, the most practical route is to incorporate in a member state with a clear application process, such as Lithuania or Estonia, where Consulting24 delivers directly. Alternatively, non-EU jurisdictions like Panama offer a simpler, lower-cost setup without MiCA capital requirements, suitable for businesses targeting global markets outside the EU.

License Type and Regulator

Under MiCA, the license is called a "CASP authorisation" (Crypto-Asset Service Provider). It is granted by the competent authority of the EU member state where the CASP has its registered office. National regulators (e.g., the Bank of Lithuania, the Estonian Financial Supervision Authority) act as the primary supervisors, but they must follow MiCA's uniform rules. The European Securities and Markets Authority (ESMA) plays a coordinating role, issuing guidelines and maintaining a public register of all authorised CASPs.

Each member state may have slight variations in application procedures and additional local requirements (e.g., AML registration, local presence). However, the capital requirements are fully harmonised. For those who prefer to avoid EU capital lock-up entirely, Panama offers a company setup with no minimum capital and a flat fee of EUR 6,000, though it does not provide EU market access.

Capital Requirement by Activity Tier

MiCA Article 67(1) specifies the following fixed minimum own funds for each category of service:

  • EUR 50,000 - For CASPs that only provide one or more of the following services: (a) custody and administration of crypto-assets on behalf of clients; (b) operation of a trading platform; (c) exchange of crypto-assets for funds; (d) exchange of crypto-assets for other crypto-assets; (e) execution of orders on behalf of clients; (f) placing of crypto-assets; (g) reception and transmission of orders on behalf of clients; (h) advice on crypto-assets; (i) portfolio management; (j) transfer services; (k) providing information on crypto-assets (if not covered elsewhere).
  • EUR 125,000 - For CASPs that provide any of the services listed above but also hold clients' crypto-assets or funds (i.e., custody or exchange services that involve holding client assets). This tier applies if the CASP has custody or control over client assets, even if only one such service is offered.
  • EUR 150,000 - For CASPs that provide services involving the operation of a trading platform (i.e., matching buyers and sellers) and also hold client assets. This is the highest tier, reflecting the systemic risk of platform operations.

It is important to note that the classification is based on the actual services provided, not the business model. A CASP that only offers advice and portfolio management (no custody) falls under EUR 50,000. A CASP that operates an exchange and holds client funds must meet EUR 150,000. The variable overheads calculation may increase the requirement further.

Tax Treatment of MiCA CASPs

MiCA does not harmonise tax treatment; each member state applies its own corporate tax regime. Generally, CASPs are subject to the standard corporate income tax rate of the country of incorporation. For example, Lithuania has a 15% corporate tax rate, while Estonia has a 0% rate on retained profits (20% on distributions). Some countries offer incentives for fintech or crypto businesses, but these are not guaranteed under MiCA.

Founders should also consider VAT implications and withholding taxes on cross-border transactions. Consulting24 advises clients on tax-efficient structuring within the EU. For those seeking a tax-neutral environment, Panama's territorial tax system (no tax on foreign-source income) combined with its low setup cost can be attractive, especially for businesses with non-EU clients.

Allowed Activities Under MiCA CASP Authorisation

A CASP authorised under MiCA can provide any of the services listed in the regulation, provided they are included in the authorisation scope. The main categories are:

  • Custody and administration of crypto-assets on behalf of clients
  • Operation of a crypto-asset trading platform
  • Exchange of crypto-assets for funds or other crypto-assets
  • Execution of orders on behalf of clients
  • Placing of crypto-assets
  • Reception and transmission of orders
  • Advice on crypto-assets
  • Portfolio management
  • Transfer services for crypto-assets
  • Providing information on crypto-assets (if not covered elsewhere)

Additionally, CASPs may offer ancillary services such as staking, lending, or yield products, but these may be subject to additional requirements under MiCA or other EU regulations (e.g., the proposed EU framework for crypto lending). It is crucial to define your service scope precisely in the application to avoid scope creep and potential enforcement actions.

Step-by-Step Process to Obtain MiCA CASP Authorisation

  1. Pre-application assessment: Determine the services you will offer and the corresponding capital tier. Choose a suitable EU member state for incorporation and authorisation.
  2. Company incorporation: Set up a legal entity (e.g., UAB in Lithuania, OÜ in Estonia) with a registered office and local director/AML officer.
  3. Capital deposit: Deposit the required minimum capital in a bank account in the name of the company. The funds must be unencumbered and available at all times.
  4. Documentation: Prepare a detailed business plan, risk assessment, AML/CFT policies, governance framework, and IT security documentation.
  5. Application submission: Submit the application to the national competent authority along with the application fee. The regulator has up to 3 months to assess completeness, then a further 3 months for substantive review.
  6. Review and interview: The regulator may request additional information, conduct interviews with management, and perform on-site inspections.
  7. Authorisation decision: If approved, the CASP is added to the ESMA register. The entire process typically takes 4-6 months from incorporation.

Banking and Payment Services for CASPs

One of the biggest operational challenges for CASPs is obtaining a bank account. Many traditional banks are reluctant to serve crypto businesses due to perceived risk. Under MiCA, CASPs are regulated entities, which can help open doors, but banking options remain limited. In Lithuania, several banks and payment institutions (e.g., Revolut, Paysera) accept CASPs, but they often require a minimum deposit and charge higher fees.

Founders should plan for alternative payment rails, such as EMI accounts or crypto-friendly banks in other jurisdictions. Consulting24 can introduce clients to banking partners we have vetted. For businesses that do not require EU banking, Panama offers a straightforward company setup with access to international bank accounts in jurisdictions like Switzerland or Singapore.

Benefits of MiCA CASP Authorisation

  • EU-wide passporting: Once authorised in one member state, you can provide services across all EU/EEA countries without additional licensing.
  • Regulatory clarity: MiCA provides a clear legal framework, reducing uncertainty for investors and partners.
  • Investor confidence: A regulated status enhances credibility and can attract institutional clients.
  • Access to EU market: The EU is one of the largest crypto markets globally; a CASP licence is essential for serving EU residents.
  • Harmonised rules: Consistent capital, conduct, and disclosure requirements across the bloc simplify compliance.

However, these benefits come with significant costs and ongoing obligations. For founders targeting non-EU markets, a Panama company may offer better value.

Compliance and Trust: Ongoing Obligations

After authorisation, CASPs must comply with ongoing requirements, including:

  • Maintaining the minimum capital at all times (subject to annual review).
  • Reporting to the regulator on a regular basis (e.g., quarterly financial reports, AML reports).
  • Conducting annual external audits.
  • Updating the regulator on any material changes (e.g., new services, change of management).
  • Adhering to conduct of business rules, including fair treatment of clients, disclosure of risks, and handling of complaints.

Common Mistakes When Applying for MiCA CASP Authorisation

  • Underestimating capital requirements: Some founders assume the lowest tier applies, but if they hold client assets, they must meet EUR 125,000 or EUR 150,000.
  • Incomplete documentation: Regulators expect detailed policies and procedures. Rushed applications are often rejected.
  • Choosing the wrong member state: Some regulators are slower or more demanding. Lithuania and Estonia are known for efficient processes.
  • Ignoring banking: Failing to secure a bank account before application can delay the process.
  • Not budgeting for ongoing costs: Annual compliance fees can be substantial.

Frequently asked questions

What is the minimum capital for a MiCA CASP that only offers advice and portfolio management?

If the CASP does not hold client assets, the minimum capital is EUR 50,000. This covers services like advice, portfolio management, and order execution without custody.

Does the EUR 125,000 tier apply if I only provide custody services?

Yes, if you hold clients' crypto-assets or funds, you fall under the EUR 125,000 tier, even if custody is your only service.

Can the capital requirement be higher than the fixed minimum?

Yes. The CASP must hold the higher of the fixed minimum or 25% of the previous year's fixed overheads. For a large operation, the variable amount may exceed the fixed tier.

Is the capital requirement locked indefinitely?

The capital must be maintained at all times as own funds. It can be used for losses but must be replenished. It is not a deposit that can be withdrawn freely.

What happens if I change my services after authorisation?

You must notify the regulator. If the new services require a higher capital tier, you must top up the capital before offering them.

Can I use the capital for operational expenses?

No. The capital must be held as own funds and cannot be used for day-to-day operations. It serves as a buffer for client protection.

Does Panama have a similar capital requirement for crypto businesses?

No. Panama does not have a specific crypto licence. A standard company setup costs EUR 6,000 with no minimum capital, making it a low-cost alternative for non-EU markets.

How long does it take to get a MiCA CASP authorisation?

Typically 4-6 months from company incorporation to authorisation, depending on the member state and the complexity of your application.

Can I apply for a CASP licence in any EU country?

Yes, but you must have a registered office in that country. Some regulators are faster than others. Lithuania and Estonia are popular choices.

What are the ongoing compliance costs for a CASP?

Annual costs typically range from EUR 10,000 to EUR 25,000, including audit, AML officer, and regulatory reporting. This is in addition to the locked capital.
