What is a France crypto license?

The France crypto license, issued by the AMF under the PACTE law and now aligned with MiCA, allows crypto asset service providers (CASPs) to operate legally in France. It covers activities like custody, exchange, and trading of digital assets. Since MiCA came into full force in 2024, French CASPs must comply with EU-wide standards, including capital tiers of EUR 50,000, 125,000, or 150,000 depending on services offered.

Failure to understand this regulatory framework leads to many of the common mistakes when applying for a France crypto license. For instance, some firms assume the old optional registration is still sufficient, but mandatory licensing is now the norm. The AMF also requires a detailed business plan, risk management policies, and fit-and-proper tests for directors.

In practice, the AMF expects applicants to demonstrate a deep understanding of their business model and associated risks. A common error is treating the license as a simple registration rather than a comprehensive authorization. Consulting24 helps clients prepare for the AMF's rigorous review, ensuring all documentation is aligned with MiCA and French specific requirements.

Who needs a France crypto license?

Any company offering crypto asset services to French residents must hold a license. This includes exchanges, custodians, brokers, and platforms facilitating fiat-to-crypto transactions. Even foreign firms targeting French clients need to register or passport under MiCA.

One common mistake when applying for a France crypto license is assuming that small operations are exempt. In reality, the AMF applies the rules to all service providers, regardless of size. Startups often underestimate the compliance burden, leading to incomplete applications. For example, a small crypto exchange processing under EUR 100,000 monthly might think it can avoid licensing by operating informally. However, the AMF actively monitors the market and can impose fines or shut down unlicensed operators. License type and regulator

The France crypto license is a CASP license regulated by the AMF. Under MiCA, there are three classes: Class 1 (custody and exchange of crypto assets), Class 2 (trading platforms), and Class 3 (full service). Each class has specific capital requirements and operational rules.

A frequent mistake is misclassifying the service type. For example, a simple exchange might think it needs only Class 1 capital (EUR 50,000), but if it also offers custody, it requires EUR 125,000. Consulting24 helps map services to the correct class. For comparison, Lithuania and Estonia have their own regimes, but France is often chosen for its market access.

Another common error is failing to account for ancillary services. If your platform plans to offer staking or lending in the future, you may need a higher class or additional authorization. The AMF expects applicants to declare all intended activities upfront. Consulting24 provides a service classification checklist to ensure nothing is missed.

Capital requirement

MiCA mandates minimum capital of EUR 50,000 for simple custody/exchange, EUR 125,000 for trading platforms, and EUR 150,000 for full-service CASPs. Additionally, the AMF may require higher amounts based on business volume.

One of the most common mistakes when applying for a France crypto license is failing to demonstrate that capital is unencumbered and available at all times. Some applicants use borrowed funds or crypto assets, which the AMF rejects. Consulting24 advises on structuring capital from fiat sources. For lower capital needs, consider Panama where no minimum capital is required beyond the EUR 6,000 setup fee.

Another issue is not maintaining the capital throughout the application process. The AMF may request updated bank statements showing the capital is still in place. Firms that spend the capital on operational costs before approval face rejection. Consulting24 helps clients set up a dedicated escrow account to avoid this pitfall.

Tax treatment

France taxes crypto gains as capital gains (30% flat tax for individuals) and corporate income tax for companies (25% standard rate). VAT applies to some services. A common mistake when applying for a France crypto license is ignoring tax obligations upfront. The AMF coordinates with tax authorities, so non-compliance can block licensing.

Consulting24 recommends engaging a French tax advisor early. For tax-efficient alternatives, Panama offers territorial taxation (no tax on foreign-source income), which can be advantageous for international operations.

For example, a company that plans to earn revenue from non-French clients might still be subject to French corporate tax if it is managed in France. A France CASP license permits: custody of crypto assets, exchange (fiat-to-crypto and crypto-to-crypto), operation of trading platforms, and order execution. Derivatives and securities tokens may require additional MiFID authorization.

Mistakes occur when applicants include activities not covered by the license, such as lending or staking, without proper segregation.
