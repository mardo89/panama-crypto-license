Overview: What Are El Salvador and Panama Offering in 2026?

El Salvador made history in 2021 by adopting Bitcoin as legal tender, but its crypto licensing framework remains under development. As of 2026, the country does not have a dedicated crypto license; instead, crypto businesses operate under general financial regulations or through the Bitcoin Office. This creates uncertainty for companies seeking a clear legal status. The Bitcoin Office provides guidance but no formal licensing, meaning businesses rely on ad-hoc approvals that may not be recognized internationally.

Panama, in contrast, has a well-defined crypto licensing regime under the Panama Financial Services Superintendency (SSFP). The license is issued as a "Provider of Virtual Asset Services" (VASP) and costs a flat EUR 6,000. The process is transparent, with no minimum capital requirement, and the tax regime is territorial-meaning income earned outside Panama is not taxed. This makes Panama a predictable and cost-effective choice for crypto companies. Panama's Law 23 of 2023 provides a solid legal foundation, aligning with FATF recommendations.

Both countries offer visa pathways for founders, but Panama's established financial infrastructure and banking relationships give it an edge. For a deeper dive into Panama's offering, see our Panama page.

Who Needs a Crypto License in Panama or El Salvador?

Any company engaging in virtual asset services-such as exchange, custody, transfer, or payment processing-needs a license in Panama. The SSFP requires all VASPs to be licensed, regardless of whether they serve local or international clients. In El Salvador, the legal requirement is less clear; businesses dealing with Bitcoin may need to register with the Bitcoin Office, but there is no formal licensing regime. This ambiguity can lead to compliance risks and difficulties with banking partners.

Typical applicants include:

  • Crypto exchanges (centralized and decentralized)
  • Wallet providers (custodial and non-custodial)
  • Payment processors using digital assets
  • OTC desks
  • Token issuers (subject to additional securities laws)
  • NFT marketplaces (if involving virtual asset transfers)

If your business model involves any of these activities, Panama's clear licensing path is a safer bet. For more on who needs a license, see our requirements page.

License Type & Regulator

Panama: The regulator is the Superintendencia de Servicios Financieros de Panamá (SSFP). The license type is "Provider of Virtual Asset Services" (VASP). There is one license class covering all virtual asset activities. The regime is based on Law 23 of 2023, which regulates virtual asset service providers. The SSFP is a well-respected regulator with a track record of enforcing AML/CFT standards, and the VASP license is recognized internationally, facilitating correspondent banking relationships.

El Salvador: There is no dedicated crypto license. The Bitcoin Office (Oficina del Bitcoin) oversees Bitcoin-related activities, but formal licensing is absent. Companies may need to register as financial entities under the Central Bank or comply with anti-money laundering (AML) laws. This lack of a clear regulatory framework poses risks for compliance and banking. Some businesses have reported that the Bitcoin Office issues "authorizations" that are not legally binding licenses, creating uncertainty for international partners.

Panama's SSFP is a more established regulator, providing a clear legal basis for crypto operations.

Capital Requirement

Panama: There is no minimum capital requirement for the VASP license. This is a major advantage for startups with limited funds. However, the SSFP may require proof of sufficient operational funds to cover initial expenses, such as office rent, staff salaries, and compliance costs. In practice, having at least EUR 10,000 in the company account is advisable to demonstrate financial stability.

El Salvador: No official minimum capital is specified, but companies may need to demonstrate financial stability to the Bitcoin Office or banks. In practice, a minimum of EUR 10,000-50,000 may be expected to cover legal, compliance, and banking costs. Some banks require a minimum deposit of EUR 25,000 to open an account.

Panama's zero-minimum capital is a significant draw. Compare this to EU jurisdictions like Lithuania, which require EUR 125,000 for exchange services under MiCA. For more on capital requirements, see our requirements page.

Tax Treatment

Panama: Territorial taxation applies. Income earned outside Panama is not taxed. Crypto companies that serve international clients (most do) pay 0% tax on foreign-source income. Local-source income (e.g., from Panamanian clients) is taxed at a standard corporate rate of 25%. No VAT on crypto services. No capital gains tax on crypto held as investment. This regime is highly favorable for businesses with global clients.

El Salvador: No capital gains tax on Bitcoin, but other crypto gains may be taxed as income. Corporate income tax is 30% on worldwide income for resident companies. There is no specific crypto tax regime, creating uncertainty. Additionally, El Salvador has a 13% VAT that may apply to crypto services, depending on interpretation.

Panama's territorial tax system is far more favorable for most crypto businesses. For a detailed comparison, see our vs Lithuania page.

Allowed Activities Under the License

Panama VASP license allows:

  • Exchange of virtual assets for fiat or other virtual assets
  • Transfer of virtual assets
  • Custody and administration of virtual assets
  • Payment services using virtual assets
  • Participation in ICOs/STOs (subject to securities laws)
  • Operation of crypto ATMs

El Salvador: No specific license, so activities are not explicitly defined. Bitcoin-related services are generally allowed, but other crypto activities may fall under securities or banking regulations. For example, offering stablecoins or tokens other than Bitcoin could be considered securities offerings, requiring registration with the Central Bank.

Panama's license covers a broad range of activities, giving companies flexibility. For a full list, see our exchange license page.

Step-by-Step Process to Obtain a Panama Crypto License

The process is streamlined compared to other jurisdictions. Here are the steps:

  1. Company setup: Incorporate a Panama corporation (Sociedad Anónima) with a local registered agent. This takes 1-2 weeks. You need a local director and a physical address.
  2. Prepare documentation: Business plan, AML/KYC policies, corporate documents (articles of incorporation, board resolutions), and personal information of directors and shareholders (passport copies, proof of address, CVs).
  3. Submit application: File with the SSFP. Include proof of paid-in capital (if any), a description of services, and a compliance manual. The application fee is part of the EUR 6,000.
  4. Regulatory review: The SSFP conducts due diligence on applicants and their beneficial owners. This takes 2-3 months. They may request additional information or clarifications.
  5. Approval and license issuance: Once approved, pay the license fee (EUR 6,000) and receive the license. The license is valid for one year and must be renewed annually.

Banking & Payment Services

Access to banking is a common challenge for crypto companies. Panama has a well-developed banking sector with several banks willing to work with licensed VASPs. The SSFP license is recognized by local banks, facilitating account opening. However, banks may require additional due diligence and a minimum deposit, typically between EUR 5,000 and EUR 25,000. Some banks also require a physical presence or local director.

El Salvador's banking sector is smaller and more cautious. Many international banks avoid the country due to its Bitcoin adoption. Local banks may open accounts for crypto companies, but the process is slow and uncertain. Some banks refuse to serve crypto businesses altogether, citing regulatory risks.

Panama also offers access to payment processors and fintech services, such as Stripe and PayPal, which are more readily available. For more on banking, see our company setup page.

Compliance & Trust: Why Regulation Matters

In 2026, regulators worldwide are tightening AML/CFT requirements. A license from a reputable jurisdiction like Panama signals to partners, banks, and customers that your company is compliant. The SSFP conducts regular audits and requires annual reporting, including transaction monitoring and suspicious activity reports. Panama's regime is aligned with FATF Recommendation 15, which sets standards for virtual asset service providers.

El Salvador's lack of a formal regime means companies may struggle to prove compliance. This can hinder banking relationships and partnerships. Some exchanges have reported being denied accounts due to El Salvador's unclear regulatory status. Panama's VASP license provides a solid compliance framework that is respected globally.

Common Mistakes When Choosing Between El Salvador and Panama

  • Overvaluing Bitcoin adoption: El Salvador's Bitcoin law does not equate to a friendly crypto business environment. The lack of a license creates legal risks and banking difficulties.
  • Ignoring tax implications: Panama's territorial tax is a major advantage. El Salvador's worldwide taxation can be costly for companies with international clients.
  • Underestimating banking challenges: Even with a license, banking in El Salvador is difficult. Panama offers better access to financial services.
  • Choosing based on hype: El Salvador's media attention does not replace regulatory clarity. Make a data-driven decision based on your business model.
  • Neglecting compliance costs: El Salvador's uncertain regime may lead to higher legal and compliance expenses in the long run.

Beyond Panama and El Salvador, other jurisdictions offer crypto licensing. For example, Lithuania under MiCA requires EUR 125,000 capital for exchange services, but offers EU passporting. Frequently asked questions

Is El Salvador a good jurisdiction for crypto licensing in 2026?

El Salvador has no dedicated crypto license, making it a risky choice for businesses seeking regulatory clarity. While Bitcoin is legal tender, the lack of a formal licensing regime creates uncertainty for compliance and banking. Most crypto companies are better served by Panama's clear VASP license.

How much does a Panama crypto license cost?

The Panama VASP license costs a flat EUR 6,000, which includes the application fee and first-year license fee. Ongoing costs include annual renewal (approximately EUR 1,000), local agent fees (EUR 1,500 per year), and compliance costs (EUR 2,000-5,000 per year).

What is the minimum capital for a Panama crypto license?

There is no minimum capital requirement for the Panama VASP license. However, the SSFP may require proof of sufficient operational funds. In practice, having at least EUR 10,000 in the company account is advisable to cover initial expenses.

Can I get a bank account for my crypto company in Panama?

Yes, Panama has several banks willing to work with licensed VASPs. The SSFP license facilitates account opening, but banks may require additional due diligence and a minimum deposit (typically EUR 5,000-25,000). Consulting24 can assist with banking introductions.

What activities are allowed under the Panama VASP license?

The license covers exchange, transfer, custody, payment services, and participation in ICOs/STOs (subject to securities laws). It does not cover securities or banking activities. The license is suitable for most crypto business models.

How long does it take to get a Panama crypto license?

The process takes 2-4 months from company incorporation to license issuance. The SSFP review typically takes 2-3 months, but can be faster if documentation is complete. Consulting24 streamlines the process to minimize delays.

Is Panama's tax regime favorable for crypto companies?

Yes, Panama has a territorial tax system: income earned outside Panama is tax-free. Most crypto companies serve international clients, so they pay 0% tax on foreign-source income. Local-source income is taxed at 25%. No VAT on crypto services.

A Panama license does not provide EU passporting rights. To serve EU clients, you may need a license in an EU member state under MiCA, such as Lithuania or Estonia. Panama is best for non-EU markets, particularly the Americas and Asia.

Does El Salvador offer any advantages over Panama for crypto?

El Salvador's main advantage is Bitcoin being legal tender, which may appeal to Bitcoin-focused businesses. However, the lack of a formal license, higher taxes, and banking challenges outweigh this benefit for most companies. Panama offers greater regulatory clarity and lower costs.

What is the difference between a Panama VASP license and a Lithuanian crypto license?
