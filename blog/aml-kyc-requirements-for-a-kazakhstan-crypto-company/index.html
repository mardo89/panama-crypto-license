What Are AML/KYC Requirements for a Kazakhstan Crypto Company?

AML (Anti-Money Laundering) and KYC (Know Your Customer) requirements are regulatory obligations that crypto companies must implement to prevent financial crimes. In Kazakhstan, these requirements are enforced by AFSA under the AIFC's Digital Asset Framework. They include customer due diligence (CDD), transaction monitoring, record keeping, and suspicious activity reporting.

Key components include:

  • Identity verification of customers (individuals and legal entities).
  • Beneficial ownership identification.
  • Ongoing monitoring of transactions.
  • Risk-based approach to compliance.
  • Appointment of a compliance officer.

These rules apply to all licensed crypto activities, from exchange services to custody. Failure to comply can result in fines, license suspension, or revocation. The AIFC requires that AML/KYC policies be documented in a manual that is approved by the board and reviewed annually. For example, a crypto exchange must verify each customer's identity using government-issued IDs and proof of address, and screen against sanctions lists. Transaction monitoring systems must flag unusual activity, such as rapid large-volume trades or transfers to high-risk jurisdictions. Record keeping must retain all transaction data for at least five years after the business relationship ends.

Who Needs to Comply with AML/KYC in Kazakhstan?

Any legal entity that provides digital asset services within or from Kazakhstan must comply. This includes:

  • Crypto exchanges (buying/selling digital assets).
  • Custodian wallet providers.
  • Digital asset trading platforms.
  • Initial exchange offerings (IEOs) and token issuers.
  • Brokers and dealers in digital assets.

Even if your company is registered in another jurisdiction, if you target Kazakhstani customers, you may be subject to local AML laws. For instance, a foreign exchange that advertises to Kazakh residents must register with AFSA and implement AML/KYC procedures. License Type and Regulator

The primary regulator for crypto activities in Kazakhstan is the Astana Financial Services Authority (AFSA), operating under the AIFC. AFSA issues a Digital Asset Service Provider (DASP) license, which covers multiple activities including exchange, custody, and brokerage.

There is no separate license for each activity; instead, a single DASP license authorizes a range of services, subject to compliance with AML/KYC rules. The AIFC framework is based on international standards, making it attractive for global crypto firms.

For companies not based in the AIFC, the National Bank of Kazakhstan also has oversight, but the AIFC is the primary hub for crypto licensing. AFSA's application process includes a thorough review of the applicant's AML/KYC policies, financial health, and business plan. The regulator conducts on-site inspections and may require a compliance officer to be based in Kazakhstan. Capital Requirement

Kazakhstan's AIFC does not impose a specific minimum capital requirement for DASP licenses in the law. However, the regulator expects applicants to demonstrate sufficient financial resources to cover operational costs and potential liabilities. Typically, a minimum of EUR 50,000 in paid-up capital is recommended to satisfy AFSA's assessment of financial soundness.

This is lower than the EU's MiCA capital tiers (EUR 50,000 to 150,000 depending on services). For comparison, Estonia requires EUR 250,000, while Lithuania requires EUR 125,000 for certain services. Consulting24 can advise on structuring your capital to meet expectations. In practice, AFSA may ask for proof of capital in the form of bank statements or audited financials. If your business involves holding customer assets, additional capital may be required to cover potential losses. For a jurisdiction with no capital requirement, consider Panama.

Tax Treatment for Crypto Companies in Kazakhstan

Kazakhstan offers a favorable tax regime within the AIFC. Companies registered in the AIFC are exempt from corporate income tax for the first 10 years, and there is no VAT on digital asset transactions. This makes it a competitive jurisdiction for crypto firms.

Outside the AIFC, standard corporate tax rates apply (20%), and crypto transactions may be subject to VAT. However, the AIFC's tax incentives significantly reduce the tax burden. This can be advantageous for international investors. For example, a crypto exchange registered in the AIFC can operate with zero corporate tax for a decade, while a similar company in Lithuania would pay 15% corporate tax. Allowed Activities Under the DASP License

The DASP license permits a wide range of digital asset services, including:

  • Exchange of digital assets for fiat currency or other digital assets.
  • Custody and wallet services.
  • Brokerage services.
  • Digital asset trading platform operation.
  • Initial exchange offerings (IEOs) with AFSA approval.

Activities such as derivatives trading require additional authorization. The license does not cover mining or NFT marketplaces unless they involve exchange services. AFSA also permits staking services if they are incidental to custody. For a broader range of allowed activities with no restrictions, Panama offers a more flexible framework.

Step-by-Step Process for Obtaining a Kazakhstan Crypto License

The application process involves several stages:

  1. Pre-application consultation: Discuss your business model with AFSA or a consultant. This step helps identify potential issues early.
  2. Company incorporation: Register a legal entity in the AIFC (typically a private company). This requires a local registered address and a director.
  3. AML/KYC policy development: Draft policies, procedures, and risk assessments. These must be tailored to your specific services and customer base.
  4. System implementation: Deploy AML/KYC software for identity verification and transaction monitoring. Choose a vendor that supports Kazakhstani ID documents and international sanctions lists.
  5. Submit application: File with AFSA, including business plan, compliance manual, financial statements, and proof of capital.
  6. Review and interview: AFSA reviews and may request additional information or an interview. The regulator may ask for clarifications on your risk assessment or source of funds.
  7. License issuance: If approved, you receive the DASP license. You must then pay the annual fee and submit a compliance report within 30 days.

Banking and Payment Solutions for Kazakhstan Crypto Companies

Opening a bank account for a crypto company in Kazakhstan can be challenging due to perceived risks. However, the AIFC has partnerships with local and international banks that accept licensed crypto firms. Options include:

  • Banks within the AIFC ecosystem (e.g., Bereke Bank, Halyk Bank). These banks understand crypto and have dedicated onboarding processes.
  • International banks with presence in Kazakhstan, such as Citibank or HSBC, though they may have stricter compliance requirements.
  • EMI (Electronic Money Institution) accounts for fiat on/off ramps. EMIs like Paysera or TransferWise can provide faster account opening.

You will need to provide your DASP license, AML policies, and proof of source of funds. The bank may also require a minimum deposit, typically EUR 10,000-50,000. Compliance and Trust: Ongoing Obligations

After obtaining the license, you must maintain ongoing compliance:

  • Submit annual audited financial statements to AFSA within six months of year-end.
  • Conduct regular AML/KYC audits, either internally or by an external auditor, annually.
  • Report suspicious transactions to the Financial Monitoring Agency within 24 hours of detection.
  • Renew license annually with a fee of approximately EUR 2,000-5,000.
  • Update policies as regulations change, such as new FATF recommendations or local laws.

Common Mistakes When Applying for a Kazakhstan Crypto License

Founders often make these errors:

  • Incomplete AML/KYC policies: Not tailoring to Kazakhstan's specific requirements, such as including Kazakhstani identification documents like the IIN (Individual Identification Number).
  • Underestimating timeline: Expecting approval in weeks rather than months. The average is 3-6 months.
  • Insufficient capital: Not demonstrating financial stability with at least EUR 50,000 in liquid assets.
  • Ignoring beneficial ownership disclosure: Failing to identify all ultimate owners, including those holding shares through trusts or nominees.
  • Choosing the wrong jurisdiction: Not comparing with alternatives like Panama or Lithuania that may better suit your business model.
