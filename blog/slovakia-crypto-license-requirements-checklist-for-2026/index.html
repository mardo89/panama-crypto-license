What Is the Slovakia Crypto License?

The Slovakia crypto license is an authorisation issued by the National Bank of Slovakia (NBS) under the EU Markets in Crypto-Assets (MiCA) regulation. It allows firms to legally provide crypto-asset services such as exchange, custody, and transfer within Slovakia and across the EU under passporting rights. Since MiCA is fully in force in 2026, the license replaces any previous national regimes and ensures compliance with AML, capital, and governance standards.

Key features include:

  • Single license valid across all EU/EEA member states
  • Three capital tiers based on service type: EUR 50,000, 125,000, or 150,000
  • Mandatory AML/CFT policies, secure custody, and transparent disclosure
  • Ongoing supervision by the NBS with periodic reporting

For example, a crypto exchange offering both fiat-to-crypto trading and custodial wallet services would fall under the EUR 125,000 tier. The license is a prerequisite for any firm that wants to operate legally in the EU crypto space. Without it, you risk regulatory action, fines, and reputational damage. Who Needs This License?

Any legal entity that intends to offer crypto-asset services to residents of Slovakia or other EU countries must obtain a MiCA license in an EU member state. Slovakia is a popular choice due to its efficient regulatory process and reasonable costs. The license is required for:

  • Crypto exchanges (fiat-to-crypto and crypto-to-crypto)
  • Custodial wallet providers
  • Brokers and dealers in crypto-assets
  • Platforms for initial coin offerings (ICOs) or security token offerings (STOs)
  • Advisors and portfolio managers dealing with crypto-assets

If your business falls into any of these categories, you must apply for a CASP license. For non-EU operations, Panama offers a simpler regime with a flat fee of EUR 6,000 and no minimum capital, which may be suitable for targeting markets outside the EU. License Type and Regulator

The license is a Crypto-Asset Service Provider (CASP) authorisation under MiCA. The competent authority is the National Bank of Slovakia (NBS), which oversees both prudential and conduct requirements. The NBS is also responsible for AML supervision in coordination with the Slovak Financial Intelligence Unit.

There is only one license category under MiCA, but the scope of services you register for determines your capital requirement and specific obligations. You can apply for multiple service classes under a single license. The NBS expects applicants to demonstrate strong governance, including a clear organisational structure, fit-and-proper management, and adequate internal controls. For example, you need to appoint a compliance officer and a money laundering reporting officer (MLRO) who are based in the EU.

Capital Requirement

Under MiCA, the minimum capital requirement is determined by the type of services you provide. Slovakia applies the standard EU tiers:

  • EUR 50,000 for simple services like reception and transmission of orders, or advisory services
  • EUR 125,000 for exchange services (fiat-to-crypto or crypto-to-crypto), custody, or transfer services
  • EUR 150,000 for more complex services like dealing on own account or underwriting

If you offer multiple service classes, the highest tier applies. The capital must be held in liquid assets and maintained at all times. This is a significant difference from Panama, which has no minimum capital requirement. For example, a startup offering only advisory services would need EUR 50,000, while a full-exchange platform would need EUR 125,000. The capital must be deposited in a Slovak bank account and cannot be used for operational expenses.

Tax Treatment of Crypto in Slovakia

Slovakia has a clear tax framework for crypto-assets. As of 2026, capital gains from crypto are treated as income from capital assets. Individuals are exempt from tax if the holding period exceeds one year (similar to securities). For businesses, crypto transactions are subject to corporate income tax at the standard rate of 21% (or 15% for small companies). VAT does not apply to crypto-to-fiat exchanges, but advisory services may be subject to VAT.

It is crucial to maintain detailed records of all transactions for tax reporting. Consulting24 recommends working with a local tax advisor to ensure compliance. For companies targeting non-EU markets, Panama offers territorial taxation (no tax on foreign-source income) and a flat EUR 6,000 setup fee. Slovakia's tax regime is competitive within the EU, but the one-year holding exemption for individuals is a notable benefit.

Allowed Activities Under the License

A Slovakia CASP license permits the following activities as defined by MiCA:

  • Reception and transmission of orders for crypto-assets
  • Execution of orders on behalf of clients
  • Dealing on own account (proprietary trading)
  • Portfolio management of crypto-assets
  • Provision of transfer services for crypto-assets
  • Custody and administration of crypto-assets on behalf of clients
  • Exchange of crypto-assets for fiat currency or other crypto-assets
  • Placement of crypto-assets (underwriting)
  • Advice on crypto-assets

You must specify which services you intend to offer in your application. The NBS may impose additional conditions for certain activities, such as custody requiring a secure offline storage policy. For example, if you plan to offer both exchange and custody, you need to detail your cold wallet procedures and insurance coverage. Consulting24 can help you define your service scope accurately.

Step-by-Step Application Process

The process to obtain a Slovakia crypto license involves several stages. Consulting24 advises and coordinates the application, ensuring all documents are in order.

  1. Pre-application preparation: Define your business model, service classes, and corporate structure. Prepare a detailed business plan, AML/CFT policies, risk assessment, and governance framework.
  2. Company incorporation: Register a legal entity in Slovakia (typically a limited liability company - s.r.o.) with a registered office and local directors or representatives.
  3. Capital deposit: Open a corporate bank account in Slovakia and deposit the required minimum capital. The account must be in the company's name.
  4. Submit application: File the complete application package with the NBS, including all policies, financial statements, and personal questionnaires for key personnel.
  5. NBS review: The NBS assesses the application for completeness and compliance. They may request additional information or clarifications.
  6. On-site inspection (if applicable): For complex cases, the NBS may conduct an on-site visit to verify operational readiness.
  7. Approval: Once satisfied, the NBS issues the license. You can then commence operations in Slovakia and passport to other EU states.

The entire process typically takes 3-6 months. For a faster alternative, consider Panama with a flat EUR 6,000 fee and no minimum capital, though without EU passporting. Consulting24 can manage the entire application on your behalf, from document preparation to liaising with the NBS.

Compliance and Trust Requirements

Maintaining the license requires ongoing compliance. Key obligations include:

  • AML/CFT: Implement strong KYC procedures, transaction monitoring, and suspicious activity reporting to the Slovak Financial Intelligence Unit.
  • Governance: Appoint a compliance officer, a money laundering reporting officer (MLRO), and ensure fit-and-proper requirements for management.
  • Reporting: Submit periodic reports to the NBS on financial health, transaction volumes, and AML metrics.
  • Audit: Annual financial audit by an approved auditor.
  • Capital maintenance: Always hold the minimum capital in liquid assets.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Many applicants face delays or rejections due to avoidable errors. Common pitfalls include:

  • Incomplete documentation: Missing or poorly drafted AML policies, business plans, or risk assessments.
  • Underestimating capital requirements: Not depositing the correct amount or holding it in non-liquid assets.
  • Weak governance: Appointing unfit management or lacking a clear organisational structure.
  • Ignoring local presence: Failing to establish a physical office or local representatives in Slovakia.
  • Overlooking ongoing obligations: Not preparing for regular reporting and audits.
