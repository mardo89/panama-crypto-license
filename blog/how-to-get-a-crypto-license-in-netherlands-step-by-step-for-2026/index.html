What Is a Crypto License in Netherlands?

A crypto license in Netherlands is a regulatory authorisation from DNB that allows a company to provide crypto services legally. Under MiCA, which applies from 2026, the license is a CASP authorisation. It covers activities such as operating a trading platform, exchanging crypto for fiat, custody of crypto assets, and transfer services. The Dutch regime is known for its rigorous compliance standards, including anti-money laundering (AML) and terrorist financing prevention. The license is mandatory for any entity offering these services to Dutch residents or operating from the Netherlands.

Key features of the Dutch crypto license include a minimum capital requirement based on service type, ongoing reporting obligations, and fit and proper testing for management. The regulator expects a high level of operational resilience and consumer protection. Compared to other EU jurisdictions, the Netherlands has a mature regulatory approach, but also a longer processing time.

Who Needs a Crypto License in Netherlands?

Any business that provides crypto asset services to Dutch clients or is established in the Netherlands needs a license. This includes:

  • Crypto exchanges (centralised and decentralised if offering custodial services)
  • Brokers and market makers
  • Custodial wallet providers
  • Payment processors using crypto
  • ATMs and kiosks
  • Issuers of asset-referenced tokens (ARTs) or e-money tokens (EMTs) under MiCA

If your business is based outside the EU but targets Dutch customers, you may need a branch license or passporting under MiCA. The Netherlands also applies a travel rule for crypto transfers. License Type and Regulator

The regulator for crypto licensing in the Netherlands is De Nederlandsche Bank (DNB). DNB is also the central bank and prudential supervisor. Under MiCA, the license is a CASP authorisation, which replaces the previous registration regime. The license is valid across the EU via passporting. There is no separate license for different activities; instead, the authorisation covers a list of services, and capital requirements vary by service class.

The license is divided into three tiers under MiCA: EUR 50,000 for simple services like custody and transfer, EUR 125,000 for exchange and trading, and EUR 150,000 for operating a trading platform or dealing on own account. The Netherlands may impose additional requirements, such as a local office and a compliance officer. DNB also conducts a thorough assessment of the business plan, AML policies, and IT security.

Capital Requirement

The minimum capital requirement under MiCA for CASPs in the Netherlands is tiered by service class. As of 2026, the amounts are:

  • EUR 50,000 for custody and transfer of crypto assets
  • EUR 125,000 for exchange between crypto and fiat, or between crypto assets
  • EUR 150,000 for operating a trading platform or dealing on own account

These are minimums; DNB may require higher capital based on the risk profile and volume of transactions. The capital must be held as own funds and cannot be borrowed. Additionally, the company must maintain professional indemnity insurance or a guarantee. For comparison, Panama has no minimum capital requirement for a standard company, making it a low-cost alternative for certain business models.

Tax Treatment

The Netherlands has a favourable tax regime for crypto businesses. Corporate income tax is 25.8% (2026 rate) on profits, with a lower 15% rate for the first EUR 200,000 of profit. Crypto transactions are subject to VAT only if they involve fiat currency or goods; pure crypto-to-crypto trades are generally VAT-exempt. However, the Dutch tax authority (Belastingdienst) treats crypto as property, so capital gains tax applies to individuals. For businesses, profits from crypto activities are taxed as ordinary income.

There is no specific crypto tax law; general tax principles apply. Companies must report their crypto holdings and transactions. The Netherlands also has a withholding tax on dividends (15%) and a participation exemption for subsidiaries. For a tax-neutral alternative, Panama offers territorial taxation, meaning only income sourced in Panama is taxed, and no capital gains tax on crypto.

Allowed Activities Under the License

The Dutch crypto license allows a wide range of activities, including:

  • Operation of a crypto trading platform (order book or automated matching)
  • Exchange of crypto assets for fiat currency or other crypto assets
  • Custody and administration of crypto assets on behalf of clients
  • Transfer services (sending crypto from one address to another)
  • Reception and transmission of orders for crypto assets
  • Placement of crypto assets (underwriting or placing)
  • Advice on crypto assets
  • Portfolio management of crypto assets

Activities not covered include mining, protocol development, and non-custodial software. If your business involves issuing asset-referenced tokens (ARTs) or e-money tokens (EMTs), additional requirements under MiCA apply. The license is comprehensive and allows passporting across the EU.

Step-by-Step Application Process

Obtaining a crypto license in Netherlands involves several stages. Here is a typical step-by-step process:

  1. Pre-application assessment: Evaluate your business model, determine the required capital, and prepare a business plan. Consulting24 can assist with this initial review.
  2. Company incorporation: Set up a Dutch BV (private limited company) with a registered office in the Netherlands. This is mandatory.
  3. Appoint management: Ensure directors and key personnel pass fit and proper tests. At least one director must be based in the Netherlands.
  4. Develop policies: Create AML/CFT policies, risk management framework, IT security plan, and business continuity plan.
  5. Engage external experts: Hire a legal advisor and an auditor for the application. DNB requires a legal opinion and an audit of IT systems.
  6. Submit application: File the application with DNB, including all required documents and fees. DNB will acknowledge receipt and start the assessment.
  7. Review and interviews: DNB may request additional information, conduct interviews with management, and perform on-site inspections.
  8. Approval: If satisfied, DNB grants the license. You will be listed on the DNB register.
  9. Ongoing compliance: Submit regular reports, maintain capital, and update policies as needed.

The entire process typically takes 6-12 months. For a faster alternative, consider Lithuania or Estonia, where Consulting24 delivers directly.

Compliance and Ongoing Obligations

After obtaining the license, you must comply with ongoing obligations. These include:

  • AML/CFT reporting: Submit suspicious transaction reports (STRs) to the Financial Intelligence Unit (FIU-Nederland).
  • Travel rule: Implement measures to share originator and beneficiary information for crypto transfers above EUR 1,000.
  • Capital maintenance: Always hold the required minimum capital and report any shortfall.
  • Audits: Annual financial audits and periodic IT security audits.
  • Fit and proper: Notify DNB of any changes in management or ownership.
  • Consumer protection: Provide clear information to clients, handle complaints, and ensure segregation of client assets.

Non-compliance can result in fines, suspension, or revocation of the license. This is general guidance, not legal advice. Consulting24 can help you set up compliance frameworks.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Many applicants make avoidable errors. Here are common pitfalls:

  • Incomplete application: Missing documents or insufficient detail can delay the process. Ensure all required annexes are included.
  • Underestimating capital: Applying with the minimum capital without considering operational risks may lead to rejection.
  • Weak AML policies: DNB scrutinises AML/CFT frameworks. Use templates from reputable sources and tailor them to your business.
  • Ignoring fit and proper: Directors with criminal records or poor credit history may fail the test. Check beforehand.
  • No local presence: DNB requires a physical office and at least one director resident in the Netherlands. Relying on virtual offices may not suffice.
  • Rushing the process: Expect 6-12 months. Trying to expedite can lead to errors.

Frequently asked questions

Do I need a physical office in the Netherlands to get a crypto license?

Yes, DNB requires a registered office in the Netherlands. A virtual office may not suffice; you need a physical presence where records are kept and where DNB can conduct inspections. At least one director must be based in the Netherlands.

Can I passport my Dutch crypto license to other EU countries?

Yes, under MiCA, a CASP license issued by DNB is valid across all EU/EEA member states through passporting. You can offer services in other countries without obtaining additional licenses, but you must notify the host regulator.

What is the minimum capital for a crypto exchange in the Netherlands?

For operating a trading platform or dealing on own account, the minimum capital is EUR 150,000. For exchange services (crypto-to-fiat or crypto-to-crypto), it is EUR 125,000. Custody and transfer services require EUR 50,000.

How long does the application process take?

Typically 6 to 12 months. DNB has a statutory period of 13 weeks for initial assessment, but complex cases, requests for additional information, and interviews can extend the timeline.

Is there a difference between a Dutch crypto license and a MiCA license?

No, as of 2026, the Dutch crypto license is a MiCA CASP authorisation. DNB is the competent authority. The license is harmonised with EU standards, so it is essentially the same as a MiCA license.

Can I apply for a Dutch crypto license if my company is already registered in another EU country?

If your company is based in another EU member state and holds a CASP license there, you can passport into the Netherlands. If you want to establish a branch in the Netherlands, you may need to register with DNB.

What are the ongoing costs after obtaining the license?

Annual compliance costs include legal and audit fees (EUR 10,000-30,000), AML officer salary, IT security maintenance, and DNB supervision fees (estimated EUR 5,000-15,000 per year). Total ongoing costs can be EUR 20,000-50,000 annually.

Does the Netherlands tax crypto-to-crypto transactions?

For businesses, profits from crypto-to-crypto trades are subject to corporate income tax (25.8% standard rate). However, VAT does not apply to pure crypto-to-crypto transactions. Crypto-to-fiat trades may be subject to VAT.

Can I use a Dutch crypto license to operate a DeFi platform?

If the DeFi platform involves custody of user funds or order matching, it likely requires a license. Fully decentralised, non-custodial protocols may fall outside the scope, but DNB assesses on a case-by-case basis. Seek legal advice.

What happens if I operate without a license in the Netherlands?

Operating without a license is illegal and can result in fines, criminal prosecution, and closure of the business. DNB actively monitors the market. It is essential to obtain proper authorisation before offering services.
