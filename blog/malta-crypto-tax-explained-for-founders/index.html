What Is Malta Crypto Tax?

Malta crypto tax refers to the tax treatment of income and gains from cryptocurrency transactions under Maltese law. The country applies a principles-based approach, meaning tax liability depends on the nature of the activity (trading vs. investment) and the taxpayer's status. For companies holding a Malta crypto license, the effective corporate tax rate can be as low as 5% after refunds, and capital gains on long-term holdings (over 1 year) are generally exempt from tax.

Key features include:

  • No capital gains tax on disposals of crypto held as a long-term investment (over 1 year).
  • Corporate income tax at standard 35%, but refunds reduce effective rate to 5% for trading income.
  • No VAT on crypto-to-crypto trades or exchange services.
  • Stamp duty exemptions for transfers of crypto assets.
  • No withholding tax on dividends paid to non-resident shareholders, subject to substance requirements.

This regime makes Malta attractive for both operational and holding companies in the crypto space. However, the tax benefits are conditional on meeting substance requirements and proper classification of income. For example, a company that frequently trades crypto for short-term profit will be taxed as trading income at 35% (refundable to 5%), while a company holding Bitcoin for 2 years and then selling would likely qualify for the capital gains exemption.

Founders of crypto businesses, investors, and service providers who are considering a Malta crypto license or already operating in Malta need to grasp the tax implications. Specifically:

Even if you are not tax resident in Malta, a Maltese company is subject to corporate tax on its worldwide income, so understanding the rules is critical for compliance and planning. License Type & Regulator

Malta's crypto regulatory framework is known as the Virtual Financial Assets (VFA) regime, overseen by the Malta Financial Services Authority (MFSA). Companies must obtain a VFA license to provide crypto services such as exchange, custody, and wallet management. The license types include:

  • Class 1 (Advisory): Providing advice on VFAs, no handling of client funds.
  • Class 2 (Brokerage): Executing orders on behalf of clients, but not holding client funds.
  • Class 3 (Exchange): Operating a trading platform and holding client funds.
  • Class 4 (Full Service): All activities including custody, exchange, and advisory.

For tax purposes, the license classification does not directly change the tax treatment, but the nature of the income (fee vs. trading) may affect how it is taxed. The MFSA ensures that licensed entities comply with anti-money laundering (AML) and conduct-of-business rules, which in turn supports tax transparency. As of 2026, Malta's VFA regime is fully aligned with MiCA, meaning capital requirements and operational standards match EU-wide rules.

Capital Requirement

Under Malta's VFA regime, the minimum capital requirement depends on the license class: Class 1 (advisory) requires EUR 50,000, Class 2 (brokerage) requires EUR 125,000, Class 3 (exchange) requires EUR 150,000, and Class 4 (full service) requires EUR 150,000. These amounts must be fully paid up and maintained as regulatory capital. For comparison, under the EU's MiCA framework (fully in force in 2026), capital tiers are similar: EUR 50,000 for simple services, EUR 125,000 for exchange and custody, and EUR 150,000 for full-service providers. Malta aligns with these standards.

It is important to note that the capital must be held in liquid assets, such as cash or highly liquid securities, and cannot be used for operational expenses. The capital serves as a buffer to protect clients and ensure solvency. For example, a Class 3 exchange must maintain EUR 150,000 in a segregated account at all times. Failure to maintain the required capital can result in license suspension.

Tax Treatment of Crypto in Malta

Malta's tax treatment of crypto is favourable for long-term holders and trading companies. The key rules are:

  • Capital gains: Gains from the disposal of crypto held as a capital asset (investment) are exempt from tax if the holding period exceeds 1 year. Short-term trading gains are treated as ordinary income.
  • Corporate income tax: Standard rate is 35%, but through Malta's full imputation system, shareholders can claim refunds, reducing the effective rate to 5% for trading income. The refund is typically 6/7 of the tax paid.
  • VAT: Crypto-to-crypto exchanges and transaction fees are exempt from VAT. Fiat-to-crypto exchanges may be subject to VAT at 18% on the spread, but this is often treated as a financial service exemption under the EU VAT directive.
  • Stamp duty: Transfers of crypto assets are exempt from stamp duty.
  • Withholding tax: No withholding tax on dividends paid to non-resident shareholders, provided the company has sufficient substance and the shareholder is not resident in a blacklisted jurisdiction.

These rules make Malta one of the most tax-efficient EU jurisdictions for crypto businesses. However, careful structuring is required to ensure compliance with substance requirements and anti-avoidance rules. For example, a company that only holds crypto as an investment and does not trade would pay no corporate tax on gains, but must still file annual returns. Allowed Activities Under a Malta Crypto License

A Malta VFA license permits a range of activities, including:

  • Operating a crypto exchange (order book or OTC).
  • Providing custody and wallet services.
  • Brokerage and advisory services for VFAs.
  • Portfolio management involving VFAs.
  • Initial VFA offerings (IVFAOs) - Malta's version of ICOs.
  • Market making and liquidity provision.

Each license class restricts activities: Class 1 can only advise, Class 2 can broker but not hold client funds, Class 3 can operate an exchange and hold funds, and Class 4 can do all activities. The tax treatment of income from these activities follows the general rules above. For instance, fees from advisory services are taxed as trading income at 5% effective rate, while gains from proprietary trading (if held long-term) may be exempt. It is crucial to document the nature of each income stream for tax reporting.

Step-by-Step Process to Obtain a Malta Crypto License

  1. Pre-application: Prepare business plan, AML policies, risk assessment, and corporate structure. Engage a local law firm and compliance advisor. This stage typically takes 1-2 months.
  2. Company incorporation: Register a Malta company with a minimum share capital (usually EUR 1,200 for a private limited company). Appoint directors and a company secretary. The company must have a registered office in Malta.
  3. Submit application to MFSA: Complete the VFA license application form, including detailed information on beneficial owners, source of funds, and systems. The application fee is paid upfront.
  4. Review and due diligence: MFSA conducts fit-and-proper checks on directors and shareholders. Expect interviews and requests for clarifications. This phase can take 3-6 months.
  5. Approval and licensing: If satisfied, MFSA grants the license. The process typically takes 6-12 months from start to finish.
  6. Post-licensing: Maintain ongoing compliance, submit annual returns, and pay supervisory fees. The MFSA conducts periodic inspections.

Banking & Payment Solutions for Crypto Firms in Malta

  • Traditional banks like Bank of Valletta (BOV) and HSBC Malta, but they require a strong relationship and compliance history. They may ask for a minimum deposit of EUR 10,000 and a detailed business plan.
  • EMIs such as Revolut Business, Monese, and Paysera, which offer faster onboarding with lower minimums. Revolut Business, for example, can open an account within days for licensed firms.
  • Fiat-to-crypto on-ramps like MoonPay or Banxa integrated into the platform for direct user transactions.
  • Payment processors like Checkout.com or Stripe (with crypto-specific approval) for merchant services.

It is advisable to open a bank account before applying for the license, as proof of banking is often required. Benefits of Malta Crypto Tax Regime

  • Low effective tax rate: 5% on trading income after refunds, one of the lowest in the EU.
  • No capital gains tax on long-term crypto holdings (over 1 year), encouraging investment.
  • VAT exemptions for crypto-to-crypto trades, reducing operational costs.
  • No withholding tax on dividends to non-residents, facilitating profit repatriation.
  • Double tax treaties with over 70 countries, reducing cross-border taxation and avoiding double taxation.
  • EU membership provides passporting rights for services across the EU, allowing a Malta-licensed firm to serve clients in other EU states without additional licenses.
  • Credibility with partners, investors, and regulators due to MFSA oversight and compliance with MiCA.
  • Flexibility in structuring: holding companies, trading entities, and IP companies can all benefit from the tax regime.

These benefits make Malta a strong contender for founders who prioritise tax efficiency and regulatory clarity. However, the regime requires ongoing compliance and substance, which can be a barrier for smaller startups.

Compliance & Trust: Staying on the Right Side of the Law

Malta's tax authorities (the Commissioner for Revenue) and the MFSA expect full transparency. Compliance requirements include:

  • Maintaining proper accounting records and filing annual tax returns within 9 months of the financial year-end.
  • Conducting AML/CFT checks on customers and reporting suspicious transactions to the Financial Intelligence Analysis Unit (FIAU).
  • Ensuring substance: the company must have a physical office, local directors, and employees in Malta. A minimum of 2 directors, one of whom must be resident in Malta.
  • Adhering to MiCA standards, which are now fully in force across the EU, including capital adequacy, custody rules, and consumer protection.
  • Appointing a local auditor and filing audited financial statements.

Failure to comply can result in fines, license revocation, or criminal charges. For example, in 2025, the MFSA revoked the license of a VFA firm for failing to maintain adequate AML controls. Common Mistakes in Malta Crypto Tax Planning

  • Misclassifying income: Treating trading gains as capital gains to avoid tax, when they are actually business income. The tax authority looks at frequency, intention, and holding period. Frequent trading within 1 year is almost always considered trading income.
  • Ignoring substance requirements: Having a mailbox company without real operations can lead to denial of tax benefits and even license revocation. The MFSA expects a physical office, local staff, and board meetings in Malta.
  • Overlooking VAT: Fiat-to-crypto exchanges may be subject to VAT at 18% on the spread; failing to charge it can result in liabilities and penalties. Many startups assume all crypto transactions are VAT-exempt, which is not always true.
  • Not claiming refunds: The 5% effective rate requires shareholders to claim refunds of imputed tax within a specific timeframe (usually 2 years). Missing the deadline forfeits the benefit.
  • Assuming all crypto is treated the same: Stablecoins, utility tokens, and security tokens may have different tax treatments. For example, stablecoin staking rewards may be treated as income, while capital gains on utility tokens may be exempt if held long-term.
  • Underestimating compliance costs: Annual compliance fees can reach EUR 25,000 or more, which can eat into profits for smaller firms.

Frequently asked questions

Is Malta crypto tax really 0% on capital gains?

Yes, for long-term holdings. If you hold crypto as a capital asset for more than 1 year and then dispose of it, the gain is exempt from tax. However, if you trade frequently or the crypto is part of your business inventory, it is treated as trading income and taxed at 35% (refundable to 5%). The distinction depends on facts and circumstances.

How does the 5% effective tax rate work?

A Malta company pays corporate tax at 35% on its trading income. When the company distributes dividends to shareholders, the shareholders can claim a refund of 6/7 of the tax paid, reducing the effective rate to 5%. The refund is typically processed within 6 months of the dividend payment.

Do I need a Malta crypto license to benefit from the tax regime?

Not necessarily. Any Malta company can benefit from the tax regime, but if you provide crypto services to the public, you must obtain a VFA license from the MFSA. Holding crypto as an investment does not require a license. However, licensing adds credibility and allows you to operate legally within the EU.

What is the minimum capital requirement for a Malta crypto license?

It depends on the license class: EUR 50,000 for Class 1 (advisory), EUR 125,000 for Class 2 (brokerage), and EUR 150,000 for Class 3 (exchange) and Class 4 (full service). These amounts must be fully paid up and maintained as regulatory capital.

Can I use a Malta company to hold my personal crypto investments?

Yes, but you must consider substance requirements. The company should have a bank account, local directors, and a registered office. If the company only holds crypto and does not trade, the gains are exempt from tax. However, you should consult a tax advisor to ensure the structure is not considered a sham.

Is VAT applicable on crypto transactions in Malta?

Crypto-to-crypto exchanges and transaction fees are exempt from VAT. Fiat-to-crypto exchanges may be subject to VAT at 18% on the spread, but this is often treated as a financial service exemption under EU law. It is advisable to get a binding ruling from the tax authority for your specific case.

How long does it take to get a Malta crypto license?

The process typically takes 6 to 12 months from application to approval. This includes company incorporation, preparing the application, MFSA review, and fit-and-proper checks. The timeline can be shorter if the application is complete and the business model is straightforward.

What are the ongoing compliance costs for a Malta crypto company?

Annual compliance costs include audit fees (EUR 5,000-10,000), MFSA supervisory fees (EUR 5,000-15,000), company secretary and registered office (EUR 2,000-5,000), and AML/CFT compliance (EUR 3,000-8,000). Total annual costs typically range from EUR 15,000 to EUR 35,000.

Can I open a bank account for a Malta crypto company?

Yes, but it can be challenging. Traditional banks like BOV and HSBC require a strong compliance history. EMIs like Revolut Business and Paysera are more accessible. It is best to start the banking process early, ideally before applying for the license.

How does Malta compare to Panama for crypto businesses?

Panama offers a flat EUR 6,000 setup cost, no license requirement, and 0% tax on foreign-source income. Malta has higher costs (EUR 30,000+) and requires a license, but provides EU passporting, lower effective tax on trading income (5%), and greater credibility. Panama is better for non-EU facing businesses, while Malta suits those targeting the EU market.

What happens if I don't meet the substance requirements in Malta?

Failure to meet substance requirements can lead to denial of tax benefits, such as the 5% effective rate or the capital gains exemption. The MFSA may also revoke your license. Substance includes having a physical office, local directors, and employees in Malta.
