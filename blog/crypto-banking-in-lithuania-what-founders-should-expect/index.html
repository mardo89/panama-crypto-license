What Is Crypto Banking in Lithuania?

Crypto banking in Lithuania refers to the ability for crypto asset service providers (CASPs) to open and maintain bank accounts with Lithuanian financial institutions, enabling fiat on/off ramps, payment processing, and treasury management. Under MiCA, which is fully in force in 2026, crypto firms must be licensed to offer services like exchange, custody, and transfer. Lithuania's central bank, the Bank of Lithuania, regulates these activities and has established a framework that allows licensed firms to access banking services more easily than in many other EU states.

Key aspects include:

  • Licensed CASPs can apply for bank accounts with local banks such as Revolut, Swedbank, or SEB.
  • The regulatory environment is transparent, with clear capital requirements and compliance obligations.
  • Lithuania is part of the Single Euro Payments Area (SEPA), facilitating fast and low-cost euro transfers.
  • Banks often require a minimum deposit (typically EUR 10,000-50,000) and a proven track record of compliance.

For example, a typical setup involves a Lithuanian UAB (private limited company) holding a CASP license, with a business account at Swedbank that supports SEPA instant payments. This allows your exchange to process customer deposits and withdrawals in euros within seconds, a critical feature for user experience.

Who Needs Crypto Banking in Lithuania?

Crypto banking in Lithuania is essential for any founder planning to operate a crypto exchange, wallet service, or other CASP within the EU. Specifically, you need it if:

  • You are launching a centralized exchange (CEX) or decentralized exchange (DEX) with fiat integration.
  • You offer custodial wallet services where you hold client funds.
  • You provide crypto-to-fiat conversion services.
  • You require a business bank account to pay salaries, rent, or suppliers.
  • You want to accept payments from EU customers via SEPA or credit card.

Even if your primary market is outside the EU, having a Lithuanian license and bank account can enhance credibility and simplify cross-border payments. For a broader overview of licensing options, see our Panama page or explore other jurisdictions.

Founders who operate purely non-custodial wallets or DeFi protocols may not need a license, but if you handle client funds or facilitate fiat transactions, banking in Lithuania becomes a regulatory necessity.

License Type and Regulator

The relevant license is the Crypto Asset Service Provider (CASP) license, issued by the Bank of Lithuania. This license authorizes firms to provide one or more of the following services under MiCA:

  • Exchange of crypto assets for fiat currency.
  • Exchange of crypto assets for other crypto assets.
  • Custody and administration of crypto assets on behalf of clients.
  • Transfer of crypto assets.
  • Execution of orders on behalf of clients.
  • Placement of crypto assets (underwriting).

The Bank of Lithuania is known for its pragmatic approach, but it requires strong AML/KYC procedures, a local presence (registered office and management), and compliance with MiCA's capital requirements. The regulator conducts interviews with key management and may request additional documentation if the business model is complex. For a full breakdown of the application process, see our Lithuania crypto license page.

It is important to note that the Bank of Lithuania does not license mining, non-custodial software development, or pure DeFi protocols. If your business involves these, you may not need a license, but legal advice is recommended.

Capital Requirement

Under MiCA, capital requirements depend on the services offered. For Lithuania, the minimum initial capital is:

  • EUR 50,000 for simple exchange services (crypto-to-fiat or crypto-to-crypto).
  • EUR 125,000 for custody services.
  • EUR 150,000 for combined services (exchange + custody).

These amounts must be held in a bank account in Lithuania and cannot be withdrawn without regulatory approval. The capital must be maintained throughout the license period. For comparison, Panama has no minimum capital requirement for its crypto license, which is a flat EUR 6,000. However, Panama does not offer EU passporting, so the choice depends on your target market.

It is important to note that the capital must be fully paid up in cash before the license is granted. Some founders mistakenly use crypto or other assets, which is not accepted. Consulting24 can advise on structuring your capital efficiently.

Tax Treatment

Lithuania has a competitive tax regime for crypto businesses. The corporate income tax rate is 15% (5% for small companies with under EUR 300,000 revenue and fewer than 10 employees). Crypto-to-crypto transactions are generally exempt from VAT, and capital gains from crypto trading are taxed as corporate income. However, specific tax treatment depends on the nature of your activities (e.g., mining, trading, staking).

For example, if your exchange charges trading fees, those fees are subject to VAT at the standard rate of 21%. But the exchange of crypto for fiat is exempt from VAT. Staking rewards are typically treated as income. It is crucial to engage a local tax advisor to structure your operations efficiently. Consulting24 can recommend trusted partners for tax compliance.

Lithuania also has a participation exemption for dividends received from subsidiaries, which can be beneficial for holding structures.

Allowed Activities

With a Lithuanian CASP license, you can perform the following activities:

  • Exchange services (fiat-to-crypto, crypto-to-fiat, crypto-to-crypto).
  • Custodial wallet services.
  • Crypto asset transfers.
  • Order execution on behalf of clients.
  • Placement of crypto assets (underwriting).
  • Advice on crypto assets (if registered separately).

Activities such as crypto mining, non-custodial wallet development, or DeFi protocol operation may not require a license, but legal advice is recommended. For a full list, see the Bank of Lithuania's guidelines. It is also possible to offer ancillary services like staking as a service, but these may fall under custody or investment advice depending on the structure.

Step-by-Step Process

Obtaining crypto banking in Lithuania involves several steps:

  1. Company Formation: Register a Lithuanian private limited liability company (UAB) with a registered office and local director. The company must have a physical address in Lithuania.
  2. Prepare Documentation: Draft AML/KYC policies, business plan, risk assessment, and internal controls. These must be tailored to your specific business model.
  3. Submit License Application: File with the Bank of Lithuania, including all required documents and proof of capital. The application is submitted electronically via the regulator's portal.
  4. Regulatory Review: The Bank of Lithuania reviews the application, may request clarifications, and conducts interviews with management. This stage typically takes 2-4 months.
  5. License Approval: Once approved, you receive the CASP license and can begin operations. You must then notify the regulator of any changes to your business.
  6. Open Bank Account: Apply for a business bank account with a Lithuanian bank, providing your license and compliance documentation. Banks may require a personal meeting with directors.

Access to banking is a major advantage of Lithuania. Licensed CASPs can open accounts with major banks like Swedbank, SEB, and Revolut Business. These banks offer SEPA transfers, multi-currency accounts, and payment processing. However, banks conduct their own due diligence, so having a clean compliance record is essential. Some banks may require a minimum deposit or turnover.

For example, Swedbank typically requires a minimum monthly turnover of EUR 50,000 and a detailed business plan. Revolut Business is more flexible but may limit transaction volumes initially. Compliance and Trust

Compliance is critical for maintaining your license and banking relationships. Key requirements include:

  • Appointing a local AML officer who is resident in Lithuania.
  • Conducting ongoing transaction monitoring using automated tools.
  • Reporting suspicious transactions to the Financial Crime Investigation Service (FCIS) within 24 hours.
  • Annual audits by a licensed auditor, submitted to the Bank of Lithuania.
  • Maintaining minimum capital at all times and notifying the regulator of any changes.

Failure to comply can result in fines up to EUR 100,000 or license revocation. For example, in 2024, a Lithuanian CASP had its license suspended for failing to report suspicious transactions. Common Mistakes

Founders often make these mistakes when pursuing crypto banking in Lithuania:

  • Underestimating Capital Requirements: Ensure you have the full capital amount available in cash before applying. Some founders try to use crypto assets, which is not accepted.
  • Incomplete Documentation: Missing AML policies or business plans can delay approval by months. Use templates from experienced consultants.
  • Choosing the Wrong Bank: Not all Lithuanian banks are crypto-friendly; research which ones accept CASPs. For example, some banks refuse to open accounts for crypto firms altogether.
  • Ignoring Tax Obligations: Failing to register for VAT or corporate tax can lead to penalties. Lithuania has a VAT registration threshold of EUR 45,000, but crypto exchanges often exceed this quickly.
  • Relying on Unregulated Advisors: Use experienced consultants like Consulting24 to avoid costly errors. Some advisors promise fast approvals but fail to deliver.
  • Not Preparing for Bank Due Diligence: Banks will scrutinize your source of funds, business model, and compliance history. Be ready to provide detailed explanations.

Frequently asked questions

What is the minimum capital for a crypto license in Lithuania?

Under MiCA, the minimum capital is EUR 50,000 for exchange services, EUR 125,000 for custody, and EUR 150,000 for combined services. This capital must be held in a Lithuanian bank account and maintained throughout the license period.

How long does it take to get a crypto license in Lithuania?

The process typically takes 3-6 months from company formation to license approval. Banking account opening can add 1-2 months. Delays may occur if documentation is incomplete or if the regulator requests additional information.

Can I open a bank account in Lithuania with a crypto license?

Yes, licensed CASPs can open accounts with banks like Swedbank, SEB, and Revolut Business. However, banks conduct their own due diligence, so a clean compliance record and strong business plan are essential.

What are the tax rates for crypto businesses in Lithuania?

Corporate income tax is 15% (5% for small companies with under EUR 300,000 revenue and fewer than 10 employees). Crypto-to-crypto transactions are VAT-exempt, but trading fees are subject to 21% VAT. Staking rewards are taxed as income.

Do I need a local director for a Lithuanian crypto company?

Yes, the company must have a local director who is a resident of Lithuania or another EU/EEA country. The director can be a nominee provided by a service provider like Consulting24.

Can I passport my Lithuanian license to other EU countries?

Yes, under MiCA, a Lithuanian CASP license allows you to provide services across the EU through passporting. You must notify the Bank of Lithuania of your intention to operate in other member states.

What happens if I fail to maintain the minimum capital?

Failure to maintain the required capital can result in fines, suspension, or revocation of your license. The Bank of Lithuania monitors capital levels through annual audits and may request additional information at any time.

Is it possible to use a Lithuanian license for a non-EU business?

Yes, many non-EU founders establish a Lithuanian subsidiary to access the EU market. The license is issued to the Lithuanian entity, which can then serve EU customers. You will need a local presence and management.

What are the ongoing compliance costs for a Lithuanian CASP?

Ongoing costs include AML officer salary (or outsourced service), annual audit fees, regulatory filing fees, and transaction monitoring software. Typical annual costs range from EUR 5,000 to EUR 15,000 depending on complexity.
