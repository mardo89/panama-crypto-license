What Are AML/KYC Requirements for a Poland Crypto Company?

AML (Anti-Money Laundering) and KYC (Know Your Customer) requirements are regulatory obligations that crypto companies must follow to prevent financial crime. In Poland, these rules are derived from EU directives and the Polish AML Act. They require businesses to verify customer identities, monitor transactions, report suspicious activity, and maintain records. For a Poland crypto company, compliance is mandatory for all CASPs, including exchanges, wallet providers, and custodians.

The key components include customer due diligence (CDD), enhanced due diligence (EDD) for high-risk customers, ongoing transaction monitoring, and reporting to the General Inspector of Financial Information (GIIF). Poland also requires a local AML compliance officer and a documented AML program. As of 2026, Poland has fully integrated MiCA, so the requirements are harmonized with other EU states but with local nuances. For example, Poland imposes stricter rules on privacy coins and anonymous transactions. Failure to comply can result in fines up to EUR 5 million or 10% of annual turnover, and in serious cases, criminal liability for directors. Many founders find the burden heavy and explore alternatives like Panama, where AML requirements are lighter and no capital is required.

To illustrate, a Poland crypto exchange must collect and verify the identity of every customer using a government-issued ID, proof of address, and in some cases a selfie or video verification. Transactions above EUR 1,000 trigger automatic reporting to GIIF. The AML program must include a risk assessment, policies on politically exposed persons (PEPs), and a transaction monitoring system that flags unusual patterns. Record keeping is required for at least 5 years after the business relationship ends.

Who Needs to Comply?

Any entity providing crypto asset services in Poland must comply with AML/KYC requirements. This includes:

  • Crypto exchanges (fiat-to-crypto and crypto-to-crypto)
  • Wallet providers (custodial and non-custodial if offering services)
  • Custodians and asset managers
  • Brokers and dealers
  • ATMs and payment processors
  • ICO/STO platforms (if offering secondary market services)

Even foreign companies targeting Polish customers may need to register if they actively solicit Polish clients. The KNF requires a physical presence in Poland or an EU branch, meaning a registered office and local management. This can be a barrier for startups. If you are a founder looking to minimize compliance burden, Panama offers a lighter regulatory touch with no capital requirement and a flat fee of EUR 6,000, and no need for local physical presence.

Non-compliance by unregistered entities can lead to criminal penalties, including imprisonment for up to 5 years. The KNF actively monitors the market and cooperates with the Polish police and Europol. For example, in 2025, several unregistered crypto ATMs were seized and operators fined. Therefore, it is critical to assess whether your business model triggers Polish licensing obligations.

License Type and Regulator

The regulator for crypto activities in Poland is the Polish Financial Supervision Authority (KNF). The license is called a CASP registration under the MiCA framework. Unlike the old VASP register, MiCA imposes minimum capital requirements: EUR 50,000 for simple services (e.g., wallet provision), EUR 125,000 for exchanges, and EUR 150,000 for custodians. The KNF also requires a detailed AML/CFT program, a local compliance officer, and audited financial statements.

Poland does not have a separate crypto license; it is a registration regime. However, the KNF has the power to reject applications based on AML deficiencies, and the process can be slow. The KNF also imposes fit and proper requirements on directors and shareholders. They must have a clean criminal record and relevant experience. The application must include a business plan, financial projections, and a detailed description of the AML/CFT internal controls. The KNF may request additional information and conduct interviews with management.

Capital Requirement

Under MiCA, Poland requires minimum capital based on service type: EUR 50,000 for basic services (e.g., wallet provision), EUR 125,000 for exchange services, and EUR 150,000 for custody. This capital must be maintained as equity and cannot be withdrawn, and it must be fully paid up before registration. In contrast, Panama has no minimum capital requirement for crypto licenses, making it an attractive option for startups. Consulting24 can help you structure your capital efficiently, whether in Poland or elsewhere.

The capital must be held in a Polish bank account and cannot be used for operational expenses. It serves as a buffer against losses and must be maintained throughout the license period. If the company's equity falls below the requirement, the KNF can suspend or revoke the registration. For example, a crypto exchange must always have at least EUR 125,000 in equity. This can be a significant hurdle for early-stage companies.

Tax Treatment

Poland imposes a 19% corporate income tax (CIT) on worldwide profits, plus VAT (23%) on services. Crypto transactions may be subject to capital gains tax for individuals (19% on gains). Poland has a tax treaty network but no specific crypto tax exemptions. Additionally, Poland has a withholding tax on dividends (19%, reduced under treaties). Panama, by contrast, has territorial taxation (no tax on foreign-source income) and no capital gains tax for crypto. This is a significant advantage for international founders. For example, a Panama company earning crypto trading profits from non-Panamanian clients pays 0% tax, while a Poland company would pay 19% CIT.

Poland also requires transfer pricing documentation for related-party transactions, which adds administrative burden. VAT on crypto services is complex: the Polish Ministry of Finance has issued guidance that crypto-to-fiat exchanges are exempt from VAT, but wallet services may be subject to VAT. Consulting24 recommends engaging a local tax advisor for Poland. For Panama, the tax regime is simpler: no VAT, no capital gains tax, and no withholding tax on dividends paid to non-residents.

Allowed Activities

A Poland crypto license allows: exchange of crypto for fiat, exchange of crypto for crypto, custody and administration of crypto assets, operation of trading platforms, and transfer services. Activities must be strictly within the registered scope. Poland does not allow anonymous transactions or privacy coins without enhanced due diligence. The KNF also restricts certain activities like crypto derivatives without additional licensing. Panama allows a broader range of activities with fewer restrictions, including crypto-to-fiat, crypto-to-crypto, custody, and even DeFi services, as long as they are not regulated securities.

For example, a Poland-licensed exchange cannot offer margin trading or staking without separate authorization. Panama does not have such restrictions. Additionally, Poland prohibits the use of privacy coins like Monero, while Panama has no such ban. This makes Panama more flexible for innovative business models.

Step-by-Step Process

The process to obtain a Poland crypto license involves:

  1. Incorporate a Polish company (SP. z o.o.) with a registered office and a minimum share capital of PLN 5,000 (approx. EUR 1,100).
  2. Appoint a local AML compliance officer resident in Poland.
  3. Draft an AML/KYC policy and procedures manual tailored to your business model.
  4. Open a corporate bank account (can be challenging; see banking section).
  5. Submit the CASP registration application to KNF with all documents, including business plan, AML program, and financial projections.
  6. Undergo KNF review and address any queries (typically 2-3 months).
  7. Receive registration and commence operations.

Options include local cooperative banks (e.g., BNP Paribas, Alior Bank have limited crypto acceptance), EMI licenses, or payment processors like Billon or Xapo. Benefits of a Poland Crypto License

Benefits include: access to the EU single market, high regulatory credibility, and clear MiCA framework. Poland also has a skilled workforce and relatively lower costs compared to Western Europe. The license allows you to passport services to other EU states. However, the AML/KYC burden is heavy, and the capital requirement can be a barrier for startups. For founders seeking minimal compliance, Panama offers a lighter regime with no capital requirement and territorial tax. Estonia also offers a mature ecosystem with EUR 12,000 capital, but Poland is more cost-effective for companies needing EU access.

Poland's regulatory clarity under MiCA is a double-edged sword: it provides certainty but also imposes strict obligations. For example, the KNF has published detailed guidelines on AML/CFT for CASPs, which reduces ambiguity but increases compliance costs. Companies that can afford the compliance overhead benefit from a reputable EU license that is recognized across the bloc.

Compliance and Trust

Ongoing compliance includes: transaction monitoring, periodic CDD reviews, annual AML audits, and reporting to GIIF. Poland requires a local compliance officer and annual AML training. Failure to comply can lead to fines up to EUR 5 million or 10% of turnover, and in serious cases, imprisonment. For a less burdensome compliance environment, Panama requires only basic AML procedures and no local officer.

Poland also requires the appointment of an external auditor for AML compliance. The auditor must be approved by the KNF and will review the company's AML program annually. This adds another layer of cost and complexity. In Panama, there is no mandatory AML audit for crypto companies, only a requirement to maintain basic records and report suspicious transactions.

Common Mistakes

Common mistakes include: underestimating the AML program complexity, not appointing a local compliance officer in time, using a virtual office (KNF requires physical presence), and failing to maintain proper records. Also, many founders assume Poland is cheap, but legal fees add up. For example, a founder who tried to DIY the AML program was rejected by KNF and had to reapply, costing an extra 6 months. Panama avoids many of these pitfalls: no capital, no local officer, and a flat fee.

Another mistake is ignoring the need for a Polish bank account before applying. The KNF requires proof of a corporate bank account as part of the application. Some founders apply without a bank account, only to have the application suspended. Consulting24 advises clients to open the bank account in parallel with the application. In Panama, the bank account can be opened after the license is obtained, which is easier.
