What Is a Bahamas Crypto License?

The Bahamas crypto license, officially a Digital Asset Business Registration under the DARE Act, allows companies to legally operate digital asset exchanges, custody services, and other virtual asset activities. The Securities Commission of the Bahamas (SCB) regulates and supervises all registered entities. The regime is designed to balance innovation with investor protection, aligning with FATF recommendations.

Key features include a clear classification of digital assets, mandatory anti-money laundering (AML) compliance, and periodic reporting. The license is required for any business providing digital asset services to or from the Bahamas. As of 2026, the DARE Act is fully operational, with the SCB actively processing applications and conducting oversight.

Who Needs a Bahamas Crypto License?

Any entity offering the following services in or from the Bahamas must obtain a license:

  • Digital asset exchange (trading platform)
  • Digital asset custody or wallet services
  • Digital asset brokerage or dealing
  • Digital asset investment advice
  • Issuance of digital assets (including ICOs/STOs)
  • Any other service involving digital assets as defined by the SCB

Founders targeting the Caribbean or Latin American markets, or those seeking a reputable offshore jurisdiction with a clear regulatory framework, often choose the Bahamas. However, if your primary goal is cost efficiency and speed, Panama may be more suitable, with a flat EUR 6,000 fee and no capital requirement.

License Type and Regulator

The regulator is the Securities Commission of the Bahamas (SCB). Under the DARE Act, there are two main categories:

  • Class I (Registration): For businesses with limited activities or lower risk, subject to lighter requirements.
  • Class II (Full License): For full-scale digital asset exchanges, custodians, and other high-volume services. Most commercial operators apply for Class II.

The SCB also distinguishes between 'prescribed' and 'non-prescribed' digital assets. The license is non-transferable and must be renewed annually. Capital Requirement

The Bahamas DARE Act does not prescribe a fixed minimum capital amount for all license classes. Instead, the SCB assesses capital adequacy on a case-by-case basis, considering the nature, scale, and complexity of the business. Typically, applicants are expected to maintain sufficient capital to cover operational expenses for at least 6-12 months and to meet potential liabilities.

As a general guideline, Class II license applicants should anticipate a capital requirement in the range of $100,000 to $500,000, though this is not statutory. This is significantly higher than Panama (no minimum capital) or Lithuania (EUR 125,000 for exchange services under MiCA). Tax Treatment in the Bahamas

The Bahamas is a tax-neutral jurisdiction with no corporate income tax, no capital gains tax, no VAT, and no withholding tax. This makes it highly attractive for crypto businesses. The only direct taxes are stamp duties on certain documents and property taxes. However, companies must pay annual business license fees and registration fees to the SCB.

It is important to note that while the Bahamas offers favorable taxation, founders must consider their home country tax obligations. For EU-based founders, the Bahamas is not automatically tax-transparent, and proper structuring is essential. Allowed Activities Under a Bahamas Crypto License

A Bahamas crypto license (Class II) permits the following activities:

  • Operating a digital asset exchange (order book, matching engine)
  • Providing digital asset custody services
  • Brokerage and dealing in digital assets
  • Digital asset payment services
  • Issuance of digital assets (subject to additional requirements)
  • Investment advice on digital assets

The license does not automatically cover derivatives or securities tokens unless explicitly authorized. The SCB may impose restrictions based on the business model. For a broader scope, some founders combine a Bahamas license with an exchange license in another jurisdiction.

Step-by-Step Application Process

Obtaining a Bahamas crypto license involves the following steps:

  1. Pre-incorporation planning: Determine the legal structure (typically a Bahamas International Business Company or Limited Company).
  2. Company incorporation: Register the company with the Bahamas Registrar General. This requires a registered office and local director or agent.
  3. Prepare documentation: Draft a business plan, AML/KYC policies, risk assessment, financial projections, and governance framework.
  4. Appoint local service providers: Engage a Bahamas-based compliance officer, legal counsel, and auditor.
  5. Submit application to SCB: Complete the prescribed forms and pay the application fee.
  6. SCB review and due diligence: The SCB conducts background checks on directors, shareholders, and beneficial owners.
  7. Interview or additional information: The SCB may request clarifications or an in-person meeting.
  8. Approval and license issuance: Once satisfied, the SCB issues the registration certificate.
  9. Ongoing compliance: Submit periodic reports, renew annually, and maintain AML/CFT programs.

Opening a bank account for a Bahamas crypto entity can be challenging due to the perceived risk. However, the Bahamas has a growing number of banks that accept digital asset businesses, provided the company holds a valid SCB license. Options include:

  • Traditional Bahamian banks (e.g., Scotiabank Bahamas, CIBC FirstCaribbean) - require full due diligence and often a minimum deposit.

Compliance and Trust Considerations

Maintaining a Bahamas crypto license requires ongoing compliance with the DARE Act and AML/CFT regulations. Key obligations include:

  • Appointing a local AML compliance officer
  • Conducting customer due diligence (CDD) and enhanced due diligence (EDD) for high-risk clients
  • Reporting suspicious transactions to the Financial Intelligence Unit
  • Annual audit by a Bahamas-approved auditor
  • Submitting periodic financial and compliance reports to the SCB

Failure to comply can result in fines, suspension, or revocation of the license. This is general guidance, not legal advice. Consulting24 can help you design a compliance framework tailored to your business.

Common Mistakes When Applying

Founders often make these errors during the Bahamas crypto license application:

  • Incomplete documentation: Missing business plans or AML policies cause delays.
  • Underestimating capital: The SCB expects substantial capital; insufficient funds lead to rejection.
  • Ignoring local presence: A registered office and local director are mandatory.
  • Poor due diligence: Any adverse history of directors or shareholders can derail the application.
  • Choosing the wrong license class: Applying for Class I when Class II is needed (or vice versa) wastes time.

Frequently asked questions

What is the minimum capital for a Bahamas crypto license?

The DARE Act does not set a fixed minimum capital. The SCB assesses capital adequacy case-by-case, typically expecting $100,000 to $500,000 depending on the business scope. This is higher than Panama (no minimum) or Lithuania (EUR 125,000 under MiCA).

How long does it take to get a crypto license in the Bahamas?

The process usually takes 4 to 8 months from application submission to approval, assuming complete documentation. Delays can occur if the SCB requests additional information. Panama offers a faster 2-4 week timeline.

Can I apply for a Bahamas crypto license remotely?

Yes, but you must appoint a local registered agent and director. The application can be submitted electronically, but the SCB may require an in-person interview. Consulting24 recommends having local representation.

What are the ongoing compliance requirements?

Licensees must file annual reports, undergo an annual audit, maintain AML/CFT programs, and report suspicious transactions. The SCB also conducts periodic inspections. Non-compliance can lead to fines or license revocation.

Is the Bahamas crypto license recognized in the EU?

No, the Bahamas is not an EU member state. Under MiCA, EU-based crypto service providers must be licensed in an EU country. However, a Bahamas license can be used for non-EU operations. Consulting24 can help you obtain both if needed.

What is the difference between Class I and Class II licenses?

Class I is for limited activities or lower risk, with lighter requirements. Class II is for full-scale exchanges, custodians, and brokers. Most commercial operators require Class II. The SCB determines the appropriate class based on your business model.

Can I use a Bahamas license to operate globally?

Yes, the Bahamas license allows you to serve clients worldwide, but you must comply with local laws in the jurisdictions where your clients reside. For EU clients, you may need a MiCA license. Consulting24 advises on multi-jurisdictional strategies.

What are the tax benefits of a Bahamas crypto license?

The Bahamas has no corporate income tax, capital gains tax, or VAT. Only annual business license fees and SCB registration fees apply. This makes it highly tax-efficient compared to many other jurisdictions.

How does the Bahamas compare to Panama for crypto licensing?

Panama is cheaper (EUR 6,000 flat), faster (2-4 weeks), and has no minimum capital. The Bahamas offers tax neutrality and a stronger regulatory reputation but at a higher cost and longer timeline. Consulting24 can help you choose based on your priorities.
