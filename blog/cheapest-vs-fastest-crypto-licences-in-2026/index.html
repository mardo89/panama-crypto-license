The cheapest crypto licence in 2026 is Panama's, offered at a flat fee of EUR 6,000 through Consulting24. Who Needs a Crypto Licence in 2026?

Any business that provides crypto services to customers needs a licence. This includes exchanges, wallet providers, custodians, and payment processors. In 2026, MiCA is fully in force across the EU, so any firm offering services to EU residents must hold a CASP licence from an EU member state. Outside the EU, jurisdictions like Panama offer a regulatory framework for global operations. Founders should consider their target market: if you serve EU clients, a fast EU licence is almost mandatory. If you serve non-EU clients and want to minimise costs, Panama is the cheapest option.   • Non-EU startups with limited capital: Panama.

License Type and Regulator

Panama: The regulator is the Superintendencia de Bancos de Panamá (SBP) for crypto-related activities under Law 23 of 2015 (amended). The licence is a 'Provider of Virtual Asset Services' licence. It allows exchange, transfer, custody, and wallet services. There is no specific minimum capital requirement, but the company must have a local registered office and director.

Lithuania: The regulator is the Bank of Lithuania. Under MiCA, licences are classified as CASP (Crypto Asset Service Provider). Minimum capital is EUR 125,000 for exchange and wallet services. The application process is streamlined and can be completed in 2-3 months.

Estonia: Regulated by the Financial Intelligence Unit (FIU) and now fully aligned with MiCA. The licence is a CASP licence with capital requirements of EUR 125,000 or more depending on services. Estonia has a reputation for a transparent and efficient process.

Capital Requirements

Under MiCA, EU member states impose capital tiers: EUR 50,000 for simple wallet services, EUR 125,000 for exchange and custody, and EUR 150,000 for more complex services like trading platforms. Lithuania and Estonia both require EUR 125,000 for typical exchange/wallet operations. This capital must be maintained as equity and cannot be withdrawn. Panama has no minimum capital requirement, making it the cheapest option for startups with limited funds. However, the absence of a capital requirement may affect credibility with institutional partners. Consulting24 advises on structuring capital efficiently.

For example, a startup with EUR 50,000 could only offer wallet services in the EU, but could operate a full exchange from Panama. This flexibility is a key factor in the cheapest vs fastest decision.

Tax Treatment

Panama: Territorial taxation: only income sourced in Panama is taxed. Offshore income (from non-Panamanian clients) is tax-free. Corporate income tax is 25% on local-source income, but many crypto firms structure to avoid local income. There is no VAT or capital gains tax on crypto transactions. Panama also has no withholding tax on dividends paid to non-residents.

Lithuania: Corporate income tax is 15% (or 5% for small companies under certain conditions). Crypto gains are taxed as income. VAT applies to services provided to EU customers. Lithuania has a wide double tax treaty network.

Estonia: Unique 0% corporate income tax on reinvested profits; 20% on distributed profits (dividends). No capital gains tax on crypto held as investment. Estonia is attractive for reinvesting profits. However, the tax treatment of crypto transactions is still evolving.

Tax advice should be obtained from a qualified professional. Consulting24 can connect you with tax advisors in each jurisdiction.

Allowed Activities Under Each Licence

Panama: Exchange of virtual assets for fiat or other virtual assets, transfer of virtual assets, custody and wallet services, and operation of a virtual asset trading platform. No restrictions on trading pairs or use (subject to general business laws).

Lithuania (MiCA CASP): Exchange, transfer, custody, wallet, and operation of a trading platform. Also allows receiving and transmitting orders, and placing crypto assets. Under MiCA, services are harmonised across the EU.

Estonia (MiCA CASP): Same as Lithuania, plus the ability to passport services to other EU member states. Estonia's licence is widely recognised.

All three jurisdictions prohibit unlicensed activities like issuing unbacked stablecoins without additional authorisation. Panama is more flexible for DeFi-related services, but always check with a consultant.

Step-by-Step Application Process

  1. Company Setup: Incorporate a local company (Panama: private foundation or corporation; Lithuania: UAB; Estonia: OÜ). Consulting24 handles this.
  2. Document Preparation: Gather business plan, AML/KYC policies, financial projections, and compliance manual. For EU licences, also a risk assessment and internal controls.
  3. Submission: Submit application to the regulator (SBP in Panama, Bank of Lithuania, FIU in Estonia). Pay the fee.
  4. Review and Interview: Regulator reviews documents and may interview directors. Panama often requires a local representative.
  5. Approval: After approval, you receive the licence. In Panama, this is a resolution; in Lithuania/Estonia, a formal authorisation.
  6. Post-licence Compliance: Ongoing AML reporting, audits, and regulatory filings. Consulting24 offers ongoing compliance support.

Timelines vary: Panama 4-6 months, Lithuania/Estonia 2-3 months. Our application process page has more detail.

Under MiCA, you must also comply with travel rule and secure custody standards. Failure to comply can result in fines or licence revocation. Common Mistakes When Choosing a Licence

Founders often make these mistakes:

  • Choosing based only on cost: The cheapest licence may not suit your target market. Panama is cheap but does not grant EU access.
  • Underestimating compliance costs: EU licences have high ongoing costs for AML officers, audits, and reporting. Budget for these from the start.
  • Ignoring banking: Some jurisdictions have limited banking options for crypto firms. Check bankability before applying.
  • Not preparing for MiCA: If you plan to serve EU clients, ensure your licence is MiCA-compliant. Non-compliance risks rejection.
  • Rushing the application: Incomplete documentation causes delays. Use a professional service like Consulting24 to avoid errors.

Yes, under MiCA (fully in force in 2026), any firm providing crypto services to EU residents must hold a CASP licence from an EU member state. Non-compliance can result in fines or legal action.

No, a Panama licence does not grant EU market access. You would need a separate EU licence or passporting rights. Panama is suitable for non-EU clients or as a holding company.

No, Panama has no minimum capital requirement for crypto licences. This is a major advantage for startups. However, you must have sufficient funds to cover operational costs.
