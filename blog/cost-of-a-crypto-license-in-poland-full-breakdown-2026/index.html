What Is a Crypto License in Poland?

In Poland, crypto activities are regulated under the Act on Crypto-assets (implementing MiCA). The license is formally a registration as a Virtual Asset Service Provider (VASP) with the Polish Financial Supervision Authority (KNF). It allows companies to legally offer services such as crypto exchange, custody, and wallet provision. Unlike some EU member states that had no minimum capital before MiCA, Poland now applies the EU-wide capital tiers: EUR 50,000 for simple exchange services, EUR 125,000 for custody, and EUR 150,000 for combined services. The cost of a crypto license in Poland includes these capital requirements plus application fees and professional service fees.

Poland's regulatory framework is designed to align with the EU's Markets in Crypto-Assets Regulation (MiCA), which came into full force in 2024 and is now the standard across all member states. This means that a Polish VASP registration is recognized across the EU, allowing you to passport your services to other member states without additional licensing. For founders targeting the European market, this is a significant advantage. However, the capital requirements are higher than in some non-EU jurisdictions, which is a key factor in the overall cost.

Who Needs a Crypto License in Poland?

Any business that intends to provide crypto-asset services to Polish or EU residents must obtain a Polish VASP registration. This includes cryptocurrency exchanges, custodial wallet providers, crypto-to-fiat gateways, and platforms offering staking or lending (where these are classified as crypto-asset services under MiCA). If you are a startup or an established company looking to expand into the EU, Poland offers a relatively fast and cost-effective route compared to other EU jurisdictions. However, if your target market is outside the EU, you might also consider Panama for its tax-neutral environment and flat EUR 6,000 fee.

Specifically, the following business models require a Polish crypto license:

  • Centralized exchanges (CEX) offering crypto-to-fiat or crypto-to-crypto trading.
  • Custodial wallet providers that hold private keys for clients.
  • Payment service providers facilitating crypto transfers.
  • Platforms offering crypto lending or staking as a service.
  • Brokerage services that match buyers and sellers.

If your business is purely decentralized (DeFi) with no intermediary, you may not need a license, but you should seek legal advice to confirm.

License Type and Regulator

The Polish regulator is the Komisja Nadzoru Finansowego (KNF). The license is a single registration covering all crypto-asset services, but the capital requirement depends on the service classes you apply for. The KNF is known for thorough due diligence, including background checks on beneficial owners and source of funds. The application process is handled through the Polish Financial Supervision Authority's electronic system. Consulting24 advises clients on preparing the required documentation and liaising with local legal partners in Poland.

The KNF's approach is risk-based, meaning that the more complex your services, the deeper the scrutiny. For example, a simple exchange service may require less documentation than a custody service. The regulator also expects strong AML/KYC policies, a detailed business plan, and evidence of sufficient capital. It is important to engage experienced local counsel to navigate these requirements.

Capital Requirement

Under MiCA, Poland applies the following minimum capital tiers:

  • EUR 50,000 for simple exchange services (e.g., crypto-to-crypto trading platform).
  • EUR 125,000 for custody services (holding client crypto assets).
  • EUR 150,000 for combined exchange and custody services.

This capital must be held in a Polish bank account or as a bank guarantee. It is not a fee; it remains the company's asset but must be maintained throughout the license period. For comparison, Panama has no minimum capital requirement, making it attractive for startups with limited funds.

It is critical to choose the correct service class at the outset, as changing it later may require a new application. The capital can be used for operational expenses once the license is obtained, but the balance must never fall below the minimum.

Tax Treatment for Crypto Businesses in Poland

Poland applies a standard corporate income tax (CIT) rate of 19% (9% for small taxpayers). Crypto-asset transactions are subject to VAT exemptions under EU law, but careful structuring is needed. Poland also has a withholding tax on dividends (19%, reduced under tax treaties). For founders seeking tax efficiency, Panama offers territorial taxation (no tax on foreign-source income) and zero capital gains tax, which can be significantly more advantageous. Consulting24 can help you compare the tax implications of Poland versus Panama and other jurisdictions.

Specifically, in Poland, profits from crypto trading are generally treated as capital gains and taxed at the standard CIT rate. However, there are nuances: if your business involves mining or staking, the tax treatment may differ. It is advisable to work with a tax advisor who understands both Polish tax law and crypto assets. Poland also has a withholding tax on interest and royalties, which may affect cross-border payments.

Allowed Activities Under a Polish Crypto License

A Polish VASP registration permits the following activities under MiCA:

  • Exchange of crypto-assets for fiat currency.
  • Exchange of crypto-assets for other crypto-assets.
  • Custody and administration of crypto-assets on behalf of clients.
  • Operation of a trading platform.
  • Transfer services (sending/receiving crypto on behalf of clients).
  • Advice on crypto-assets (if classified as such).

Activities not covered include crypto mining (generally unregulated) and decentralized finance (DeFi) protocols without an intermediary. If your business model is purely DeFi, Poland may not be the right fit, and you might consider Panama or other jurisdictions with more flexible frameworks.

It is worth noting that the Polish license does not automatically authorize payment services (e.g., issuing e-money). If your business combines crypto with fiat payment services, you may need a separate payment institution license. Step-by-Step Application Process

  1. Pre-application preparation: Engage a local legal partner, draft AML/KYC policies, business plan, and risk assessment. Consulting24 coordinates this phase.
  2. Company incorporation: Register a Polish limited liability company (sp. z o.o.) with a registered office in Poland. Minimum share capital is PLN 5,000 (~EUR 1,100).
  3. Capital deposit: Open a corporate bank account in Poland and deposit the required minimum capital (EUR 50,000-150,000).
  4. Submit application: File the VASP registration with the KNF via the electronic system, including all required documents.
  5. KNF review: The regulator conducts background checks and may request additional information. This stage takes 2-4 months.
  6. License issuance: Upon approval, the company is registered as a VASP and can commence operations.

Banking and Payment Services

Opening a corporate bank account in Poland for a crypto company can be challenging due to risk aversion among traditional banks. However, Poland has several banks that accept crypto businesses, especially if you have a Polish VASP registration. Options include Alior Bank, mBank, and some fintech platforms like PayDo or Satchel. Consulting24 can introduce you to banking partners with experience in the crypto sector. Some banks also require a personal meeting with the directors. Having a licensed status can significantly ease the process, as it demonstrates regulatory approval.

Compliance and Trust: Ongoing Obligations

Once licensed, Polish VASPs must comply with ongoing AML/CFT obligations, including appointing an AML officer, conducting regular audits, and submitting reports to the KNF. The company must also maintain the minimum capital and ensure that beneficial owners are transparent. Failure to comply can result in fines or license revocation. Specifically, the AML officer must be a person with relevant experience and be based in Poland. The company must also implement transaction monitoring systems and report suspicious transactions to the General Inspector of Financial Information (GIIF). Annual audits must be conducted by an independent auditor and submitted to the KNF. The cost of ongoing compliance can be significant, but it is essential for maintaining the license.

Common Mistakes When Applying for a Polish Crypto License

  • Insufficient documentation: Incomplete business plans or AML policies are the top reasons for rejection.
  • Underestimating capital requirements: Choosing the wrong service class can lead to a higher capital deposit than necessary.
  • Ignoring beneficial ownership disclosure: The KNF scrutinizes all ultimate beneficial owners.
  • Not securing a bank account in advance: Without a local bank account, you cannot deposit the capital.
  • Assuming the process is self-service: Professional guidance is strongly recommended due to the complexity.

Another common mistake is failing to translate documents into Polish. While the KNF may accept English documents initially, official submissions must be in Polish. Working with a local translator or legal partner is essential. Also, some applicants underestimate the time required for background checks on beneficial owners, especially if they are from high-risk jurisdictions.

Frequently asked questions

How much does a crypto license in Poland cost in total?

The total cost for the first year ranges from approximately EUR 75,000 to EUR 185,000, including government fees, legal setup, capital deposit (which is refundable), and advisory fees. Exact pricing depends on the service class and complexity.

What is the minimum capital requirement for a Polish crypto license?

Under MiCA, the minimum capital is EUR 50,000 for exchange-only services, EUR 125,000 for custody, and EUR 150,000 for combined services. This capital must be maintained throughout the license period.

How long does it take to get a crypto license in Poland?

The typical timeline is 4-8 months from application submission, depending on the completeness of documentation and the KNF's workload. Pre-application preparation can add 1-2 months.

Can I passport a Polish crypto license to other EU countries?

Yes, under MiCA, a Polish VASP registration allows you to provide services across all EU member states without additional licensing, subject to notification procedures.

What are the ongoing compliance obligations for a Polish crypto license?

Ongoing obligations include appointing an AML officer, conducting annual audits, submitting reports to the KNF, maintaining minimum capital, and implementing transaction monitoring. Annual compliance costs are typically EUR 5,000-10,000.

Is Poland a good jurisdiction for crypto startups with limited capital?

Poland's capital requirements (EUR 50,000-150,000) may be prohibitive for early-stage startups. For those with limited funds, Panama (no minimum capital) or Lithuania (lower total cost) may be more suitable.

What activities are allowed under a Polish crypto license?

Allowed activities include crypto-to-fiat exchange, crypto-to-crypto exchange, custody, operation of a trading platform, transfer services, and advisory services. Mining and pure DeFi are generally not covered.

Do I need a local bank account for a Polish crypto license?

What are the tax implications for a crypto company in Poland?

Poland applies a 19% CIT (9% for small taxpayers) on profits. Crypto transactions are VAT-exempt. Dividends are subject to 19% withholding tax, reduced under tax treaties. Panama offers more favorable tax treatment for non-EU income.
