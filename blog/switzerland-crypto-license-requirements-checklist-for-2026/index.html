What Is the Switzerland Crypto License?

The Switzerland crypto license, formally a FINMA license under the Anti-Money Laundering Act (AMLA) and the Financial Services Act (FinSA), is required for financial intermediaries dealing with cryptocurrencies. In 2026, FINMA classifies crypto activities into three categories: payment tokens (cryptocurrencies like Bitcoin), utility tokens (access to services), and asset tokens (representing assets). Depending on your business model, you may need a license as a financial intermediary, a securities dealer, or a bank. The regime is principles-based, meaning FINMA focuses on the economic function of the token rather than its label.

For most crypto startups, the relevant license is the FINMA license for financial intermediaries, which includes AML obligations. This is distinct from the simpler registration required for pure blockchain projects without financial functions. The key is to determine whether your activity qualifies as a 'financial service' under Swiss law. Consulting24 can help you interpret FINMA's guidelines and assess your specific case.

Who Needs a Switzerland Crypto License?

Any entity that professionally provides crypto-related financial services in or from Switzerland must obtain a license. This includes:

  • Crypto exchanges (centralized and decentralized if operated commercially)
  • Custodial wallet providers
  • Token issuers (ICOs, STOs) where tokens have financial characteristics
  • Brokers and dealers in cryptocurrencies
  • Asset managers dealing with crypto assets
  • Payment service providers using crypto

If your business merely develops blockchain software or provides non-financial services (like mining or node operation), you typically do not need a license. However, if you hold customer funds or facilitate transfers, you fall under FINMA's purview. Capital Requirement

The minimum capital requirement for a Switzerland crypto license as a financial intermediary is CHF 100,000 (approximately EUR 100,000). This must be fully paid up in cash or liquid assets before the license is granted. For securities dealers, the requirement is CHF 1.5 million, and for banks, CHF 10 million. The capital must be held in a Swiss bank account and may not be used for operational expenses until the license is active. FINMA may require additional capital based on the risk profile of your business, such as higher transaction volumes or complex token structures. Tax Treatment of Crypto in Switzerland

Switzerland has a favorable tax regime for crypto businesses. Corporate income tax rates vary by canton but typically range from 12% to 24%. Cryptocurrencies are treated as assets for tax purposes, and capital gains from private crypto trading are generally tax-free for individuals. For businesses, profits from crypto trading and services are subject to ordinary corporate tax. VAT is not applied to crypto transactions (as of 2026), but fees for services may be VAT-able. Stamp duty may apply to securities transactions. It is essential to consult a Swiss tax advisor, as cantonal tax laws differ. Allowed Activities Under the License

A Switzerland crypto license permits a wide range of activities, including:

  • Operating a cryptocurrency exchange (fiat-to-crypto and crypto-to-crypto)
  • Providing custodial wallet services
  • Issuing tokens (STOs, ICOs) subject to prospectus requirements
  • Brokerage and trading of crypto assets
  • Payment processing with crypto
  • Asset management of crypto portfolios

Activities not covered include taking deposits (requires a banking license) and offering derivatives without a securities dealer license. FINMA may impose conditions on your license, such as transaction limits or reporting obligations. Always verify your specific business model against FINMA's guidelines. Step-by-Step Application Process

Obtaining a Switzerland crypto license involves several stages. Below is a typical process:

  1. Pre-assessment: Evaluate your business model against FINMA requirements. Consulting24 offers a preliminary assessment to identify gaps.
  2. Company setup: Incorporate a Swiss company (usually AG or GmbH) with a registered office in Switzerland. This requires a notarized deed and registration in the commercial register.
  3. AML compliance: Implement AML policies, appoint a compliance officer (must be Swiss resident), and set up KYC/AML software.
  4. Capital deposit: Deposit the minimum capital in a Swiss bank account. You will need to provide proof of the deposit.
  5. Application submission: Submit the application to FINMA with all required documents: business plan, AML manual, financial projections, organizational chart, and personal questionnaires for directors.
  6. Review and feedback: FINMA reviews the application and may request additional information. This takes 2-6 months. Expect at least one round of questions.
  7. License issuance: Once approved, you receive the license and can commence operations. You must publish the license in the Swiss Official Gazette.
  8. Ongoing compliance: Submit regular reports, undergo annual audits, and maintain AML standards. Failure to comply can lead to sanctions.

Compliance and Trust Requirements

Compliance is the backbone of the Swiss crypto license. Key requirements include:

  • Appointment of a compliance officer with Swiss residency
  • Implementation of AML/KYC procedures aligned with FINMA guidelines
  • Regular external audits (annually)
  • Reporting of suspicious transactions to the Money Laundering Reporting Office (MROS)
  • Maintenance of records for at least 10 years

Failure to comply can result in fines, license revocation, or criminal liability. This is general guidance, not legal advice. Common Mistakes When Applying

Founders often make these errors during the application process:

  • Underestimating the timeline: Expect at least 6 months, often longer. Plan your business launch accordingly.
  • Incomplete AML documentation: FINMA requires a detailed AML manual tailored to your business. Generic templates are rejected.
  • Insufficient capital proof: You must show the capital is fully paid up and held in a Swiss bank account. Proof of deposit is essential.
  • Lack of Swiss presence: You need a registered office and a compliance officer in Switzerland. Virtual offices may not suffice.

Frequently asked questions

What is the minimum capital for a Switzerland crypto license?

The minimum capital for a financial intermediary license is CHF 100,000 (approx. EUR 100,000). For securities dealers it is CHF 1.5 million, and for banks CHF 10 million. The capital must be fully paid up and held in a Swiss bank account.

How long does it take to get a Switzerland crypto license?

The typical timeline is 6 to 12 months from application to approval. This includes company incorporation, AML setup, FINMA review, and potential requests for additional information. The process can be faster if your application is complete and your business model is straightforward.

What are the main compliance requirements for a Swiss crypto license?

You must appoint a Swiss-resident compliance officer, implement AML/KYC procedures, conduct annual external audits, report suspicious transactions to MROS, and maintain records for 10 years. Ongoing compliance costs can be significant.

Can I operate a crypto exchange with a Swiss license?

Yes, a financial intermediary license allows you to operate a crypto exchange (fiat-to-crypto and crypto-to-crypto), provided you comply with AML and reporting obligations. You may also need a securities dealer license if you offer derivatives.

Is Switzerland part of the EU MiCA framework?

No, Switzerland is not an EU member and does not apply MiCA directly. However, its regulatory framework is aligned with international standards and has mutual recognition agreements with the EU, facilitating cross-border business.

What are the tax implications for a Swiss crypto license holder?

Corporate income tax rates range from 12% to 24% depending on the canton. Crypto transactions are generally VAT-exempt. Capital gains from private trading are tax-free for individuals. Business profits are subject to ordinary corporate tax.

How does the Switzerland crypto license compare to Panama?

Panama offers a flat fee of EUR 6,000 with no minimum capital and a 2-3 month timeline, making it much cheaper and faster. Switzerland provides greater regulatory prestige and access to EU markets, but at a higher cost and longer timeline.
