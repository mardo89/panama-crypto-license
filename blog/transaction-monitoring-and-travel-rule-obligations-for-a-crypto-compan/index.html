Overview: What Are Transaction Monitoring and Travel Rule Obligations?

Transaction monitoring refers to the continuous surveillance of crypto transactions to identify suspicious patterns, such as money laundering, terrorist financing, or fraud. The travel rule, derived from FATF Recommendation 16, requires CASPs to collect and share specific information about the originator and beneficiary of a transfer, including names, wallet addresses, and transaction amounts. In 2026, both obligations are enforced across most regulated jurisdictions, including all EU member states under MiCA.

Key requirements include:

  • Real-time monitoring of all transactions above a threshold (often EUR 1,000 or equivalent).
  • Automated screening against sanctions lists and politically exposed persons (PEPs).
  • Secure transmission of travel rule data between CASPs using standardized formats like IVMS101.
  • Record keeping for at least five years, with data accessible to regulators on request.

Failure to comply can result in penalties of up to 5% of annual turnover or EUR 5 million, whichever is higher, under MiCA. In Panama, fines can reach USD 1 million for serious violations. For example, in 2024, a major European exchange was fined EUR 3 million for inadequate transaction monitoring, highlighting the real risks.

Transaction monitoring systems typically use machine learning and rule-based algorithms to flag unusual activity. Travel rule solutions like Notabene or Sygna facilitate secure data sharing between CASPs. Both systems must be integrated into your platform's workflow, which requires careful planning and testing.

Who Needs to Comply?

Any crypto asset service provider (CASP) that facilitates transfers of virtual assets must comply. This includes:

  • Centralized exchanges (CEXs) and decentralized exchanges (DEXs) with a central operator.
  • Wallet providers (custodial and non-custodial if they control private keys).
  • OTC desks and payment processors handling crypto.
  • Brokers and dealers in crypto assets.
  • Kiosk operators and ATM providers.

In the EU, MiCA applies to all CASPs licensed in any member state. Outside the EU, jurisdictions like Panama, the UK, and Singapore have similar requirements. If your company operates in multiple jurisdictions, you must comply with the strictest rules applicable. For instance, a Panama-licensed company serving EU clients must also meet MiCA travel rule standards for those transactions.

Even non-custodial services may be affected if they control any aspect of the transfer process. Consulting24 recommends a thorough legal review to determine your obligations. We have seen cases where DAOs and DeFi protocols were caught off guard by travel rule requirements, leading to enforcement actions.

Common Mistakes in Transaction Monitoring and Travel Rule Compliance

  • Insufficient threshold setting: Setting too high a threshold for monitoring can miss suspicious activity. Most regulators require monitoring of all transactions above EUR 1,000. Some jurisdictions require monitoring of all transactions regardless of amount.
  • Ignoring decentralized finance (DeFi): If your platform interacts with DeFi protocols, you may still be responsible for travel rule compliance. For instance, a DEX aggregator must share travel rule data for trades executed through its interface.
  • Poor data security: Travel rule data includes personal information; inadequate security can lead to data breaches and fines under GDPR. Use encryption and access controls.
  • Failure to update sanctions lists: Sanctions lists change frequently; automated updates are essential. A company using outdated lists was fined EUR 500,000 in 2025.
  • Not testing travel rule data transmission: Ensure your system can send and receive travel rule data in the required format (e.g., IVMS101). Many failures occur during integration testing.
  • Overlooking non-custodial wallets: If your service interacts with non-custodial wallets, you must still collect travel rule data from the user. This is often overlooked.
