Applying for a crypto license in Germany is a rigorous process. The Federal Financial Supervisory Authority (BaFin) enforces strict standards under the German Banking Act (KWG) and, as of 2026, the EU's Markets in Crypto-Assets Regulation (MiCA). Many founders stumble on common pitfalls that delay or derail their applications. This guide identifies those mistakes and shows how to avoid them, whether you pursue a German license or consider alternatives like Panama.

Germany requires a full banking license for crypto custody and proprietary trading, with minimum capital of EUR 125,000 for custody and EUR 150,000 for trading under MiCA. The application timeline typically spans 6-12 months. Without expert preparation, errors in documentation, capital proof, or business model description can lead to rejection. What Is a Germany Crypto License?

Since January 2020, Germany requires crypto custody businesses to obtain a license from BaFin under the KWG. This applies to companies that hold, store, or transfer cryptocurrencies for clients. In 2026, MiCA harmonises EU rules, but Germany retains its own licensing framework, which is considered one of the strictest in Europe. The license covers crypto custody, proprietary trading, and brokerage activities, each with specific capital and compliance requirements.

Key points:

  • Regulator: BaFin (Federal Financial Supervisory Authority)
  • Legal basis: KWG (Banking Act) and MiCAR
  • Minimum capital: EUR 125,000 (custody), EUR 150,000 (trading), EUR 50,000 (brokerage)
  • Timeline: 6-12 months, sometimes longer
  • Ongoing compliance: Anti-money laundering (AML), risk management, reporting

Understanding these basics is the first step to avoiding mistakes. Many founders confuse the German license with lighter regimes like the former Estonian VASP registration, which had no minimum capital. Under MiCA, all EU member states now apply capital tiers, but Germany's interpretation remains particularly stringent. For example, BaFin requires a detailed organisational chart showing clear separation of duties, which many startups fail to provide.

Who Needs a Germany Crypto License?

Any company that offers commercial crypto custody services, operates a crypto exchange, or engages in proprietary trading with crypto assets must hold a BaFin license. This includes:

  • Custodians holding private keys for clients
  • Exchanges facilitating crypto-to-fiat or crypto-to-crypto trades
  • Brokers matching buyers and sellers
  • Market makers trading on own account

Foreign companies targeting German customers also need a license or must rely on reverse solicitation exemptions, which are narrowly interpreted. Many founders mistakenly assume a simple registration suffices. In Germany, it is a full authorisation process, not a mere register entry like in some other EU states pre-MiCA. A common mistake is assuming that a Polish or Czech license can passport into Germany without additional requirements. While MiCA allows passporting, Germany imposes top-up obligations for custody activities. License Type and Regulator

BaFin grants a "Crypto Custody License" (Kryptoverwahrgeschäft) as a subcategory of the banking license. It is not a separate crypto-specific license but an extension of the KWG. The regulator also oversees MiCA authorisation for CASPs (Crypto Asset Service Providers), but Germany applies its own provisions for custody. The license is issued as a "Finanzdienstleistungsinstitut" (financial services institution) or a "Kreditinstitut" (credit institution) depending on the scope.

Common mistake: Founders apply for the wrong license type or assume a lighter regime applies. BaFin does not offer a simplified crypto license. All applicants must meet the same standards as traditional financial institutions. For instance, if you plan to offer both custody and trading, you need capital for each activity class, and the application must clearly delineate the services. Many startups submit a single application covering multiple activities without specifying capital allocation, leading to requests for amendment. Capital Requirement

Under MiCA, effective in full from 2026, Germany applies capital tiers: EUR 50,000 for simple brokerage, EUR 125,000 for custody, and EUR 150,000 for proprietary trading. This capital must be held as own funds and cannot be borrowed. It must be demonstrated with audited financial statements or bank guarantees.

Common mistake: Founders fail to provide proof of source of funds or use capital that is not freely available. BaFin scrutinises the origin of capital and may reject applications if funds appear linked to high-risk sources. Also, capital must be maintained at all times; dipping below triggers immediate regulatory action. For example, if a company uses part of its capital for operational expenses, it may fall below the threshold and face license suspension. Consulting24 advises clients to maintain a buffer of at least 20% above the minimum. For comparison, Panama has no capital requirement, making it attractive for startups with limited funds.

Tax Treatment

Germany taxes crypto assets as private sales (if held less than one year) or as business income if traded frequently. Licensed entities are subject to corporate income tax (around 30% effective) plus trade tax (varies by municipality). VAT is not charged on crypto transactions under EU law, but custody fees may be VAT-exempt.

Common mistake: Not accounting for trade tax, which can add 15-20% on top of corporate tax. Also, founders often overlook the requirement to register for VAT if providing taxable services. Consulting with a German tax advisor is essential. For tax-friendly alternatives, consider El Salvador or Panama, where crypto gains are tax-free. In Panama, companies are taxed only on Panama-source income, and crypto trading is generally considered offshore if the counterparty is abroad. Allowed Activities

A Germany crypto license permits:

  • Crypto custody (holding private keys)
  • Proprietary trading in crypto assets
  • Brokerage (matching orders)
  • Exchange services (crypto-to-fiat, crypto-to-crypto)
  • Transfer services (sending crypto on behalf of clients)

Activities not covered: investment advice (requires separate license), asset management (MiFID), and issuing own tokens (prospectus required). Founders often try to include too many activities without proper capital allocation. Each activity class may require additional capital or organisational measures. For example, offering both custody and trading requires separate risk management systems. A common mistake is applying for a custody license but planning to operate an exchange, which BaFin will flag. Consulting24 recommends limiting the initial scope to one activity and expanding later.

Step-by-Step Application Process

1. Pre-application: Engage legal counsel, prepare business plan, AML policies, and risk management framework.
2. Submit application to BaFin with all documents, including proof of capital, fit-and-proper tests for managers, and organisational charts.
3. BaFin reviews (3-6 months) and may request additional information. Expect a formal hearing.
4. If approved, you receive a license and must start operations within a specified period.
5. Ongoing compliance: appoint an AML officer, conduct regular audits, and submit reports.

Common mistake: Submitting incomplete or inconsistent documents. BaFin expects a coherent business model that matches the license scope. Also, founders often underestimate the fit-and-proper test requirements for all managing directors. For instance, each director must provide a CV, police certificate, and references from previous employers. If any director has a criminal record or regulatory sanction, the application may be denied. Consulting24 advises on document preparation and can coordinate with local partners. For a faster process, consider Panama, where no fit-and-proper test is required.

Banking and Payment Services

Licensed crypto firms in Germany need a bank account for operational funds and client segregation. Many traditional banks refuse to open accounts for crypto companies due to compliance concerns. Specialised fintech banks like Solarisbank or Fidor may offer accounts, but onboarding is lengthy.

Common mistake: Not securing a banking relationship before applying. BaFin requires proof of a business bank account. Also, founders sometimes mix client and company funds, which violates segregation rules. Using a licensed crypto-friendly bank is critical. Compliance and Trust

Ongoing compliance includes AML/KYC procedures, transaction monitoring, regular reporting to BaFin, and annual audits. The AML officer must be resident in Germany and have relevant experience. BaFin conducts on-site inspections and can impose fines or revoke licenses for non-compliance.

Common mistake: Appointing an inexperienced AML officer or outsourcing compliance without proper oversight. BaFin expects the licensed entity to retain responsibility. Also, failing to update policies as regulations change is a frequent violation. For instance, MiCA introduced new reporting requirements for transaction data, which many firms missed. Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. Underestimating the timeline: Many founders expect 3-4 months; reality is 6-12 months.
2. Incomplete business plan: BaFin requires detailed financial projections, market analysis, and risk assessment.
3. Insufficient capital: Not meeting the minimum or not proving source of funds.
4. Poor fit-and-proper documentation: Managers must submit CVs, police certificates, and references.
5. Ignoring ongoing costs: Budget for compliance staff, audits, and legal fees.
6. Applying without local presence: BaFin expects a physical office in Germany and local management.
7. Mixing license types: Applying for custody when you need trading, or vice versa.

By avoiding these, you increase your chances of approval. Consulting24 can guide you through the process or recommend alternative jurisdictions like Panama, where the process is simpler and faster. Frequently asked questions

What is the minimum capital for a Germany crypto license in 2026?

Under MiCA, Germany requires EUR 50,000 for brokerage, EUR 125,000 for custody, and EUR 150,000 for proprietary trading. This capital must be held as own funds and proven with audited statements.

How long does it take to get a Germany crypto license?

The application process typically takes 6 to 12 months. Delays often occur due to incomplete documentation or BaFin requests for additional information. Some applications take longer if the business model is complex.

Can I passport a German crypto license to other EU countries?

Yes, under MiCA, a German license allows passporting across the EU. However, Germany imposes top-up obligations for custody activities, so you may need to notify BaFin before operating in another member state.

What are the ongoing compliance costs for a German crypto license?

Ongoing costs typically range from EUR 30,000 to EUR 100,000 per year, including AML officer salary, external audits, reporting, and legal fees. These costs are often underestimated by applicants.

Do I need a physical office in Germany to get a crypto license?

Yes, BaFin expects a physical office in Germany with local management. You must have a registered address and at least one managing director resident in Germany.

What activities are not covered by a Germany crypto license?

Investment advice, asset management, and issuing own tokens are not covered. These activities require separate licenses under MiFID or prospectus regulation.

Is Panama a good alternative to Germany for crypto licensing?

Panama is an excellent alternative for many startups. It has no capital requirement, no tax on foreign-source income, and a fast setup process. Consulting24 offers Panama company setup at a flat fee of EUR 6,000.

What is the difference between a German crypto license and a Lithuanian one?

Lithuania's license is cheaper (EUR 10,000-20,000) and faster (2-4 months), but capital requirements under MiCA are similar. Germany offers higher credibility but at a higher cost. Consulting24 can help you choose.

Can I apply for a German crypto license without legal counsel?

It is highly discouraged. BaFin requires a detailed application with legal analysis. Most successful applicants engage specialised law firms. Consulting24 can recommend partners.

What happens if my German crypto license application is rejected?

BaFin will provide reasons for rejection. You can appeal or reapply after addressing the issues. Common reasons include insufficient capital, poor fit-and-proper tests, or an incomplete business plan.
