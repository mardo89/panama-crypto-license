What Is the Bahamas Crypto Gambling License?

The Bahamas crypto gambling license is not a single license but a combination of regulatory approvals. Under the DARE Act, the Securities Commission of The Bahamas (SCB) regulates digital asset businesses, including those that facilitate gambling or betting using cryptocurrencies. Operators must register as a Digital Asset Business (DAB) and also obtain a gambling license from the Gaming Board of The Bahamas.

Key features of the regime include:

  • Regulation by the SCB for digital asset activities and the Gaming Board for gambling operations.
  • Requirement for a physical presence in the Bahamas, including a registered office and local directors.
  • Compliance with anti-money laundering (AML) and counter-terrorism financing (CTF) obligations.
  • Transparency in operations and regular reporting.

The Bahamas is known for its stable political environment and strong legal system, making it an attractive hub for crypto gambling businesses seeking legitimacy. The DARE Act was one of the first comprehensive digital asset laws in the Caribbean, and the SCB has developed a reputation for thorough but fair oversight. For operators targeting the Americas, the time zone (EST/EDT) is a practical advantage.

Who Needs a Bahamas Crypto Gambling License?

Any business that offers gambling services using cryptocurrencies - such as online casinos, sports betting platforms, poker rooms, or lottery sites - and wishes to operate legally in or from the Bahamas needs this license. This includes:

  • Startups launching a crypto-native gambling platform.
  • Existing online gambling operators expanding into crypto.
  • White-label providers offering crypto gambling solutions.
  • Affiliates and aggregators that process crypto transactions for gambling.

The license is essential for building trust with players, payment processors, and banking partners. Without it, operators risk legal action and reputational damage. Additionally, many crypto payment gateways require proof of licensing before onboarding. If you plan to accept Bitcoin, Ethereum, or stablecoins, the SCB registration gives you credibility. For operators who also want to serve fiat customers, the Gambling Board license covers both.

License Type & Regulator

The Bahamas regulatory framework involves two main authorities:

  • Securities Commission of The Bahamas (SCB): Regulates digital asset businesses under the DARE Act. You must register as a Digital Asset Business (DAB) to handle crypto transactions.
  • Gaming Board of The Bahamas: Issues gambling licenses under the Gaming Act. The board oversees all forms of gambling, including online and crypto-based operations.

The license type is typically a 'Restricted Gambling License' for online operations, combined with DAB registration. The SCB categorizes DABs into classes (I, II, III) based on activities, with crypto gambling usually falling under Class II or III. Class II covers trading and exchange services, while Class III includes custody and wallet services. For gambling, you may need both Class II (for crypto-to-crypto swaps) and Class III (for holding player funds). The Gaming Board license is separate and focuses on game fairness, responsible gambling, and player protection.

Capital Requirement

The Bahamas does not prescribe a fixed minimum capital for crypto gambling licenses. However, the regulators expect applicants to demonstrate sufficient financial resources to cover operational costs, player liabilities, and regulatory compliance. Typically, a minimum paid-up capital of $100,000 to $500,000 is required, but this can vary based on business scale.

Applicants must provide audited financial statements or a business plan showing adequate funding. The SCB may impose additional capital requirements based on risk assessment. For example, if you plan to hold large player balances, the SCB might require higher capital or a surety bond. The Gaming Board also looks at financial strength to ensure player winnings can be paid. In practice, most approved operators have at least $250,000 in liquid assets.

Tax Treatment

The Bahamas offers a tax-neutral environment for crypto gambling operators:

  • No corporate income tax: Profits from gambling operations are not taxed.
  • No capital gains tax: Gains from crypto trading or investments are tax-free.
  • No VAT or sales tax: Services are not subject to indirect taxes.
  • Gaming tax: A small percentage of gross gaming revenue (typically 5-15%) is payable to the Gaming Board.

This tax regime makes the Bahamas highly attractive for crypto gambling businesses seeking to maximize profits. However, note that the gaming tax is based on gross gaming revenue (GGR), not net profit. For example, if your platform generates $1 million in GGR annually, you might pay $50,000 to $150,000 in gaming tax. This is still low compared to many jurisdictions. Also, there are no withholding taxes on dividends or interest, making it easy to repatriate profits.

Allowed Activities

A Bahamas crypto gambling license permits the following activities:

  • Operating online casinos, sportsbooks, poker rooms, and bingo sites using cryptocurrencies.
  • Accepting deposits and withdrawals in Bitcoin, Ethereum, stablecoins, and other approved digital assets.
  • Offering provably fair games and smart contract-based gambling.
  • Providing crypto-to-fiat conversion services for players.
  • Marketing and affiliate programs for crypto gambling.

Prohibited activities include unlicensed lotteries, games of chance without proper RNG certification, and services to restricted jurisdictions (e.g., US, UK). The SCB also prohibits mixing services or anonymity-enhanced transactions. All games must be tested by an approved lab like GLI or iTech Labs. If you plan to offer live dealer games, you need additional approvals from the Gaming Board.

Step-by-Step Application Process

Applying for a crypto gambling license in the Bahamas involves several stages:

  1. Company Formation: Incorporate a company in the Bahamas with a registered office and local directors.
  2. DAB Registration: Submit an application to the SCB for Digital Asset Business registration, including AML policies, business plan, and financial statements.
  3. Gaming License Application: Apply to the Gaming Board with detailed information on games, software, and responsible gambling measures.
  4. Due Diligence: Both regulators conduct background checks on directors and shareholders.
  5. Compliance Review: Ensure AML/CTF policies, data protection, and technical standards are met.
  6. Approval & Licensing: Once both approvals are granted, pay the license fees and commence operations.

  • Appoint a local compliance officer and money laundering reporting officer.

Frequently asked questions

What is the difference between the SCB registration and the Gaming Board license?

The SCB registration under DARE Act authorizes you to handle digital assets, while the Gaming Board license permits gambling operations. Both are required for crypto gambling in the Bahamas. The SCB focuses on crypto custody, exchange, and AML compliance; the Gaming Board oversees game fairness, responsible gambling, and player protection.

How long does it take to get a crypto gambling license in the Bahamas?

The process typically takes 6 to 12 months. The SCB review takes 3-4 months, followed by 2-3 months for the Gaming Board. Delays can occur if documentation is incomplete or if background checks raise issues. Engaging an experienced advisor can help streamline the process.

What are the minimum capital requirements for a Bahamas crypto gambling license?

There is no fixed minimum capital, but regulators expect at least $100,000 to $500,000 in paid-up capital. The exact amount depends on your business scale and risk profile. You must demonstrate sufficient funds to cover operational costs and player liabilities.

Can I operate a crypto casino from the Bahamas without a license?

No. Operating without the required SCB registration and Gaming Board license is illegal and can result in fines, asset seizure, and criminal charges. Players and payment processors will also avoid unlicensed operators. Licensing is essential for legitimacy and access to banking services.

What taxes do crypto gambling operators pay in the Bahamas?

The Bahamas has no corporate income tax, capital gains tax, or VAT. The only direct tax is a gaming tax of 5-15% on gross gaming revenue. This makes the Bahamas one of the most tax-efficient jurisdictions for crypto gambling.

Do I need a physical office in the Bahamas?

Yes. Both the SCB and Gaming Board require a physical presence, including a registered office and local directors. You cannot use a virtual office; you need a real workspace with staff. This is a key requirement for substance.

Can I use the Bahamas license to serve players from the US or UK?

No. The Bahamas license does not authorize you to serve players in restricted jurisdictions like the US, UK, or EU without additional local licenses. You must geoblock players from these regions. The Gaming Board will check your geolocation controls during audits.

What are the ongoing compliance obligations after obtaining the license?

You must submit periodic reports to the SCB and Gaming Board, conduct AML audits, file suspicious transaction reports, ensure game certification, and maintain responsible gambling tools. Annual fees range from $30,000 to $120,000. You also need a local compliance officer.

Is the Bahamas crypto gambling license recognized by banks?

Yes, but banks will conduct enhanced due diligence. Having both the SCB registration and Gaming Board license significantly improves your chances of opening a bank account. Some banks may still refuse due to the high-risk nature of crypto gambling. Consulting24 can introduce you to crypto-friendly banks.

How does the Bahamas compare to Panama for crypto gambling?

Panama offers a lower cost (EUR 6,000 flat) and faster setup, but its regulatory framework is less crypto-specific. The Bahamas has a more strong digital asset law (DARE Act) and a stronger reputation, but at a higher cost and longer timeline. Panama is better for startups; the Bahamas suits established operators.
