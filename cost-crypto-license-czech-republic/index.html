What Is the Czech Republic Crypto License?

The Czech Republic crypto license is a mandatory registration for businesses providing virtual asset services, such as exchange, custody, or wallet services. It is not a full license but a VASP registration under the Act on Selected Services in the Digital Economy (Act No. 186/2020 Coll.). The Czech National Bank (CNB) oversees compliance with AML/CFT obligations. As of 2026, the regime is stable and well-established, with clear requirements for capital, internal controls, and reporting.

This registration allows companies to operate legally within the Czech Republic and serve EU clients under the passporting rules of the EU's AML framework. However, note that MiCA (Markets in Crypto-Assets) is fully in force in 2026 for EU member states, but the Czech Republic has not yet fully transposed MiCA into national law; it continues to apply its national regime until full implementation. This means the cost Czech Republic crypto license may differ from MiCA-compliant jurisdictions.

Who Needs a Czech Republic Crypto License?

Any legal entity intending to provide virtual asset services in or from the Czech Republic must obtain VASP registration. This includes:

  • Crypto exchanges (fiat-to-crypto and crypto-to-crypto)
  • Cryptocurrency wallet providers (custodial and non-custodial)
  • Payment processors using virtual assets
  • Brokerage services for digital assets
  • ATMs and kiosks dealing in virtual currencies

Foreign companies targeting Czech clients may also need to register or establish a local presence. If you are a founder from outside the EU, you can set up a Czech company (typically a s.r.o.) and then apply for VASP registration. License Type & Regulator

The Czech Republic crypto license is formally a VASP registration. The regulator is the Czech National Bank (Česká národní banka, CNB). The CNB is responsible for licensing, supervision, and enforcement of AML rules. Unlike some EU countries that have a separate financial regulator, the CNB handles both monetary policy and financial market oversight.

There is no tiered license system under the current Czech regime. All VASPs must meet the same basic capital and compliance requirements. However, the scope of services you intend to offer may affect the depth of scrutiny. The registration is valid indefinitely, but the CNB conducts periodic reviews and may impose conditions. Capital Requirement

The Czech Republic VASP registration does not impose a statutory minimum capital requirement in the law. However, the CNB expects that the company has sufficient financial resources to operate and meet its AML obligations. In practice, most applicants maintain at least EUR 20,000 - 50,000 in share capital or liquid reserves to demonstrate financial stability. Tax Treatment of Crypto in the Czech Republic

The Czech Republic has a favorable tax regime for crypto businesses. Corporate income tax is a flat 19% on profits. Capital gains from crypto trading are subject to the same rate. VAT is not applied to crypto-to-fiat exchanges, but may apply to other services. There is no specific crypto tax law; general tax principles apply. However, the tax authority (Financial Administration) has issued guidelines treating crypto as intangible assets for tax purposes.

For individuals, crypto gains are taxed at 15% (or 23% for high incomes) if held as an investment. Businesses must account for crypto transactions in their accounting records. Allowed Activities Under the Czech Crypto License

The VASP registration permits a wide range of activities, including:

  • Exchange of virtual assets for fiat currency or other virtual assets
  • Transfer of virtual assets (including on behalf of clients)
  • Custody and administration of virtual assets or private keys
  • Participation in and provision of financial services related to an issuer's offer or sale of virtual assets
  • Operation of a trading platform for virtual assets

Notably, the Czech regime does not cover services related to security tokens or derivative products; those fall under MiFID II. Also, crypto mining and non-custodial software development are generally not regulated. If you plan to offer services beyond basic exchange and custody, you may need additional licensing. Step-by-Step Application Process

Obtaining the Czech Republic crypto license involves several stages. Here is a typical process:

  1. Company Incorporation: Set up a Czech s.r.o. (limited liability company) with a registered address and a designated director. Minimum share capital is CZK 1 (approximately EUR 0.04), but we recommend at least EUR 20,000 for credibility.
  2. AML Policy & Procedures: Draft comprehensive AML/CFT policies, risk assessment, and internal controls. This is the most critical part of the application.
  3. Appoint an AML Officer: Designate a person responsible for AML compliance, either internal or outsourced. The officer must be resident in the Czech Republic or the EU.
  4. Submit Application: File the VASP registration with the CNB via the official portal. Include all required documents: company deeds, AML policy, beneficial ownership information, and financial projections.
  5. CNB Review: The CNB reviews the application within 60 days, but may request additional information, extending the timeline.
  6. Approval & Registration: Once approved, the company is added to the CNB's register of VASPs. You can then commence operations.

Banking & Payment Services

Opening a corporate bank account for a crypto company in the Czech Republic can be challenging. Traditional banks are often cautious. However, several banks accept VASP-registered companies, especially if you have a strong compliance framework. Options include:

  • Major banks: Česká spořitelna, ČSOB, Komerční banka (may require higher minimum deposits)
  • Fintech-friendly banks: Fio banka, Air Bank (more flexible but may have lower limits)
  • EMI accounts: Revolut Business, Wise, or other electronic money institutions for multi-currency operations

Compliance & Trust: Ongoing Obligations

After registration, VASPs must maintain ongoing compliance. Key obligations include:

  • AML/CFT compliance: Conduct customer due diligence (CDD), monitor transactions, and report suspicious activities to the Financial Analytical Office (FAO).
  • Annual reporting: Submit annual financial statements and AML reports to the CNB.
  • Audit: Some companies may require an external audit depending on size.
  • Beneficial ownership register: Maintain up-to-date information in the Czech register.
  • Data protection: Comply with GDPR for client data.

Common Mistakes in the Czech Crypto License Application

Founders often make errors that delay or derail their application. Avoid these pitfalls:

  • Incomplete AML policy: The CNB expects a detailed, risk-based AML manual. Generic templates are not accepted.
  • Lack of substance: The company must have a physical office and local management. A virtual office may not suffice.
  • Insufficient capital: While no minimum is set, the CNB will assess if you have enough funds to operate. Too low capital raises red flags.
  • Ignoring beneficial ownership: All ultimate owners must be disclosed; opaque structures are rejected.
  • Not appointing a local AML officer: The officer must be reachable and knowledgeable about Czech AML law.
