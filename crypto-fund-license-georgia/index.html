What is a Crypto Fund License in Georgia?

A crypto fund license in Georgia is a regulatory authorization issued by the National Bank of Georgia (NBG) that permits a legal entity to manage, administer, or operate a collective investment scheme focused on crypto assets. This license is part of Georgia's broader VASP registration regime, which came into full effect in 2023 and has been refined through 2026. The license covers activities such as portfolio management, fund administration, and the offering of fund shares to qualified investors.

Unlike some EU jurisdictions that require a minimum capital of EUR 125,000 or more under MiCA, Georgia's capital requirement is significantly lower, typically around GEL 100,000 (approximately EUR 30,000) for fund managers. This makes Georgia an attractive entry point for smaller fund managers. The license does not fall under MiCA, as Georgia is not an EU member, but it aligns with FATF recommendations. Consulting24 advises clients on structuring their fund to meet NBG requirements, including having a local registered office, a compliance officer, and a board of directors with relevant experience.

Who Needs a Crypto Fund License in Georgia?

The crypto fund license in Georgia is required for any entity that manages a pool of crypto assets on behalf of investors, whether as a closed-end fund, open-end fund, or a special purpose vehicle. This includes:

  • Venture capital funds investing in crypto startups
  • Hedge funds trading digital assets
  • Private equity funds focusing on blockchain companies
  • Fund of funds that allocate to other crypto funds

Exemptions may apply for funds with fewer than 20 investors or where all investors are professional and the fund is not publicly marketed. However, most commercial fund structures will require a license. Consulting24 recommends a preliminary assessment to determine if your fund model falls under NBG's scope. Even if exempt, many funds choose to license voluntarily to signal credibility to investors and banks.

License Type & Regulator

The primary regulator for crypto fund licenses in Georgia is the National Bank of Georgia (NBG). The NBG registers VASPs, including fund managers, under the AML/CFT framework. There is no separate securities regulator for crypto funds; the NBG handles both AML supervision and licensing. The license type is a "VASP registration with fund management authorization." This is not a full investment firm license but a tailored regime for digital asset funds.

As of 2026, the NBG requires fund managers to submit a detailed business plan, AML policies, and proof of capital. The regulator also conducts a fit-and-proper test on directors and beneficial owners. Georgia's approach is principles-based, allowing flexibility in fund structures. However, the NBG expects a local presence, including a physical office and a local compliance officer. Capital Requirement

The minimum capital requirement for a crypto fund license in Georgia is GEL 100,000 (approximately EUR 30,000 as of 2026). This capital must be fully paid up in cash and held in a Georgian bank account. The NBG may require higher capital if the fund's assets under management exceed a certain threshold, but the baseline is low compared to EU standards. For comparison, Lithuania requires EUR 125,000 for CASP licenses under MiCA, and Estonia requires EUR 100,000. Georgia's lower barrier makes it accessible for early-stage fund managers.

Capital must be maintained throughout the license period. It can be used for operational expenses but must be replenished if it falls below the minimum. Consulting24 advises on capital planning and ensures that the source of funds is documented to satisfy NBG's AML checks.

Tax Treatment for Crypto Funds in Georgia

Georgia offers a highly attractive tax regime for crypto funds. Corporate income tax is 15%, but there is a territorial tax system: income earned outside Georgia is not taxed if not repatriated. For fund managers, management fees are subject to 15% corporate tax, but capital gains from crypto trading may be exempt if the fund is structured as a foreign entity. Additionally, Georgia has no VAT on financial services, and no capital gains tax for individuals. The Estonian-style deferred tax system (tax on distributed profits only) applies to many Georgian companies, including fund management companies.

Consulting24 recommends structuring the fund as a limited partnership or a joint stock company to optimize tax. A tax ruling from the Georgian Revenue Service can provide certainty. As of 2026, Georgia has not introduced a specific crypto tax law, so general corporate and income tax rules apply. This creates opportunities for tax planning but requires careful documentation.

Allowed Activities Under the Crypto Fund License

Holders of a crypto fund license in Georgia are permitted to engage in the following activities:

  • Managing portfolios of crypto assets for investors
  • Issuing fund shares or units to qualified investors
  • Administering the fund, including valuation and reporting
  • Investing in initial coin offerings (ICOs) and security token offerings (STOs)
  • Staking and lending crypto assets as part of fund strategy
  • Providing custody of crypto assets for the fund (if licensed separately or outsourced)

Activities that require additional licensing include direct retail brokerage, exchange services, or proprietary trading outside the fund. The NBG prohibits fund managers from commingling fund assets with their own. Consulting24 helps define the scope of activities in the application to avoid regulatory breaches.

Step-by-Step Process to Obtain a Crypto Fund License in Georgia

The process involves several stages, which Consulting24 coordinates with local partners:

  1. Initial consultation: Assess your fund structure and determine suitability for Georgia.
  2. Company incorporation: Register a Georgian company (usually a LLC or JSC) with the National Agency of Public Registry. Minimum share capital GEL 100,000.
  3. Document preparation: Draft AML/KYC policies, business plan, risk assessment, and fund prospectus.
  4. Appoint local representatives: Hire a compliance officer and a board of directors with relevant experience.
  5. Open a bank account: Deposit the minimum capital in a Georgian bank.
  6. Submit application: File with the NBG, including all documents and the application fee.
  7. NBG review: The regulator conducts fit-and-proper checks and may request additional information.
  8. License issuance: Once approved, you receive a VASP registration with fund management authorization.
  9. Ongoing compliance: Submit annual AML reports, maintain capital, and update the NBG on material changes.

Common Mistakes When Applying for a Crypto Fund License in Georgia

Many applicants encounter pitfalls that delay or derail their license. Common mistakes include:

  • Insufficient local presence: The NBG expects a physical office and local staff. Virtual offices are not accepted.
  • Poorly drafted AML policies: Generic templates without specific procedures for crypto funds are rejected.
  • Unclear source of funds: The NBG requires detailed documentation for all capital contributions.
  • Ignoring fit-and-proper requirements: Directors with criminal records or lack of experience are disqualified.
  • Underestimating timeline: Rushing the application leads to errors and requests for information.

License holders can manage crypto asset portfolios, issue fund shares, administer the fund, invest in ICOs/STOs, and engage in staking or lending as part of fund strategy. Retail brokerage or exchange services require separate licenses.

No, Georgia is not an EU member and is not subject to MiCA. Its crypto fund licensing is based on its own AML/CFT law and FATF recommendations, offering more flexibility than EU jurisdictions.
