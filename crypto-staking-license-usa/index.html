What Is a Crypto Staking License in the USA?

In the US, a crypto staking license is not a single permit but a combination of registrations and licenses. The SEC requires entities offering staking-as-a-service (where customers delegate tokens to a validator pool) to register as a broker-dealer or investment company, or qualify for an exemption. At the state level, money transmitter licenses (MTLs) are often needed if you handle customer funds. Some states also have specific crypto custody or virtual currency business licenses.

For example, the New York BitLicense is one of the most stringent state-level requirements. Other states like Wyoming have more crypto-friendly frameworks. The key is to determine which activities trigger federal securities laws and which state laws apply based on your customer locations. A typical staking business may need 3-5 state licenses initially, scaling up as customer base grows. The SEC's 2023 action against Kraken set a precedent that custodial staking-as-a-service is likely a security, requiring registration or exemption. This means even if you hold state licenses, you may still need to comply with federal securities laws.

Who Needs a Crypto Staking License in the US?

Any business that offers staking services to US customers likely needs some form of licensing. This includes:

  • Centralized exchanges that stake customer tokens (e.g., Coinbase, Kraken)
  • Staking pools or validator services that accept delegated tokens
  • Yield-generating products tied to staking
  • Decentralized finance (DeFi) protocols with staking features (though regulatory clarity is evolving)

If you operate a non-custodial staking service where customers retain control of private keys, you may face lower regulatory risk. However, the SEC has indicated that even non-custodial staking can be a security if there is an expectation of profits from the efforts of others. For example, the SEC's action against Kraken in 2023 for its staking program set a precedent that custodial staking-as-a-service is likely a security. Even if you are a non-US company, if you have US customers, you may be subject to US regulations. Consulting24 advises on structuring your business to minimize regulatory exposure.

License Type and Regulator

The primary regulator for staking activities is the SEC, which classifies most staking-as-a-service as a security offering. This means you must either register the staking program as a security (e.g., under the Securities Act of 1933) or find an exemption (e.g., Regulation D for accredited investors). Additionally, if you hold customer assets, you may need to register as a broker-dealer or a transfer agent.

At the state level, the Conference of State Bank Supervisors (CSBS) provides a model Money Transmitter Licensing framework, but each state has its own requirements. The New York Department of Financial Services (NYDFS) issues the BitLicense, which is required for virtual currency business activity in New York. Other states like California, Texas, and Florida also require MTLs for crypto businesses. As of 2026, over 40 states have some form of crypto regulation. Capital Requirement

Capital requirements vary by state and license type. For state MTLs, typical minimum net worth requirements range from $25,000 to $500,000, depending on transaction volume. For example, New York requires a minimum of $200,000 for a BitLicense. SEC registration does not have a fixed capital requirement but expects adequate financial resources. Some states also require a surety bond, often 10% of transaction volume with a minimum of $10,000.

It is important to note that these are minimums; regulators may require higher capital based on risk. For instance, if you handle over $1 million in daily transactions, expect higher net worth requirements. Consulting24 advises on structuring your capital to meet multi-state requirements efficiently. In practice, you should budget at least $100,000 in liquid capital to cover initial requirements across 3-5 states.

Tax Treatment of Staking Rewards

The IRS treats staking rewards as taxable income at the time of receipt, based on the fair market value of the tokens. This applies to both individuals and businesses. If you are a staking service provider, you may need to report rewards earned on behalf of customers, depending on the structure. In 2023, the IRS issued guidance clarifying that staking rewards are income, not capital gains, until sold. As of 2026, this remains the prevailing view.

For businesses, staking rewards are ordinary income. If you operate as a corporation, you may also be subject to corporate income tax on staking income. Additionally, if you are a non-US company with US customers, you may have withholding tax obligations on staking rewards distributed to US persons.

Allowed Activities Under a US Crypto Staking License

Depending on the licenses you hold, allowed activities include:

  • Operating a staking pool or validator node
  • Offering staking-as-a-service to customers
  • Managing delegated tokens for staking
  • Distributing staking rewards to customers
  • Providing staking-related analytics or software (may not require licensing)

Activities that are generally not allowed without additional licensing include: lending customer assets, offering unregistered securities, and commingling customer funds without proper safeguards. Each state may have specific prohibitions. For example, California's MTL prohibits using customer funds for proprietary staking without disclosure. If you plan to offer both staking and lending, you may need a separate lending license or a broker-dealer registration.

Step-by-Step Process to Obtain a US Crypto Staking License

  1. Determine your business model and which activities trigger SEC and state regulations.
  2. Engage legal counsel experienced in crypto securities and money transmission laws.
  3. Prepare SEC filing (e.g., Form D for exemption or registration statement) if staking is deemed a security.
  4. Identify target states for customers and apply for MTLs or virtual currency licenses.
  5. Develop compliance program including AML/KYC, cybersecurity, and consumer protection policies.
  6. Submit applications to each state regulator, including background checks and financial statements.
  7. Respond to regulator inquiries and undergo examinations.
  8. Maintain ongoing reporting and renew licenses annually.

Frequently asked questions

Do I need a federal license for crypto staking in the US?

There is no single federal license. You may need to register with the SEC if your staking program is deemed a security. Additionally, you need state money transmitter licenses in each state where you have customers. The SEC and state regulators work in tandem, so compliance with both is essential.

What is the cost of a US crypto staking license?

Costs vary widely. Expect to spend $50,000 to $200,000+ on SEC registration, $5,000 to $30,000 per state for MTLs, and $20,000 to $100,000 on compliance setup. Ongoing annual costs can be $50,000 to $500,000+. A full multi-state launch can cost over $300,000 in the first year.

How long does it take to get a US crypto staking license?

The timeline depends on the number of states. SEC registration takes 6-12 months. Each state MTL takes 3-9 months. New York BitLicense can take 12-18 months. Overall, expect 12-24 months for comprehensive coverage. Consulting24 can help expedite with proper preparation.

What is the minimum capital requirement for a US crypto staking business?

State MTL minimum net worth ranges from $25,000 to $500,000. New York BitLicense requires $200,000. SEC registration has no fixed capital requirement but expects adequate resources. You may also need a surety bond. Budget at least $100,000 in liquid capital for initial requirements.

Are staking rewards taxable in the US?

Yes, the IRS treats staking rewards as ordinary income at the fair market value when received. For businesses, this is taxable income. If you distribute rewards to customers, you may have reporting obligations. Consult a tax professional for your specific situation.

Can I offer staking to US customers from a non-US company?

If you have US customers, you are likely subject to US regulations. The SEC and state regulators can assert jurisdiction over foreign entities serving US persons. You may need to register with the SEC and obtain state licenses. Consulting24 advises on structuring to minimize US exposure.

What is the difference between custodial and non-custodial staking for licensing?

Custodial staking (where you hold customer private keys) is more likely to trigger money transmitter and securities regulations. Non-custodial staking (customers retain keys) may have lower risk, but the SEC has indicated it can still be a security. Both require careful legal analysis.

Which US states are most crypto-friendly for staking?

Wyoming has a progressive crypto framework with special purpose depository institutions. Texas and Florida have relatively straightforward MTL processes. New York is the most stringent due to the BitLicense. Many businesses start in 3-5 friendly states and expand later.

Do I need a separate license for each state?

Yes, each state has its own money transmitter or virtual currency license. There is no federal preemption. You must apply in every state where you have customers. Some states have reciprocity, but you still need to file separate applications. Consulting24 can help prioritize states.

What happens if I operate without a license?

Unlicensed staking services face enforcement actions from the SEC and state regulators. Penalties can include fines, disgorgement of profits, and criminal charges. For example, in 2023, the SEC charged Kraken $30 million for its unregistered staking program. Compliance is essential.

Can I use a Panama license to serve US customers?

No, a Panama license does not authorize you to serve US customers. US regulations apply to any business with US customers, regardless of where the company is incorporated. Panama is best for non-US markets. For US customers, you need US licenses.
