What Is the Crypto Stablecoin License in Anjouan?

The crypto stablecoin license in Anjouan is a regulatory authorization issued by the Anjouan Financial Services Authority (AFSA) that permits companies to issue, manage, and distribute stablecoins. Stablecoins are digital assets designed to maintain a stable value relative to a reference asset, such as a fiat currency (e.g., USD, EUR) or a commodity (e.g., gold). The license covers activities like token creation, reserve management, and redemption services.

Anjouan's framework is particularly attractive for startups and mid-sized firms due to its relatively low cost and flexible requirements. As of 2026, the license is fully operational, and the AFSA has established clear guidelines for stablecoin issuers. The jurisdiction does not impose a minimum capital requirement as high as some EU member states under MiCA, making it a viable alternative for those seeking a lighter regulatory touch. For example, a project issuing a USD-backed stablecoin with a small user base may find Anjouan's requirements more proportional than the EUR 150,000 capital needed for a CASP under MiCA. The AFSA focuses on the sufficiency of the reserve backing the stablecoin rather than a fixed capital floor, which can reduce upfront costs for well-capitalized projects.

In practice, the license allows you to issue a stablecoin that can be used for payments, remittances, or as a store of value. The AFSA requires that the stablecoin's value be maintained through a reserve of assets held by a qualified custodian. This reserve must be audited regularly to ensure transparency. For instance, a stablecoin pegged to the euro must hold euro-denominated assets in a segregated account, with monthly attestations published to the public. The license also covers the redemption process, where users can exchange the stablecoin for the underlying asset at any time.

License Type and Regulator

The regulator for the crypto stablecoin license in Anjouan is the Anjouan Financial Services Authority (AFSA). AFSA is the primary financial regulator for the island, responsible for licensing and supervising financial services, including digital assets. The license type is a 'Stablecoin Issuance License' under the Anjouan Digital Assets Act (as amended).

This license is distinct from a general crypto exchange or wallet license. It specifically authorizes the issuance and management of stablecoins, including reserve custody and redemption. The AFSA requires issuers to maintain a reserve of assets backing the stablecoin's value, with regular audits to ensure transparency. For example, a stablecoin pegged to USD must hold equivalent USD reserves in a segregated account, audited quarterly. The AFSA also mandates that the issuer have a clear redemption policy and a mechanism for handling technical failures or market disruptions.

Compared to other regulators, AFSA is known for its pragmatic approach. It does not impose prescriptive capital requirements but instead evaluates each application on its merits. This can be advantageous for innovative stablecoin models that do not fit neatly into traditional categories. However, the regulator expects a high standard of compliance, particularly in AML/KYC and consumer protection. Capital Requirement

The Anjouan stablecoin license does not have a fixed statutory minimum capital requirement like the EU's MiCA (which imposes EUR 50,000 to 150,000 depending on service class). Instead, the AFSA evaluates the applicant's financial standing on a case-by-case basis. Typically, the regulator expects the company to have sufficient capital to cover operational expenses for at least 12 months and to maintain a reserve that fully backs the issued stablecoin.

As a guideline, most applicants are advised to have at least EUR 50,000 in paid-up capital, but this can vary. Consulting24 can provide a tailored estimate during a consultation, based on your specific stablecoin model and business plan. For instance, a simple fiat-backed stablecoin with low volume may require less capital than an algorithmic stablecoin with complex mechanics. The capital is not required to be held in a specific form; it can be in cash or liquid assets. However, the reserve backing the stablecoin must be held in a segregated account with a licensed custodian. The AFSA may also require a surety bond or insurance for certain risks, such as cyber attacks or fraud.

Tax Treatment

Anjouan offers a favorable tax regime for licensed entities. Corporate income tax is typically 0% for offshore companies that do not conduct business within the Comoros. Stablecoin issuers licensed under AFSA are generally considered offshore and thus benefit from this exemption. However, it is essential to confirm the tax status with a local tax advisor, as the Comoros Union may have different rules for entities that interact with local residents.

Value-added tax (VAT) is not applicable to digital asset services in Anjouan. There are no withholding taxes on dividends or interest paid to non-resident shareholders. This tax-neutral environment makes Anjouan attractive for stablecoin projects seeking to minimize tax leakage. For example, a project earning revenue from transaction fees on stablecoin transfers would pay 0% corporate tax in Anjouan. However, you should be aware of tax obligations in the jurisdictions where your users are located. Many stablecoin issuers also establish a presence in a tax treaty jurisdiction to avoid double taxation. Consulting24 can recommend tax advisors who specialize in crypto and offshore structures.

Allowed Activities Under the License

The Anjouan stablecoin license permits a range of activities, including:

  • Issuance of stablecoins (fiat-backed, commodity-backed, or algorithmic).
  • Management of the reserve assets backing the stablecoin.
  • Redemption of stablecoins for the underlying asset.
  • Distribution and marketing of the stablecoin to investors and users.
  • Integration with third-party platforms, such as exchanges and payment processors.
  • Staking or lending of reserve assets (if permitted by the license terms).

Activities that are not automatically allowed include operating a crypto exchange (unless separately licensed) or providing custodial wallet services beyond the stablecoin's reserve. If your project requires these, additional licensing or amendments may be needed. For example, if you want to offer a trading platform for your stablecoin, you would need a separate exchange license. Similarly, if you plan to offer interest-bearing accounts based on the stablecoin, that may require a banking license. The AFSA can provide guidance on the scope of your license during the application process.

Step-by-Step Application Process

The application process for an Anjouan stablecoin license involves several stages. Consulting24 advises and coordinates each step.

  1. Pre-application consultation: Discuss your project with our experts to determine suitability and prepare a roadmap.
  2. Company incorporation: Register a legal entity in Anjouan (typically a Limited Liability Company or International Business Company). This involves filing with the Anjouan Registrar of Companies and obtaining a tax identification number.
  3. Documentation: Prepare a business plan, financial projections, compliance policies (AML/KYC), and a technical white paper for the stablecoin. The white paper should describe the stablecoin's design, consensus mechanism, and risk factors.
  4. Submit application: File the application with AFSA, including all supporting documents and the application fee. The application must be signed by a director and a company secretary.
  5. Regulatory review: AFSA reviews the application, which may involve requests for additional information or clarifications. This stage typically takes 2-4 months.
  6. License issuance: Once approved, the license is granted, and the company can commence stablecoin operations. The license is valid for one year and must be renewed annually.
  7. Ongoing compliance: Submit periodic reports, maintain the reserve, and undergo annual audits. Failure to comply can result in fines or license revocation.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

When applying for an Anjouan stablecoin license, founders often make several mistakes that can delay or derail the process. Here are the most common ones:

  • Incomplete documentation: Submitting a business plan without detailed financial projections or a white paper that lacks technical depth. The AFSA expects a thorough explanation of the stablecoin's mechanics and risk management.
  • Weak AML/KYC policies: Using generic templates that do not address the specific risks of stablecoins, such as anonymity features or cross-border transactions. The AFSA requires policies that comply with FATF standards.
  • Underestimating reserve requirements: Failing to arrange a qualified custodian for the reserve assets before applying. The AFSA will ask for proof of a custodial agreement.
  • Ignoring tax implications: Assuming that the 0% tax in Anjouan eliminates all tax obligations. Issuers must still comply with tax laws in the jurisdictions where they have a presence or where their users reside.
  • Overlooking ongoing compliance: Treating the license as a one-time event. The AFSA requires annual renewals, audits, and reporting. Non-compliance can lead to revocation.
