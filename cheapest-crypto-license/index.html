What Is the Cheapest Crypto License?

The cheapest crypto license refers to the lowest-cost regulatory approval required to operate a cryptocurrency business legally in a given jurisdiction. As of 2026, Panama offers the most affordable option with a fixed all-in fee of EUR 6,000. This license is issued by the Panamanian financial regulator and allows companies to provide crypto-related services such as exchange, custody, and payment processing.

Unlike EU member states that now enforce MiCA capital tiers of EUR 50,000 to EUR 150,000, Panama has no minimum capital requirement. This drastically reduces the upfront financial burden for startups. However, founders must still meet compliance standards, including AML/KYC procedures and beneficial ownership transparency.

Panama's crypto license is not a passport to the EU, but it is ideal for operations targeting Latin America, Asia, or global markets without the high costs of European regulation. The license is a general financial services authorization that covers virtual asset service provider (VASP) activities. It is regulated by the Superintendencia de Bancos de Panamá (SBP) and the Financial Analysis Unit (UAF) for AML compliance.

Who Needs the Cheapest Crypto License?

This license is suitable for a range of crypto businesses, especially those with limited capital or a focus on non-EU markets. Typical applicants include:

  • Early-stage crypto startups that need legal compliance but cannot afford EUR 50,000+ in capital.
  • Companies operating peer-to-peer exchanges, wallet services, or payment processing for global clients.
  • Businesses targeting Latin America, Asia, or the Middle East, where Panama's time zone and trade links are advantageous.
  • Founders who want a fast and cost-effective entry into the regulated space without complex bureaucracy.

If you plan to serve EU customers, you may need a Lithuania crypto license or an Estonia crypto license, which are more expensive but offer EU-wide access under MiCA. Panama is not for everyone, but for many global operations it is the cheapest path to compliance.

License Type and Regulator

Panama's crypto license is not a standalone 'crypto license' but a regulated financial services authorization that covers virtual asset service provider (VASP) activities. The primary regulator is the Superintendencia de Bancos de Panamá (SBP), which oversees all financial services. The Financial Analysis Unit (UAF) handles AML/CFT oversight and conducts due diligence on applicants.

There is no crypto-specific regulator, which simplifies the process. The license allows you to operate as a VASP under Panamanian law, provided you comply with local AML regulations and beneficial ownership disclosure. The SBP reviews your business plan, compliance policies, and financial stability. Once approved, you must maintain ongoing reporting and submit to periodic audits.

For comparison, EU jurisdictions like Lithuania have dedicated crypto licenses under MiCA, with the Bank of Lithuania as regulator. Panama's approach is more flexible but lacks EU passporting.

Capital Requirement

Panama has no minimum capital requirement for crypto license applicants. This is a major advantage over EU jurisdictions where MiCA mandates capital of EUR 50,000 for basic services, EUR 125,000 for exchanges, and EUR 150,000 for custodial wallets. Panama's zero capital requirement makes it the cheapest crypto license in terms of upfront cash.

However, you must demonstrate sufficient operational funds to run the business. The regulator expects a sound business plan and proof of financial stability, but there is no fixed minimum. For example, you might need to show you have enough to cover salaries, rent, and technology costs for at least six months. This is typically a few thousand euros, not tens of thousands.

This low barrier to entry is ideal for startups that want to test the market before committing large sums.

Tax Treatment

Panama operates a territorial tax system: only income generated within Panama is taxed. Crypto businesses that serve international clients typically pay 0% tax on foreign-sourced income. There is no capital gains tax, no VAT on crypto services, and no withholding tax on dividends. This makes Panama highly attractive for tax efficiency.

Companies must file annual tax returns and pay a flat annual license fee (around EUR 300-500). Compared to EU corporate tax rates of 15-25%, Panama offers significant savings. For example, a company earning EUR 500,000 from global clients would pay zero tax in Panama, versus EUR 75,000-125,000 in the EU.

However, you must comply with transfer pricing rules and substance requirements if you have related parties. Consulting24 can advise on tax planning. This is general guidance, not legal advice.

Allowed Activities

With a Panama crypto license, you can legally conduct a range of crypto-related activities. These include:

  • Cryptocurrency exchange (fiat-to-crypto and crypto-to-crypto)
  • Digital wallet services (custodial and non-custodial)
  • Payment processing using crypto
  • Token issuance and management (subject to securities laws)
  • OTC trading and brokerage
  • Initial exchange offerings (IEOs) with additional compliance

Activities that are not allowed include operating a crypto ATM network without additional permits and offering services to sanctioned jurisdictions. The license does not cover securities tokens unless separately registered. If you plan to offer derivatives or margin trading, you may need a separate securities license.

For EU-focused businesses, a Lithuania crypto license covers similar activities but with MiCA harmonisation.

Step-by-Step Process

  1. Company incorporation in Panama (included in the EUR 6,000 fee). We set up a Panama corporation with nominee directors if needed.
  2. Document preparation: business plan, AML/KYC policies, beneficial ownership structure, and compliance manual. We draft these based on your business model.
  3. Submission to the Superintendencia de Bancos de Panamá (SBP) via a licensed agent. The application includes all corporate documents and compliance policies.
  4. Due diligence by the Financial Analysis Unit (UAF) on shareholders and directors. This involves background checks and source of funds verification.
  5. Approval and issuance of the license, typically within 3-6 months. You will receive a formal resolution from the SBP.
  6. Post-licensing: register with the tax authority (DGI) and set up a local bank account. We assist with banking introductions.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Underestimating compliance costs: Even with a cheap license, you need AML software, legal fees, and a compliance officer. Budget at least EUR 5,000-10,000 annually for compliance.
  • Ignoring tax obligations: Even territorial tax requires filing. Non-filing can lead to penalties. You must file an annual tax return even if you owe zero tax.
  • Choosing the wrong jurisdiction: Panama is not for everyone. If you need EU passporting, consider Estonia or Lithuania. If you need a strong banking reputation, Lithuania may be better.
  • Not preparing a solid business plan: The regulator expects a clear description of your business model, risk management, and source of funds. A weak plan can delay approval.
  • Overlooking substance requirements: You need a local registered agent and physical address. Some licenses require a local director or employee.
