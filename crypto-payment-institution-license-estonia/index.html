What Is the Crypto Payment Institution License Estonia?

The crypto payment institution license Estonia is a regulatory authorisation that allows a company to provide virtual asset services as a payment institution. It is distinct from a simple VASP registration because it covers payment-related activities such as exchanging fiat for crypto, executing payment transactions, and issuing crypto payment instruments. The license is governed by the Money Laundering and Terrorist Financing Prevention Act (MLTFPA) and, as of 2026, operates under MiCA's harmonised rules.

Key characteristics:

  • Issued by the Estonian Financial Intelligence Unit (FIU) with oversight transitioning to the Financial Supervision Authority (Finantsinspektsioon).
  • Minimum capital requirement: EUR 125,000 for payment services (tier 2 under MiCA).
  • Allows passporting within the EU/EEA after authorisation.
  • Requires a physical presence in Estonia, including a local board member and registered office.

This license is ideal for businesses that want to offer both crypto exchange and payment processing under one regulatory umbrella. For example, a fintech startup building a mobile app that lets users buy, sell, and spend crypto at merchants would need this license. The license also covers wallet services where the provider holds the private keys on behalf of clients.

Who Needs a Crypto Payment Institution License in Estonia?

Any company that intends to provide crypto-to-fiat exchange services, wallet custody, or payment processing for virtual assets must obtain this license. Typical candidates include:

  • Cryptocurrency exchanges that support fiat on-ramps and off-ramps.
  • Payment processors facilitating merchant acceptance of crypto.
  • Wallet providers that allow users to send and receive fiat balances.
  • Fintech startups building crypto payment apps.

If your business model involves handling fiat currency in connection with virtual assets, the crypto payment institution license is the appropriate route. For pure crypto-only services (no fiat), a simpler VASP registration may suffice, but the payment license offers greater credibility and access to banking partners. For instance, a company that only offers crypto-to-crypto exchange without fiat might not need this license, but adding fiat conversion would trigger the requirement. License Type and Regulator

The license is categorised as a payment institution license under Estonian law, specifically for virtual asset services. The primary regulator is the Estonian Financial Intelligence Unit (FIU), which handles anti-money laundering (AML) supervision. However, under MiCA implementation, the Financial Supervision Authority (Finantsinspektsioon) will take over prudential supervision for larger firms. As of 2026, both bodies collaborate.

Key regulatory references:

  • Estonian FIU: https://www.politsei.ee/en/instructions/financial-intelligence-unit
  • Finantsinspektsioon: https://www.fi.ee/en

The license allows activities classified under MiCA as CASP (Crypto Asset Service Provider) with payment institution features. It is not a credit institution or e-money license, but a specialised payment license for crypto. The FIU focuses on AML compliance, while Finantsinspektsioon oversees financial soundness. This dual oversight ensures strong regulation.

Capital Requirement

Under MiCA, the minimum capital for a crypto payment institution license in Estonia is EUR 125,000. This is the tier 2 capital requirement for payment services that involve fiat currency exchange and custody. The capital must be held in liquid assets and maintained throughout the license period.

If your business also provides ancillary services like investment advice or portfolio management, higher capital may be required (up to EUR 150,000). Consulting24 can help structure your capital allocation to meet the exact threshold for your planned activities. For example, if you plan to offer both exchange and custody, you need EUR 125,000; adding investment advice would push it to EUR 150,000. The capital can be in the form of cash or cash equivalents, and it must be deposited in an Estonian bank account.

Tax Treatment

Estonia has a unique corporate income tax system: profits are not taxed until distributed (i.e., dividends). For crypto businesses, this means no annual corporate tax on retained earnings. The standard corporate income tax rate on distributed profits is 20% (14% for regular dividends). VAT treatment: crypto-to-fiat exchange is generally exempt from VAT, but advisory services may be subject to 20% VAT.

Personal income tax for employees and directors is 20%, with social tax of 33%. Crypto transactions are treated as capital gains for individuals, taxed at 20% on realisation. It is advisable to consult a local tax advisor for your specific structure. For example, if your company holds crypto as an investment, unrealised gains are not taxed; only when you sell or distribute profits. This makes Estonia attractive for long-term holders.

Allowed Activities

With a crypto payment institution license Estonia, you can perform the following activities:

  • Exchange between fiat currencies and virtual assets.
  • Exchange between one or more virtual assets.
  • Transfer of virtual assets on behalf of clients.
  • Custody and administration of virtual assets (wallet services).
  • Execution of payment transactions in fiat and crypto.
  • Issuing crypto payment instruments (e.g., prepaid cards).

Activities that require additional licensing (e.g., lending, derivatives) are not covered. The license is strictly for payment and exchange services. For example, if you want to offer crypto-backed loans, you would need a separate credit institution license. The FIU may also restrict certain activities based on your business model, so it is important to define your scope clearly in the application.

Step-by-Step Application Process

Applying for the crypto payment institution license Estonia involves these steps:

  1. Company incorporation: Register a private limited company (OÜ) in Estonia with at least EUR 2,500 share capital. Appoint a local board member.
  2. Prepare documentation: Business plan, AML/CTF policies, risk assessment, internal controls, and financial projections.
  3. Submit application: File with the FIU via the e-business register. Include proof of capital, background checks on directors, and a description of the IT system.
  4. Regulatory review: The FIU assesses compliance with AML requirements. This may take 2-4 months.
  5. Approval and licensing: Once approved, you receive the license and can start operations. Ongoing reporting to the FIU is required.

Compliance and Ongoing Obligations

License holders must adhere to strict AML/CTF rules, including:

  • Customer due diligence (CDD) and enhanced due diligence (EDD) for high-risk clients.
  • Transaction monitoring and suspicious activity reporting.
  • Annual AML audit by an external auditor.
  • Reporting to the FIU on a quarterly basis.
  • Maintaining minimum capital at all times.

  • No local presence: the regulator expects a physical office and local management.
  • Ignoring tax obligations: even with no tax on retained earnings, filing is mandatory.
  • Rushing the application: incomplete submissions cause delays.

Frequently asked questions

What is the minimum capital for a crypto payment institution license in Estonia?

The minimum capital is EUR 125,000 for payment services under MiCA tier 2. This must be held in liquid assets and maintained throughout the license period. If you offer additional services like investment advice, the requirement may increase to EUR 150,000.

How long does it take to get a crypto payment institution license in Estonia?

The typical timeline is 3 to 6 months from application to approval. This depends on the completeness of your documentation and the regulator's workload. Consulting24 can help streamline the process by preparing a thorough application.

Can I passport my Estonian crypto license to other EU countries?

Yes, under MiCA, once you are licensed in Estonia, you can passport your services to any EU/EEA member state without needing additional licenses. You simply notify the regulator of your intent to operate cross-border.

What activities are not allowed under this license?

The license does not cover lending, derivatives, or investment advice. For these activities, you would need additional authorisation. The license is strictly for payment and exchange services related to virtual assets.

Is a physical presence required in Estonia?

Yes, you must have a registered office in Estonia and appoint a local board member. The regulator expects a physical presence for supervision purposes. Consulting24 can help you set up the local infrastructure.

What are the ongoing compliance obligations?

You must conduct customer due diligence, transaction monitoring, and suspicious activity reporting. An annual AML audit is required, and you must report to the FIU quarterly. Maintaining minimum capital is also an ongoing obligation.

How does Estonia tax crypto businesses?

Estonia does not tax retained earnings. Corporate income tax is only applied when profits are distributed (20% on dividends). Crypto-to-fiat exchange is VAT-exempt. Personal capital gains from crypto are taxed at 20%.

Can I use an e-residency to apply for the license?

Yes, e-residency allows you to incorporate a company remotely, but you still need a local board member and physical office. The application process can be managed online, but the regulator may require an in-person meeting.

What happens if I fail to comply with AML rules?

Non-compliance can result in fines, suspension, or revocation of the license. The FIU conducts regular inspections. It is essential to have strong AML policies and procedures in place. Consulting24 offers ongoing compliance support.

How does Estonia compare to Panama for crypto licensing?

Panama offers a flat fee of EUR 6,000 with no minimum capital and a faster timeline of 2-4 months. However, it does not provide EU passporting. Estonia is more expensive but allows access to the entire EU market.
