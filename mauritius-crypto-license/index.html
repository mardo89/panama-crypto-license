What is the Mauritius Crypto License?

The Mauritius crypto license is a regulatory authorisation issued by the FSC under the VAITOS Act. It allows businesses to offer virtual asset services, including exchange, custody, and token issuance, in compliance with anti-money laundering (AML) and counter-terrorism financing (CTF) rules.

The regime is designed to balance innovation with investor protection. Licensees must meet capital requirements, implement strong governance, and submit regular reports. The FSC maintains a public register of licensed entities, enhancing trust with partners and clients.

Mauritius is not a member of the European Union, so it is not subject to MiCA. However, its framework aligns with FATF recommendations, making it a respected jurisdiction for global crypto operations. The FSC has issued over 30 licenses since 2022, covering exchanges, custodians, and token issuers. For example, a typical exchange license allows matching orders for fiat-to-crypto and crypto-to-crypto pairs, with mandatory segregation of client assets. The regulator also requires licensees to have a local office with at least two employees, including a compliance officer.

Compared to Estonia or Lithuania, Mauritius offers a non-EU alternative with lower operational costs and a favourable tax regime. However, the application process is more document-intensive, requiring detailed business plans, financial projections, and AML policies.

Who Needs a Mauritius Crypto License?

Any entity providing virtual asset services in or from Mauritius must hold a license. This includes:

  • Crypto exchanges (centralised and peer-to-peer)
  • Digital wallet providers (custodial and non-custodial)
  • Token issuers (ICOs/STOs)
  • Custodians of virtual assets
  • Brokers and dealers in virtual assets
  • Virtual asset transfer services (including payment processors)

If you plan to serve African markets or seek a credible offshore jurisdiction with English common law, Mauritius is a strong candidate. The license also suits businesses that want a stepping stone to more complex regimes like Dubai or Switzerland. For example, a fintech startup targeting remittances between Europe and Africa might use Mauritius as a base due to its time zone alignment and double taxation treaties.

Businesses that only provide software development or consulting without handling client assets may not need a license, but the FSC recommends a preliminary assessment. Consulting24 offers a free initial consultation to determine if your activities fall under VAITOS.

License Type and Regulator

The regulator is the Financial Services Commission (FSC) of Mauritius. The VAITOS Act defines two main categories:

CategoryScope
Virtual Asset Service Provider (VASP)Exchange, transfer, custody, and administration of virtual assets
Initial Token Offering (ITO) Service ProviderIssuance and management of token offerings

Both categories require a physical presence in Mauritius, including a registered office and at least one director resident in Mauritius. The FSC conducts fit-and-proper checks on all key personnel, including shareholders with more than 10% ownership. The FSC also requires a local compliance officer who is a resident of Mauritius and has relevant experience in AML/CTF.

In 2024, the FSC introduced a sandbox regime for innovative projects, allowing limited operations without a full license for up to 12 months. However, most commercial operations require a standard license. The FSC publishes a list of licensed entities on its website, which is updated quarterly.

Capital Requirement

The VAITOS Act does not prescribe a fixed minimum capital in the law itself. However, the FSC requires applicants to demonstrate adequate financial resources based on the nature and scale of operations. In practice, a minimum capital of EUR 50,000 to 100,000 is typical for a standard VASP license. This amount must be maintained as paid-up capital and may be held in a bank account in Mauritius.

For ITO service providers, the capital requirement may be higher, reflecting the greater risk profile. The FSC assesses each application on a case-by-case basis. For example, a startup planning to hold client assets in custody may need EUR 100,000, while a pure exchange platform might be approved with EUR 50,000. Capital can be in the form of cash or liquid assets. The FSC does not accept cryptocurrencies as capital. Licensees must also maintain professional indemnity insurance of at least EUR 1 million.

Tax Treatment

Mauritius offers a favourable tax regime for licensed crypto businesses. The corporate tax rate is a flat 15%, with a partial exemption system that can reduce the effective rate to as low as 3% for certain foreign-source income. There is no capital gains tax, no withholding tax on dividends, and no VAT on digital services.

Mauritius has an extensive network of double taxation agreements (DTAs) with over 40 countries, which can further optimise your global tax position. However, tax planning must comply with the OECD's Base Erosion and Profit Shifting (BEPS) requirements. Always seek local tax advice.

For example, a crypto exchange earning fees from clients in the UK could benefit from the DTA to avoid double taxation. Additionally, the Global Business Licence (GBL) regime, which many crypto companies use, offers a reduced tax rate of 3% on foreign-source income that meets substance requirements. The FSC and the Mauritius Revenue Authority coordinate to ensure compliance.

Allowed Activities

A Mauritius crypto license permits a wide range of activities, including:

  • Operating a virtual asset exchange (order book, OTC, P2P)
  • Providing custodial and non-custodial wallet services
  • Issuing and managing token offerings (ITO/STO)
  • Transferring virtual assets on behalf of clients
  • Advisory services on virtual assets
  • Staking and lending (subject to FSC approval)

Activities not allowed include gambling-related tokens, privacy coins (unless explicitly approved), and services that violate international sanctions. The FSC also prohibits commingling client assets with operational funds without proper segregation. For example, a platform that offers margin trading must hold additional capital to cover potential losses. The FSC may also restrict certain high-risk activities for new licensees, with a phased approach to expanding services.

If you plan to offer derivatives or futures on virtual assets, you may need a separate license under the Securities Act. Consulting24 can help you scope your activities correctly.

Step-by-Step Application Process

  1. Pre-application consultation - We assess your business model and advise on suitability. This includes a gap analysis of your AML policies and financial projections.
  2. Company incorporation - Register a company in Mauritius with a local registered office and director. The company must have a minimum of two directors, one of whom must be resident in Mauritius.
  3. Document preparation - Compile business plan, AML/CTF policies, financial projections, and fit-and-proper declarations for all key personnel. The business plan must detail your target market, revenue model, and risk management framework.
  4. Submit application - File with the FSC along with the application fee. The FSC will acknowledge receipt within 5 business days.
  5. FSC review - The regulator conducts due diligence and may request additional information (2-4 months). This includes background checks on directors and shareholders.
  6. License issuance - Once approved, you receive the license and are listed on the FSC register. The license is valid for one year and must be renewed annually.
  7. Ongoing compliance - Submit annual reports, maintain capital, and undergo audits. The FSC may conduct on-site inspections.

Benefits of a Mauritius Crypto License

  • Regulatory clarity - The VAITOS Act provides a clear legal framework for virtual assets, with defined rules on custody, exchange, and token issuance.
  • Tax efficiency - Low effective tax rate of 3-15% and no capital gains tax, making it attractive for trading profits.
  • International credibility - FSC oversight enhances trust with partners and clients, especially in Africa and Asia.
  • Access to African markets - Mauritius is a gateway to Africa, with strong trade links and a stable political environment.
  • English common law - Familiar legal system for international investors, with a well-established court system.
  • No citizenship requirement - Foreign directors are allowed (with a local resident director).
  • No VAT on digital services - Reduces operational costs for B2B transactions.

Compared to Malta, which has higher capital requirements (EUR 100,000+) and a more complex regulatory framework, Mauritius offers a simpler path. However, Malta provides EU passporting under MiCA, which Mauritius does not.

Compliance and Trust

Compliance is at the heart of the Mauritius regime. Licensees must implement strong AML/CTF measures, including customer due diligence (CDD), transaction monitoring, and suspicious activity reporting. The FSC conducts periodic inspections and can impose sanctions for non-compliance, including fines up to EUR 500,000 or license revocation.

We recommend appointing a local compliance officer and using automated screening tools like Chainalysis or Elliptic. In 2025, the FSC increased its scrutiny of beneficial ownership, requiring licensees to maintain a register of ultimate beneficial owners (UBOs) with more than 25% ownership. Failure to comply can result in penalties. The FSC also participates in the Global Forum on Transparency and Exchange of Information for Tax Purposes.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Incomplete documentation - Missing business plans or AML policies delay the process. Ensure your business plan includes a detailed risk assessment and revenue model.
  • Underestimating capital requirements - The FSC expects sufficient working capital, not just the minimum. A common mistake is to apply with the bare minimum, leading to rejection.
  • Ignoring local presence - You need a physical office and a resident director. Some applicants try to use virtual offices, which the FSC does not accept.
  • Overlooking tax reporting - Even with low taxes, annual filings are mandatory. Late filings can result in penalties.
  • Choosing the wrong jurisdiction - Compare with other jurisdictions to find the best fit. For example, if you need EU passporting, Lithuania or Estonia may be better.
  • Not preparing for FSC interviews - The FSC may interview key personnel. Prepare by reviewing your business plan and AML policies.
