What Is the Offshore Czech Republic Crypto License?

The offshore Czech Republic crypto license is a registration with the Czech National Bank (CNB) under the Act on Anti-Money Laundering (AML Act). It allows non-resident companies to provide virtual asset services, such as exchange and custody, from the Czech Republic. Unlike full MiCA authorization, this is a simplified registration for VASPs, but it still requires compliance with AML/KYC rules and beneficial ownership disclosure.

Key characteristics in 2026:

  • Regulator: Czech National Bank (CNB)
  • License type: VASP registration (not a full MiCA license)
  • Minimum capital: None explicitly, but EUR 50,000-150,000 may be required depending on services (as per MiCA tiers)
  • Tax: Corporate income tax 19% (standard rate)
  • Timeline: 3-6 months for registration

The Czech regime is often used by offshore companies due to its relatively low entry barriers and EU passporting possibilities under MiCA. However, the CNB has tightened scrutiny in 2026, requiring substance and a local presence. For example, the CNB now expects a physical office and at least one local director or a nominee with decision-making authority. This shift aims to prevent shell companies from registering without real operations. Consulting24 helps you meet these substance requirements efficiently.

License Type and Regulator

The Czech crypto license is a VASP registration under the AML Act, overseen by the Czech National Bank (CNB). The CNB is the central bank and financial regulator, responsible for anti-money laundering supervision of virtual asset service providers. In 2026, the CNB also enforces MiCA requirements for larger firms, but the basic VASP registration remains available for smaller entities.

Key regulatory points:

  • Registration is mandatory for exchange between virtual and fiat currencies, exchange between virtual currencies, and custody of virtual assets.
  • No separate license for crypto-to-crypto exchange (covered under the same registration).
  • Beneficial ownership must be disclosed to the CNB.
  • Annual AML compliance reports required.

The CNB maintains a public register of VASPs, which enhances credibility with partners and banks. However, the register also means your company details are visible, which some offshore founders may want to avoid. For a more private option, consider Panama, where the regulator does not publish a public register of licensees.

Capital Requirement

The Czech Republic does not impose a statutory minimum capital for VASP registration. However, in 2026, MiCA capital tiers apply to firms that passport services across the EU. For exchange and custody services, the minimum capital is EUR 50,000 for basic services, EUR 125,000 for more complex offerings, and EUR 150,000 for full-service platforms. The CNB may also require proof of sufficient financial resources to cover operational risks.

For pure Czech-only operations, no minimum capital is mandated, but the CNB expects adequate reserves. Consulting24 recommends maintaining at least EUR 50,000 in share capital to demonstrate substance. For specific requirements, consult our requirements page. In practice, most successful applicants show at least EUR 50,000 in paid-up capital, even if not legally required, to satisfy the CNB's substance expectations.

Tax Treatment

Czech corporate income tax is a flat 19% on profits, one of the lowest in the EU. Crypto transactions are treated as barter for tax purposes, with gains taxed as income. VAT is not applicable on crypto-to-crypto exchanges, but fiat conversions may be subject to standard VAT (21%).

Key tax points:

  • No specific crypto tax regime; general tax rules apply.
  • Capital gains from crypto are taxed as corporate income.
  • Withholding tax on dividends: 15% (reduced under tax treaties).
  • Transfer pricing rules apply for related-party transactions.
  • Annual tax returns must be filed by the company.

Compared to Panama's territorial tax system (0% on foreign-source income), the Czech rate is higher but offers EU market access. Allowed Activities

The Czech VASP registration permits the following activities:

  • Exchange services between virtual currencies and fiat currencies.
  • Exchange services between one or more virtual currencies.
  • Custody and administration of virtual assets or private keys.
  • Transfer services of virtual assets (limited).
  • Participation in and provision of financial services related to an issuer's offer and sale of virtual assets (under MiCA).

Activities not covered: operating a crypto ATM (requires additional license), mining, and non-custodial wallet software (if not providing custody). The CNB may restrict certain high-risk activities, such as privacy coins or unregistered token offerings. For a full list, review the application process. If your business involves activities not covered by the Czech license, we can advise on alternative jurisdictions like Lithuania or Panama that may offer broader scopes.

Step-by-Step Application Process

Obtaining the Czech crypto license involves these steps:

  1. Company incorporation: Register a Czech s.r.o. (limited liability company) with a registered address and local director (or nominee). The minimum share capital for s.r.o. is CZK 1 (approx. EUR 0.04), but we recommend at least EUR 50,000 for credibility.
  2. AML/KYC program: Develop internal policies, risk assessment, and procedures compliant with Czech AML Act and MiCA. This includes customer due diligence, transaction monitoring, and suspicious activity reporting.
  3. Beneficial ownership register: File beneficial owners with the Czech register. The CNB cross-checks this with the commercial register.
  4. VASP application: Submit the registration form to the CNB with supporting documents (business plan, AML manual, financial statements, etc.). The application must be in Czech or accompanied by a certified translation.
  5. CNB review: The CNB has 30 days to approve or request additional information. In practice, the process takes 2-4 months due to back-and-forth.
  6. Post-registration: Appoint an AML officer, register with the tax office, and open a bank account. The AML officer must be a Czech resident or a qualified professional.

Banking and Payment Processing

Opening a bank account for a Czech VASP can be challenging due to compliance concerns. Most traditional banks require the license or registration before opening an account. In 2026, some Czech banks (e.g., CSOB, Komercni banka) accept VASPs with proper documentation, but many still refuse.

Alternative solutions:

  • EMI (electronic money institution) accounts with crypto-friendly providers like Paysera, Revolut Business, or Monese.
  • Payment processors like Stripe or Checkout.com that support crypto businesses.
  • Fiat-to-crypto on-ramps via licensed partners like MoonPay or Simplex.

Compliance and Ongoing Obligations

Once registered, the VASP must comply with ongoing AML/KYC requirements:

  • Customer due diligence (CDD) for all transactions over EUR 1,000.
  • Transaction monitoring and suspicious activity reporting.
  • Annual AML audit and reporting to the CNB.
  • Beneficial ownership updates within 30 days of changes.
  • Record keeping for 10 years.

Non-compliance can result in fines up to EUR 5 million or 10% of annual turnover. This is general guidance, not legal advice. Common Mistakes and How to Avoid Them

Founders often make these errors:

  • Insufficient substance: The CNB requires a local office and director. Using a virtual office without real presence can lead to rejection. Ensure you have a physical lease and a local director who can make decisions.
  • Poor AML documentation: Generic AML policies are not accepted. Tailor them to your business model, including specific risk factors for your services and customer base.
  • Ignoring MiCA capital tiers: If you plan to passport, ensure you meet the capital requirements from the start. Retroactively increasing capital is difficult.
  • Delaying bank account opening: Start the process early; it can take months. Apply for an EMI account in parallel with the license application.

Frequently asked questions

What is the difference between a Czech VASP registration and a MiCA license?

A Czech VASP registration is a national AML registration under the Czech AML Act, while a MiCA license is a full EU authorization under the Markets in Crypto-Assets regulation. The VASP registration is simpler and cheaper but does not automatically allow passporting across the EU. MiCA licenses require higher capital and compliance but offer full EU market access.

Can I apply for a Czech crypto license as a non-resident?

Yes, non-residents can apply, but the company must have a registered office in the Czech Republic and at least one local director or a nominee. The CNB expects substance, so a virtual office alone is not sufficient. Consulting24 can help you set up the required local presence.

What are the ongoing costs after obtaining the license?

Annual costs include registered office fees (EUR 1,000-2,000), AML officer services (EUR 2,000-4,000), compliance audits (EUR 2,000-5,000), and accounting/tax filing (EUR 1,500-3,000). Total ongoing costs typically range from EUR 6,000 to 14,000 per year.

Is the Czech crypto license recognized by other EU countries?

The VASP registration is recognized for AML supervision purposes, but it does not automatically grant passporting rights for services. For cross-border services, you may need to register in each EU member state or obtain a full MiCA license. The Czech license is best for domestic or B2B operations.

How long does the CNB take to process the application?

The CNB has a statutory 30-day review period, but in practice, the process takes 2-4 months due to document preparation and potential requests for additional information. Consulting24 helps prepare a complete application to minimize delays.

Can I operate a crypto exchange with the Czech license?

Yes, the license covers exchange between virtual and fiat currencies, as well as crypto-to-crypto exchange. Custody services are also included. However, operating a crypto ATM requires a separate license. Check the allowed activities section for details.

What are the tax implications for crypto transactions in the Czech Republic?

Crypto gains are taxed as corporate income at 19%. Crypto-to-crypto exchanges are treated as barter and taxed accordingly. VAT at 21% applies to fiat conversion services. There is no specific crypto tax regime, so general tax rules apply. Consulting24 can provide tax modeling.

Do I need to have a physical office in the Czech Republic?

Yes, the CNB requires a registered office and expects a physical presence. A virtual office is not sufficient. You need a lease agreement and a local director or nominee. Consulting24 can arrange a physical office space and local representation.

What happens if I don't comply with AML obligations?

Non-compliance can result in fines up to EUR 5 million or 10% of annual turnover, and the CNB may revoke your registration. It is essential to maintain strong AML/KYC procedures and submit annual reports. Consulting24 offers compliance support to help you stay compliant.

How does the Czech license compare to Panama's crypto license?

Panama's license costs a flat EUR 6,000 with no minimum capital and 0% tax on foreign-source income. The Czech license costs EUR 12,000-30,000, has higher ongoing costs, and 19% tax. However, the Czech license offers EU recognition. Panama is better for offshore operations, while the Czech Republic is suitable for EU market access.
