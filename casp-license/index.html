What Is a CASP License Crypto License?

A CASP license is the regulatory authorisation required under the EU's MiCA framework to offer crypto asset services. It covers activities such as operating a trading platform, providing custody, executing orders, and more. Unlike the previous national regimes, MiCA creates a single rulebook, meaning a license obtained in one EU member state is valid across all others via passporting.

The European Securities and Markets Authority (ESMA) coordinates supervision, but national competent authorities (NCAs) issue licenses. Key requirements include minimum capital, a registered office in the EU, fit-and-proper management, and strong AML/CFT controls. The license is technology-neutral, applying to both centralised and decentralised services where there is a central entity.

Who Needs a CASP License?

Any legal person providing crypto asset services to EU residents on a professional basis must hold a CASP license. This includes:

  • Exchanges (order-book or DEX operators with a central entity)
  • Custodial wallet providers
  • Brokers and dealers in crypto assets
  • Platforms offering staking, lending, or borrowing
  • Advisory services on crypto assets
  • Portfolio managers dealing with crypto assets

If you are a non-EU firm targeting EU clients, you must establish an EU entity and obtain a CASP license. DeFi projects that are sufficiently decentralised may fall outside scope, but most commercial services are captured. Even NFT marketplaces may be in scope if the NFTs qualify as crypto assets under MiCA.

License Type & Regulator

The CASP license is a single authorisation under MiCA. The regulator is the national competent authority (NCA) of the EU member state where you establish your registered office. For example, in Lithuania it is the Bank of Lithuania; in Malta, the Malta Financial Services Authority (MFSA); in France, the Autorité des Marchés Financiers (AMF). ESMA provides oversight and coordination to ensure consistent application across the EU.

There are no sub-types, but the license scope depends on the services you intend to offer. Each service class carries its own capital requirement (see below). Capital Requirement

MiCA sets minimum capital based on the type of services provided:

  • EUR 50,000 for certain services (e.g., reception and transmission of orders, advice)
  • EUR 125,000 for custody and administration of crypto assets
  • EUR 150,000 for operating a trading platform or exchange

If you offer multiple services, the highest applicable tier applies. Capital must be held as liquid assets (cash or equivalents) and cannot be used for operational expenses. It serves as a buffer for client protection. Some NCAs may require additional capital based on the volume of transactions or risk profile. Tax Treatment

Tax treatment of crypto assets under MiCA is not harmonised. Each member state applies its own corporate income tax, VAT, and capital gains rules. Typical corporate tax rates range from 12% (Lithuania) to 25% (Germany). Some countries offer exemptions for long-term holdings or specific activities. For example, Malta has a flat 5% effective tax rate on distributed profits for certain structures. VAT treatment also varies: crypto-to-fiat exchanges are generally VAT-exempt, but advisory services may be taxable.

Allowed Activities Under a CASP License

A CASP license permits one or more of the following services as defined in MiCA:

  • Operating a trading platform for crypto assets
  • Exchanging crypto assets for fiat currency
  • Exchanging crypto assets for other crypto assets
  • Custody and administration of crypto assets on behalf of clients
  • Placing of crypto assets
  • Reception and transmission of orders for crypto assets
  • Execution of orders for crypto assets on behalf of clients
  • Providing advice on crypto assets
  • Portfolio management of crypto assets

Staking and lending may also be covered if they fall under custody or execution. The license scope is defined in your application. Common Mistakes When Applying

  • Incomplete documentation - Missing policies or unclear business plans delay approval. For example, failing to detail your AML risk assessment is a common pitfall.
  • Underestimating capital - Not having sufficient liquid capital at application. Some firms apply with the minimum but later need more for operational expenses.
  • Choosing the wrong jurisdiction - Some NCAs are faster or more crypto-friendly. For instance, Lithuania is known for efficiency, while others may have backlogs.
  • Ignoring passporting rules - Not understanding that the license covers all EU, not just the issuing country. You must still comply with local marketing rules.
  • Poor fit-and-proper checks - Management with adverse backgrounds may be rejected. Disclose all relevant information upfront.
  • Delaying bank account setup - Banks take time; start the process early to avoid delays in capital deposit.

Alternatives and Comparisons

While the CASP license is the only EU-wide option, some firms consider non-EU jurisdictions. Panama offers a flat-fee license at EUR 6,000 with no minimum capital, but it does not provide EU passporting. It is suitable for firms targeting Latin America or global markets without EU focus. Other alternatives include licensing in Switzerland (FINMA) or the UK (FCA), each with different capital and scope. Switzerland requires a minimum of CHF 100,000 for certain activities, while the UK's FCA regime has no fixed minimum but expects substantial resources.

Frequently asked questions

What is a CASP license?

A CASP (Crypto Asset Service Provider) license is an authorisation under the EU's MiCA regulation. It allows firms to offer crypto services across all EU member states with a single license. The license covers activities like exchange, custody, and advisory services.

Who needs a CASP license?

Any company providing crypto asset services to EU residents on a professional basis, including exchanges, wallet providers, brokers, and advisors. Non-EU firms targeting EU clients must establish an EU entity and obtain a CASP license.

What is the minimum capital for a CASP license?

Capital requirements range from EUR 50,000 to EUR 150,000 depending on the services offered. For example, operating a trading platform requires EUR 150,000. The capital must be held in liquid assets and cannot be used for operations.

How long does it take to get a CASP license?

The process typically takes 6 to 12 months from application to authorisation, depending on the jurisdiction and completeness of documentation. Pre-application preparation can add 1-3 months.

Can a non-EU company get a CASP license?

Yes, but you must establish a legal entity with a registered office in an EU member state. The license is issued to that entity. The parent company must also meet fit-and-proper requirements.

What activities are allowed under a CASP license?

Allowed activities include operating a trading platform, custody, exchange (fiat and crypto), order execution, advice, and portfolio management. Staking and lending may also be covered if they fall under custody or execution.

Is a CASP license valid in all EU countries?

Yes, under MiCA's passporting regime, a CASP license obtained in one EU member state allows you to provide services in all 27 member states without additional authorisation. You must still comply with local marketing rules.

What are the ongoing compliance requirements?

Ongoing requirements include AML/CFT compliance, transaction monitoring, regular audits, capital maintenance, and reporting to the national regulator. You must appoint a compliance officer and MLRO.

How much does a CASP license cost?

Total costs vary by jurisdiction but typically range from EUR 25,000 to EUR 90,000 in professional fees plus the capital deposit. Exact pricing is confirmed in a consultation. Ongoing annual compliance costs EUR 10,000-30,000.

Can I use a CASP license for DeFi?

Fully decentralised protocols may fall outside MiCA scope. However, if you have a centralised entity or provide services, a CASP license is likely required. The EU is still clarifying rules for DeFi.

What happens if I don't get a CASP license?

Providing crypto services without a license in the EU is illegal and can result in fines, forced cessation of operations, and criminal liability. MiCA enforcement is strict as of 2026.
