What is the Jersey Crypto Broker License?

The Jersey crypto broker license is a regulatory authorization issued by the JFSC to firms that want to operate as a broker in crypto assets. It falls under the existing financial services framework, requiring compliance with anti-money laundering (AML) and counter-terrorist financing (CTF) obligations. In 2026, the license is essential for businesses that facilitate the buying, selling, or exchange of crypto assets for clients.

Jersey's approach is principles-based, focusing on the substance and control of the business. The license is not a standalone 'crypto license' but rather an extension of the regulated financial services regime. This means brokers must meet the same standards as traditional financial institutions. For example, a broker must have a physical office in Jersey, employ local directors with relevant experience, and maintain strong AML/CTF procedures. The JFSC expects the business to be managed and controlled from Jersey, not just a mailbox presence.

Compared to other jurisdictions, Jersey offers a stable, low-tax environment but with higher substance requirements. For a lighter regulatory touch, consider Panama where company setup is EUR 6,000 flat with no specific crypto license. However, Jersey's regulatory clarity can be a strong trust signal for institutional clients.

Who Needs This License?

Any entity that provides crypto brokerage services in or from Jersey needs this license. This includes:

  • Firms that execute orders to buy or sell crypto assets on behalf of clients.
  • Platforms that match buyers and sellers of crypto assets (order-book exchanges).
  • Businesses that provide crypto-to-fiat exchange services.
  • Firms that offer custody of crypto assets as part of brokerage.
  • Advisory services on crypto investments if they involve execution.

If you are a crypto broker targeting the Jersey market or planning to establish a presence in Jersey, this license is mandatory. Even if your clients are outside Jersey, if the business is managed from Jersey, the license applies. Consulting24 advises on whether your business model requires this license. For example, a broker that only refers clients to an offshore platform may not need a license, but direct execution does.

License Type and Regulator

The regulator is the Jersey Financial Services Commission (JFSC). The license type is typically a 'Registered Person' under the Proceeds of Crime (Supervisory Bodies) (Jersey) Law, or a 'Financial Services License' under the Financial Services (Jersey) Law, depending on the specific activities. For crypto brokerage, the JFSC generally requires a financial services license.

The JFSC is known for its rigorous but fair approach. It expects firms to have a physical presence in Jersey, including local directors and staff. The regulatory framework is aligned with international standards, including FATF recommendations. The JFSC conducts thorough background checks on directors and beneficial owners. It also reviews the business plan, risk management, and compliance policies in detail.

Jersey is not part of the European Union, so MiCA does not directly apply. However, the JFSC follows similar principles. For EU passporting, consider Lithuania or Estonia under MiCA. See our Lithuania crypto license page for details.

Capital Requirement

The JFSC does not prescribe a fixed minimum capital for crypto brokers. Instead, it requires firms to maintain adequate financial resources based on the nature and scale of their business. As a guide, expect a minimum of EUR 50,000 to EUR 200,000 in capital, but exact figures depend on your business plan and risk profile. Consulting24 can help you determine the appropriate capital level.

Capital must be held in liquid assets and be readily available. The JFSC may require a higher amount if the broker holds client funds or provides custody. For example, a broker holding EUR 1 million in client crypto assets may need EUR 100,000 capital. This is in contrast to Lithuania under MiCA, which has fixed tiers: EUR 50,000 for simple brokerage, EUR 125,000 for custody, and EUR 150,000 for exchange. See our requirements page for more.

Tax Treatment

Jersey offers a favorable tax regime. The standard corporate income tax rate is 0% for most companies, including crypto brokers. However, certain activities (e.g., regulated financial services) may be subject to a 10% rate. There is no capital gains tax, no VAT, and no withholding tax on dividends. This makes Jersey attractive for crypto businesses. Consulting24 advises on tax structuring to optimize your position.

For example, a broker earning EUR 500,000 in profit would pay 0% tax if not subject to the 10% rate. However, the JFSC may require the 10% rate for certain regulated activities. It is essential to get a tax ruling from the Jersey tax authority. Compare this to Panama which has territorial taxation (only income sourced in Panama is taxed) and no corporate income tax on foreign-source income.

Allowed Activities

A Jersey crypto broker license typically permits:

  • Brokering crypto assets (executing client orders).
  • Exchanging crypto for fiat and vice versa.
  • Providing custody services for client crypto assets.
  • Operating a crypto trading platform (matching orders).
  • Offering advisory services on crypto investments.

Activities not covered include issuing crypto assets (unless separately licensed) and operating a crypto exchange that uses proprietary capital (may require additional authorization). If you plan to issue tokens, you may need a separate license under the Financial Services (Jersey) Law. Consulting24 can advise on the scope of your license.

Compliance and Trust

Compliance with JFSC regulations is ongoing. Brokers must submit annual returns, maintain AML/CTF procedures, and undergo regular audits. The JFSC conducts periodic inspections. Non-compliance can result in fines or license revocation. Consulting24 provides ongoing compliance support to ensure your firm meets all obligations. This is general guidance, not legal advice.

Specific obligations include: appointing a Money Laundering Reporting Officer (MLRO), conducting customer due diligence (CDD) on all clients, screening for sanctions, and reporting suspicious transactions. The JFSC also requires annual AML/CTF training for staff. Failure to comply can lead to penalties of up to EUR 1 million or imprisonment for individuals. Common Mistakes

Common pitfalls include:

  • Underestimating the substance requirements: Jersey expects a real physical presence with local directors and staff. A virtual office is not sufficient.
  • Insufficient AML/CTF policies: The JFSC scrutinizes these closely. Generic policies will be rejected.
  • Unrealistic business plans: Projections must be credible. Overly optimistic revenue forecasts raise red flags.
  • Delaying bank account opening: Start early to avoid delays. Some banks take 3-6 months to open accounts.
  • Ignoring ongoing compliance: Post-license, you must file annual returns and maintain records. Many firms fail to do this.
