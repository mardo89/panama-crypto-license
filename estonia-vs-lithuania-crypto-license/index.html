What is an Estonia vs Lithuania Crypto License?

An Estonia vs Lithuania crypto license refers to the regulatory authorization required to operate a virtual asset service provider (VASP) in these EU member states. Under MiCA, which is fully in force in 2026, both countries have updated their regimes to align with EU standards. In Estonia, the license is issued by the Financial Intelligence Unit (FIU) and is required for activities such as exchanging fiat for crypto, crypto-to-crypto exchange, and wallet services. Lithuania's license is granted by the Bank of Lithuania and covers similar activities but with different capital thresholds and compliance obligations.

Both licenses allow you to serve clients across the EU under the passporting rights of MiCA, but the application process, ongoing costs, and regulatory scrutiny vary. Estonia has a reputation for rigorous due diligence, while Lithuania is known for a more business-friendly approach. Understanding these differences is key to choosing the right path for your crypto venture. License Type and Regulator

Estonia: The regulator is the Financial Intelligence Unit (FIU). The license is a 'Virtual Asset Service Provider (VASP) license'. Estonia does not issue separate licenses for different activities; one license covers all VASP services. However, the FIU imposes strict AML/KYC requirements and expects a substantial local presence, including a physical office or registered address and a local compliance officer.

Lithuania: The regulator is the Bank of Lithuania. The license is also a VASP license under MiCA, but Lithuania offers a more modular approach. You can apply for different classes of services, each with its own capital requirement. The Bank of Lithuania is known for its efficiency and clear guidelines, often providing pre-application feedback. Both regulators are part of the EU's single market, so licenses are recognized across member states. However, the application process and ongoing supervision differ in intensity. Capital Requirement

Under MiCA, capital requirements are tiered based on the type of services offered. For Estonia, the minimum capital is EUR 100,000 for all VASP activities. This is a fixed requirement regardless of service class. Lithuania, however, follows the MiCA tiers: EUR 50,000 for certain services (e.g., wallet provision), EUR 125,000 for exchange services, and EUR 150,000 for custodial services. This modular approach allows businesses to start with lower capital if they offer limited services.

Capital must be held in a bank account and cannot be used for operational expenses until the license is granted. Consulting24 advises on structuring your capital to meet regulatory expectations. For example, a startup focusing only on wallet services in Lithuania could begin with EUR 50,000, while the same activity in Estonia would require EUR 100,000. Tax Treatment

Tax treatment is a key differentiator in the Estonia vs Lithuania crypto license comparison. Estonia has a unique corporate tax system: profits are not taxed until distributed (i.e., dividends). This can be advantageous for reinvesting profits. However, crypto transactions may be subject to VAT or capital gains tax depending on the nature of the activity. Estonia does not charge VAT on crypto-to-crypto exchanges, but fiat-to-crypto may be subject to VAT in some cases.

Lithuania applies a standard corporate income tax of 15% (or 5% for small businesses). Crypto transactions are generally treated as financial transactions and are exempt from VAT. Capital gains from crypto trading are taxed as income. Lithuania also offers tax incentives for R&D and innovation. Consulting24 recommends consulting with a tax advisor to optimize your tax structure. For a jurisdiction with no corporate tax on crypto, explore Panama as an alternative.

Allowed Activities

Both Estonia and Lithuania allow a broad range of crypto activities under their licenses. These include:

  • Exchange of fiat currency for virtual currencies
  • Exchange of virtual currencies for fiat currency
  • Exchange of virtual currencies for other virtual currencies
  • Transfer of virtual currencies
  • Custodial wallet services
  • Participation in and provision of financial services related to an issuer's offer and sale of virtual currencies

Estonia's license covers all these activities under one authorization. Lithuania allows you to apply for specific service classes, which can reduce capital requirements if you only offer limited services. Both jurisdictions prohibit unlicensed activities such as operating a crypto ATM without a license. Consulting24 can help you define your service scope to minimize costs and compliance burdens.

Step-by-Step Application Process

The application process for an Estonia vs Lithuania crypto license involves several steps. Consulting24 advises and coordinates each stage.

  1. Pre-application preparation: Prepare your business plan, AML/KYC policies, risk assessment, and internal controls. Estonia requires more detailed documentation on beneficial ownership and source of funds.
  2. Company incorporation: You must have a registered company in the jurisdiction. Estonia offers e-Residency for easy online incorporation. Lithuania requires a physical office or registered address.
  3. Submit application: File the application with the respective regulator. Estonia's FIU requires a detailed questionnaire and supporting documents. Lithuania's Bank of Lithuania uses an online portal.
  4. Regulatory review: The regulator assesses your application, conducts background checks on directors and shareholders, and may request additional information. Estonia's review is more thorough and can take up to 6 months.
  5. Approval and licensing: Once approved, you receive your license. You must then maintain ongoing compliance, including regular reporting and audits.

Lithuania has a more open banking environment. The Bank of Lithuania encourages fintech and crypto businesses, and several banks and payment service providers cater to VASPs. Frequently asked questions

What is the main difference between Estonia and Lithuania crypto licenses?

The main difference lies in capital requirements, timeline, and regulatory approach. Estonia requires EUR 100,000 minimum capital for all services, while Lithuania uses tiered capital from EUR 50,000 to EUR 150,000. Lithuania's process is faster (2-4 months) compared to Estonia (3-6 months). Estonia's FIU is stricter, whereas Lithuania's Bank of Lithuania is more business-friendly.

Can I passport my license to other EU countries?

Yes, both Estonia and Lithuania licenses are under MiCA, allowing passporting across all EU member states. You can serve clients in any EU country without additional licensing, subject to notification requirements. This is a key advantage for businesses targeting the European market.

What are the ongoing compliance costs for each license?

Annual compliance costs in Estonia range from EUR 5,000 to EUR 10,000, covering audits, AML reporting, and legal updates. In Lithuania, costs are lower at EUR 3,000 to EUR 7,000. These estimates assume a standard VASP operation; complex activities may increase costs. Consulting24 provides detailed budgeting during consultation.

Is it possible to switch from an Estonia license to a Lithuania license later?

Yes, but it requires a new application in Lithuania and surrendering the Estonia license. You cannot simply transfer licenses. The process involves re-submitting documentation and meeting Lithuania's capital requirements. Consulting24 can assist with the transition to minimize downtime.

Do I need a physical office in Estonia or Lithuania?

Estonia requires a registered address and a local compliance officer, but a physical office is not mandatory. Lithuania may require a physical office depending on the regulator's assessment. Both jurisdictions expect a local presence for regulatory communication. Consulting24 can help arrange virtual office solutions.

How long does the application process take in each country?

Estonia typically takes 3-6 months due to thorough background checks and document review. Lithuania is faster at 2-4 months, thanks to a more streamlined online process. Delays can occur if documentation is incomplete. Consulting24's preparation helps expedite the process.

What happens if I fail to maintain compliance?

Non-compliance can lead to fines, suspension, or revocation of the license. Both regulators conduct periodic audits and inspections. Estonia's FIU is particularly strict. Consulting24 advises on ongoing compliance to avoid penalties. This is general guidance, not legal advice.

Can I apply for a license if my company is not based in Estonia or Lithuania?

Yes, you can incorporate a subsidiary in Estonia or Lithuania to apply. Estonia's e-Residency makes incorporation easy for non-residents. Lithuania requires a local director or representative. Consulting24 can assist with company setup in both jurisdictions.

Estonia taxes profits only when distributed, beneficial for reinvestment. Lithuania applies a 15% corporate tax (5% for small businesses) and exempts crypto-to-crypto exchanges from VAT.
