What Is the Anjouan Crypto Gambling License?

The Anjouan crypto gambling license is a regulatory authorization issued by the AFSC that permits operators to offer online gambling services using cryptocurrencies. It covers a wide range of activities, including casino games, sports betting, poker, and lottery, all transacted in digital assets like Bitcoin, Ethereum, and stablecoins.

Anjouan's framework is designed to be accessible: no requirement for a physical presence on the island, a simple corporate structure (typically an International Business Company or IBC), and a tax-efficient regime. The license is recognized internationally, though operators must ensure compliance with the laws of the markets they serve. License Type and Regulator

The regulator is the Anjouan Financial Services Commission (AFSC), which oversees all financial services, including gambling. The license is issued as a Class 1 (casino and games) or Class 2 (sports betting and lottery) license, or a combined license covering both. There is no separate 'crypto-specific' license; the standard gambling license permits crypto transactions. The AFSC also offers a Class 3 license for software providers and aggregators, though most operators opt for Class 1 or 2.

The AFSC requires the operator to be incorporated as an IBC in Anjouan or another approved jurisdiction. The license is valid for one year and must be renewed annually. The AFSC conducts background checks on directors and shareholders but does not impose a mandatory physical office or local staff requirement. The regulator expects the operator to have a registered agent in Anjouan, which Consulting24 can arrange. The AFSC publishes a list of licensed operators on its website, adding a layer of transparency for players and partners.

Capital Requirement

Anjouan does not prescribe a fixed minimum capital requirement for gambling licensees. However, the AFSC expects the company to demonstrate sufficient financial resources to operate responsibly. In practice, a minimum paid-up capital of EUR 50,000 to EUR 100,000 is advisable to show solvency. This capital can be held in cryptocurrency or fiat, and there is no requirement to maintain it in a local bank account. The AFSC may ask for a bank reference letter or proof of funds as part of the due diligence process.

For comparison, EU MiCA-compliant jurisdictions require EUR 50,000 to 150,000 depending on service class. Anjouan's flexibility makes it attractive for smaller operators. For example, a startup with EUR 60,000 in capital can meet the AFSC's expectations, whereas the same business would need at least EUR 125,000 under MiCA for a CASP license covering custody and exchange services. Tax Treatment

One of the biggest advantages of Anjouan is its tax regime. There is no corporate income tax, no capital gains tax, and no withholding tax on dividends or interest for IBCs. Gambling revenues are also tax-exempt at the local level. However, operators must pay a flat annual license renewal fee (typically EUR 10,000 - 20,000) and a small turnover-based levy (around 0.5% of gross gaming revenue, capped at EUR 50,000 per year). This levy is calculated on the operator's worldwide revenue, but the cap ensures predictability for high-volume operators.

This tax structure allows operators to retain a higher proportion of profits compared to jurisdictions like Malta or the UK. For instance, a crypto casino generating EUR 5 million in annual gross gaming revenue would pay approximately EUR 25,000 in levy plus the renewal fee, totaling around EUR 35,000-45,000 in local taxes. In Malta, the same operator would pay an effective corporate tax of 5% (EUR 250,000) plus gaming taxes. Step-by-Step Application Process

The process to obtain an Anjouan crypto gambling license involves the following steps:

  1. Company incorporation: Set up an IBC in Anjouan or another approved jurisdiction. Consulting24 handles the registration, including drafting the memorandum and articles of association.
  2. Document preparation: Gather certified copies of passports, proof of address (utility bill or bank statement), bank references, and a detailed business plan. The business plan must include financial projections, target markets, and a description of the gaming platform.
  3. Due diligence: Submit background checks for all directors and shareholders (no criminal record required, but full disclosure). The AFSC may request police certificates from the applicant's country of residence.
  4. Application submission: File the application with the AFSC, including the business plan, compliance policies (AML/KYC), responsible gambling policy, and technical description of the platform. The AML policy must cover customer due diligence, transaction monitoring, and suspicious activity reporting.
  5. Review and approval: The AFSC reviews the application and may request additional information. This typically takes 4-6 weeks. The regulator may also require an interview with the beneficial owner.
  6. License issuance: Once approved, pay the license fee and receive the certificate. The license is valid for one year from the date of issuance.
  7. Post-licensing: Set up banking or payment processing, implement geo-blocking, integrate AML/KYC software, and launch operations. Consulting24 can assist with selecting payment providers and compliance tools.

Common Mistakes When Applying

Avoid these pitfalls:

  • Incomplete documentation: Missing certified copies or translations can delay the process. Ensure all documents are translated into English by a certified translator.
  • Unrealistic business plans: The AFSC expects a credible plan with financial projections. Avoid overly optimistic revenue forecasts without supporting assumptions.
  • Ignoring target market laws: Operating in restricted jurisdictions can lead to legal issues and payment processor termination. Research the gambling laws of each country you plan to target.
  • Choosing the wrong license class: Ensure you apply for the correct class (Class 1 for casino, Class 2 for sports betting, or combined). Applying for the wrong class may require a new application.
  • Underestimating ongoing costs: Annual renewal fees, compliance costs, and payment processor fees add up. Budget for at least EUR 15,000-25,000 per year in recurring costs.
  • Neglecting AML/KYC policies: The AFSC requires strong AML policies. Failing to implement them can lead to license revocation.
