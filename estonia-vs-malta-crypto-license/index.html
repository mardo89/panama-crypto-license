Overview: What Is an Estonia vs Malta Crypto License?

An Estonia vs Malta crypto license comparison centres on two distinct regulatory philosophies. Estonia was an early mover in crypto licensing, introducing a virtual currency service provider regime in 2017. As of 2026, Estonia's framework is a full license under the Financial Intelligence Unit (FIU), requiring compliance with AML/CFT rules and MiCA-aligned capital tiers. Malta, often called 'Blockchain Island', introduced the Virtual Financial Assets (VFA) framework in 2018, regulated by the Malta Financial Services Authority (MFSA). Both are EU member states, so MiCA applies, but Malta's regime is more comprehensive and costly.

Both licenses allow companies to offer crypto exchange, wallet, and transfer services. However, Malta's VFA license is a full MiFID-style passportable license, while Estonia's is a lighter but still strong registration. The choice depends on your target market, budget, and long-term goals. For example, a startup focusing on Nordic markets may find Estonia sufficient, while a firm planning to offer token issuance or investment services across the EU would benefit from Malta's broader scope. Who Needs an Estonia or Malta Crypto License?

Any business that wants to legally offer crypto services in the EU needs a license. This includes:

  • Cryptocurrency exchanges (centralized and decentralized)
  • Wallet providers (custodial and non-custodial)
  • OTC desks
  • Payment processors handling crypto
  • Token issuers (if tokens qualify as VFAs in Malta)

If your target market is primarily Baltic or Nordic, Estonia may be sufficient. If you plan to passport services across the EU and need a higher credibility stamp, Malta is often preferred. For instance, a crypto payment gateway serving merchants in multiple EU countries would likely benefit from Malta's passporting rights, while a local exchange in Tallinn could operate effectively under an Estonian license.

License Type and Regulator

In Estonia, the regulator is the Financial Intelligence Unit (FIU). The license is called a 'Virtual Currency Service Provider License' (VCSP). As of 2026, it is a full license under MiCA, not a simple registration. In Malta, the regulator is the Malta Financial Services Authority (MFSA). The license is a 'Virtual Financial Assets (VFA) License' under the VFA Act. Malta also offers a 'Class 3' license for more complex activities.

Both are EU-compliant, but Malta's regime is more rigorous, requiring a detailed business plan, IT security audit, and fit-and-proper tests for all shareholders and directors. Estonia's process is less intensive but still demands strong AML policies. The FIU in Estonia reviews applications within 30 days initially, but the full process can take 3-6 months. Malta's MFSA conducts a thorough assessment that often includes an in-person interview with key personnel.

Capital Requirement

Under MiCA, capital requirements are tiered by service class. As of 2026:

  • Estonia: EUR 50,000 for basic exchange/wallet services; EUR 125,000 for more complex services; EUR 150,000 for full CASP.
  • Malta: Same MiCA tiers apply, but Malta may impose additional own funds requirements based on operational risk. Typically, EUR 125,000 minimum for VFA license.

Both jurisdictions require the capital to be held as liquid assets (cash or equivalents) and maintained throughout the license period. For example, an Estonian exchange offering only fiat-to-crypto trades would need EUR 50,000, while a Maltese exchange offering margin trading might need EUR 125,000 or more. Tax Treatment

Tax is a key differentiator. Estonia has a unique corporate income tax system: 0% on retained and reinvested profits. Only distributed profits (dividends) are taxed at 20% (effective 20/80 = 25% on gross distribution). This is advantageous for crypto startups that reinvest earnings. Malta has a full imputation system: corporate tax rate 35%, but shareholders can claim refunds, reducing effective rate to 5-10% for non-residents. Both are attractive, but Estonia's deferred tax is simpler for growth-stage companies.

VAT on crypto services: In both jurisdictions, crypto-to-fiat exchanges are VAT-exempt under EU law. Crypto-to-crypto trades may be subject to VAT in some cases; professional advice is recommended. For instance, a company earning revenue from transaction fees may benefit from Estonia's tax deferral, while a firm distributing profits to international investors might prefer Malta's refund mechanism.

Allowed Activities

Both licenses cover:

  • Exchange of virtual currencies against fiat
  • Exchange of virtual currencies against other virtual currencies
  • Transfer of virtual currencies
  • Custodial wallet services

Malta's VFA license also allows:

  • Investment services in VFAs (e.g., portfolio management)
  • Operation of a VFA exchange (order-matching platform)
  • Initial VFA offerings (IVFAOs) - token issuance

Estonia's license is more limited and does not cover token issuance or investment services. For a broader scope, Malta is better. For simple exchange/wallet, Estonia suffices. For example, a startup planning to launch a security token offering must choose Malta, while a simple P2P exchange can operate under Estonia's license.

Step-by-Step Application Process

The process for both jurisdictions involves several stages. Consulting24 advises and coordinates each step.

  1. Pre-application: Business plan, AML/KYC policies, risk assessment, IT security documentation. For Malta, a detailed business plan covering 3 years is required.
  2. Company incorporation: Register a legal entity in the chosen jurisdiction. For Estonia, e-residency simplifies this. In Malta, a local registered office is mandatory.
  3. Capital deposit: Minimum capital must be deposited in a bank account and verified. Estonia requires a bank confirmation letter; Malta may ask for a notarized statement.
  4. Submission: Submit application to FIU (Estonia) or MFSA (Malta) with all supporting documents. Malta uses an online portal; Estonia accepts email submissions.
  5. Review and interview: Regulator reviews; may request additional information or interview directors. In Malta, interviews are common and can last 1-2 hours.
  6. Approval: License granted; ongoing compliance obligations begin. Both issue a license certificate and publish the entity on their register.

Timeline varies: Estonia 3-6 months, Malta 6-12 months. Delays often occur due to incomplete documentation or banking issues.

Banking and Payment Processing

Banking remains a challenge for crypto companies. Estonia has a more open banking environment for crypto, with several banks (e.g., LHV, SEB) accepting crypto clients after due diligence. Malta's banks are more conservative; many crypto firms use payment processors or e-money institutions. Both jurisdictions require a local bank account for capital deposit and operational transactions. Consulting24 can introduce you to compliant banking partners.

For payment processing, both support SEPA transfers and major card networks. Crypto-to-fiat on-ramps are available through third-party providers like MoonPay or Simplex. In Estonia, you can also use the e-residency digital ID to open accounts remotely. In Malta, physical presence may be required for bank account opening. We recommend starting the banking process early, as it can take 1-3 months.

Benefits of Each Jurisdiction

Estonia:

  • Fast and cost-effective licensing (3-6 months, EUR 13k-25k)
  • E-residency for remote company management
  • 0% tax on retained profits
  • Digital-first government with efficient online services

Malta:

  • Full VFA license with passporting rights across EU
  • Broader allowed activities (token issuance, investment services)
  • Strong regulatory reputation enhancing investor confidence
  • Effective tax rate as low as 5% for non-residents

Both offer EU market access. Choose Estonia for speed and cost; Malta for scope and prestige. For example, a DeFi startup might prefer Malta's comprehensive framework, while a simple exchange could thrive under Estonia's lighter regime.

Compliance and Ongoing Obligations

Both jurisdictions require ongoing compliance under MiCA and AML/CFT rules. This includes:

  • Annual AML audit by an external auditor
  • Suspicious transaction reporting (STR) to the local FIU
  • Transaction monitoring for unusual patterns
  • Travel rule compliance for transfers over EUR 1,000
  • Periodic reporting to regulator (quarterly or annually)

Non-compliance can result in fines, suspension, or revocation of license. Consulting24 provides ongoing compliance support. Note: This is general guidance, not legal advice. Always consult a qualified lawyer. In practice, many firms hire a local compliance officer (EUR 2,000-5,000/month) to manage these obligations. For Malta, the compliance officer must be a resident and approved by the MFSA.

Common Mistakes When Choosing Between Estonia and Malta

Founders often make these errors:

  • Underestimating timeline: Malta takes longer; plan accordingly. Some founders assume 6 months but face 12-month delays.
  • Ignoring tax differences: Estonia's 0% on retained profits is great for reinvestment; Malta's refund system benefits profit distribution. Mismatching your business model to tax regime can be costly.
  • Choosing based on cost alone: A cheap license may not allow desired activities. For example, a token issuer cannot use Estonia.
  • Neglecting banking: Ensure you can open a bank account before applying. Many applications fail because the company cannot secure a bank account.
  • Not considering MiCA alignment: Both are MiCA-compliant, but Malta's regime is more mature. Some firms find Malta's requirements more burdensome.

Frequently asked questions

What is the main difference between Estonia and Malta crypto licenses?

Estonia offers a faster, cheaper license (3-6 months, EUR 13k-25k) but with limited activities (exchange/wallet only). Malta provides a more comprehensive VFA license (6-12 months, EUR 35k-60k) allowing token issuance and investment services, with full EU passporting.

Which license is better for a startup?

For a startup with limited budget, Estonia is better due to lower costs and faster timeline. However, if you plan to offer token issuance or need EU-wide passporting, Malta's broader scope may be worth the extra investment.

Do I need to be physically present in Estonia or Malta to apply?

For Estonia, e-residency allows remote application and management. For Malta, you need a local registered office and often a physical presence for bank account opening and MFSA interviews.

What are the ongoing compliance costs?

Annual AML audits, compliance officer salaries, and reporting software. In Estonia, expect EUR 5,000-15,000 per year; in Malta, EUR 10,000-30,000 per year. Consulting24 can provide detailed estimates.

Can I passport my license to other EU countries?

Yes, both licenses allow passporting under MiCA. However, Malta's VFA license is more widely recognized and easier to passport due to its comprehensive nature.

What happens if I don't meet the capital requirement?

The regulator will reject your application or revoke the license. Capital must be maintained at all times. Consulting24 can help you structure your capital deposit correctly.

Is it possible to switch from Estonia to Malta later?

Yes, but you would need to apply for a new license in Malta and close the Estonian one. This is costly and time-consuming, so it's better to choose the right jurisdiction from the start.

How long does the application process take in practice?

Estonia typically 3-6 months, Malta 6-12 months. Delays often occur due to incomplete documentation, banking issues, or regulator backlogs. Consulting24 helps streamline the process.

What are the tax advantages of each jurisdiction?

Estonia taxes only distributed profits (20% effective rate), ideal for reinvesting companies. Malta has a 35% corporate rate but refunds reduce effective tax to 5-10% for non-residents.

Can I offer crypto derivatives under these licenses?

No, crypto derivatives are typically regulated under MiFID II, not the crypto license. You would need a separate investment firm license for derivatives.
