Cyprus vs Singapore Crypto License: Overview

Both Cyprus and Singapore are established financial hubs that have embraced crypto regulation. Cyprus, as an EU member state, aligns with MiCA, which categorises crypto-asset service providers (CASPs) and sets capital requirements based on service type. Singapore's MAS regulates crypto under the PSA, requiring a Major Payment Institution (MPI) license for certain activities. The choice depends on your target market, budget, and operational needs.

Key differences include capital requirements, tax rates, and regulatory timelines. Cyprus offers a lower initial cost but higher operational complexity due to MiCA's harmonised rules. Singapore demands higher capital but provides a clear, stable regime with a 17% corporate tax rate and no capital gains tax. Consulting24 helps you weigh these factors.

For example, a startup focusing on EU retail investors might prefer Cyprus for its passporting rights, while an institutional exchange targeting Asian clients would lean toward Singapore. Both jurisdictions require strong AML/CFT frameworks, but the specific compliance obligations differ. Cyprus mandates a local compliance officer, while Singapore requires at least one executive director to be a resident.

Who Needs a Cyprus or Singapore Crypto License?

Founders involved in the following activities typically require a license in either jurisdiction:

  • Operating a cryptocurrency exchange (centralised or decentralised)
  • Providing custody or wallet services
  • Offering crypto-to-fiat conversion or payment services
  • Running an automated market maker (AMM) or DeFi platform
  • Issuing stablecoins or utility tokens

If your target market is the EU, Cyprus offers passporting rights. For Asia-Pacific, Singapore is ideal. Consulting24 can help you determine which license fits your business model.

Additionally, businesses that engage in crypto lending or staking may fall under specific regulatory scopes. In Cyprus, staking services are permitted but require clear disclosure of risks. In Singapore, DeFi activities are not explicitly regulated under the PSA, but MAS may consider them if they involve payment services. License Type & Regulator

Cyprus: The Cyprus Securities and Exchange Commission (CySEC) is the regulator. Under MiCA, crypto-asset service providers must register as CASPs. The license covers multiple service classes, each with its own capital requirement.

Singapore: The Monetary Authority of Singapore (MAS) issues a Major Payment Institution (MPI) license under the Payment Services Act (PSA). This license covers digital payment token (DPT) services, including exchange, transfer, and custody.

Both regulators are respected and enforce strict AML/CFT compliance. CySEC is known for its proactive approach, while MAS is considered one of the most stringent regulators globally. Consulting24 has experience working with both authorities and can guide you through their expectations.

In Cyprus, the CASP license is a single license covering all service classes, but you must specify which classes you intend to offer. In Singapore, the MPI license is a single license for payment services, but you must apply for specific payment service types.

Capital Requirement

Cyprus (MiCA): Capital requirements vary by service class: EUR 50,000 for custody and transfer services, EUR 125,000 for exchange services, and EUR 150,000 for trading platforms. These are minimums and must be maintained as own funds.

Singapore (PSA): The MPI license requires a minimum base capital of SGD 250,000 (approx. EUR 170,000). Additionally, MAS may impose higher capital based on transaction volume. There is no separate capital for different activities.

Both jurisdictions require the capital to be held in liquid assets and available for regulatory purposes. In Cyprus, the capital must be held in a bank account in Cyprus or another EU member state. In Singapore, it can be held in a Singapore bank account or in certain approved instruments.

For example, a Cyprus exchange offering custody and exchange services would need at least EUR 125,000, while a Singapore exchange offering similar services would need SGD 250,000. Consulting24 can help you structure your capital to meet these requirements efficiently.

Tax Treatment

Cyprus: Corporate tax rate is 12.5% on net profits. There is no specific crypto tax law, but profits from crypto trading are generally taxable. VAT may apply on certain services. Cyprus has a wide double tax treaty network.

Singapore: Corporate tax rate is 17%, with a partial tax exemption for new companies (first SGD 100,000 exempt). No capital gains tax. Crypto transactions are treated as goods or services for GST purposes. Singapore also has an extensive treaty network.

Both jurisdictions are tax-friendly, but Singapore's lack of capital gains tax is attractive for long-term holders. For example, a crypto fund holding assets for appreciation would pay no capital gains tax in Singapore, while in Cyprus, gains from trading would be taxed as income. However, Cyprus offers a lower corporate tax rate. Consulting24 can advise on tax structuring for your specific operations.

Allowed Activities

Cyprus (CASP): Permitted activities include custody and administration of crypto assets, operation of a trading platform, exchange of crypto for fiat or other crypto, execution of orders, placement, transfer services, and advisory services. DeFi and staking are allowed but require specific disclosures.

Singapore (MPI): Covers digital payment token services: exchange, transfer, and custody. Cross-border money transfer and merchant acquisition are also included. DeFi and staking are not explicitly regulated but may fall under existing laws if they involve payment services.

Both allow a broad range of activities, but Singapore's scope is narrower. For instance, Cyprus allows crypto advisory services, while Singapore does not. If you plan to offer multiple services, Cyprus may be more flexible. Consulting24 can map your activities to the appropriate license class.

Singapore benefits: Stable regulatory environment, no capital gains tax, strong banking sector, and access to Asian markets. Preferred for high-volume exchanges and institutional services.

Frequently asked questions

What is the difference between Cyprus and Singapore crypto licenses?

Cyprus operates under EU MiCA regulation, offering passporting across the EEA. Singapore's MAS license is for Asian markets. Cyprus has lower capital minimums (EUR 50k-150k) vs Singapore (SGD 250k). Tax rates are 12.5% (Cyprus) vs 17% (Singapore).

How long does it take to get a Cyprus crypto license?

Typically 6-12 months from application to approval. The timeline depends on the completeness of your documentation and CySEC's review queue. Consulting24 can help streamline the process.

Can I use a Singapore license to serve EU clients?

No, a Singapore license does not cover EU clients. For EU operations, you need a license from an EU member state like Cyprus, which allows passporting under MiCA.

What is the minimum capital for a Cyprus CASP license?

It ranges from EUR 50,000 for custody/transfer services to EUR 125,000 for exchanges and EUR 150,000 for trading platforms. These are minimum own funds requirements.

Does Singapore tax crypto gains?

Singapore does not impose capital gains tax. However, profits from crypto trading as a business are subject to corporate income tax at 17%. Crypto used for payment may incur GST.

Can I apply for both Cyprus and Singapore licenses?

Yes, you can hold multiple licenses. However, each requires separate compliance and capital. Consulting24 can advise on the optimal structure if you need both markets.

What activities are not allowed under a Cyprus CASP license?

Activities like unregulated DeFi lending, algorithmic stablecoins, and certain derivatives may be restricted. Always check with CySEC or consult with Consulting24 for your specific services.

Is a physical office required in Singapore?
