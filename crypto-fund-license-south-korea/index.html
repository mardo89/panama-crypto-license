What Is a Crypto Fund License in South Korea?

A crypto fund license in South Korea is a regulatory authorization issued by the Financial Services Commission (FSC) that allows a firm to manage collective investment schemes (funds) that invest primarily in virtual assets such as Bitcoin, Ethereum, and other cryptocurrencies. The license is part of the FSCMA, which was amended in 2024 to bring virtual asset funds under the same regulatory umbrella as traditional securities funds.

Key features of the license include:

  • Registration as a "virtual asset fund manager" with the FSC.
  • Compliance with capital adequacy, risk management, and investor protection rules.
  • Ongoing reporting obligations to the FSS.
  • Requirement to appoint a local compliance officer and maintain a physical office in South Korea.

Unlike some jurisdictions that offer a simple registration, South Korea imposes stringent requirements to ensure fund managers operate with transparency and financial stability. For example, the FSC requires a detailed business plan outlining the fund's investment strategy, risk management framework, and target investor base. The regulator also expects fund managers to have at least two full-time employees with relevant experience in finance or crypto.

Who Needs a Crypto Fund License in South Korea?

Any entity that manages a pooled investment vehicle investing more than 50% of its assets in virtual assets must obtain a crypto fund license from the FSC. This includes:

  • Hedge funds and venture capital funds focusing on crypto assets.
  • Tokenized asset funds (e.g., real estate tokenization funds).
  • Funds of funds that invest in other crypto funds.
  • Foreign fund managers seeking to market or manage crypto funds for Korean investors.

If you are a crypto fund manager based outside South Korea but wish to raise capital from Korean investors or manage a fund domiciled in Korea, you need this license. Even if your fund is domiciled elsewhere, marketing to Korean residents may trigger licensing requirements. For instance, a Hong Kong-based fund manager that advertises its crypto fund to Korean accredited investors through a Korean broker would need to register with the FSC. License Type & Regulator

The relevant regulator is the Financial Services Commission (FSC), South Korea's top financial regulator. The FSC oversees the registration and supervision of virtual asset fund managers. The specific license type is a "Virtual Asset Fund Manager" registration under the FSCMA.

Key regulatory aspects:

  • The FSC sets capital requirements, governance standards, and operational rules.
  • The Financial Supervisory Service (FSS) conducts on-site inspections and ongoing supervision.
  • Fund managers must also comply with the Act on Reporting and Use of Specific Financial Transaction Information (the "Specific Financial Information Act") for anti-money laundering (AML) purposes.

As of 2026, the FSC has not yet implemented a separate "crypto fund" category under the EU-style MiCA framework; instead, it applies existing fund management rules adapted for virtual assets. This means that the regulatory burden is similar to that of traditional fund managers, but with additional requirements for virtual asset custody, valuation, and disclosure. The FSC also requires fund managers to use only licensed exchanges for trading, such as Upbit, Bithumb, Coinone, and Korbit.

Capital Requirement

The minimum capital requirement for a crypto fund manager in South Korea is set by the FSC at KRW 500 million (approximately USD 375,000) as of 2026. This capital must be maintained as paid-up share capital and cannot be used for operational expenses. It serves as a buffer to cover potential losses and ensure the firm's financial stability.

Additional capital may be required based on the size and risk profile of the funds under management. For example, if the fund's assets exceed KRW 10 billion, the regulator may demand a higher capital base. The capital must be held in a Korean bank account and is subject to annual verification by the FSS. In practice, many fund managers choose to maintain a capital of KRW 1 billion (approx. Tax Treatment of Crypto Funds in South Korea

South Korea's tax treatment of crypto funds is evolving. As of 2026, the following rules apply:

  • Corporate income tax: Fund managers are subject to the standard corporate tax rate of 9% to 24% (progressive) on their profits.
  • Capital gains tax: Gains from the sale of virtual assets by the fund are taxed at the fund level. However, the government has delayed the implementation of a separate crypto capital gains tax for individuals until 2027. For funds, gains are treated as ordinary business income.
  • Withholding tax: Distributions to foreign investors may be subject to withholding tax at a rate of 20%, unless reduced under a double tax treaty.
  • VAT: Management fees are subject to 10% VAT.

Tax planning is essential. Consult a local tax advisor to optimize your fund's structure. For example, setting up a fund as a partnership may allow for pass-through taxation, avoiding double taxation at the fund and investor levels. Additionally, South Korea has tax treaties with many countries that can reduce withholding tax rates on dividends and interest. Consulting24 can connect you with experienced tax advisors in Seoul.

Allowed Activities Under the License

With a South Korea crypto fund license, a manager may engage in the following activities:

  • Establish and manage collective investment schemes (funds) that invest in virtual assets.
  • Market and sell fund units to qualified and retail investors (subject to restrictions).
  • Trade virtual assets on behalf of the fund on licensed exchanges (e.g., Upbit, Bithumb).
  • Provide investment advice related to virtual assets to the fund.
  • Manage tokenized securities funds (e.g., security token offerings).

Prohibited activities include direct lending of virtual assets, operating a crypto exchange, and engaging in proprietary trading outside the fund structure without separate licensing. Additionally, fund managers cannot commingle fund assets with their own assets and must maintain segregated accounts for each fund. The FSC also restricts the use of use; as of 2026, crypto funds in South Korea are not allowed to borrow more than 200% of the fund's net asset value.

Step-by-Step Application Process

The application process for a crypto fund license in South Korea involves several stages. Consulting24 advises and coordinates each step with local legal partners.

  1. Pre-application preparation: Assemble a team of local directors, prepare a business plan, and set up a Korean corporation with the required capital.
  2. Draft compliance documentation: Create AML/KYC policies, risk management procedures, and internal controls.
  3. Submit application to FSC: File the application form along with supporting documents (business plan, financial statements, background checks, etc.).
  4. FSC review: The FSC reviews the application and may request additional information. This stage typically takes 3-6 months.
  5. FSS inspection: After preliminary approval, the FSS conducts an on-site inspection of your office and systems.
  6. Final approval: If all requirements are met, the FSC issues the license. You can then begin fund operations.

Common Mistakes When Applying

Many applicants make avoidable errors that delay or derail their license application. Common mistakes include:

  • Insufficient local presence: Not having a physical office or local directors. The FSC requires a registered office in South Korea and at least one director who is a resident of Korea.
  • Incomplete business plan: Failing to provide a detailed investment strategy, risk management framework, and financial projections.
  • Poor AML documentation: Submitting generic AML policies that do not address the specific risks of virtual assets.
  • Underestimating capital requirements: Not maintaining the minimum capital throughout the application process.
  • Ignoring banking requirements: Assuming that a bank account will be easy to open after licensing. In reality, you should start the banking process early.

Frequently asked questions

What is the minimum capital for a crypto fund license in South Korea?

The minimum capital is KRW 500 million (approximately USD 375,000) as of 2026. This must be paid-up share capital and maintained throughout the license period. Additional capital may be required for larger funds.

How long does it take to get a crypto fund license in South Korea?

The FSC typically processes applications within 6 to 12 months. The timeline depends on the completeness of your application and the FSC's workload. Consulting24's coordination can help avoid delays.

Can a foreign company apply for a South Korea crypto fund license?

Yes, but you must incorporate a Korean subsidiary with a local office and at least one resident director. Foreign fund managers can also apply, but the process is more complex and requires local representation.

What are the tax rates for crypto funds in South Korea?

Corporate income tax is progressive from 9% to 24%. Capital gains from virtual assets are taxed as ordinary business income. Withholding tax on distributions to foreign investors is 20%, subject to treaty relief. Management fees incur 10% VAT.

Is a physical office required in South Korea?

Yes, the FSC requires a registered physical office in South Korea. The office must have dedicated space and equipment, and the FSS may conduct on-site inspections. Virtual offices are not accepted.

Can I use a South Korea crypto fund license to operate in other countries?

The license is specific to South Korea. To operate in other jurisdictions, you need separate licenses. However, the South Korea license can enhance your credibility when applying elsewhere. Consulting24 can help you with multi-jurisdictional licensing.
