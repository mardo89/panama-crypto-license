What Is a Singapore Crypto License?

The term 'Singapore crypto license' generally refers to a Major Payment Institution (MPI) licence under the Payment Services Act (PSA), regulated by the Monetary Authority of Singapore (MAS). This licence is required for businesses that provide digital payment token (DPT) services, including cryptocurrency exchanges, custodians, and OTC desks. The PSA came into force in 2020 and has been updated to include DPT services. As of 2026, the regime is fully operational and strictly enforced.

The MPI licence allows firms to conduct payment services exceeding certain thresholds without needing multiple licences. It is widely considered one of the most reputable crypto licences globally, offering a pathway to serve Singaporean clients and access the broader Asian market. The application process is thorough and can take 6 to 12 months, but the reward is a licence that signals trust and compliance to partners and customers alike. For example, a licensed exchange can list on global indices and attract institutional investors who require regulatory oversight.

Who Needs a Singapore Crypto License?

Any entity that provides any of the following services in Singapore must hold an MPI licence or a smaller Standard Payment Institution (SPI) licence:

  • Digital payment token exchange service (e.g., buying/selling cryptocurrencies like Bitcoin or Ethereum)
  • Digital payment token custodial service (holding private keys on behalf of customers)
  • OTC trading of digital payment tokens (large block trades outside public order books)
  • Cross-border money transfer involving DPTs (e.g., remittance using stablecoins)

If your business handles more than SGD 3 million per month in transaction volume or stores more than SGD 5 million in e-money, you must apply for an MPI licence. Smaller operators may qualify for an SPI licence, which has lower thresholds but also limited scope. For instance, an SPI licence caps total payment transactions at SGD 3 million per month and e-money float at SGD 5 million. Most serious crypto businesses target the MPI licence from the start to avoid growth constraints.

License Type and Regulator

The regulator is the Monetary Authority of Singapore (MAS). The relevant licence is the Major Payment Institution (MPI) licence under the Payment Services Act (PSA). The MAS also grants Standard Payment Institution (SPI) licences for smaller operators, but the MPI is the standard for crypto businesses.

Key regulatory requirements include:

  • Anti-Money Laundering (AML) and Countering the Financing of Terrorism (CFT) policies tailored to DPT risks
  • Technology risk management and cybersecurity measures, including regular penetration testing
  • Audit and compliance reporting, with annual external audits
  • Segregation of client assets from operational funds

Capital Requirement

For an MPI licence, the minimum base capital requirement is SGD 250,000 (approximately EUR 170,000). This capital must be maintained at all times. For an SPI licence, the requirement is lower at SGD 100,000. The capital must be held in liquid assets and cannot be used for operational expenses. Additionally, the MAS may require higher capital based on the volume of transactions. For example, a large exchange processing over SGD 100 million monthly may face a capital requirement of SGD 500,000 or more.

This is a significant commitment, so founders should plan their funding accordingly. The capital must be in the form of cash or cash equivalents, not cryptocurrency. Tax Treatment

Singapore offers a favourable tax regime for crypto businesses. The corporate income tax rate is a flat 17%. There is no capital gains tax, and cryptocurrency transactions are generally treated as ordinary income or capital gains depending on the nature of the business. The Inland Revenue Authority of Singapore (IRAS) has issued guidelines stating that businesses dealing in digital payment tokens are subject to normal income tax rules.

Additionally, Singapore does not impose goods and services tax (GST) on the exchange of digital payment tokens, making it a tax-efficient jurisdiction. For detailed tax planning, consulting with a local tax advisor is recommended. This tax clarity is a major draw for crypto firms compared to jurisdictions with ambiguous tax laws. For instance, a trading firm can deduct losses against profits without complex tax calculations, unlike in some EU countries where crypto gains are taxed as miscellaneous income.

Allowed Activities Under the License

An MPI licence permits the following activities:

  • Operating a digital payment token exchange (buying/selling DPTs like Bitcoin, Ethereum, or stablecoins)
  • Providing custodial services for DPTs (holding private keys for customers)
  • Facilitating cross-border money transfers using DPTs (e.g., remittance via blockchain)
  • OTC trading of DPTs (large block trades off-exchange)
  • Issuing digital payment tokens (subject to additional approval from MAS)

The licence does not cover derivatives or securities tokens, which would require a separate Capital Markets Services (CMS) licence from MAS. Step-by-Step Application Process

The application process for a Singapore crypto license involves several stages:

  1. Pre-application preparation: Develop AML/CFT policies, appoint a compliance officer, and set up a local entity (Singapore company with at least one local director).
  2. Submit application to MAS: File Form 1 (for MPI) or Form 2 (for SPI) via the MAS online portal, along with supporting documents including business plan, financial projections, and due diligence on shareholders.
  3. MAS review: MAS will assess the application, conduct background checks on directors and shareholders, and may request additional information. This stage can take 3 to 6 months.
  4. In-principle approval (IPA): If MAS is satisfied, it issues an IPA, allowing the applicant to set up operations under certain conditions, such as a sandbox environment.
  5. Operational readiness: Implement technology systems, conduct a security audit by an accredited firm, and finalize compliance procedures. This may take 2 to 4 months.
  6. Final approval: MAS grants the full licence after verifying operational readiness. The entire journey can take 12 to 18 months for complex cases.

Compliance and Trust

Compliance is at the heart of the Singapore regime. MAS requires ongoing reporting, annual audits, and regular AML/CFT training. Failure to comply can result in fines, licence suspension, or revocation. Key compliance obligations include:

  • Submit annual audited financial statements to MAS within 5 months of financial year end
  • File periodic transaction reports for suspicious activities
  • Maintain a compliance officer resident in Singapore who is a senior manager
  • Conduct regular risk assessments and update policies accordingly

Common Mistakes in the Application Process

Many applicants face delays or rejection due to avoidable errors. Common mistakes include:

  • Incomplete documentation: Missing business plan, AML policy, or source of funds declarations. MAS expects a comprehensive submission; any gap can trigger a request for information that adds months.
  • Inadequate AML/CFT framework: MAS expects a detailed, risk-based approach. A generic policy copied from another jurisdiction will be rejected. You must tailor it to DPT risks, including blockchain analytics.
  • Unfit directors or shareholders: Past criminal records or regulatory sanctions can disqualify individuals. MAS conducts background checks on all beneficial owners. Ensure your team has a clean record.
  • Underestimating timeline: Expect 12 months minimum. Rushing the process leads to errors. Plan your business launch accordingly.

Frequently asked questions

What is the difference between an MPI and SPI licence in Singapore?

An MPI licence is for larger payment service providers that exceed transaction volume thresholds (SGD 3 million/month) or e-money float (SGD 5 million). It allows unlimited transaction volume. An SPI licence is for smaller operators with caps on these amounts. Most crypto businesses aim for MPI to avoid growth restrictions.

How long does it take to get a Singapore crypto license?

The typical timeline is 6 to 12 months from application to final approval, but it can extend to 18 months if MAS requests additional information or if deficiencies are found. The process includes pre-application preparation, MAS review, in-principle approval, and operational readiness checks.

What is the minimum capital requirement for a Singapore crypto license?

For an MPI licence, the minimum base capital is SGD 250,000 (approx. EUR 170,000). For an SPI licence, it is SGD 100,000. Higher capital may be required based on transaction volume. The capital must be held in liquid assets and cannot be used for operational expenses.

Can a foreign company apply for a Singapore crypto license?

Yes, but you must incorporate a Singapore company (private limited) with at least one local director (Singapore resident). The company must have a registered address and a compliance officer based in Singapore. MAS also requires that directors and shareholders pass fit and proper tests.

What activities are allowed under the Singapore MPI licence?

The MPI licence permits digital payment token exchange services, custodial services, OTC trading, and cross-border money transfers using DPTs. It does not cover derivatives or securities tokens, which require a separate Capital Markets Services licence from MAS.

Is there any tax exemption for crypto businesses in Singapore?

Singapore has a flat corporate income tax rate of 17% and no capital gains tax. There is no GST on the exchange of digital payment tokens. However, businesses must pay income tax on profits from crypto trading or services. Specific tax incentives may be available for fintech firms.

What are the common reasons for rejection of a Singapore crypto license application?

Common reasons include incomplete documentation, inadequate AML/CFT policies, unfit directors or shareholders (e.g., criminal records), and failure to demonstrate operational readiness. MAS also rejects applications if the business model poses high risks of money laundering or terrorism financing.

How does Singapore compare to Panama for crypto licensing?

Singapore offers higher regulatory prestige and access to Asian markets but is expensive (SGD 85,000+) and slow (6-18 months). Panama is cheaper (EUR 6,000 flat), faster (2-3 months), and has no minimum capital, but its licence is less recognised globally. Consulting24 advises based on your budget and target market.

Do I need a local director for a Singapore crypto license?

Yes, MAS requires at least one director who is a Singapore resident (citizen, permanent resident, or Employment Pass holder). This director must be fit and proper. Consulting24 can help you find a nominee director if needed, but we recommend using a genuine local partner.

Can I use a Singapore crypto license to serve customers in other Asian countries?

Yes, but you must comply with local regulations in each country. The Singapore licence allows you to serve customers in Singapore directly. For other countries, you may need additional registrations or rely on exemptions. MAS expects you to have appropriate controls for cross-border activities.
