What Is the Crypto Payment Institution License Thailand?

The Crypto Payment Institution License Thailand is a regulatory authorization issued by the Thai SEC under the Digital Asset Businesses Decree (B.E. 2561). It allows licensed entities to provide digital asset payment services, such as facilitating crypto transactions, operating exchanges, and managing wallets. This license is part of Thailand's proactive approach to integrating digital assets into the financial system while ensuring consumer protection and anti-money laundering compliance.

Unlike a simple registration, this license requires meeting strict capital, governance, and operational standards. It is designed for businesses that handle customer funds and execute payments using cryptocurrencies or stablecoins. The license is valid for one year and must be renewed annually, with ongoing supervision by the SEC and the Bank of Thailand. As of 2026, the SEC has increased scrutiny on AML controls, requiring licensed entities to implement transaction monitoring systems and report suspicious activities within 24 hours.

For a practical example, a payment processor handling 10,000 transactions per month must demonstrate strong KYC procedures and maintain a minimum of 50 million THB in registered capital. The license also mandates that at least one director be a Thai resident, which is a common requirement in many Asian jurisdictions. Consulting24 can help you navigate these specifics.

Who Needs This License?

Any business offering digital asset payment services in Thailand must obtain this license. This includes:

  • Crypto payment processors enabling merchants to accept digital currencies like Bitcoin or USDT.
  • Digital asset exchanges that facilitate trading between fiat and crypto, such as a platform allowing THB to BTC swaps.
  • Wallet providers offering custodial services for customer funds, including multi-signature wallets.
  • Remittance services using cryptocurrencies for cross-border transfers, for example, sending funds from Thailand to Myanmar.
  • Stablecoin issuers or distributors operating within Thailand, such as a company issuing a THB-pegged stablecoin.

Foreign companies targeting the Thai market also need this license. For instance, a European crypto payment gateway wanting to onboard Thai merchants must incorporate a Thai subsidiary and obtain the license. License Type & Regulator

The Crypto Payment Institution License Thailand is regulated by the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) of Thailand, in coordination with the Bank of Thailand. The license falls under the Digital Asset Businesses category, specifically for payment services. There are two main types:

  • Type 1 - Digital Asset Exchange License: For platforms that facilitate trading of digital assets, such as a centralized exchange like Bitkub.
  • Type 2 - Digital Asset Broker/Dealer License: For intermediaries that buy/sell digital assets on behalf of clients, similar to a brokerage service.
  • Type 3 - Digital Asset Payment Service License: For payment services using digital assets (the focus of this page).

Each type has specific requirements, but the payment service license is the most relevant for crypto payment institutions. The SEC oversees licensing, while the Bank of Thailand monitors payment system risks. The SEC also requires that all licensed entities become members of the Digital Asset Operators Association of Thailand, which sets industry standards.

Capital Requirement

The Thai SEC imposes minimum capital requirements for crypto payment institutions. As of 2026, the required registered capital is at least 50 million THB (approximately EUR 1.3 million) for payment service licenses. This capital must be fully paid up and maintained throughout the license period. The capital can be in cash or liquid assets approved by the SEC, such as government bonds.

For comparison, other jurisdictions like Panama have lower capital thresholds (EUR 6,000 flat for company setup, but no specific crypto license capital requirement). Thailand's higher capital reflects its stringent regulatory approach. Tax Treatment

Thailand imposes a 7% VAT on digital asset transactions, but crypto payment services may be exempt under certain conditions if the service is considered a financial service. Corporate income tax is 20% on net profits. However, the Thai Revenue Department has clarified that capital gains from crypto trading are subject to 15% withholding tax for individuals, while corporate entities are taxed at the standard rate.

For licensed payment institutions, transaction fees are considered taxable income. The SEC and Revenue Department provide guidelines on tax compliance. For example, if a payment processor earns 10 million THB in fees annually, it would owe 2 million THB in corporate tax. Allowed Activities

With a Crypto Payment Institution License Thailand, you are permitted to:

  • Operate a digital asset payment system for merchants and consumers, such as a point-of-sale solution for accepting crypto.
  • Facilitate the exchange of digital assets for fiat currency, including THB and other major currencies.
  • Provide custodial wallet services for digital assets, with features like multi-factor authentication.
  • Offer remittance services using cryptocurrencies, for instance, enabling workers to send money home.
  • Issue and redeem stablecoins (subject to Bank of Thailand approval).

Activities not allowed include securities brokerage (requires separate license) and unregistered initial coin offerings. The SEC strictly monitors compliance with anti-money laundering (AML) and know-your-customer (KYC) rules. For example, all transactions above 50,000 THB must be reported to the Anti-Money Laundering Office (AMLO).

Step-by-Step Application Process

Applying for the Crypto Payment Institution License Thailand involves several stages:

  1. Pre-application consultation: Assess your business model and eligibility with Consulting24. We review your business plan and identify gaps.
  2. Company incorporation: Register a Thai company with the Ministry of Commerce (minimum capital 50 million THB). This typically takes 1-2 weeks.
  3. Prepare documentation: Business plan, AML/KYC policies, risk management framework, audited financials, and board resolutions. The AML policy must include customer due diligence procedures and a transaction monitoring system.
  4. Submit application: File with the SEC via their online portal, along with the application fee. The SEC will acknowledge receipt within 7 days.
  5. SEC review: The SEC reviews within 90 days, may request additional information. Common requests include clarification on source of funds for capital.
  6. On-site inspection: SEC officials may visit your office to verify operations, such as checking IT infrastructure and physical security.
  7. License issuance: Upon approval, pay the issuance fee and receive the license. The license is valid for one year.
  8. Ongoing compliance: Submit quarterly reports, maintain capital, and renew annually. Consulting24 can assist with reporting templates.

Banking & Payments

Licensed crypto payment institutions in Thailand can open corporate bank accounts with local banks, though due diligence is rigorous. Banks require proof of SEC license, AML policies, and source of funds. Some banks, like Kasikornbank and Siam Commercial Bank, have dedicated units for crypto businesses. However, many banks still view crypto as high-risk, so rejection rates are high.

For payment processing, you can integrate with Thai payment gateways (e.g., PromptPay) for fiat on/off ramps. Cross-border payments may require additional approvals from the Bank of Thailand. Compliance & Trust

Maintaining the license requires ongoing compliance with SEC regulations, including:

  • Annual renewal and fee payment, which is typically 50% of the issuance fee.
  • Submission of quarterly financial reports within 45 days of the quarter end.
  • AML/KYC audits by an external firm, such as a Big Four auditor.
  • Cybersecurity assessments, including penetration testing every six months.
  • Capital maintenance at all times; if capital falls below 50 million THB, the SEC may suspend operations.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Applicants often make these errors:

  • Insufficient capital: Underestimating the 50 million THB requirement and failing to have proof of funds.
  • Poor AML documentation: Not having a strong AML policy in line with Thai law, such as lacking a risk-based approach.
  • Incomplete business plan: Lacking details on revenue model and risk management, e.g., no contingency for market volatility.
  • Ignoring local partnerships: Not engaging a local director or compliance officer, which is mandatory.
  • Rushing the process: Expecting approval in less than 6 months, leading to incomplete submissions.
