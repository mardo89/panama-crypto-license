Overview: What Is a Crypto License in Switzerland and the Czech Republic?

A crypto license is an official authorization that allows a company to offer services involving virtual currencies, such as exchange, custody, or trading. In Switzerland, the Swiss Financial Market Supervisory Authority (FINMA) regulates crypto activities under the Anti-Money Laundering Act (AMLA) and the Financial Market Infrastructure Act (FMIA). Depending on the service, firms may need a banking license, securities dealer license, or a simple AML registration. In the Czech Republic, the Czech National Bank (CNB) oversees crypto asset service providers under the new MiCA framework, which is fully in force in 2026. The Czech license is a unified authorization for CASPs, replacing previous fragmented registrations.

Both jurisdictions require strong AML/KYC procedures, but the scope and depth of regulation differ. Switzerland's framework is principle-based and flexible, while the Czech Republic's is more prescriptive under MiCA. Who Needs a Crypto License in These Jurisdictions?

Any business that provides virtual currency services to residents of Switzerland or the Czech Republic must obtain the appropriate license. This includes cryptocurrency exchanges, wallet providers, custodians, brokers, and trading platforms. In Switzerland, even companies that merely facilitate crypto payments or offer initial coin offerings (ICOs) may require a FINMA license or at least an AML registration. In the Czech Republic, under MiCA, any entity offering crypto asset services, including transfer, exchange, custody, and advisory, must be authorized by the CNB.

Founders from outside the EU often choose the Czech Republic for its accessibility and lower costs, while established players seeking prestige and a global hub often opt for Switzerland. License Type and Regulator

Switzerland: The regulator is FINMA. The license type depends on the activity: a simple AML registration for pure exchange services (no custody), a securities dealer license for trading platforms, or a banking license for custodial services. Most crypto startups opt for the AML registration initially, which is less burdensome but still requires a Swiss presence and compliance with AMLA.

Czech Republic: The regulator is the Czech National Bank (CNB). Under MiCA, the license is a unified Crypto Asset Service Provider (CASP) authorization. This covers all major services: exchange, custody, transfer, and advisory. The CNB is the competent authority, and the license is recognized across the EU under the MiCA passport.

Capital Requirement

Switzerland: There is no statutory minimum capital for AML registration, but FINMA expects adequate financial resources. For a securities dealer license, minimum capital is CHF 1.5 million (approx. EUR 1.5 million). For a banking license, it is CHF 10 million (approx. EUR 10 million). Most crypto firms start with AML registration, which has no fixed capital requirement but requires proof of sufficient funds.

Czech Republic: Under MiCA, capital requirements are tiered: EUR 50,000 for exchange and transfer services, EUR 125,000 for custody, and EUR 150,000 for trading platforms. These are mandatory and must be maintained at all times. The Czech Republic's capital requirements are lower than Switzerland's for most activities, making it more accessible for startups.

Tax Treatment

Switzerland: Switzerland has a favorable tax regime for crypto. Private investors are exempt from capital gains tax, but professional traders and businesses pay corporate income tax (CIT) at cantonal rates (typically 12-20%). VAT is not applicable on crypto transactions, but mining and staking rewards may be subject to income tax. Switzerland also has no withholding tax on dividends for Swiss companies.

Czech Republic: The Czech Republic taxes crypto as property. For individuals, crypto gains are subject to 15% income tax (or 23% for high earners) if held for less than 3 years; after 3 years, gains are tax-exempt. For companies, corporate income tax is 19%. VAT is not charged on crypto transactions. The Czech tax system is straightforward, but compliance requires careful record-keeping.

Both jurisdictions offer competitive tax environments, but Switzerland is often preferred for long-term holding due to its capital gains exemption for individuals.

Allowed Activities

Switzerland: FINMA allows a wide range of activities under its flexible framework: exchange between crypto and fiat, crypto-to-crypto trading, custody, wallet services, ICO/STO issuance, and decentralized finance (DeFi) operations. However, activities that involve lending or taking deposits may require a banking license. Derivatives and securities tokens are regulated under FMIA.

Czech Republic: Under MiCA, the Czech license covers all services defined in the regulation: exchange, custody, transfer, advisory, and portfolio management. It also allows operation of a trading platform. Activities not covered by MiCA, such as DeFi lending without a license, are prohibited. The Czech Republic is more restrictive than Switzerland for innovative models but provides clarity and legal certainty.

Banking and Payment Services

Access to banking is critical for crypto businesses. In Switzerland, major banks like UBS and Credit Suisse (now part of UBS) offer accounts to licensed crypto firms, but due diligence is strict. Some cantonal banks are more open. In the Czech Republic, banks are generally more accommodating to CASPs under MiCA, but compliance with AML is still rigorous. Both jurisdictions require a local bank account for operations.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Applying for a crypto license without proper preparation can lead to delays or rejection. Common mistakes include: insufficient AML/KYC documentation, underestimating capital requirements, choosing the wrong corporate structure, and failing to appoint a local compliance officer. In Switzerland, many applicants overlook the need for a physical office and Swiss director. In the Czech Republic, errors in MiCA classification of services are frequent.

Frequently asked questions

What is the main difference between a Swiss and Czech crypto license?

The Swiss license is regulated by FINMA and offers flexibility with multiple license types (AML registration, securities dealer, banking). The Czech license is a unified CASP authorization under MiCA, with lower capital requirements and faster approval. Switzerland is more expensive and prestigious; the Czech Republic is more cost-effective and EU-passportable.

How much does a Czech crypto license cost in 2026?

The estimated cost is EUR 10,000 to 30,000, including company incorporation and advisory fees. Exact pricing is confirmed in a consultation. This is significantly lower than Switzerland's EUR 50,000 to 150,000+.

What is the minimum capital for a Czech crypto license?

Under MiCA, capital requirements are tiered: EUR 50,000 for exchange and transfer services, EUR 125,000 for custody, and EUR 150,000 for trading platforms. These must be maintained at all times.

Does Switzerland require a minimum capital for crypto license?

For AML registration, there is no statutory minimum, but FINMA expects adequate financial resources. For a securities dealer license, minimum capital is CHF 1.5 million. For a banking license, it is CHF 10 million.

How long does it take to get a crypto license in Switzerland?

Typically 6 to 12 months, depending on the license type and completeness of the application. FINMA conducts thorough due diligence, which can extend the timeline.

Can I passport a Czech crypto license to other EU countries?

Yes, under MiCA, a Czech CASP authorization is recognized across the EU. You can provide services in other member states without additional licensing, subject to notification procedures.

What are the tax benefits of Switzerland for crypto?

Private investors are exempt from capital gains tax. Corporate income tax rates are 12-20% depending on the canton. No VAT on crypto transactions. No withholding tax on dividends for Swiss companies.

Is the Czech Republic tax-friendly for crypto?

Yes. Individuals pay 15% income tax on gains (23% for high earners) if held under 3 years; after 3 years, gains are tax-exempt. Corporate tax is 19%. No VAT on crypto transactions.

Which jurisdiction is better for a startup: Switzerland or Czech Republic?

The Czech Republic is generally better for startups due to lower costs (EUR 10,000-30,000), faster timeline (3-6 months), and lower capital requirements. Switzerland is better for established firms seeking prestige and institutional clients.

What activities are allowed under a Czech CASP license?

Exchange, custody, transfer, advisory, portfolio management, and operation of a trading platform. Activities not covered by MiCA, such as unlicensed DeFi lending, are prohibited.

Do I need a local office for a crypto license in Switzerland?

Yes, Switzerland requires a physical registered office and a local director or representative. This is a key compliance requirement for FINMA registration.
