What Is a Crypto Gambling License in Malta?

A crypto gambling license in Malta is an authorisation from the Malta Gaming Authority (MGA) that allows operators to offer gambling services using cryptocurrencies. It is not a separate license but a standard MGA license that permits the use of virtual currencies for deposits, wagers, and withdrawals. The MGA has updated its rules to align with MiCA, ensuring that any crypto assets used are compliant with EU standards.

This license covers casino games, sports betting, poker, and other gambling products where crypto is accepted. Operators must also comply with anti-money laundering (AML) and counter-terrorist financing (CTF) obligations, including the EU's 5th and 6th AML Directives. The MGA is known for its rigorous yet clear regulatory framework, making Malta a trusted hub for crypto gambling. For example, an online casino accepting Bitcoin must have its platform audited by an MGA-approved lab and demonstrate that player funds are segregated. The MGA also requires that all crypto transactions are traceable, meaning anonymous coins like Monero are not permitted.

Who Needs a Crypto Gambling License in Malta?

Any business that wants to offer gambling services to Maltese residents or target the EU market using cryptocurrencies needs this license. This includes:

  • Online casinos accepting Bitcoin, Ethereum, or other coins
  • Sports betting platforms with crypto payment options
  • Poker rooms and bingo sites using digital assets
  • White-label operators providing crypto gambling services

Even if your company is based outside Malta, if you serve EU customers, you likely need an MGA license or a license from another EU member state that is recognised under MiCA. License Type and Regulator

The regulator is the Malta Gaming Authority (MGA). The license type is a Class 1, 2, 3, or 4 license depending on the activities:

  • Class 1: Online casino, slots, and table games (operator controls the game)
  • Class 2: Sports betting and exchange betting
  • Class 3: Peer-to-peer games like poker and bingo
  • Class 4: Fantasy sports and skill games

For crypto gambling, the MGA requires that any virtual currency used is compliant with MiCA. The license itself is a single authorisation, but the operator must specify which crypto assets are accepted. The MGA also mandates that all funds are held in segregated accounts and that player funds are protected. Each class has specific technical and financial requirements. For instance, a Class 1 operator must provide a detailed description of the random number generator (RNG) and have it certified by an accredited testing lab like Gaming Laboratories International (GLI). Class 2 operators need to demonstrate the integrity of their betting exchange system.

Capital Requirement

The MGA imposes minimum capital requirements based on the license class and projected turnover. As of 2026, the general guidance is:

  • Class 1: Minimum 100,000 - 200,000 EUR
  • Class 2: Minimum 100,000 - 150,000 EUR
  • Class 3: Minimum 80,000 - 120,000 EUR
  • Class 4: Minimum 50,000 - 80,000 EUR

These figures are indicative; the MGA assesses each applicant's financial standing. The capital must be held in a Maltese bank account or a reputable EU bank. For crypto operators, the MGA may require additional liquidity buffers to cover volatility risks. For instance, if you accept Bitcoin, the MGA might ask for a reserve equivalent to 120% of your average daily trading volume to protect against price swings. Tax Treatment

Malta offers a favourable tax regime for gaming companies. The effective corporate tax rate can be as low as 5% after refunds, thanks to Malta's full imputation system. However, for crypto gambling, specific rules apply:

  • Gaming revenue is subject to a 5% tax on gross gaming revenue (GGR) for most classes
  • No VAT on gambling services
  • Withholding tax on dividends may apply but can be reduced via double tax treaties

Cryptocurrency gains are treated as trading income and taxed accordingly. MiCA compliance may affect how crypto assets are classified for tax purposes. For example, if you hold Bitcoin as an investment, any appreciation is subject to capital gains tax at the standard corporate rate of 35% (before refunds). Consulting24 recommends a tax review with a local advisor to optimise your structure. In contrast, Panama imposes 0% tax on offshore income, making it attractive for operators targeting non-EU markets.

Allowed Activities

With a Malta crypto gambling license, you can:

  • Offer casino games, slots, and live dealer games using crypto
  • Accept Bitcoin, Ethereum, and other MiCA-compliant tokens
  • Provide sports betting, including esports, with crypto wagering
  • Operate peer-to-peer poker and bingo rooms
  • Run fantasy sports and skill-based competitions

You cannot offer unregulated gambling products or use anonymous cryptocurrencies. All crypto transactions must be traceable and comply with AML/KYC requirements. The MGA also prohibits offering services to countries where gambling is illegal. For example, you cannot accept players from the United States unless you hold a separate license there. Additionally, the MGA requires that all games are fair and that the RNG is certified. If you plan to offer a new type of game, you must submit it for approval before launch.

The MGA also conducts periodic technical audits to ensure your platform remains compliant. Non-compliance can result in fines or license revocation. Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Underestimating capital requirements: The MGA expects sufficient liquidity, especially for crypto volatility.
  • Poor AML/KYC procedures: Crypto gambling is high-risk; weak AML can lead to rejection.
  • Ignoring MiCA compliance: All crypto assets must be MiCA-compliant; using unregulated tokens can cause issues.
  • Incomplete technical documentation: The MGA requires detailed system descriptions and testing reports.
  • Choosing the wrong license class: Some operators apply for Class 1 when they need Class 2, causing delays.

Frequently asked questions

What is the difference between a Malta crypto gambling license and a standard MGA license?

There is no separate crypto license; it is a standard MGA license that permits the use of cryptocurrencies. The MGA requires that all crypto assets used are MiCA-compliant and that operators adhere to enhanced AML measures. The license class (1-4) determines the gambling activities allowed.

How long does it take to get a Malta crypto gambling license?

The process typically takes 4 to 8 months after full submission. Delays can occur if documents are incomplete or if the MGA requires additional audits. Consulting24 helps streamline the process by preparing a thorough application.

What are the capital requirements for a Malta crypto gambling license?

Minimum capital ranges from 50,000 EUR for Class 4 to 200,000 EUR for Class 1, depending on projected turnover. The MGA may require additional buffers for crypto volatility. Capital must be held in a Maltese or EU bank account.

Can I use any cryptocurrency with a Malta gambling license?

Only MiCA-compliant cryptocurrencies are allowed. Anonymous coins like Monero are prohibited. The MGA requires that all crypto transactions are traceable and that operators implement strong AML/KYC procedures.

Is a Malta gambling license recognised in other EU countries?

Yes, under MiCA, a Malta license is recognised across the EU, allowing you to offer services to customers in all member states. However, you must comply with local gambling laws in each country, which may require additional notifications.

What are the ongoing costs for a Malta crypto gambling license?

Annual license fees range from 25,000 to 50,000 EUR depending on class and revenue. You also need to budget for compliance audits, legal fees, and bank account maintenance. Total ongoing costs can be 30,000 to 60,000 EUR per year.

Do I need a physical office in Malta?

Yes, the MGA requires a physical office in Malta with at least one local director or a EU company with a branch. The office must be staffed during business hours and serve as the registered address.
