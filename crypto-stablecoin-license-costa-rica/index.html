What Is a Crypto Stablecoin License in Costa Rica?

As of 2026, Costa Rica does not have a single, dedicated license titled "crypto stablecoin license." Instead, stablecoin-related activities fall under broader virtual asset service provider (VASP) regulations. The regulatory framework is still developing, with the Central Bank of Costa Rica and SUGEF issuing guidelines on anti-money laundering (AML) and counter-terrorist financing (CTF) for crypto businesses. A stablecoin project would typically need to register as a VASP and comply with local AML/KYC obligations.

Key characteristics of the Costa Rican approach:

  • No formal licensing regime for stablecoins as of 2026; registration as a VASP is required.
  • Regulation is primarily AML/CTF-focused, not prudential.
  • Stablecoin issuers must demonstrate adequate reserves and transparency, but no specific capital requirement is mandated.
  • Tax treatment follows standard corporate income tax (30%) and VAT (13%) rules.

For stablecoin issuers, the lack of a dedicated framework means that the regulator may apply additional scrutiny on a case-by-case basis. Consulting24 advises clients to prepare a detailed business plan and reserve management policy to address regulatory expectations.

Who Needs a Crypto Stablecoin License in Costa Rica?

The following entities typically require registration or licensing for stablecoin activities in Costa Rica:

  • Stablecoin issuers - companies that create and manage a stablecoin pegged to a fiat currency or other asset.
  • Exchanges - platforms that list stablecoins for trading.
  • Custodial wallet providers - services that hold private keys for stablecoin users.
  • Payment processors - businesses that facilitate stablecoin payments.
  • OTC desks - over-the-counter trading desks dealing in stablecoins.

Any entity that provides virtual asset services to Costa Rican residents or operates from Costa Rica must comply. If your primary market is international, Panama's more established framework may be more suitable. For example, a stablecoin issuer targeting global users may find Panama's territorial tax regime and faster setup more advantageous.

License Type and Regulator

The primary regulator for crypto activities in Costa Rica is the Superintendencia General de Entidades Financieras (SUGEF), in coordination with the Central Bank. There is no single "crypto license"; instead, businesses must register as a VASP under the AML/CTF law (Law 8204). The registration process is overseen by SUGEF and the Financial Analysis Unit (UAF).

For stablecoin-specific operations, additional guidelines may apply regarding reserve backing and audit requirements, but these are not codified into a separate license. Consulting24 advises clients to prepare a comprehensive compliance program covering AML, KYC, and reserve attestation. The regulator expects a local legal representative and a physical office presence, though the requirements are less stringent than in EU jurisdictions.

Capital Requirement

Costa Rica currently does not impose a statutory minimum capital requirement for VASP registration. However, the regulator expects the company to have sufficient financial resources to operate and comply with AML obligations. In practice, a minimum of $10,000 - $50,000 is advisable to demonstrate substance and cover initial costs. Stablecoin issuers may need higher capital to back the stablecoin reserves, but this is a business decision, not a regulatory mandate.

In contrast, Panama has no minimum capital requirement for its crypto license, and the overall cost is lower and more predictable. For EU jurisdictions like Lithuania, the minimum capital under MiCA is EUR 125,000 for most CASP services, making Costa Rica a lower-cost entry point for smaller projects.

Tax Treatment

Costa Rica's tax regime for crypto businesses includes:

  • Corporate income tax: 30% on net profits (standard rate).
  • VAT: 13% on most services, but crypto transactions may be exempt if treated as financial services.
  • Withholding tax: 15% on dividends paid to non-residents (reduced under tax treaties).
  • Capital gains: Taxed as ordinary income at 30%.

Stablecoin issuers should seek local tax advice to optimize structure. Panama offers territorial taxation (only Costa Rica-source income taxed) and no VAT on most services, making it more tax-efficient for international operations. For example, a stablecoin issuer earning revenue from global users would pay 0% tax in Panama on non-Costa Rica income, versus 30% in Costa Rica.

Allowed Activities

Under the current Costa Rican framework, a registered VASP may engage in the following activities with stablecoins:

  • Issuance and redemption of stablecoins.
  • Exchange of stablecoins for fiat or other crypto assets.
  • Custody and wallet services for stablecoins.
  • Payment processing using stablecoins.
  • OTC trading of stablecoins.

Activities that require a banking license (e.g., lending, deposit-taking) are not permitted under a VASP registration. Stablecoin issuers must ensure full backing of tokens with liquid reserves and provide regular attestation reports. The regulator may also require disclosure of the reserve composition and audit frequency.

Step-by-Step Process

Obtaining a crypto VASP registration in Costa Rica involves the following steps:

  1. Company incorporation - Register a Costa Rican corporation (Sociedad Anónima) with a legal representative. This includes drafting bylaws and registering with the national registry.
  2. AML compliance program - Develop and implement policies covering KYC, transaction monitoring, and reporting. The program must be tailored to stablecoin risks, such as anonymity and cross-border transfers.
  3. Submit application - File the VASP registration with SUGEF, including business plan, AML manual, and ownership details. The application must demonstrate the company's ability to comply with AML/CTF obligations.
  4. Regulatory review - SUGEF evaluates the application; may request additional information or clarifications. This stage can take 3-6 months.
  5. Approval - Once approved, the company is registered as a VASP and can commence operations. The registration is valid indefinitely but subject to annual renewal.
  6. Ongoing compliance - File periodic reports, maintain records, and undergo audits. Failure to comply can result in suspension or revocation.

Banking and Payments

Opening a bank account for a crypto business in Costa Rica can be challenging. Traditional banks are often reluctant to serve VASPs due to perceived risks. Some local banks may open accounts for registered entities, but expect enhanced due diligence. Alternative options include:

  • Using payment processors or fintechs that offer crypto-friendly accounts, such as those specializing in Latin American markets.
  • Opening an account in Panama (where banking is more accommodating) and operating remotely. Panama has several banks that accept crypto businesses with proper licensing.
  • Using stablecoin-based payment rails to reduce reliance on fiat banking, but this may complicate fiat on-ramps.

  • Ongoing transaction monitoring and suspicious activity reporting to the UAF.
  • Annual external audit of financial statements and AML compliance, conducted by a licensed auditor.

Does Costa Rica have a specific stablecoin license?

What activities are allowed under a Costa Rica VASP registration?

Allowed activities include stablecoin issuance and redemption, exchange, custody, payment processing, and OTC trading. Activities requiring a banking license, such as lending or deposit-taking, are not permitted. Stablecoin issuers must maintain full reserve backing.
