What Is a Crypto Gambling License in Switzerland?

A crypto gambling license in Switzerland refers to the authorization required to offer gambling services (such as online casino, poker, or sports betting) where players can deposit, wager, and withdraw using cryptocurrencies. The license itself is a gambling permit issued by the Swiss Federal Gambling Board (SFGB), but the crypto element adds extra regulatory layers.

Switzerland does not have a separate “crypto gambling” license. Instead, the gambling license covers the gaming activity, while any crypto-related services (e.g., exchange, custody) must comply with FINMA’s Anti-Money Laundering (AML) and financial market regulations. This dual oversight means founders must address both sets of requirements.

Key points:

  • Gambling license: issued by SFGB for online casinos, betting, etc.
  • Crypto compliance: FINMA registration for AML, plus possible licensing as a securities dealer or bank if holding customer funds.
  • No unified license: two separate authorizations are needed.
  • Game fairness certification: required by SFGB from accredited labs (e.g., GLI, BMM).

Who Needs This License?

Any entity that wants to legally offer gambling services to Swiss residents using cryptocurrencies needs this license. This includes:

  • Online casinos accepting Bitcoin, Ethereum, or other digital assets.
  • Sports betting platforms that allow crypto deposits and withdrawals.
  • Poker rooms operating with crypto tokens.
  • Fantasy sports or skill games that fall under gambling law and use crypto.
  • Lottery and instant win platforms integrating crypto payments.

If your platform is based outside Switzerland but targets Swiss players, you may still need a Swiss gambling license. The SFGB enforces strict territorial restrictions. For example, a startup with limited capital may find Panama’s EUR 6,000 flat fee and no minimum capital much more accessible.

License Type & Regulator

The gambling license in Switzerland is a single type: the “concession” for online casinos and betting. It is regulated by the Swiss Federal Gambling Board (SFGB), which is part of the Federal Department of Justice and Police. FINMA oversees the financial aspects, including AML for crypto transactions.

For crypto gambling, the operator must also register with FINMA as a financial intermediary under the Anti-Money Laundering Act (AMLA). If the platform holds customer crypto assets, a FINMA license as a securities dealer or bank may be required, depending on the volume and structure. For instance, a platform that holds customer funds in a custodial wallet would need a banking license, which is extremely difficult to obtain.

There is no “crypto-specific” gambling license in Switzerland. The approach is to combine a standard gambling concession with FINMA compliance for crypto activities. This dual structure is unique and adds complexity compared to jurisdictions like Lithuania, where a single CASP license covers crypto services.

Capital Requirement

Switzerland imposes capital requirements for gambling licenses. As of 2026, the minimum capital for an online casino concession is CHF 2 million (approximately EUR 2.1 million). This must be maintained as paid-up capital or a bank guarantee. For crypto-related activities, FINMA may require additional capital based on the volume of crypto assets held (e.g., for custody services, capital adequacy under Basel III principles may apply).

This is a significant barrier for startups. In contrast, Panama has no minimum capital requirement for its crypto license, making it more accessible for early-stage projects. For example, a small crypto casino with a budget under EUR 100,000 would find Switzerland impossible, whereas Panama is achievable.

Tax Treatment

Switzerland has a favorable tax regime for gambling winnings: players are not taxed on gambling gains (except for professional gamblers). For operators, gambling revenue is subject to corporate income tax at the cantonal and federal level, typically 12-18% effective rate depending on the canton. There is no specific gambling tax in Switzerland; instead, operators pay a levy to the SFGB (around 0.5-2% of gross gaming revenue) to fund addiction prevention and other programs.

Crypto transactions may be subject to VAT if considered a service, but crypto-to-crypto trades are generally exempt. Consulting24 advises on structuring to optimize tax efficiency. For comparison, Panama offers 0% tax on foreign-source income, which can be more attractive for operators targeting international players.

Allowed Activities

A Swiss gambling license permits the following activities:

  • Online casino games (slots, table games, live dealer).
  • Sports betting (fixed odds, live betting).
  • Poker tournaments and cash games.
  • Lotteries and instant win games.
  • Virtual sports and e-sports betting (if classified as gambling).

All games must be certified for fairness by an accredited testing lab (e.g., GLI, BMM). Crypto payments are allowed as a deposit/withdrawal method, but the operator must comply with FINMA’s AML rules, including KYC for all players. The license does not cover crypto exchange or custody services beyond what is necessary for gambling. If you want to offer standalone crypto exchange services, you would need a separate FINMA license.

  6. Banking setup: Open a corporate bank account with a crypto-friendly bank.
  7. Approval & launch: Once both licenses are granted, launch the platform. Ongoing reporting to SFGB and FINMA.

Banking & Payments

Swiss banks are generally crypto-friendly, but gambling operators face stricter scrutiny. Many traditional banks refuse to serve gambling businesses due to reputational risk. However, specialized fintech banks and crypto-friendly institutions (e.g., Sygnum, SEBA Bank) offer accounts for licensed operators. These banks often require a minimum deposit and charge higher fees.

For crypto payments, operators can integrate direct blockchain payments or use payment processors that handle conversion. The operator must ensure that all crypto transactions are AML-compliant, including transaction monitoring and suspicious activity reporting. Some payment gateways like Coinify or BitPay can facilitate crypto-to-fiat conversion, but they may charge 1-2% per transaction.

Compared to Panama, where banking is more flexible and there are no restrictions on crypto payments, Switzerland’s banking landscape is more challenging but still manageable with proper compliance.

Benefits of a Swiss Crypto Gambling License

Despite the high cost and complexity, a Swiss license offers unique advantages:

  • Reputation: Switzerland is a globally respected financial center. A Swiss license signals trust and compliance to players and partners.
  • Stability: Clear legal framework and political stability.
  • Tax efficiency: Low corporate tax rates and no gambling tax on winnings.
  • Access to Swiss market: Only licensed operators can target Swiss players legally.

  • Passporting: While not EU, Switzerland has bilateral agreements that may facilitate access to certain markets.

Compliance & Trust

Compliance is critical for maintaining a Swiss license. Operators must adhere to strict AML/KYC requirements, report suspicious transactions, and undergo regular audits by the SFGB and FINMA. Game fairness must be certified by approved labs. Data protection under the Swiss Federal Act on Data Protection (FADP) applies, which is similar to GDPR but with Swiss-specific nuances.

Failure to comply can result in fines, license revocation, or criminal charges. For example, an operator that fails to report a suspicious transaction could face penalties up to CHF 500,000.

Common Mistakes

Founders often make these errors when pursuing a Swiss crypto gambling license:

  • Underestimating capital: CHF 2 million minimum is a hard requirement. Many startups assume they can raise it later, but the capital must be available at application.
  • Ignoring FINMA: Focusing only on the gambling license and neglecting crypto AML registration leads to delays or rejection.
  • Incomplete game certification: Submitting uncertified games or using uncertified RNGs causes immediate rejection.
  • Poor AML policies: Not having a strong AML program tailored to crypto, including transaction monitoring for blockchain analytics.
  • Choosing the wrong jurisdiction: Switzerland may not be suitable for small startups; Panama or Estonia might be better alternatives.

Frequently asked questions

Is there a specific crypto gambling license in Switzerland?

No, Switzerland does not have a single crypto gambling license. You need a gambling concession from the SFGB and separate compliance with FINMA for crypto activities. This dual requirement adds complexity.

What is the minimum capital for a Swiss crypto gambling license?

The minimum capital for an online casino concession is CHF 2 million (approximately EUR 2.1 million). This must be paid-up or guaranteed. Additional capital may be required for crypto custody services.

How long does it take to get a crypto gambling license in Switzerland?

The process typically takes 8-18 months, depending on the complexity of your application and the completeness of your documentation. FINMA registration may take 2-4 months separately.

Can I use cryptocurrencies for deposits and withdrawals with a Swiss gambling license?

Yes, but you must comply with FINMA's AML rules, including KYC for all players. Crypto transactions must be monitored for suspicious activity, and you may need a FINMA license for custody.

What are the tax implications for a Swiss crypto gambling operator?

Operators pay corporate income tax at 12-18% effective rate depending on the canton. There is no specific gambling tax, but a levy of 0.5-2% of GGR is paid to the SFGB. Players are not taxed on winnings.

Is Switzerland a good jurisdiction for a small crypto gambling startup?

Generally no, due to the high capital requirement (CHF 2M) and long timeline. Startups with limited budgets often prefer Panama (EUR 6,000 flat) or Lithuania (EUR 125,000 capital) as more accessible alternatives.

Do I need a Swiss company to apply for a gambling license?

Yes, you must incorporate a Swiss company (AG or GmbH) with the required capital. The company must have a registered office in Switzerland and a local director in some cases.

What happens if I operate without a license in Switzerland?

Operating without a license is illegal and can result in criminal prosecution, fines, and seizure of assets. The SFGB actively monitors and blocks unlicensed gambling sites targeting Swiss players.

What are the ongoing compliance requirements after obtaining the license?
