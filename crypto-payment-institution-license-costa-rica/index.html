What Is the Crypto Payment Institution License Costa Rica?

The crypto payment institution license in Costa Rica is a regulatory authorization issued by SUGEF that allows companies to provide payment services involving virtual assets. It is part of the broader financial licensing regime under the Ley Orgánica del Banco Central de Costa Rica and related regulations. The license is designed for businesses that facilitate the exchange, transfer, and custody of cryptocurrencies, as well as issuing electronic money and payment instruments.

This license is not a full banking license but a specialized payment institution license tailored for digital assets. It requires compliance with anti-money laundering (AML) and counter-terrorism financing (CTF) obligations, as well as operational and capital requirements. The license is valid for an indefinite period, subject to ongoing supervision and annual renewal fees.

For crypto founders, this license offers a regulated pathway to operate in Costa Rica, a country with a stable legal system and a growing fintech ecosystem. It also provides access to the Central American market and can be a stepping stone for expansion into other Latin American jurisdictions.

License Type and Regulator

The regulatory authority for the crypto payment institution license in Costa Rica is the Superintendencia General de Entidades Financieras (SUGEF). SUGEF oversees all financial entities, including banks, savings and loan associations, and payment institutions. For crypto-related activities, SUGEF has issued specific guidelines under the framework for payment service providers.

The license is classified as a Payment Institution License with authorization to operate with virtual assets. It is not a separate crypto license but an extension of the existing payment institution regime. This means applicants must meet the same standards as traditional payment institutions, with additional requirements for crypto-specific risks such as cybersecurity and volatility.

There is no separate regulator for virtual assets in Costa Rica; SUGEF is the sole supervisor. This centralized oversight can simplify compliance, as you deal with one regulator. However, it also means that SUGEF expects a high level of operational maturity and AML compliance from applicants.

Capital Requirement

The minimum capital requirement for a crypto payment institution license in Costa Rica is USD 100,000 (approximately EUR 92,000 as of 2026). This capital must be fully paid up and maintained throughout the life of the license. The capital can be in the form of cash or liquid assets acceptable to SUGEF.

This requirement is lower than in many European jurisdictions, where capital can range from EUR 50,000 to 150,000 under MiCA. However, it is higher than in some offshore centers like the Cayman Islands. The capital serves as a buffer against operational risks and demonstrates financial stability to the regulator.

It is important to note that the capital must be held in a Costa Rican bank account or a regulated financial institution approved by SUGEF. Consulting24 can assist with opening a corporate bank account in Costa Rica as part of the setup process.

Tax Treatment

Costa Rica offers a favorable tax regime for crypto payment institutions. Corporate income tax is levied at a rate of 30% on net profits, but there are no specific taxes on cryptocurrency transactions or holdings. However, companies must comply with general tax rules, including value-added tax (VAT) on services and withholding taxes on dividends.

One key advantage is that Costa Rica does not impose capital gains tax on the sale of cryptocurrencies, as they are not classified as financial assets for tax purposes. This makes it attractive for trading and investment activities. Additionally, there are no stamp duties or transaction taxes on crypto transfers.

Allowed Activities

Under the crypto payment institution license, the following activities are permitted:

  • Exchange of virtual assets for fiat currency and vice versa
  • Transfer of virtual assets on behalf of clients
  • Custody and safekeeping of virtual assets
  • Issuance of electronic money and payment instruments backed by virtual assets
  • Payment initiation services using virtual assets

Activities that are not allowed include deposit-taking (banking), lending, and investment advice. These require separate licenses. The license is strictly for payment services, not for operating as a bank or securities firm.

Step-by-Step Application Process

The application process for a Costa Rica crypto payment institution license involves several stages:

  1. Pre-application consultation with Consulting24 to assess eligibility and prepare documentation.
  2. Company incorporation in Costa Rica, including registration with the Registro Nacional and obtaining a tax ID (cédula jurídica).
  3. Drafting of policies including AML/CTF program, business plan, risk management framework, and cybersecurity protocols.
  4. Submission of application to SUGEF with all required documents, including ownership structure, financial statements, and background checks on directors.
  5. Review and due diligence by SUGEF, which may take 3-6 months. The regulator may request additional information or clarifications.
  6. Approval and license issuance once SUGEF is satisfied. The license is typically granted for an indefinite period, subject to annual renewal.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Based on our experience, here are common pitfalls when applying for a Costa Rica crypto license:

  • Incomplete AML program that does not meet SUGEF standards. Ensure your program covers all required elements, including customer due diligence, transaction monitoring, and record-keeping.
  • Insufficient capital proof without clear source of funds. SUGEF will scrutinize the origin of capital, so be prepared with documentation.
  • Lack of local presence such as a registered office and local director. You must have a physical address in Costa Rica and at least one director who is a resident.
  • Underestimating timeline and failing to plan for delays. Start the process early and allow for contingencies.

Frequently asked questions

What is the minimum capital for a crypto payment institution license in Costa Rica?

The minimum capital requirement is USD 100,000 (approximately EUR 92,000). This must be fully paid up and maintained throughout the license period. Capital can be in cash or liquid assets acceptable to SUGEF.

How long does it take to get the license?

The typical timeline is 4 to 8 months from application submission to approval. This depends on the completeness of your application and SUGEF's review process. Delays can occur if additional information is requested.

What activities are allowed under this license?

Allowed activities include exchange of virtual assets for fiat, transfer of virtual assets, custody, issuance of electronic money backed by virtual assets, and payment initiation services. Deposit-taking and lending are not permitted.

Is there a separate crypto regulator in Costa Rica?

No, the Superintendencia General de Entidades Financieras (SUGEF) is the sole regulator for payment institutions, including those dealing with virtual assets. There is no dedicated crypto authority.

What are the tax implications for crypto businesses in Costa Rica?

Corporate income tax is 30% on net profits. There is no capital gains tax on cryptocurrency transactions, and no specific crypto taxes. VAT applies to services. It is advisable to consult a local tax advisor.

Can I use the Costa Rica license to serve clients in other countries?

Yes, the license allows you to serve international clients, but you must comply with local laws in the client's jurisdiction. The license is valid for operations from Costa Rica, not for cross-border marketing without proper registration.

Do I need a physical office in Costa Rica?

Yes, you must have a registered office in Costa Rica and at least one director who is a resident. This is a requirement for company incorporation and licensing.

How does the Costa Rica license compare to Panama?

Panama has a flat fee of EUR 6,000 and no specific crypto regulator, making it cheaper and faster (2-4 months). However, it offers less regulatory clarity. Costa Rica provides a regulated framework under SUGEF with higher capital requirements.
