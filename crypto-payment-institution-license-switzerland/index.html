What Is the Crypto Payment Institution License in Switzerland?

The crypto payment institution license is a regulatory authorization under Switzerland's Financial Institutions Act (FinIA), effective since 2020. It allows companies to offer payment services using crypto assets, such as accepting crypto payments, executing transfers, and issuing e-money backed by crypto or fiat. FINMA oversees this license, ensuring firms comply with strict anti-money laundering (AML) and know-your-customer (KYC) rules.

Unlike a full banking license, the payment institution license is lighter but still requires substantial capital and operational safeguards. It is designed for businesses that process payments rather than take deposits or lend. For example, a crypto exchange that also offers payment processing would need this license. The license covers activities like merchant acceptance of crypto, peer-to-peer transfers, and remittances.

Key features include:

  • Authorization to handle crypto-to-fiat and crypto-to-crypto payment transactions
  • Issuance of e-money and payment instruments
  • Client asset segregation requirements
  • Ongoing reporting to FINMA

Switzerland's approach is principles-based, meaning firms must demonstrate sound risk management. FINMA has published guidelines on how it treats tokens (payment, utility, asset) and applies AML rules to crypto transactions. This clarity is a major advantage for businesses planning long-term operations.

Who Needs a Swiss Crypto Payment Institution License?

Any business that provides payment services involving crypto assets in Switzerland or to Swiss residents typically needs this license. This includes:

  • Crypto payment processors for merchants
  • E-money issuers that use blockchain technology
  • Remittance services using crypto
  • Digital wallet providers with payment functionality
  • Companies offering crypto-to-fiat conversion as part of payment services

If your company only trades crypto assets (e.g., a crypto exchange) without offering payment services, you may need a different license, such as a securities dealer or asset manager license. The payment institution license is strictly for payment transactions. Consulting24 can help you determine the correct license type for your business model. For example, a startup building a crypto debit card would likely need this license, while a pure exchange would not.

License Type & Regulator: FINMA

The regulator is the Swiss Financial Market Supervisory Authority (FINMA), based in Bern. FINMA classifies payment institutions under the Financial Institutions Act (FinIA) and the Anti-Money Laundering Act (AMLA). The license is officially called a "payment institution license" and covers activities defined in the FinIA.

For crypto-specific activities, FINMA applies its existing framework to blockchain-based assets. The regulator has issued guidelines on how it treats tokens (payment, utility, asset) and applies AML rules to crypto transactions. The license requires a minimum capital of CHF 100,000 (approximately EUR 100,000 as of 2026) for the basic payment institution license, but higher if your transaction volume exceeds certain thresholds.

FINMA's approach is principles-based, meaning firms must demonstrate sound risk management and compliance. The regulator conducts regular audits and can impose sanctions for non-compliance. Compared to EU jurisdictions under MiCA, Switzerland's framework is more flexible but requires a higher capital commitment. For a comparison with EU options, see our comparison with Lithuania.

Capital Requirement

The minimum capital for a crypto payment institution license in Switzerland is CHF 100,000 (approximately EUR 100,000). This capital must be fully paid up in cash and maintained at all times. If your firm's annual payment transaction volume exceeds CHF 100 million, FINMA may require additional capital up to CHF 500,000.

The capital serves as a buffer for operational risks and client asset protection. It must be held in a Swiss bank account or in highly liquid assets. Unlike some EU jurisdictions, Switzerland does not have tiered capital based on service classes; the requirement is flat for most payment institutions. This is a significant commitment compared to Panama, where there is no minimum capital requirement for the crypto license.

Tax Treatment for Crypto Payment Institutions in Switzerland

Switzerland offers a favorable tax environment for crypto businesses. Corporate income tax rates vary by canton, ranging from 11% to 21% (effective rate). The federal corporate tax rate is 8.5% (effective ~12-13% total with cantonal tax).

Value-added tax (VAT) is not charged on crypto-to-fiat exchange services, but applies to other services like wallet fees. Crypto assets held for trading are treated as current assets and taxed accordingly. Capital gains from crypto trading are generally tax-free for companies, but income from mining or staking is taxable. Switzerland has no withholding tax on dividends paid to foreign shareholders.

Consulting24 recommends a tax structuring review to optimize your position. For comparison, Panama offers territorial taxation with 0% on foreign-source income, which can be more tax-efficient for international operations. Many crypto founders choose Panama for its tax neutrality.

Allowed Activities Under the License

Holders of a Swiss crypto payment institution license can perform the following activities:

  • Acceptance of crypto payments for merchants
  • Execution of payment transactions in crypto and fiat
  • Issuance of e-money (including stablecoins)
  • Provision of digital wallets with payment functionality
  • Remittance services using crypto
  • Conversion of crypto to fiat and vice versa as part of payment services

Activities that are NOT allowed include taking deposits (requires a banking license), lending crypto assets (requires a securities dealer license), or offering investment advice (requires asset manager license). The license is strictly for payment services. If your business model includes trading on own account, you may need a different authorization.

Step-by-Step Application Process

The application process for a Swiss crypto payment institution license involves several stages:

  1. Pre-assessment: Consulting24 reviews your business model and prepares a gap analysis against FINMA requirements.
  2. Documentation: You prepare a business plan, AML/KYC policies, risk management framework, organizational documents, and financial projections.
  3. Capital deposit: You open a Swiss bank account and deposit the minimum capital of CHF 100,000.
  4. Submission: FINMA receives the application package and charges the fee (CHF 5,000-15,000).
  5. Review: FINMA examines the application, often requesting additional information or clarifications. This stage typically takes 3-6 months.
  6. Approval: If compliant, FINMA grants the license. The total timeline is 6-12 months.

Opening a bank account in Switzerland for a crypto payment institution can be challenging due to banks' risk appetite. Many traditional Swiss banks are cautious with crypto firms. However, there are specialized banks and fintech-friendly institutions that work with licensed entities, such as Sygnum, SEBA Bank, and some cantonal banks.

Consulting24 can introduce you to partner banks that accept crypto clients. Typically, you need a corporate account for operational funds and a segregated client account for safeguarding client assets. Some firms also use third-party payment processors for fiat on/off ramps. The process can take 1-3 months and requires a thorough due diligence.

For a simpler banking environment, consider Panama, where banking is more accessible for crypto businesses and no minimum capital is required.

Compliance & Trust: Ongoing Obligations

After obtaining the license, you must maintain ongoing compliance:

  • Annual audit by an external auditor approved by FINMA
  • Regular AML/KYC reviews and transaction monitoring
  • Reporting of suspicious transactions to the Money Laundering Reporting Office Switzerland (MROS)
  • Capital maintenance at all times (CHF 100,000 minimum)
  • Notification of any material changes to business operations or ownership
  • Submission of annual financial statements and regulatory reports

Failure to comply can result in fines, license suspension, or revocation. Consulting24 offers compliance support to ensure you meet these obligations. This is general guidance, not legal advice. For a lighter compliance burden, explore Panama or Estonia.

Common Mistakes When Applying for a Swiss Crypto License

Avoid these pitfalls:

  • Insufficient capital: Ensure you have the full CHF 100,000 available in cash and can prove the source of funds.
  • Weak AML policies: FINMA requires strong procedures; generic templates are rejected. You need custom policies for your business model.
  • Underestimating timeline: Plan for 6-12 months; do not commit to clients prematurely.
  • Choosing the wrong license type: Some firms apply for a payment institution license when they need a securities dealer license (e.g., if they offer crypto lending).
  • Ignoring tax structuring: Switzerland's cantonal tax differences can significantly affect your effective rate. Optimize your canton choice.
  • Poor documentation: Incomplete business plans or missing risk assessments cause delays.

What is the minimum capital for a Swiss crypto payment institution license?

The minimum capital is CHF 100,000 (approximately EUR 100,000). This must be fully paid up in cash and maintained at all times. If annual transaction volume exceeds CHF 100 million, additional capital up to CHF 500,000 may be required.

How long does it take to get a Swiss crypto payment institution license?

The typical timeline is 6 to 12 months from application submission to approval. The process includes documentation preparation, FINMA review, and potential requests for additional information. Consulting24 can help streamline the process.

Can I use a Swiss crypto license to serve EU customers?

Switzerland is not an EU member, so the license does not automatically passport into the EU. However, Swiss licenses are recognized by some EU countries under bilateral agreements. For EU-wide access, consider a MiCA license in Lithuania or Estonia.

What activities are not allowed under a Swiss payment institution license?

The license does not permit taking deposits (requires banking license), lending crypto assets (securities dealer license), or investment advice (asset manager license). It is strictly for payment services.

Is Switzerland's tax regime favorable for crypto payment institutions?

Yes, Switzerland offers low corporate tax rates (11-21% effective, depending on canton), no VAT on crypto-to-fiat exchange, and no withholding tax on dividends. However, Panama's territorial tax system (0% on foreign income) can be more advantageous for international operations.

Do I need to have a physical office in Switzerland?

Yes, FINMA requires a registered office and substance in Switzerland. You need a local director or manager and adequate premises. Consulting24 can help arrange these requirements.
