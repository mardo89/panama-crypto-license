A Poland crypto license allows you to operate across the entire European Economic Area (EEA) with a single authorisation, making it an attractive alternative to more expensive or slower EU hubs.

What Is a Poland Crypto License?

The Poland crypto license is a MiCA CASP authorisation granted by the KNF. It replaced the previous registration-based system for virtual currency activities, aligning fully with the EU's harmonised framework. Under MiCA, a CASP licence covers services such as exchanging crypto for fiat or other crypto, operating trading platforms, custody and administration of crypto-assets, and transfer services.

Key features of the Polish regime:

  • Regulator: Komisja Nadzoru Finansowego (KNF)
  • Legal basis: MiCA Regulation (EU) 2023/1114, transposed into Polish law
  • Passporting: Full EEA passport - one licence, 27+ markets
  • Capital requirements: Tiered: €50,000 (simple services), €125,000 (exchanges/custody), €150,000 (complex services)
  • Timeline: Typically 4-8 months from application to approval

Poland’s approach is considered pragmatic and business-friendly, with clear guidelines and a reasonable cost structure compared to jurisdictions like Malta or Switzerland. The KNF has published a detailed application handbook, which we help you interpret and comply with. For example, the regulator expects a strong business plan that demonstrates operational readiness, not just a theoretical model.

Who Needs a Poland Crypto License?

Any entity that provides crypto-asset services to clients in the EU must hold a MiCA CASP licence. Specifically, you need a Poland crypto license if you are:

  • Exchange operators - offering crypto-to-fiat or crypto-to-crypto trading
  • Custodians - holding or administering clients’ crypto-assets
  • Platform operators - running a trading venue for crypto-assets
  • Transfer service providers - moving crypto between addresses on behalf of clients
  • DeFi protocols - if they have a centralised operator or provide services in a regulated manner

Poland is particularly suitable for startups and scale-ups that want a cost-efficient EU entry point without sacrificing regulatory credibility. The KNF is known for being responsive and transparent, making the process smoother than in some other member states. For example, a mid-sized exchange with custody services would fall under the €125,000 capital tier and can expect a 6-month timeline if documentation is complete.

License Type & Regulator

The Poland crypto license is a single MiCA CASP authorisation. Unlike the old system where firms simply registered as a “virtual currency activity,” the new licence is a full authorisation with ongoing supervision. The regulator is the Polish Financial Supervision Authority (KNF), which also oversees banking, insurance, and capital markets.

Key regulatory features:

  • Application: Detailed business plan, AML/CFT policies, governance, and capital proof
  • Ongoing compliance: Regular reporting, capital maintenance, and AML audits
  • Sanctions: The KNF can impose fines, suspend licences, or revoke authorisation for non-compliance

The KNF has published official guidelines for CASP applicants, which we help you navigate. For an overview of other EU options, see our comparison with Lithuania. Unlike the pre-MiCA register, the KNF now conducts thorough background checks on beneficial owners and management, including criminal record checks and professional references.

Capital Requirement

Under MiCA, Poland applies the standard EU capital tiers based on the type of services offered:

  • €50,000 - for firms providing only one or more of: reception and transmission of orders, execution of orders on behalf of clients, or investment advice
  • €125,000 - for firms operating a trading platform, exchanging crypto for fiat, or providing custody and administration
  • €150,000 - for firms that combine multiple high-risk services (e.g., exchange + custody + own trading)

The capital must be held in a Polish bank account and maintained at all times. It can be used for operational expenses but must not fall below the threshold. The KNF may require additional capital if the business volume or risk profile increases. For instance, if your trading volume exceeds €5 million monthly, the regulator might demand a capital add-on of up to 25% of the minimum.

Tax Treatment

Poland offers a relatively favourable tax environment for crypto businesses, though it is not a tax haven. Key points:

  • Corporate income tax (CIT): Standard rate of 19% (9% for small taxpayers with revenue below €2 million)
  • VAT: Crypto-to-fiat exchanges are exempt from VAT; crypto-to-crypto is also generally exempt
  • Withholding tax: 0% on dividends paid to EU parent companies (subject to conditions)
  • Thin capitalisation rules: Apply, but generous

Poland has a wide network of double tax treaties. For specific tax planning, we recommend engaging a local tax advisor. For comparison, see our Czech Republic guide. Note that Poland does not offer specific crypto tax incentives, but the standard CIT rate is competitive within the EU.

Allowed Activities

A Poland crypto license permits the following activities under MiCA:

  • Exchange services: Crypto-to-fiat and crypto-to-crypto
  • Operation of a trading platform
  • Custody and administration of crypto-assets on behalf of clients
  • Reception and transmission of orders
  • Execution of orders on behalf of clients
  • Placement of crypto-assets
  • Transfer services for crypto-assets
  • Advice on crypto-assets

Activities not covered include issuing asset-referenced tokens (ARTs) or e-money tokens (EMTs), which require separate authorisation under MiCA. Stablecoin issuance is regulated under Title III and IV of MiCA. If your business model includes both CASP and stablecoin activities, you will need dual authorisation, which we can coordinate.

Step-by-Step Process

Our advisory and coordination process for a Poland crypto license involves the following stages:

  1. Initial consultation: We assess your business model, services, and eligibility.
  2. Company setup: Incorporate a Polish limited liability company (Sp. z o.o.) with appropriate share capital.
  3. Documentation: Prepare business plan, AML/CFT policies, risk assessment, governance framework, and operational procedures.
  4. Capital deposit: Open a Polish bank account and deposit the required minimum capital.
  5. Application submission: File the application with KNF, including all supporting documents.
  6. KNF review: The regulator may request additional information or clarifications; we handle responses.
  7. Approval: Once satisfied, KNF grants the CASP licence. You can then start operations.
  8. Post-licence compliance: Ongoing reporting, AML audits, and capital maintenance.

For a detailed breakdown, see our application process guide. Each stage typically takes 2-4 weeks, with the KNF review being the longest phase (2-4 months). We provide a project timeline with milestones to keep you informed.

Compliance & Trust

Maintaining a Poland crypto license requires ongoing compliance. Key obligations include:

  • AML/CFT: Implement strong KYC procedures, transaction monitoring, and suspicious activity reporting
  • Capital maintenance: Keep minimum capital at all times; report any shortfall
  • Annual reports: Submit audited financial statements and compliance reports to KNF
  • Governance: Ensure fit-and-proper management, board oversight, and internal controls

Non-compliance can result in fines up to €5 million or 5% of annual turnover, licence suspension, or revocation. We provide ongoing compliance support to help you meet these obligations. This is general guidance, not legal advice. For tailored advice, consult a qualified legal professional.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Firms often make avoidable errors when applying for a Poland crypto license. Common pitfalls include:

  • Incomplete business plan: The KNF expects detailed financial projections, operational workflows, and risk assessments. A generic plan will be rejected.
  • Insufficient AML policies: Your AML/CFT manual must cover transaction monitoring, sanctions screening, and suspicious activity reporting procedures tailored to your services.
  • Underestimating capital requirements: Ensure you have the correct tier and that funds are liquid and held in a Polish bank account.
  • Poor governance: All directors and beneficial owners must pass fit-and-proper checks; any criminal record or financial irregularities can derail the application.
  • Ignoring ongoing compliance: After approval, you must submit regular reports and maintain capital. Neglecting this can lead to licence revocation.

We help you avoid these mistakes by providing a comprehensive checklist and reviewing all documents before submission.

Frequently asked questions

What is the difference between the old Polish crypto register and the new MiCA CASP licence?

The old system was a simple registration for virtual currency activities with no minimum capital and limited oversight. The new MiCA CASP licence is a full authorisation with tiered capital requirements (€50k-€150k), comprehensive AML/CFT obligations, and ongoing supervision by the KNF. It also allows passporting across the EEA.

How long does it take to get a Poland crypto license in 2026?

Typically 4-8 months from application submission to approval. The timeline depends on the complexity of your services, completeness of documentation, and KNF workload. Simple exchange-only applications may be approved in 4 months, while complex custody and trading platforms can take up to 8 months.

Can I use a Poland crypto license to serve clients in other EU countries?

Yes, a MiCA CASP licence from Poland allows you to passport your services across all EEA member states (EU plus Iceland, Liechtenstein, Norway) without needing additional licences. You simply notify the KNF of your intent to operate in other countries.

What are the minimum capital requirements for a Poland crypto license?

Under MiCA, capital tiers are: €50,000 for simple services (e.g., order reception), €125,000 for exchanges and custody, and €150,000 for combined high-risk services. The capital must be held in a Polish bank account and maintained at all times.

Is Poland a tax-friendly jurisdiction for crypto businesses?

Poland offers a standard CIT rate of 19% (9% for small firms) and VAT exemptions for crypto-to-fiat and crypto-to-crypto exchanges. It has a wide tax treaty network. However, it is not a tax haven; you should consult a local tax advisor for your specific situation.

Can I apply for a Poland crypto license if my company is already registered in another EU country?

Yes, but you would need to establish a Polish entity (Sp. z o.o.) and apply through the KNF. If you already hold a MiCA licence from another EU member state, you can passport into Poland without a separate Polish licence. However, if you want Poland as your home regulator, you must incorporate there.

What documents are required for the KNF application?

You need a detailed business plan, AML/CFT policies, risk assessment, governance framework, financial projections, proof of capital, and personal questionnaires for directors and beneficial owners. All documents must be in Polish or officially translated. We help you compile and submit these.

Does the KNF require a physical office in Poland?

Yes, you need a registered office address in Poland. It can be a virtual office or co-working space, but it must be a physical location where regulatory correspondence can be received. The KNF may conduct on-site inspections.

What happens if I fail to maintain the minimum capital after receiving the licence?

The KNF can impose fines, suspend your licence, or revoke it. You must report any capital shortfall immediately and restore the required amount within a specified period. We recommend setting up monitoring systems to avoid this.

Can Consulting24 guarantee approval of my Poland crypto license?

No, we cannot guarantee approval. The KNF makes the final decision based on your application. However, we have a strong track record of successful applications by ensuring all documents are complete and compliant. Our advisory and coordination services significantly reduce the risk of rejection.

How does Poland compare to Panama for crypto licensing?

Panama offers a non-EU license with no capital requirement and a flat fee of €6,000, but no passporting. Poland is EU-regulated with capital requirements and higher costs, but allows EEA-wide operations. Choose Panama if your target market is outside the EU; choose Poland for EU access.
