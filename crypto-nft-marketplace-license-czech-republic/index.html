What Is a Crypto NFT Marketplace License in the Czech Republic?

A crypto NFT marketplace license in the Czech Republic is an authorization required to operate a platform where users can buy, sell, and trade non-fungible tokens (NFTs) using cryptocurrencies. Under MiCA, this activity falls under the provision of crypto asset services, specifically operating a trading platform for crypto assets. The license is issued by the Czech National Bank (CNB) and aligns with the EU-wide regulatory framework, ensuring a high standard of investor protection and market integrity.

The license covers the operation of an NFT marketplace that facilitates the exchange of crypto assets (including NFTs) for fiat currency or other crypto assets. It also includes custody and administration of crypto assets on behalf of clients, which is common for marketplaces that hold NFTs in escrow during transactions. As of 2026, the Czech Republic has fully transposed MiCA into national law, replacing its previous registration regime with a more comprehensive licensing system.

Who Needs a Crypto NFT Marketplace License in the Czech Republic?

Any legal entity that operates a platform for trading NFTs against fiat or other crypto assets needs a license. This includes:

  • NFT marketplaces that accept cryptocurrency payments and facilitate peer-to-peer trading.
  • Platforms that offer primary sales of NFTs (minting and selling) where the platform acts as an intermediary.
  • Businesses that provide custody services for NFTs, such as holding private keys for users.
  • Marketplaces that offer staking or yield generation on NFTs.

If your platform only provides information or a listing service without handling any crypto assets or fiat currency, you may not need a license. However, most NFT marketplaces that process transactions will fall under the scope of MiCA and require authorization. License Type and Regulator

The relevant regulator is the Czech National Bank (CNB), which acts as the competent authority for crypto asset service providers under MiCA. The license type is a CASP (Crypto Asset Service Provider) license, which replaces the previous registration regime that was in place before MiCA. The CNB issues licenses for specific service categories, including operating a trading platform (which covers NFT marketplaces), custody and administration, and exchange services.

The CNB is known for its thorough but fair assessment process. Applicants must demonstrate strong AML/CFT controls, proper governance, and adequate capital. The CNB also requires that the entity have a physical presence in the Czech Republic, including a registered office and at least one director or manager residing in the country. Capital Requirement for a Czech Crypto License

Under MiCA, the minimum capital requirement for a CASP license depends on the types of services provided. For operating a trading platform (NFT marketplace), the capital requirement is EUR 150,000. This is one of the higher tiers under MiCA, reflecting the risk associated with operating a platform that handles multiple assets and facilitates trading.

The capital must be held in liquid assets and maintained at all times. It can be in the form of cash, bank guarantees, or highly liquid securities. The CNB may require additional capital if the business model involves higher risks, such as offering margin trading or complex financial products. Tax Treatment for Crypto NFT Marketplaces in the Czech Republic

The Czech Republic has a favorable tax regime for crypto businesses. As of 2026, corporate income tax is a flat 19% on profits. Capital gains from crypto assets are treated as income and subject to the same rate. VAT is not applicable to crypto-to-crypto transactions, but fees charged by the marketplace (e.g., listing fees, transaction fees) are subject to standard VAT at 21%.

For individuals, crypto gains are tax-free if held for more than three years and the total annual income from crypto does not exceed CZK 100,000 (approx. EUR 4,000). However, for businesses, all gains are taxable. The Czech Republic also has double taxation treaties with many countries, which can reduce withholding taxes on dividends and interest. Allowed Activities Under a Czech Crypto NFT Marketplace License

A CASP license in the Czech Republic permits the following activities related to NFTs and crypto assets:

  • Operation of a trading platform for crypto assets, including NFTs.
  • Exchange of crypto assets for fiat currency or other crypto assets.
  • Custody and administration of crypto assets on behalf of clients.
  • Execution of orders on behalf of clients.
  • Placement of crypto assets (e.g., initial NFT offerings).
  • Reception and transmission of orders for crypto assets.

Activities that are not allowed without additional licenses include providing banking services (e.g., lending fiat), issuing securities (unless under a prospectus), or offering derivatives. The license is specific to crypto assets and does not cover traditional financial instruments. Consulting24 advises clients to clearly define their service scope to avoid regulatory breaches.

Step-by-Step Process to Get a Czech Crypto NFT Marketplace License

The application process involves several stages:

  1. Pre-application preparation: Define the business model, prepare AML/KYC policies, and draft internal procedures. Engage a local legal advisor.
  2. Company incorporation: Set up a Czech limited liability company (s.r.o.) with a registered office and at least one director resident in the Czech Republic.
  3. Capital deposit: Deposit the minimum capital of EUR 150,000 into a Czech bank account and obtain a confirmation from the bank.
  4. Application submission: Submit the application to the CNB, including business plan, AML policies, organizational structure, and financial projections.
  5. CNB review: The CNB reviews the application, which may take 3-6 months. They may request additional information or clarifications.
  6. License issuance: If approved, the CNB issues the CASP license. The company must then comply with ongoing reporting and audit requirements.

Common Mistakes When Applying for a Czech Crypto License

Many applicants make avoidable errors that delay or derail their application:

  • Incomplete AML documentation: The CNB expects detailed AML policies tailored to the business model.
  • Insufficient capital proof: The capital must be fully deposited and available at the time of application.
  • Lack of local presence: The company must have a physical office and at least one local director.
  • Unclear business model: The CNB needs a clear description of how the platform operates, including fee structures and custody arrangements.
  • Ignoring MiCA requirements: Some applicants still rely on pre-MiCA rules, which are no longer valid.
