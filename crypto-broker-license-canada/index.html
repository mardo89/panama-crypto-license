What Is a Crypto Broker License in Canada?

A crypto broker license in Canada refers to the registration and compliance framework required to operate a virtual currency exchange or brokerage. Unlike a single license, Canada uses a dual regulatory approach: federal registration with FINTRAC as an MSB, and provincial securities registration with regulators like the Canadian Securities Administrators (CSA) or the Ontario Securities Commission (OSC).

Under the Proceeds of Crime (Money Laundering) and Terrorist Financing Act (PCMLTFA), crypto brokers must register with FINTRAC, implement AML/ATF programs, and report suspicious transactions. Provincially, crypto brokers are considered securities dealers under the CSA's Staff Notice 46-307, requiring registration as a restricted dealer or marketplace operator. This hybrid system ensures strong oversight of digital asset trading.

For example, a platform offering trading in Bitcoin and Ethereum must register with FINTRAC and also with the securities regulator in each province where it operates. The OSC has been particularly active, requiring platforms like Binance to cease operations in Ontario unless fully registered. As of 2026, the CSA's proposed regulations aim to harmonize requirements across provinces, but differences remain. Consulting24 can help you navigate these variations and ensure full compliance.

Who Needs a Crypto Broker License in Canada?

Any business that facilitates the exchange of virtual currency for fiat currency, or vice versa, or transfers virtual currency, must register. This includes:

  • Centralized exchanges (CEXs) offering spot trading pairs
  • Over-the-counter (OTC) desks
  • Bitcoin ATMs and kiosk operators
  • Brokerage platforms that match buyers and sellers
  • Payment processors handling crypto transactions

Exemptions exist for businesses dealing solely in utility tokens or non-fungible tokens (NFTs) that do not qualify as securities. However, most commercial crypto activities fall under the MSB definition. Consulting24 can assess your business model and advise on registration obligations. For instance, a crypto ATM operator must register with FINTRAC and may also need provincial registration if the ATM allows trades in securities tokens. Even a small OTC desk with monthly volumes under CAD 50,000 is not exempt from FINTRAC registration. The threshold for MSB registration is low, so most crypto businesses will need to comply.

License Type & Regulator

The primary regulators are:

  • FINTRAC - Federal agency overseeing AML/ATF compliance for MSBs. Registration is mandatory for all crypto brokers.
  • Canadian Securities Administrators (CSA) - Provincial securities regulators. Crypto brokers dealing in securities (e.g., tokens deemed securities) must register as a restricted dealer or marketplace.
  • Ontario Securities Commission (OSC) - Ontario's regulator, which has been particularly active in enforcing compliance, including against Binance and Bybit.

As of 2026, the CSA requires crypto trading platforms to register and comply with enhanced investor protection rules. The registration type is typically "Restricted Dealer" or "Marketplace Operator." For example, a platform offering trading in tokens that are considered securities must register as a restricted dealer with the OSC and other provincial regulators. Consulting24 can help determine which registration type applies to your business. The distinction matters: a restricted dealer has fewer obligations than a full marketplace operator, but cannot hold client assets in the same way. We guide you to the most appropriate classification based on your business model.

Capital Requirement

Canada does not have a statutory minimum capital for FINTRAC MSB registration. However, provincial securities regulators impose capital adequacy requirements. For restricted dealers, the OSC requires minimum capital of CAD 100,000 (or higher, depending on the business volume). Additionally, platforms must maintain liquid assets equal to at least 100% of client funds held.

As of 2026, the CSA's proposed rules may introduce higher capital thresholds for platforms offering margin or derivatives. Consulting24 recommends budgeting at least CAD 200,000 in operational capital to cover compliance costs and potential capital requirements. For example, a platform with CAD 5 million in client funds must hold CAD 5 million in liquid assets, which can be a significant burden for startups. This requirement is designed to protect clients in case of insolvency. We advise clients to maintain a capital buffer above the minimum to ensure smooth operations and regulatory compliance.

Tax Treatment

Cryptocurrency transactions in Canada are subject to income tax and GST/HST. Key points:

  • Income tax: Crypto gains are treated as business income or capital gains, depending on the activity. Brokers must report all transactions.
  • GST/HST: Brokering fees are generally exempt from GST/HST, but the underlying crypto trades may be subject to tax.
  • Corporate tax rate: Federal + provincial rates range from 11% to 16%, depending on the province.

Consulting24 advises clients to work with a Canadian tax specialist to ensure compliance with Canada Revenue Agency (CRA) guidelines. For example, a broker earning CAD 1 million in fees may pay around CAD 150,000 in corporate tax, plus GST/HST on certain services. The CRA has issued guidance on crypto taxation, but the rules can be complex. We recommend keeping detailed records of all transactions and consulting a tax professional to avoid penalties. For businesses looking for a simpler tax regime, Panama offers territorial taxation with no tax on foreign-source income, making it an attractive alternative for certain business models.

Allowed Activities

A registered crypto broker in Canada can perform the following activities:

  • Buying and selling virtual currency for fiat (CAD, USD, etc.)
  • Exchanging one virtual currency for another
  • Operating a trading platform (order book or OTC)
  • Providing custody services for client assets
  • Facilitating peer-to-peer transfers

Activities such as margin trading, staking, or lending may require additional registration as a derivatives dealer or investment fund manager. The CSA has strict rules on offering "securities" tokens. For instance, a platform offering staking rewards may need to register as an investment fund manager if the staking pool is considered a security. Step-by-Step Process

  1. Business structure: Incorporate a Canadian entity (federal or provincial). Choose a structure that allows for future expansion.
  2. AML program: Develop a compliant AML/ATF program, including risk assessment, policies, and training. This must be tailored to your specific business.
  3. FINTRAC registration: Submit the MSB registration application with required documents. This includes details on your business, ownership, and AML program.
  4. Provincial registration: File with the relevant securities commission (e.g., OSC) as a restricted dealer. This involves a detailed application and review.
  5. Compliance review: Undergo a compliance audit and implement any required changes. Regulators may request modifications to your AML program or operational procedures.
  6. Operational launch: After approvals, begin operations with ongoing reporting. This includes quarterly FINTRAC reports and annual provincial filings.

What is the difference between FINTRAC registration and provincial securities registration?
