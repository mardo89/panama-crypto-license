What Is a Crypto Broker License in Costa Rica?

Costa Rica does not issue a specific "crypto broker license." Instead, crypto brokerage activities fall under general business registration and AML supervision. The main regulator is the Superintendencia General de Entidades Financieras (SUGEF), which oversees financial entities, and the Unidad de Inteligencia Financiera (UIF) for AML compliance. Crypto brokers typically register as a Sociedad Anónima (S.A.) or a Limited Liability Company (SRL) and must comply with Law 8204 on narcotrafficking and related money laundering, which applies to all financial intermediaries.

In practice, this means you can operate a crypto brokerage legally by registering your company, opening a bank account, and implementing AML/KYC procedures. There is no minimum capital requirement set by law for crypto brokers, but banks and payment providers may impose their own requirements. As of 2026, the Central Bank of Costa Rica has not issued specific regulations for crypto assets, but the government has signaled a willingness to formalize the sector. However, this lack of specificity can be a double-edged sword: it provides flexibility but also creates uncertainty for founders seeking clear regulatory guidance.

For comparison, in Panama, Panama offers a company setup with an AML/CTF programme at a flat fee of EUR 6,000, which provides a clear legal status. In the EU, a CASP license under MiCA requires capital of EUR 50,000 to EUR 150,000 depending on services. Costa Rica's approach is best suited for founders who prioritize low initial costs and are comfortable with a less defined regulatory environment.

Who Needs This License?

Any business that facilitates the exchange of cryptocurrencies for fiat or other crypto assets, acts as a broker, or provides custody services may need to register in Costa Rica. This includes:

  • Cryptocurrency exchanges (centralized and peer-to-peer)
  • OTC desks
  • Brokerage platforms offering trading services
  • Custodial wallet providers

If your target market is Latin America, Costa Rica offers a stable political environment and a growing crypto community. However, many founders choose Costa Rica as a base for regional operations, while also obtaining a more formal license in jurisdictions like Estonia or Lithuania for EU access. For example, a broker might incorporate in Costa Rica for Latin American clients but also hold a Lithuanian CASP license to serve European customers under MiCA. This dual approach is common among our clients.

License Type and Regulator

In Costa Rica, crypto brokerage is not a regulated financial activity per se, but the business must comply with AML regulations under the supervision of SUGEF and the UIF. The registration process involves:

  • Incorporating a Costa Rican company (S.A. or SRL)
  • Registering with the Registro Nacional
  • Obtaining a tax identification number (Cédula Jurídica)
  • Filing a declaration of beneficial ownership
  • Implementing an AML program and appointing a compliance officer

There is no application fee for a license, but company registration costs and legal fees apply. The regulator does not issue a formal license; instead, the company operates under general commercial law with AML obligations. This means you cannot point to a specific license document to reassure partners or banks. In contrast, Panama provides a formal license certificate, which can be critical for banking relationships.

Capital Requirement

Costa Rica does not impose a statutory minimum capital for crypto brokerage companies. However, banks and payment processors may require a minimum deposit or capital to open an account, typically between $10,000 and $50,000. Additionally, to demonstrate financial stability to clients and partners, a working capital of $20,000 - $50,000 is advisable. This contrasts with EU jurisdictions under MiCA, where capital requirements range from EUR 50,000 to EUR 150,000 depending on services offered. For example, a broker offering custody services in Lithuania needs at least EUR 125,000. In Costa Rica, the absence of a legal minimum can be an advantage for startups, but it also means you must self-fund adequate capital to build trust.

Tax Treatment

Costa Rica taxes corporate income at a flat rate of 30% on net profits. However, there is no specific crypto tax law; the tax treatment of crypto transactions follows general income tax rules. Capital gains from crypto trading are considered ordinary income and taxed accordingly. VAT (13%) applies to services provided in Costa Rica, but crypto-to-fiat exchanges may be exempt if structured properly. Transfer pricing rules apply if you have related parties abroad. For a more tax-efficient structure, many founders compare Costa Rica with Panama, which has no capital gains tax and a territorial tax system (only income sourced in Panama is taxed). Panama also offers a flat EUR 6,000 license fee, making it attractive for offshore operations. Consulting24 can help you model the tax implications for your specific business.

Allowed Activities

Under current Costa Rican law, a registered company can engage in the following crypto-related activities:

  • Exchange of cryptocurrencies for fiat currency
  • Cryptocurrency-to-cryptocurrency trading
  • OTC brokerage
  • Wallet services (custodial and non-custodial)
  • Payment processing using crypto
  • Mining and staking (if registered appropriately)

However, activities that involve taking deposits or offering interest-bearing accounts may be considered banking and require a separate license. The lack of specific regulation provides flexibility but also uncertainty; it is advisable to seek legal advice before launching. For example, if you plan to offer staking rewards, you must ensure it does not constitute a security under local law. This is general guidance, not legal advice.

Step-by-Step Process

  1. Choose a company structure: S.A. or SRL. S.A. is more common for larger operations.
  2. Reserve a company name with the Registro Nacional.
  3. Draft and notarize incorporation documents (escritura constitutiva).
  4. Register with the Tax Authority (Hacienda) to obtain the Cédula Jurídica.
  5. Open a corporate bank account (this is challenging for crypto businesses; expect a thorough due diligence process).
  6. Implement AML/KYC policies and appoint a compliance officer.
  7. Register with the UIF as a financial intermediary (if required).
  8. Obtain a digital signature for online filings.
  9. Begin operations after confirming compliance with all legal requirements.

Banking and Payments

Banking is one of the biggest challenges for crypto brokers in Costa Rica. Most traditional banks are reluctant to work with crypto businesses due to perceived risks. Options include:

  • Local banks (e.g., Banco Nacional, BAC Credomatic) with strict due diligence
  • International payment processors like Payoneer or Wise (limited)
  • Cryptocurrency-friendly banks in other jurisdictions (e.g., Lithuania or Panama)

Some founders choose to incorporate in Costa Rica but maintain banking in Panama, which has a more developed crypto-friendly banking sector. Consulting24 can advise on the best banking strategy for your business. For example, we often recommend opening a bank account in Panama alongside a Costa Rican company to facilitate fiat on/off ramps. This dual approach can reduce friction, but it adds complexity to your compliance structure.

  • Low entry barrier: No specific license or minimum capital required by law.
  • Cost-effective: Incorporation and compliance costs are lower than in the EU or USA.

Compliance and Trust

Operating without a formal crypto license means you must build trust through strong compliance. Key steps include:

  • Implementing a comprehensive AML program that meets FATF recommendations
  • Conducting KYC on all clients and beneficial owners
  • Maintaining transaction records for at least 5 years
  • Reporting suspicious transactions to the UIF
  • Undergoing periodic audits by an external firm

Common Mistakes

  • Ignoring AML obligations: Even without a license, AML compliance is mandatory. Failure can result in fines or criminal charges.
  • Underestimating banking challenges: Many founders assume they can easily open a bank account; in reality, it is often the hardest part.
  • Not registering with the UIF: If you handle fiat currency, you may need to register as a financial intermediary.
  • Confusing Costa Rica with Panama: Panama has a more established crypto-friendly regime with a formal license; Costa Rica is more informal.
  • Overlooking ongoing costs: Annual compliance, local agent fees, and potential travel for bank meetings add up. Budget at least $5,000 per year for maintenance.

Is there a specific crypto broker license in Costa Rica?
