What Is the Crypto Token Issuance License in Bulgaria?

The crypto token issuance license in Bulgaria is the authorisation required under the Bulgarian Markets in Crypto-Assets Act (implementing MiCA) to offer tokens to the public or seek their admission to trading on a crypto-asset platform. It covers both asset-referenced tokens (ARTs) and e-money tokens (EMTs), as well as other utility tokens that fall under MiCA's scope.

The regulator is the Financial Supervision Commission (FSC). The license is not a single permit but a classification-based authorisation depending on the token type. For example, issuers of ARTs must publish a white paper and obtain FSC approval, while issuers of EMTs must be authorised as credit institutions or e-money institutions. For utility tokens below certain thresholds, only a white paper notification is required, not a full license.

Who Needs This License?

Any legal entity that wishes to offer crypto-assets to the public in Bulgaria or seek their admission to trading on a trading platform for crypto-assets needs this license. This includes:

  • Startups launching utility tokens for fundraising or ecosystem development. For example, a blockchain gaming project issuing an in-game token to EU users.
  • Companies issuing asset-referenced tokens (e.g., stablecoins backed by a basket of assets like a currency index).
  • Firms issuing e-money tokens (e.g., a digital euro equivalent pegged 1:1 to fiat).
  • Entities conducting security token offerings (STOs) that qualify as crypto-assets under MiCA. However, if the token qualifies as a financial instrument under MiFID II, a different license applies.

If you are a non-EU company, you must establish a legal presence in Bulgaria (or another EU member state) to apply. Capital Requirement

Under MiCA, capital requirements for token issuers depend on the token type. These are strictly enforced as of 2026:

  • Asset-Referenced Tokens (ARTs): Minimum capital of EUR 350,000 or 2% of the average amount of reserve assets, whichever is higher. This capital must be held in liquid assets and maintained at all times.
  • E-Money Tokens (EMTs): Minimum capital of EUR 350,000 or the higher amount required under e-money regulations. Issuers must also comply with safeguarding requirements for funds received.
  • Other crypto-assets (utility tokens): No specific minimum capital under MiCA, but issuers must have sufficient financial resources to cover liabilities. Typically, EUR 50,000 is recommended as a prudent buffer for operational risks.

These figures are based on MiCA's requirements as implemented in Bulgarian law. Consulting24 can help you structure your capital to meet these thresholds, including advice on reserve custody and audit requirements.

Tax Treatment

Bulgaria has a flat corporate income tax rate of 10%, one of the lowest in the EU. For token issuers, the tax treatment depends on the token's classification:

  • Utility tokens: Proceeds from issuance may be treated as income, taxed at 10%. VAT may apply if the token provides access to goods or services.
  • Security tokens: Subject to capital gains tax and possibly VAT exemptions. Dividends or profit-sharing may be taxed at 5% withholding tax.
  • Stablecoins (ARTs/EMTs): May be treated as financial instruments, with specific tax rules. Interest or redemption gains could be taxable.

Bulgaria does not have a specific crypto tax law; general tax principles apply. Allowed Activities

With a Bulgarian crypto token issuance license, you can:

  • Issue tokens to the public within Bulgaria and across the EU (passporting under MiCA). This includes conducting an initial offering (ICO, IEO, or STO).
  • Apply for admission of tokens to trading on a crypto-asset trading platform. You can approach platforms like Binance, Kraken, or local EU exchanges.
  • Market and advertise your token offering to EU investors, subject to MiCA marketing rules.
  • Operate a reserve of assets (for ARTs) and manage redemptions in accordance with the white paper.
  • Communicate with token holders and provide ongoing disclosures (e.g., reserve composition, audit reports).

Activities not covered include operating a trading platform or providing custody services, which require separate licenses. For a broader scope, consider exchange license options in other jurisdictions. Consulting24 can advise on which activities fit your business model.

Banking and Payment Services

Opening a bank account for a crypto token issuance company in Bulgaria can be challenging. Bulgarian banks are cautious about crypto-related businesses due to AML concerns. Options include:

  • Traditional banks: Some (e.g., UniCredit Bulbank, DSK Bank) may accept if you have a license and strong compliance. Expect lengthy due diligence (4-8 weeks).
  • EMI accounts: Electronic money institutions like Revolut, Payoneer, or Monese offer faster onboarding, but may have lower transaction limits.
  • Payment processors: Specialised crypto-friendly providers such as Coinbase Commerce, BitPay, or Stripe for fiat ramps.
  • Multi-currency accounts: For holding reserve assets in EUR or USD, consider banks in Lithuania or Estonia that are more crypto-friendly.

Compliance and Trust

Operating under a Bulgarian crypto token issuance license requires strong compliance. Key obligations include:

  • Publishing a white paper with no misleading information. The white paper must be filed with the FSC and made publicly available.
  • Maintaining a reserve of assets (for ARTs) and ensuring redemption rights at all times. Reserves must be held with a qualified custodian.
  • Reporting to the FSC on a periodic basis (e.g., quarterly for ARTs on reserve composition, annually on financial statements).
  • Implementing AML/KYC procedures for investors, including identity verification and transaction monitoring.
  • Appointing a compliance officer and auditor. The auditor must be registered with the Bulgarian Institute of Certified Public Accountants.
  • Conducting regular stress tests and business continuity planning.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Token issuers often make these errors when applying for a Bulgarian license:

  • Misclassifying the token: Incorrect classification leads to wrong requirements and delays. Get expert advice early. For example, a token that gives voting rights may be a security, not a utility token.
  • Incomplete white paper: Missing risk disclosures, technical details, or governance descriptions can cause rejection. The FSC expects a thorough document.
  • Insufficient capital: Underestimating capital needs for ARTs/EMTs can halt the process. Remember that capital must be maintained at all times.
  • Ignoring ongoing compliance: After approval, failing to report or maintain reserves can result in fines or license revocation. Set up a compliance calendar.
  • Choosing the wrong jurisdiction: Bulgaria may not suit every project. Compare with Estonia crypto license or Lithuania crypto license. For a non-EU alternative, Panama is a strong option.
